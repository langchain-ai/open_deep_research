## Definition of “Independent Director” under NASDAQ Rules

An "independent director," as defined by NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2), is a director who is:

- Not an executive officer or employee of the company or any of its subsidiaries; and
- Does not have any relationship that, in the board of directors’ opinion, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director.

This definition is principles-based, focusing on both objective "bright-line" disqualifiers and subjective evaluations of independence. The company’s board must make an "affirmative determination" of each director’s independence for annual disclosures and governance compliance[1][2][3][4].

Ownership of company stock does **not** in itself preclude independence, but relevant facts and relationships—such as business, familial, or financial ties—must be taken into account[5].

Rule 5605 incorporates additional, more restrictive requirements for certain committees (especially the audit committee), referencing both NASDAQ standards and SEC Rule 10A-3[1][3][6].

## Eligibility and Disqualification Criteria

Qualification as an independent director requires both meeting the objective NASDAQ criteria and passing a holistic board review. The following automatically disqualify a director from being considered independent under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2) and related SEC rules:

### Familial Relationships

- **Definition of Family Member:** Includes spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers-/fathers-in-law, sons-/daughters-in-law, brothers-/sisters-in-law, and anyone (excluding domestic employees) who shares the director’s home. Stepchildren are excluded unless they reside in the home or the board determines otherwise[7][8].
- **Employment:** If a director’s family member is, or was, an executive officer of the company or its subsidiaries (parent or otherwise) at any time during the preceding three years, the director is not independent.
- **Other Involvement:** Family ties are scrutinized for potential conflicts of interest, especially in situations involving compensation or executive authority[1][4][7].

### Financial Relationships

- **Compensation:** A director (or a family member) who receives, directly or indirectly, more than $120,000 in compensation from the company or subsidiaries during any 12-month period within the last three years (other than for board service or certain limited, non-discretionary benefits), is not considered independent.
- **Business Transactions:** Directors or their family members acting as partners, controlling shareholders, or executive officers of entities doing business with the company (receiving/payments exceeding the greater of $200,000 or 5% of recipient’s consolidated gross revenues in the current or any of the past three years) are not independent. Charitable contributions do not count for this test[1][4][7].
- **Audit Firms:** If a director (or family member) is a current partner of the company’s outside auditor, or was such a partner or employee who worked on the company’s audit in the past three years, they are not independent[1][3][5][7].

### Professional Relationships

- **Interlocking Directorships:** Service as an executive officer at another company where any of the company’s executive officers serve(d) on its compensation committee within the past three years also disqualifies independence[1][3][4].
- **Employment with Service Providers:** Employment by a professional firm providing substantial services to the company (and exceeding financial thresholds as above) is a disqualification[1][7].

### Other Disqualifications and Board Determinations

- The board must **annually affirm and document** each director’s independence, considering facts and circumstances beyond the outright “bright-line” tests. Relationships not specifically disqualifying may still impair independence in the board's judgment[1][4][5][8].
- **Corporate Secretary Exception:** Uncompensated or non-policy making “corporate secretary” roles do not disqualify a director, per SEC guidance, though compensation or significant involvement would be reviewed closely[9].
- **Stock Ownership:** Simple stock ownership is not viewed as a conflict; only when combined with other relationships does it affect independence[4][5].

## Companies Subject to Independent Director Requirements and Applicable Exemptions

### Domestic Operating Companies

All domestic operating companies listed on any tier of NASDAQ (Global Select, Global, and Capital Markets) must have:

- A **majority** of the board comprising independent directors;
- Audit and compensation committees (and generally nominating/corporate governance committees) consisting **solely** of independent directors, barring explicit exceptions (see below)[1][3][5][10].

### Exempted or Differently Treated Categories

#### Foreign Private Issuers (FPIs)

FPIs may elect to follow their home country’s corporate governance standards **rather than** certain NASDAQ requirements, with the following conditions:

- **Disclosure Required:** The FPI must publicly disclose its choice of home country practices and how these diverge from NASDAQ rules, typically in its annual Form 20-F and on its website.
- **Audit Committee Independence:** No exemption exists from the requirement to have an audit committee that meets SEC Rule 10A-3 independence criteria (at least three members, no affiliations, etc.)[11][12][13].
- **Curing Loss of FPI Status:** If an issuer ceases to be an FPI, it has six months to comply fully with all NASDAQ governance rules[12][13][14].

#### Controlled Companies

Companies with a person, group, or entity holding more than 50% of voting power are “controlled companies.” They:

- Are exempt from the requirement to have a majority independent board or fully independent nominating/compensation committees;
- Are still subject to the requirement for a fully independent audit committee in compliance with SEC Rule 10A-3;
- Must publicly disclose their status and the basis for exemption[3][4][6].

#### Investment Companies, ETFs, ABS Issuers

Certain investment companies and products are exempt:

- **Closed-End Funds:** Must have an audit committee consistent with SEC Rule 10A-3 but may be otherwise exempt.
- **Open-End Funds, ETFs, Asset-Backed Securities Issuers:** Generally exempt from most NASDAQ corporate governance rules, including the majority independent board requirement[3][5][12].

#### Smaller Reporting Companies

These issuers are **not** exempt from board independence or committee requirements. They must comply with the same independence rules as other domestic companies, unless they qualify and claim another exemption (e.g., controlled company)[5][10].

## Committee-Specific Independence Standards

### Audit Committee

- Must be comprised solely of independent directors, both by NASDAQ definition and SEC Rule 10A-3.
- No member may accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee from the company or any subsidiary (other than for board/committee service).
- Must not be an "affiliated person" of the issuer or any subsidiary.
- Requires at least three independent directors, with at least one possessing “financial sophistication.”
- No phase-in or exception for independence once the company is listed—the rule applies immediately, except for certain IPO phase-ins[1][3][5][6][12].

### Compensation Committee

- Must be comprised entirely of independent directors, as defined by NASDAQ Rule 5605(d). Boards must consider all relevant factors, including the source of any compensation (including consulting or advisory fees) paid to the director and any affiliate relationships[3][6].
- At least two independent directors are required, and a written charter specifying committee responsibilities must be adopted and reviewed annually.
- NASDAQ allows a non-independent director to serve under “exceptional and limited circumstances,” with notice and robust public disclosure, but never on the audit committee[3][5][10].

### Nominating/Corporate Governance Committee

- Generally must be fully independent; “controlled companies” are exempt.
- Alternatively, director nominees can be selected/recommended by a majority of independent directors if a separate committee is not established.
- Any use of non-independent directors in “exceptional circumstances” is subject to substantial disclosure requirements and time limits[5][6][13].

## Phase-In, Exceptions, Disclosure, and Regulatory Changes (Including 2026 Updates)

### Phase-In Schedules and Cure Periods

- **IPO and Newly Public Companies:** May phase in full board and committee (especially audit committee) independence:
  - At least one independent audit committee member immediately upon listing;
  - A majority within 90 days;
  - Full compliance (all required members) within one year[3][11][12].
- **Carve-Outs/Spin-Offs/Bankruptcy Emergence:** Similar phase-in periods as IPOs, with deadlines aligned to closing or listing date, and subject to new detailed rule codifications as of August 2024.
- **Cure Periods:** After expiration of the phase-in, companies generally **do not** receive a further cure period unless they were previously in compliance and fall out due to unexpected events. Immediate notice to NASDAQ is required for noncompliance[14][15].

### Special Exceptions and Exceptional Circumstances

- **Exceptional and Limited Circumstances:** A non-independent director may be appointed to the compensation or nominating committee if the board determines the individual is not currently an employee or officer (or family member thereof) and that this is in the best interests of the company and shareholders. Time-limited to two years, with disclosure obligations.
- **Audit Committee:** No “exceptional circumstances” exception applies; all members must be fully independent[1][5][6].

### Disclosure Obligations

- Companies must:
  - File annual proxies (or Form 20-F for FPIs) with detailed information about board and committee independence;
  - Promptly disclose any use of exemptions, deviations, or phase-in periods;
  - Notify NASDAQ of any noncompliance (often via Form 8-K Item 3.01 or equivalent form)[3][5][6][14].

### Recent Regulatory and Enforcement Changes (2025–2026)

- **Phase-In/Cure Period Clarification (2024 rules, effective for 2026):** SEC approved amendments providing detailed, scenario-specific phase-in rules and restricting use of cure periods after phase-in expiration[14][15][16].
- **New Insider Reporting for FPIs (Section 16(a)):** Effective March 18, 2026, directors and officers of most FPIs must file insider trading disclosure reports (Forms 3, 4, and 5) unless exempted by home country rules deemed “substantially similar.” Companies are urged to prepare in advance for these filings[17][18][19][20].
- **Nasdaq Enforcement Discretion:** New rules allow Nasdaq to deny or delist a company if governance, management, or business integrity concerns exist, even if quantitative standards are met. This is aimed at protecting market integrity rather than changing independence definitions[21][22].
- **Diversity Standard Vacated (December 2024):** Board diversity disclosure mandated by the 2021 rule is no longer in effect; board independence remains unchanged[5][16].

### Best Practices and Addressing Ambiguities

- **Annual Questionnaires:** Maintain annual director and officer questionnaires that comprehensively gather relationship information.
- **Board Resolutions:** Document the rationale for each independence determination, especially where relationships fall into “gray areas.”
- **Immediate Updates:** Update independence analysis with any new relationships or changes in fact.
- **Seek Guidance:** Use official NASDAQ resources, SEC guidance, and legal counsel for unique or ambiguous cases[1][5][14].
- **Proactive Disclosure:** When uncertain, err on the side of disclosure in proxy statements and board materials to reduce enforcement risk and meet investor expectations.
