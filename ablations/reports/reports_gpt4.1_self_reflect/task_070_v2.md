# Enforceability of a Verbal Remote Work Promise Under Texas Law: Analysis of Promissory Estoppel, Integration Clauses, and Disputed Facts

## Introduction

This report addresses whether Jordan Hayes, a software engineer, can enforce a verbally promised full remote work arrangement under Texas law through the doctrine of promissory estoppel, despite signing an offer letter with a comprehensive integration/merger clause limiting the agreement to a "hybrid schedule per company policy." Critical facts include Hayes' sale of his Seattle home at a loss, relocation to Texas, and substantial reliance on the manager's verbal promise, which was later confirmed by email but not by an authorized representative. Subsequent company conduct, ambiguity in offer letter language, and exceptions made for other employees further complicate the matter. This analysis systematically applies Texas law and case precedent to each required element, evaluates the role of written contractual terms, and identifies key unresolved issues that could preclude summary judgment.

## I. Elements of Promissory Estoppel Under Texas Law

### A. Clear and Definite Promise

Texas law requires that a promissory estoppel claim be based on a clear and definite promise—statements that are too vague or aspirational are generally insufficient to support reliance damages. According to the Texas Supreme Court in *English v. Fischer*, the promise must be unequivocal and of such a nature that a reasonable person would rely upon it[1]. In practice, courts look for specificity in the alleged promise and the context in which it was made.

- In this case, the manager’s verbal commitment to “full remote work” during negotiations was later confirmed in a June email. Standing alone, this could potentially meet the clarity standard for a promise, especially since Hayes undertook major, specific actions in reliance.

- However, the offer letter Hayes signed after the conversations stated only "hybrid schedule per company policy," which was left undefined in terms of frequency and specifics.

- The presence of such ambiguity in official documents often weighs against finding a “clear promise,” but the manager’s explicit language and confirmation by email (even if after signing) could allow a court to find this prong met, particularly at the summary judgment stage where inferences favor the non-movant[1][2].

### B. Foreseeability and Reasonableness of Reliance

Texas courts enforce promissory estoppel only if it was foreseeable to the promisor that the promisee would rely on the promise, and if such reliance was reasonable and justified[1][3].

- Hayes took significant, costly steps: selling a home at a significant loss, relocating, and signing a Texas lease based on the verbal promise.

- The company actively recruited Hayes and two similarly situated engineers received written remote work exceptions, making reliance more plausible under the circumstances.

- However, the written offer with an explicit merger/integration clause—voiding prior oral representations—and ambiguous “hybrid” language seriously undermines the reasonableness of reliance on earlier verbal statements in the eyes of Texas courts[2][4][5]. If an employee signs a clear, final agreement that does not include the promise, continued reliance on oral side promises is generally deemed unreasonable, especially where the written contract states otherwise.

### C. Substantial Detrimental Reliance

To succeed on promissory estoppel, the injured party must prove substantial, detrimentally induced action, such as changing residence or incurring significant expenses[1][6][7].

- Hayes’ $40,000 loss from selling his home and subsequent relocation are substantial and directly attributable, on the available facts, to the company’s hiring negotiations.

- Texas courts have recognized similar reliance as substantial detriment sufficient to support promissory estoppel in appropriate cases, although, as noted below, recovery is generally limited to expenses incurred rather than lost benefit of the bargain[6][7][8].

### D. Injustice if Promise Is Not Enforced

A final element is that only enforcement of the promise can prevent injustice. The doctrine is meant to address scenarios where the party induced another into detrimental reliance and then attempts to disclaim the promise[1][9].

- On these facts, Hayes cannot return to the status quo ante and incurs unrecoverable costs. The company arguably benefited by filling its position through an inducement.

- However, Texas courts often refuse to find injustice when a written contract states terms contrary to the allegedly relied-upon promise, particularly in the absence of fraud, or where the reliance is not objectively reasonable given merger disclaimers or written contradictions[2][4][5].

## II. Effect of the Merger/Integration Clause and Parol Evidence Rule

### A. General Principles

A merger or integration clause in a Texas contract makes it the sole and exclusive expression of the parties’ agreement, superseding all prior or contemporaneous representations covering the same subject matter[2][4][5][10].

- The parol evidence rule bars introduction of prior oral or written agreements that contradict or modify the written contract.

- In *Barkley v. Connally*, summary judgment was affirmed where a merger clause barred claims based on prior oral promises, and the related promissory estoppel claim was rejected because it would create, effectively, an unwritten side agreement inconsistent with the integrated writing[2].

- Unless the oral promise is collateral to the contract and independently supported, promissory estoppel typically cannot override these clauses in Texas[4][5].

### B. Application to Hayes’ Circumstances

- The offer letter’s merger clause here is strong and explicit, stating that all prior verbal representations are superseded.

- The “hybrid schedule per company policy" language, however, is ambiguous and undefined. If the written contract is ambiguous regarding remote work, Texas courts may allow parol evidence to determine the intent of the parties[11][12].

- The mere presence of a merger/integration clause typically bars estoppel claims based on pre-contractual promises *unless* fraud is involved or the side promise is truly independent, but ambiguity in the written terms and company course of conduct may allow certain evidence to fill in undefined terms[12].

## III. Texas Cases on Promissory Estoppel, Pre-Contractual Promises, and Authority of Company Representatives

### A. Pre-Contractual Promises and Employment Law

- In *Wheeler v. White*, the Texas Supreme Court recognized promissory estoppel where a contractor took costly actions in reliance on a funding commitment. However, the promise was clear, and there was no signed final agreement nullifying that promise[13].

- Texas employment law adheres to a strong at-will presumption. When a written contract exists—including an integration clause—courts are reluctant to override its terms unless fraud, collateral agreement, or legitimate ambiguity is shown[5][7].

- In *Moore' Burger, Inc. v. Phillips Petroleum*, the court looked closely at whether substantial reliance and the nature of the promise justified an estoppel exception, but this was in the context of a statutory requirement, not a clear merger clause[14].

### B. Lack of Authority and Apparent Authority

- An employer is generally bound only by promises made by employees with actual or apparent authority. Oral promises by a manager shown to lack such authority—and especially if the non-binding nature is apparent or evident to the employee—will not bind the company under Texas precedent[6][15].

- Hayes' manager confirmed the remote promise in a June email, but was not authorized by company policy or HR to modify the standard offer terms, and retired shortly after. Courts have generally refused to enforce promises made without authority, unless the circumstances would cause a reasonable person to believe the manager had such authority (apparent authority)[6].

### C. Course of Conduct and Disparate Treatment

- Texas courts recognize that the company’s conduct—permitting Hayes to work fully remote for two months, while contemporaneously granting written exceptions to others—could serve as evidence of intent or waiver, especially if the policy language is ambiguous[12][16].

- However, unless clear evidence of ratification or an established company-wide policy of allowing such remote arrangements exists, these facts typically only create an issue for trial, not for summary judgment[11][16].

## IV. Ambiguity, Company Policy, and Factual Disputes Precluding Summary Judgment

### A. Ambiguity in “Hybrid Schedule per Company Policy”

- “Hybrid schedule per company policy” is undefined in the offer letter. If there was no easily ascertainable written policy at the time, or if the company’s practice was inconsistent, Texas courts may find the language ambiguous[11][12].

- Ambiguity allows for extrinsic evidence of the parties’ intent (including prior promises, the manager’s statements, and company practice), thus precluding summary judgment and requiring fact-finding[11].

### B. Course of Dealing and Peer Exceptions

- Hayes was permitted to work remotely for two months before the policy was formalized, and two peers received written remote work approval. This unequal enforcement, combined with prior assurances, may support Hayes’s claim that company practice varied from official policy[12][16].

- Texas courts have denied summary judgment where employee handbook policies or contractual terms are interpreted one way but the employer acts in another, especially if the company’s conduct undermines its asserted position[11].

### C. Open-Ended, Critical Factual Issues

- **Definition and communication of "company policy" at time of offer and during employment**: Was a remote work arrangement an approved exception? Was policy clearly available, or fluid and unwritten?

- **Authority of the Promising Manager**: Did Hayes have reason to believe the manager’s promise was authorized? Did HR or company executives ratify the arrangement by silence or action?

- **Documentation and Intent**: Is there further documentation (emails, HR communications) supporting Hayes' understanding of the work arrangement?

- **Basis for Peer Remote Approvals**: Are these exceptions routinely made, or is Hayes being unfairly denied a similarly situated benefit?

When such genuine disputes of material fact exist, Texas law precludes summary judgment for either party and the dispute must go to a jury[11][12].

## V. Assessment of Legal Likelihood and Practical Outlook

### A. Likelihood Hayes Can Enforce the Remote Promise on Summary Judgment

- Texas courts are generally deferential to the express language of written contracts, particularly those with merger/integration clauses, and are reluctant to let promissory estoppel defeat clear terms absent ambiguity, fraud, or unauthorized promises being ratified[2][5][7][10][11].

- In this scenario, significant barriers exist: the merger clause, absence of clear written remote approval, and manager’s lack of authority.

- Unless Hayes can show either (1) genuine ambiguity in “hybrid schedule per company policy” or (2) that the company, through conduct or ratification, effectively accepted a remote arrangement, summary judgment for Hayes is unlikely.

- However, factual disputes remain regarding the meaning and implementation of "hybrid," the company conduct (both initial remote allowance and differential treatment of peers), and how reasonable Hayes' reliance was in view of all communications.

- As a result, summary judgment for the company is also not assured. If a factfinder could reasonably conclude that (a) the company's course of conduct modified or clarified the agreement, (b) the offer language is ambiguous, or (c) apparent authority/rubber-stamping of exceptions occurred, the case would almost certainly proceed to trial[11][12][16].

### B. Most Likely Outcome

- Hayes is unlikely to win on summary judgment, but the presence of significant disputed facts would likely prevent summary judgment for the company.

- The ultimate outcome will hinge on the factfinder’s resolution of open-ended questions regarding company policy clarity, the scope of authority and ratification, reasonableness of Hayes's reliance, and whether an injustice can be avoided only by enforcing the remote promise.

## VI. Key Recommendations and Next Steps

- Seek discovery regarding:
    - The definition and availability of company remote work/hybrid policy at the time of hiring
    - Internal communications about the promises made to Hayes and peers
    - The company’s policy and procedure for exceptions and whether HR or executives knowingly ratified the remote work arrangement
    - Treatment and documentation of remote exceptions for similarly situated employees

- Hayes (or similarly situated employees) should document all communications and, where possible, seek express written confirmation from HR or legal representatives before relying on significant promises not memorialized in official documents.

- Companies are strongly encouraged to review and define their hybrid/remote work policies and ensure all offer letters both accurately reflect intended terms and are supported by proper authorization processes.
