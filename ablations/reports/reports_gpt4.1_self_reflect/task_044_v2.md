# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms: California, European Union, and China (2020–2024)

## Introduction

Carbon pricing is a cornerstone of global climate mitigation strategy. The three largest carbon pricing systems—California’s Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China’s National Carbon Market—provide unique models reflecting regional priorities, regulatory maturity, and economic structure. This comparative analysis covers 2020–2024, emphasizing allowance price trajectories, quantified emissions reductions versus baseline projections, revenue generation and allocation, compliance performance, COVID-19-related market responses, integration with complementary policies, and evidence for carbon leakage and border measures. The report synthesizes official data, peer-reviewed studies, and primary program documents to inform global policy design.

---

## 1. Allowance Price Trajectories (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade

- Allowance auction prices trended upward post-pandemic, climbing from ~$17.80/ton in 2021 to over $41 in early 2024, reflecting economic recovery and expectations for climate ambition.
- In May 2024, prices fell 11%, attributed to regulatory uncertainty and potential cap tightening; the price floor rose annually, reaching $25.87 in 2025, with a 2024 price ceiling at $88.22 to limit cost volatility.
- The compound annual price growth from 2019 to 2023 was 13.6%, but volatility increased in 2024 as policy reforms were debated[1][2][3][4].

### European Union ETS

- EUA (European Union Allowance) prices nearly tripled from €24 in January 2020 to more than €80 by late 2023. In 2024, average prices settled around €75, down from a peak of €105.73 in early 2023, but are projected to trend higher in the late 2020s as the cap tightens and new sectors enter the system.
- The Market Stability Reserve (MSR) played a key role in withdrawing surplus allowances (267 million in 2024–2025), stabilizing prices and preventing extreme fluctuation[5][6][7][8][9].

### China National Carbon Market

- The national ETS started full operation in 2021, with allowance prices rising from CNY 40–60/ton (~$6–$9) to CNY 98/ton (~$13.40) by 2024—a 50% increase year-on-year.
- On April 24, 2024, the price exceeded CNY 100/ton for the first time, with continuing growth linked to expanded sector coverage and tightening benchmarks[10][11][12][13][14].

## 2. Emissions Reductions Achieved Versus Baseline Projections

### California Cap-and-Trade

- The program achieved its 2020 emissions reduction target ahead of schedule. Emissions fell 8% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels by 2022 despite economic growth—a reduction surpassing baseline projections for covered sectors.
- Power sector emissions dropped 48% below a modeled counterfactual due largely to policy-driven fuel switching.
- The system ensures compliance with an overarching 2030 target (48% below 1990 levels) and carbon neutrality by 2045, acting as a backstop for sector-specific regulations[5][15][18].

### EU ETS

- Between 2005 and 2024, emissions from stationary installations in the EU fell by approximately 51%.
- In 2023, covered emissions fell 16.5% year-on-year, with a further 5.8–7% decline in 2024, well ahead of cap trajectories. The system is on track for a 62% reduction from 2005 levels by 2030, with projections of up to 66% if current trends and policy enhancements continue.
- The sharpest reductions occurred in the power sector, driven by both carbon pricing and renewables deployment; industrial cuts have been more gradual, with aviation emissions rebounding post-pandemic[6][7][8][10].

### China National Carbon Market

- In 2024, the national ETS covered over 2,200 enterprises, representing about 5.1 billion tonnes of CO2 (more than 40% of national emissions). Expansion to steel, cement, and aluminum in late 2024 increased coverage to over 60%.
- Pilot ETS programs achieved 18.2% emissions reductions versus site baselines. Power sector emissions have stabilized, and renewables now outpace thermal capacity.
- National total emissions have been flat or declining since March 2024; however, China is set to narrowly miss its official carbon intensity target for 2020–2025, emphasizing the need for stronger price signals and sectoral expansion[10][11][17][19].

## 3. Revenue Generation and Allocation to Climate or Equity Programs

### California Cap-and-Trade

- The program generated more than $28 billion in auction revenue by 2024, with nearly $33 billion projected by end-2025.
- Revenues flow into the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF), supporting 89+ programs administered by 24 state agencies, with at least 35% mandated for disadvantaged and low-income communities.
- Key allocations include utility bill credits ($16 billion to electricity, $6.9 billion to gas), affordable housing, rail and transit expansion, wildfire prevention, and community air protection[3][6][7][15][21].

### EU ETS

- More than €258 billion in total auction revenues since 2013, with nearly €44 billion generated in 2023 and €24.4 billion flowing directly to member states in 2024.
- Post-2023, Member States must allocate 100% of revenue to climate and energy transition, funding the Innovation Fund, Modernisation Fund, and the €65–87 billion Social Climate Fund.
- Major spend items include renewables development, energy efficiency upgrades, clean technology deployment, and support for vulnerable populations during the green transition[8][12][13][14].

### China National Carbon Market

- Total transaction value exceeded CNY 43 billion (~$6 billion) by end-2024, with 2024 alone seeing a record CNY 18.1 billion in activity.
- Specific public reporting on the allocation of revenues to climate or social programs is limited; revenue allocation mechanisms are evolving, especially as the market matures and sectoral expansion proceeds. The recently re-launched China Certified Emission Reduction (CCER) scheme channels investment into renewable energy and carbon abatement projects[1][4][6][18].

## 4. Compliance Rates Among Regulated Entities

### California Cap-and-Trade

- Compliance rates have consistently approached 100%, ensured by strict verification and penalty mechanisms.
- All compliance cycles through 2024 reported full allowance surrender from regulated entities[2][11].

### EU ETS

- By 2024, compliance remained extremely high, supported by annual verified reporting, accredited third-party audits, and financial penalties (€100/ton excess emissions, inflation-adjusted).
- Deadline enforcement intensified as monitoring expanded to maritime transport and smaller new sectors[17].

### China National Carbon Market

- Compliance rates neared 99.9% or higher in all years since launch; in 2023, 99.98% of covered entities were compliant.
- Robust digital MRV systems and severe fines (up to 10 times the violation value) underpin high performance[1][3][5][9].

## 5. Market Responses to COVID-19 and Recovery

### California Cap-and-Trade

- Emissions fell sharply in 2020 due to the COVID-19 economic shock; allowance prices leveled but did not collapse.
- No major emergency rule or banking changes were implemented. The primary effect was a build-up of banked allowances due to emission slowdowns.
- Policy focus and regulatory schedules shifted but the system proved resilient, recovering rapidly in 2021–2022[1][12].

### EU ETS

- EUA prices dipped briefly during the 2020 shutdowns but rapidly rebounded as economic activity and political consensus for green recovery policies took hold.
- The MSR absorbed surplus permits and helped maintain price stability. Cumulative reductions in emissions were “locked in” due to allowance invalidation rules.
- The pandemic highlighted the ETS’s adaptability and reinforced the rationale for integrating climate and economic recovery[8][18][21].

### China National Carbon Market

- No documented temporary rule changes or compliance extensions were reported.
- Market operations, sectoral expansion, and compliance cycles remained on schedule, with steady increases in volume and price.
- The pandemic period is characterized as a “proactive and strategic” phase—continued operation and expansion, with no pandemic-induced slowdown[3][6].

## 6. Interactions with Complementary Policies (Renewables, Coal Phase-Out)

### California Cap-and-Trade

- The program works in tandem with California’s Renewable Portfolio Standard (60% renewables by 2030, 100% by 2045), Low Carbon Fuel Standard, vehicle policies, and energy efficiency mandates.
- Cap-and-Trade acts as a “backstop” to ensure emission targets; research demonstrates that most emission reductions in the power sector resulted from both Cap-and-Trade and strict renewables/efficiency rules.
- Regular policy updates coordinate renewables accounting (via WREGIS), air quality, and environmental justice goals[5][15][18][17].

### EU ETS

- A surge in renewables (solar up 24.6% in 2023–24) and ongoing coal phase-outs have amplified ETS-driven emissions reductions.
- National energy plans and the Fit for 55 package introduced stricter caps, accelerated renewables deployment, and a 2030 full coal exit goal for the power sector.
- Interactions occasionally create policy overlap but generally reinforce emissions reductions, with cost containment through renewables subsidies and just transition funding[20][23][24].

### China National Carbon Market

- The ETS interacts with China’s massive renewables buildout: as of 2025, renewables outpace thermal capacity, and additional market-based mechanisms (RECs, CCER) direct investment to clean energy.
- The ETS’s intensity-based allocation design incentivizes efficiency in coal-fired plants but does not currently drive fuel switching to renewables as strongly as EU/CA models.
- A transition to absolute caps and further integration with coal phase-out and regional renewable targets is underway[16][17][18][19].

## 7. Carbon Leakage and Border Adjustment Mechanisms

### California Cap-and-Trade

- Generous free allocation for trade-exposed sectors (100% of industry allocation to 2030) mitigated leakage risk; simulation and empirical studies found modest leakage in electricity but limited evidence elsewhere.
- Resource shuffling (allocating high-emission generation outside state boundaries) identified as a challenge but addressed through power sector accounting improvements.
- Linkage discussions (with Quebec, potentially Washington) and potential for border carbon adjustments remain ongoing[13][20][21].

### EU ETS

- Historically, carbon leakage risk was addressed via generous free allocation; 63 industrial sectors classified as leakage-prone.
- The EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), legislated in 2023, phases in from 2026, replacing free allocation for key imports (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, hydrogen, electricity). First CBAM price: €75.36/ton (April 2026).
- Empirical studies suggest prior leakage was limited, but CBAM is projected to accelerate decarbonization in trading partners, especially as it applies to indirect emissions in later years.
- Maritime sector inclusion in 2024 presents new leakage/competition challenges for EU ports versus neighboring non-EU hubs[24][27][31][35].

### China National Carbon Market

- No significant evidence of outward carbon leakage (industrial relocation); a recent study found slight contraction in the outward investment of regulated firms, suggesting regulatory caution but not wholesale relocation.
- China’s response to EU CBAM includes strengthening its ETS, expanding coverage, and aligning carbon accounting to maintain competitiveness.
- Ongoing concerns that CBAM could affect specific sectors (steel, aluminum), but projected direct macro leakage is <0.2%. China views CBAM as a de facto trade barrier and promotes multilateral coordination[21][24].

## 8. Recent Developments, Open Questions, and Future Research

- **California:** The Cap-and-Trade Program was extended (AB 1207, SB 840, 2025) as “Cap-and-Invest,” through 2045, with expanded environmental justice mandates, new offset rules, scheduled decline in allowance supply, and pending linkage discussions. Facility-level equity caps, market reserves, and refinements to free allocation post-2030 are under public consultation[8][14][17][21][22].
- **EU ETS:** Scope expansion (shipping 2024, waste 2025–26, road transport/buildings ETS2 in 2027), CBAM rollout, a 2030 cap for a 62% reduction, and the Social Climate Fund are in implementation. Ongoing debates surround MSR and interaction with national climate plans.
- **China:** The launch of the nation’s first specialized climate legislation (Interim Regulations, May 2024), deployment of rigorous MRV, expansion to new sectors, and a roadmap toward absolute emission caps (expected pre-2027) are critical milestones. The allocation and earmarking of carbon trading revenues remain insufficiently transparent; integration with renewables and a just transition are flagged priorities.

**Open Issues Include:**
- For all regions, the future coordination of carbon pricing and sector-specific policies, tightening supply to meet net-zero ambitions, transparent revenue use, and integrating justice/equity priorities.
- For the EU, potential trade friction from CBAM pending WTO outcomes and impact on developing country exporters.
- For China, clarity around revenue use, further sectoral expansion, and the shift from intensity-based to absolute cap design.

---

## Conclusion

California, the EU, and China have advanced carbon pricing as a tool to drive emissions reductions and climate investment, each tailoring design to jurisdictional priorities and industrial structure. The EU ETS stands out for its price signal maturity, sectoral coverage, and evolving border adjustments. California demonstrates strong integration with complementary policies, robust revenue recycling, and environmental justice innovation. China has achieved rapid market expansion, high compliance, and significant emission intensity gains, but faces challenges aligning revenue allocation with climate priorities and transitioning to an absolute cap-and-trade model.

Despite diverse approaches, all three systems underline the importance of:
- Flexibility and resilience in the face of economic shocks;
- Integrating carbon markets with comprehensive domestic climate policy and green investment;
- High-quality MRV and compliance enforcement;
- Ongoing attention to carbon leakage and equity, particularly as international carbon border measures proliferate.

Continued rigorous evaluation and responsive design will be critical to sustaining emissions reductions and influencing the evolution of global climate policy in the years ahead.
