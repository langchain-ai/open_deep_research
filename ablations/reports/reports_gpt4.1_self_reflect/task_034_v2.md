# Comparative Analysis of Vertical Integration Strategies in Global Cocoa Processing: Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi), 2018–2024

## Introduction

From 2018 to 2024, Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) have been the dominant global cocoa processors, shaping supply chains through ambitious vertical integration, upstream investments in West African cocoa farming cooperatives, and leading sustainability certification programs. This report presents a detailed comparative analysis of their approaches to direct sourcing versus broker-purchased cocoa, capital expenditure in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana, farmer income impacts, compliance with the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), and the resulting effects on their ability to secure premium chocolate manufacturing contracts. All findings are sourced from direct company disclosures, industry reports, trade publications, and regulatory filings.

## Barry Callebaut

### Upstream Investments and Vertical Integration

Barry Callebaut uses a fully integrated model, connecting farm-level production to finished chocolate via longstanding partnerships, owned subsidiaries, and deep engagement with cocoa farming cooperatives. Its signature initiatives—“Forever Chocolate” and “Cocoa Horizons”—emphasize direct technical support, agroforestry, and traceability innovation powered by digital solutions such as the Katchilè platform. By 2025, the company aims to have lifted over 500,000 cocoa farmers out of poverty, covering over 557,000 farmers as of the 2024/25 season[1][2][3][4].

### Sourcing Mix: Direct vs. Broker-Purchased

- As of 2024, approximately 40% of Barry Callebaut’s cocoa is directly sourced from farmers, cooperatives, or local traders; the remainder is brokered through intermediaries[5][6].
- Sustainable or certified sourcing has increased: 47% in 2018/19 to over half by 2023/24[7].
- Farm-level traceability for direct supply chains reached 83% in 2023/24, with over 669,000 cocoa farm plots mapped[1][8].

### Capital Expenditures in West Africa

- Operates six major processing facilities in Côte d’Ivoire and significant capacity in Ghana, including in the Tema Export Processing Zone[9].
- While precise site-level capex is undisclosed, ongoing investments have modernized facilities with sustainability, traceability, and digital monitoring tools, underlining readiness for regulatory shifts such as EUDR[1][9][10].
- Sector context: Côte d’Ivoire’s primary processing capacity approached 1 million tonnes in 2024, equivalent to around EUR 1 billion cumulative sectoral capex, with Barry Callebaut as a leading contributor[11][12].

### Documented Changes in Farmer Incomes

- By 2024/25, more than 557,700 farmers in Barry Callebaut’s supply chain have risen above the World Bank $1.90/day poverty line—a 30% increase year-on-year[2][13].
- Programs provide direct input support, farm business planning, premium payments, women’s empowerment, and financial inclusion, with significant increases in yields (60% improvement reported from key programs) and more than 197,000 farmers receiving on-farm services in 2023/24[2][13][14].
- Independent impact studies show that less than 10% of farmers currently achieve full living income, but the share above poverty benchmarks is steadily rising[13][15].

### EUDR Compliance and Traceability

- Barry Callebaut is an early advocate for EUDR, mapping 669,174 farm plots by 2023/24 and reaching 100% traceability in Ghana, and 40% in Côte d’Ivoire[1][6][16].
- Uses GPS, satellite analytics, and its Katchilè digital platform to document supplier compliance and ensure exclusion of cocoa from protected or deforested areas.
- All cocoa destined for the EU is mapped to farm level as of 2024/25, with annual public disclosure of supply chain maps and active exclusion of non-compliant volumes[1][17].
- Remains committed to being fully EUDR compliant by the legal deadline: “We have now excluded any cocoa purchases from farms fully or partly located within a protected area boundary”[6].

### Competitive Position: Premium Contracts

- Barry Callebaut commands ~25% global market share and is established as a preferred supplier to Lindt, Ferrero, and Mondelēz due to its vertically integrated, traceable supply platform[18][19].
- Its BC Next Level program advances digitalization, factory optimization, and customer-tailored sustainability, addressing premium customer demands around compliance, ingredient transparency, and climate action[1][20].
- The company’s traceability leadership and certified supply underpin its ability to secure and maintain premium contracts in a market where regulatory compliance and ESG claims are decisive factors[21].

## Cargill

### Upstream Investments and Vertical Integration

Cargill’s “Cocoa Promise” centers on professionalizing and empowering farmer organizations, supporting robust cooperative governance, and investing in digital innovation such as polygon farm mapping and traceability (CocoaWise™ platform). Partnerships with NGOs and sector institutions facilitate broad-based income and social infrastructure support[22][23].

### Sourcing Mix: Direct vs. Broker-Purchased

- As of 2024, Cargill reports that 54% of cocoa volumes are sourced sustainably (certified under Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade, or Cargill Promise Verified)[24].
- Exact direct vs. brokered sourcing share is not published; however, Cargill’s direct supply chain in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana is fully polygon mapped and digitally traceable from farm to first purchase—the gold standard for EUDR compliance[24][25].
- Approximately 76.6% of Promise program farmers utilize first-mile digital traceability[25].

### Capital Expenditures in West Africa

- Over $113 million invested since 2019: $100 million for expansion at Yopougon (Côte d’Ivoire) and $13 million at Tema (Ghana), increasing respective capacities by 50% and 20%[26][27].
- Additional $12.3 million in sustainability and traceability, supporting local livelihoods, child labor prevention, GPS mapping, and digitalized cooperative management[26][28].
- Yopougon is now Africa’s largest cocoa grinding facility, reflecting a commitment to local value addition and regulatory alignment[27].

### Documented Changes in Farmer Incomes

- Over 211,000 certified farmers in West Africa are part of Cargill’s Cocoa Promise, receiving training, input support, and income diversification assistance.
- Specific case: PROCOCO farmers in Ghana increased earnings from $72 to $239 through improved practices and financial access[29].
