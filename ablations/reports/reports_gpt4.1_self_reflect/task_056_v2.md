# Comprehensive Analysis of the New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market (2018–2024)

## Introduction

This report provides an exhaustive analysis of New Zealand’s construction metalworks sector as it relates specifically to structural steel fabrication from 2018 to 2024. The analysis covers market size and growth (including revenue, employment, and tonnage), identifies and profiles the top five fabricators as of 2023, outlines New Zealand’s import tariff regime for raw construction-grade steel, profiles three major infrastructure projects (including City Rail Link), and evaluates the top three strategic risks and opportunities in the current market environment. All data points are referenced to official or industry-leading sources, and any public data limitations are clearly flagged.

---

## Market Size and Growth (2018–2024)

### Revenue and Industry Value

- The value of New Zealand’s Structural Steel Fabricating industry was approximately NZ$1.8 billion in 2025 and remained at that level through 2026. The sector saw a -6.0% contraction in 2025 and is projected to shrink a further -3.3% in 2026. Between 2021 and 2026, the market showed a modest positive CAGR of 2.1%, reflecting a volatile five-year period shaped by COVID-19 impacts, project pipeline fluctuations, and economic headwinds[1].
- An alternative estimate from Grand View Research cites the market size at USD 50.3 million in 2024, expected to rise to USD 68.5 million by 2030 (CAGR ~6%), with non-residential construction being the primary demand driver[2].
- The industry’s client base is weighted towards non-residential and infrastructure, with structural steel holding more than 50% share in multilevel construction and over 80% in Christchurch, highlighting its seismic advantages[3].

### Employment

- As of 2025, the industry employed approximately 4,100 people, with forecasts showing a slight decline to around 4,011 by 2026. Employment shrank by an average of -8.9% per year from 2020 to 2025, but the rate of decline slowed to a projected -0.9% per year from 2021–2026. The sector is composed of around 153 fabrication companies, implying an average of 15 employees per business[4][5][6].
- Despite falls in headcount, revenue per employee has increased, and the industry heavily invests in formal training and apprenticeships; over 10% of the workforce is in active skills development[6].

### Tonnage of Structural Steel Fabricated

- Sector capacity is estimated at 120,000–150,000 tonnes per annum, with output typically around 100,000 tonnes each year for construction-related projects[6]. The industry maintained an estimated 28% spare capacity for the year ending March 2024, reflecting resilience and ability to respond to surges in project demand[6].
- Investments in new facilities and technology have increased total installed capacity over the period, supporting rapid response to local demand peaks led by infrastructure and seismic-driven rebuilding[6].

### Data Gaps and Caveats

- Official annual revenue, employment, and tonnage fabricated by year (2018–2024) are not available from government sources such as Stats NZ or MBIE. Available figures are snapshots or averages over multi-year periods published by IBISWorld and industry associations, not time-series breakdowns[1][4][6].
- Official databases (Stats NZ Infoshare, MBIE Sector Trends) provide aggregated data for the broad manufacturing or construction sector but do not isolate structural steel fabricating alone[7][8][9][10].

## New Zealand Import Tariff Rates for Construction-Grade Raw Steel (HS 7207–7216) and Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

### Tariff Rates by Product (2024)

- Most construction-grade raw steel imports under HS codes 7207–7216 enter New Zealand at **0% tariff (duty free)**, thanks to sweeping trade liberalisations and multiple FTAs[21][22][23].
- MFN (Most Favoured Nation) standard tariff rates are **5%** for certain steel products, but the majority of structural steel inputs are “free” (0%) for imports from Australia, China, ASEAN and many others[21][22][24].
- Tariff schedules and current chapter-by-chapter rates can be checked in the [Working Tariff Document of New Zealand][21].
- Where applicable, tariff concessions are also available for certain project or product types, as detailed in Parts II and Customs Notices[25][26].

**How to Check Specific Product Tariffs**:  
Users should consult the [Working Tariff Document][21] for the current HS subheading rate and eligibility for preferential rates. The searchable [BorderWise][21] system also allows fast rate lookup.

### Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

- Standard import tariffs on raw steel have minimal direct impact on fabricator margins, as most primary construction inputs are imported duty-free under various FTAs[22][23][24].
- No NZ government-published analysis exists for the isolated impact of MFN steel tariffs (5%) on downstream fabricator profits. However, recent MBIE anti-dumping/injury reports confirm that higher tariffs or anti-dumping duties (ranging 2.5–12.6%) imposed on imported steel can increase fabrication costs and reduce margins unless these are passed to end customers[27].
- Fabricators are far more exposed to swings in global prices, shipping rates, and FX risk than to NZ Customs import tariffs, given the open trade environment[23][28].

**Explicit Data Gaps**:  
No standalone New Zealand government report quantifies the direct impact of current standard steel tariffs on local structural steel fabricator margins as of 2024. This absence is explicitly flagged.[27]

### 1. City Rail Link (Auckland)

- **Description**: Auckland’s largest-ever transport project—3.45 km underground twin rail tunnels, new and upgraded stations
- **Estimated Structural Steel Demand**: ~4,000 tonnes of structural steel (plus ~25,000 tonnes reinforcing steel)[29][30][31]
- **Completion Timeline**: Construction due to complete by November 2025, operational launch in 2026[32][33][34]

- **Description**: 25,000-seat (expandable to 37,000) covered stadium, key anchor project after the Canterbury earthquakes

## Strategic Risks and Opportunities for Structural Steel Fabricators (2024)

### 1. Project Pipeline Uncertainty and Economic Conditions

- Construction sector activity contracted in 2024–2025, with a fall in total project pipeline value. Residential and non-residential consents have dropped 22% and 12% year-on-year respectively, and construction insolvencies rose by 37% to May 2025[40][41].
- Rising deferrals in public-sector projects and uncertainty around infrastructure funding have suppressed forward order books for fabricators, who are highly reliant on the pipeline of large and infrastructure-led builds[41][8].

### 2. Raw Material Supply Chain Volatility and Trade Exposure

- Structural steel fabrication relies heavily on imported raw materials. While tariffs are effectively zero for most key sources, volatility around global steel prices, rising shipping costs (Red Sea disruptions), and major external tariff hikes (e.g., US tariffs of 50% on NZ steel exports) all add material risk[42][43][44].
- Market overcapacity—especially from Asia, notably oversupplied Chinese mills—can suppress local prices and pressure margins, while supply chain delays and FX swings can rapidly alter input cost structures[42][43].
- Dependence on a narrow range of source countries for key grades and specialty steels increases business continuity risk in the event of further logistics or trade crises[43][44].

### 3. Decarbonization, Technological Development, and Workforce Constraints

- Regulatory and client demands for low-carbon, green, and traceable steel are rising. Initiatives such as Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) deployment (e.g., at NZ Steel) create competitive advantage, especially where sustainable procurement is mandated[45]. The sector recycles 95% of steel waste, and new capacity for “green steel” is coming online.
- The sector continues to face acute skills shortages and high staff turnover, exacerbated by wage competition from Australia and the inflow of inexperienced new entrants. This drives up costs for training and compliance and suppresses productivity[46][47].
- Technology adoption (automation, digital twins, advanced analytics) has accelerated, with the sector investing over NZ$780 million in digital transformation in 2024 alone, but data silos and supply chain fragmentation still impede productivity gains[47].

**Opportunities**:

- Increasing demand for steel in renewable energy and resilient construction (earthquake, flood, and climate adaptation infrastructure)[45].
- High spare capacity (~28%), world-class compliance, and a robust base of SFC-certified fabricators position NZ suppliers to meet projected domestic renewables and infrastructure demand, especially as the construction cycle recovers post-2026[6][45].
