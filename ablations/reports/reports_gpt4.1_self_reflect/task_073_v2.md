# Comprehensive Comparison of Autism Diagnosis: DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019) with Emphasis on Sensory Processing

## Introduction

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is primarily diagnosed using two international standards: the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition (DSM-5), published by the American Psychiatric Association, and the International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision (ICD-11), established by the World Health Organization. Both systems underwent major revisions in the past decade, notably by incorporating sensory processing differences as part of their diagnostic frameworks. This report offers a thorough, evidence-based analysis of the similarities and differences between DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria for autism diagnosis, focusing heavily on the definition and importance of sensory processing differences. The report also integrates the latest research comparing diagnostic performance, assesses positions from guidelines and advocacy organizations, examines diagnostic and service risks, and summarizes coding and payer issues.

## Side-by-Side Comparison Table: DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 Autism Diagnosis

|---------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Core Social-Communication Criteria** | - Persistent deficits in social communication and interaction across multiple contexts. <br> - Requires all three: social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal communicative behaviors, relationships. | - Persistent deficits in initiating and sustaining reciprocal social communication and interaction, outside the expected range for age and level. <br> - Emphasis on verbal and nonverbal understanding, empathy, relationship maintenance based on age/cognition[1][2][3][4]. |
| **Sensory Processing Mention/Weighting** | - Explicitly included as one of four types of restricted/repetitive behaviors (Criterion B4: “hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input or unusual interests in sensory aspects”). <br> - Must exhibit at least two of four RRBs, so sensory may or may not be present[1][2][3][5]. | - Unusual sensory sensitivities or responses included as one possible (not required) feature of restricted, repetitive, and inflexible behaviors. <br> - Sensory symptoms now explicitly named in criteria, reflecting current science, but not mandatory for diagnosis[3][6][7]. |
| **Severity Specifiers** | - Three levels of severity for both social communication and RRBs: Level 1 (needs support), Level 2 (substantial support), Level 3 (very substantial support). <br> - Severity is tied to current support needs in each domain[2][3][5]. | - No formal severity levels. <br> - Profiles specified by the presence or absence of intellectual and/or language impairment, and any history of developmental regression. <br> - Functional impact and individual description emphasized[4][6]. |
| **Age/Onset Requirements** | - Symptoms present in early developmental period, but may not be fully manifest until social demands increase or may be masked/compensated[2][3][4][5]. | - Onset during developmental period, typically early childhood. <br> - Diagnosable even if full symptoms emerge later, especially under increased demands[4][6]. |
| **Functional Impairment Thresholds** | - Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other essential areas[2][5]. | - Symptoms result in significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other major areas, even if masked by effort[4][6]. |

## Synthesis of Peer-Reviewed Research: Sensitivity, Specificity, and Clinical Utility

### 1. Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses

- **Critical Reflection on ICD-11 vs. DSM-5**
  - Type: Conceptual critical review (Nature, 2023)
  - Highlights: ICD-11 permits a vast array of symptom combinations without mandatory criteria, resulting in diagnostic heterogeneity and reduced specificity compared to DSM-5. Clinical utility is challenged by overlaps with other disorders and “autism-like” traits. The broadened criteria may result in imprecision, higher rates of false positives, and hinder progress toward precision medicine and reproducibility of research[8].
  - GRADE: Narrative/opinion (cannot be directly assessed by standard GRADE, but methodologically strong as an expert, peer-reviewed review)
  - [8] 

### 2. Primary Empirical Studies

- **Sensitivity and Specificity of DSM-5 Criteria (McPartland et al., 2012)**
  - Design: Secondary analysis of DSM-IV field trial data
  - Sample: 933 individuals (657 ASD, 276 non-ASD)
  - Findings: DSM-5 criteria identified 60.6% of clinical ASD cases (overall sensitivity), with high specificity (94.9%). Sensitivity was lower in cognitively able (IQ ≥ 70) and those with "Asperger's"/PDD-NOS (25–28%), highlighting risk of missed diagnoses in these groups. DSM-5 is highly specific but may exclude people with subtler or masked presentations[9].
  - GRADE: Moderate-to-high (large sample, published in leading journal)
  - [9]

- **Sensitivity and Specificity of DSM-5 in Spanish Child/Adolescent Sample (Blázquez Hinojosa et al., 2021)**
  - Design: Retrospective review using ADI-R/clinical records
  - Sample: 118 children/adolescents
  - Findings: DSM-5 sensitivity ranged from 0.69–1.00 (higher in females), specificity 0.64–0.73. Most “autism disorder” cases retained ASD diagnosis, but many with PDD-NOS did not, with some shifting to social (pragmatic) communication disorder. Supports prior data showing DSM-5 may under-identify those with less typical profiles[10].
  - GRADE: Moderate (strong design, limited by moderate sample size and retrospective approach)
  - [10]

- **Comparative Literature and Summary Points**
  - Most empirical literature addresses DSM-5 versus DSM-IV-TR, with limited direct head-to-head studies of DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 due to ICD-11’s recent roll-out. Conceptual and expert reviews note that ICD-11's broader, less prescriptive approach may increase inclusivity for atypical and marginalized presentations, but at the potential cost of diagnostic precision, increased heterogeneity, and challenges in research translation[8].
  - Research consistently agrees that DSM-5 is more prescriptive and likely to under-identify individuals whose presentations are less stereotypically “classic,” especially intellectually able, female, or minority individuals, and those with predominantly sensory or masked features[9][10].

## Clinical Guideline Positions: WHO, APA, AAP, NICE

### World Health Organization (WHO)

- **ICD-11** is the global diagnostic standard for ASD, guiding service design, data collection, and public health worldwide. ICD-11 explicitly includes sensory sensitivities as part of the diagnostic requirements and treats autism as a neurodevelopmental disorder[6][11]. ICD-11 criteria are designed for global health system applicability and flexibility, accounting for cultural variations in diagnosis[4][11].

### American Psychiatric Association (APA)

- **DSM-5** (and 2022 DSM-5-TR) remains the US reference for clinical and research diagnosis of ASD. Sensory symptoms are included explicitly as one of four RRBs (B4), and severity levels are required for each core domain. The APA has not expressed a position on ICD-11 adoption for ASD in the US and continues to reference DSM-5 in guidelines and practice[2][5][12].

### American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

- Endorses **DSM-5** as the diagnostic system for ASD in US pediatric care, recommending thorough developmental history, DSM-5-focused interview, behavioral and sensory assessment, and team-based approach. Sensory assessment is considered important, but there is no guideline for special weighting of sensory processing relative to other core features. There is no official AAP guidance recommending transition to ICD-11 for US practice at this time[13][14].

### National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, UK)

- **NICE** guidelines for children and adults endorse both ICD-11 and DSM-5 for autism diagnosis. UK NHS services predominantly use ICD-11 as per WHO guidance. NICE mandates that sensory sensitivities be included in assessment but not given greater diagnostic weight than social interaction/communication and behavioral features. Diagnosis must be multidisciplinary, holistic, and include individual support needs[15][16].

## Autism Advocacy Organization Perspectives on Neurodiversity-Affirming Diagnosis

### Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

- Supported consolidation of subtypes into a single-spectrum diagnosis in DSM-5, inclusion of sensory sensitivity, and explicit recognition of masking/camouflaging behaviors. Criticizes severity levels when used as service gatekeeping and calls for criteria that better encompass the diverse lived experiences of autistic people, particularly women, minorities, and non-speaking individuals. Wants diagnostic frameworks to align more closely with neurodiversity values by broadening valid presentations and ensuring access to support, regardless of masking or atypical traits[17][18].

### National Autistic Society (NAS, UK)

- Supports inclusion of sensory features in both DSM-5 and ICD-11. Recognizes that many autistic people experience profound sensory differences and advocates for systematic assessment of sensory profiles as critical for diagnosis and support, though notes that sensory processing disorder is not separately recognized, potentially limiting needed services for those with strong sensory-driven difficulties[19][20].

### Autism Speaks

- Supports the recognition of sensory processing differences in DSM-5 and the consolidation of subtypes. Stresses that diagnosis should increase access to support, not restrict it based on severity or number of features. Explicitly refrains from ranking either manual as more neurodiversity-affirming but acknowledges the step forward that sensory inclusion represents in both systems[21][22].

## Diagnostic Safety Risks: Misdiagnosis and Impact of Criteria Differences

- **Over- and under-diagnosis Risks**
  - ICD-11: Broader and more flexible, potentially more inclusive for less classic or marginalized presentations but carries risk of false positives and imprecise diagnosis, especially where symptom descriptions are subjective or overlap with other conditions (e.g., ADHD, anxiety, personality disorders)[8].
  - DSM-5: More prescriptive, higher specificity but at risk of under-diagnosing individuals with non-classical, masked, higher-IQ, female, or predominantly sensory presentations[9][10][8].

- **Sensory-Focused Presentations**
  - Both DSM-5 and ICD-11 require restricted/repetitive behaviors, but individuals whose main difficulties are sensory without other RRBs may not meet the threshold, potentially leading to missed diagnosis or exclusion from support, especially as standalone “sensory processing disorder” is not officially recognized[19][20].

- **Systematic Barriers and Clinical Risks**
  - Delayed or missed diagnosis is particularly pronounced in girls/women, minorities, and those with well-masked traits[8][9][10]. Poor translation of criteria across cultures further increases risk in global health settings.
  - Diagnostic overlap with other developmental or mental health conditions complicates clear identification, sometimes leading to inappropriate interventions or denial of ASD-specific resources[23].

- **Recommendations**
  - Multidisciplinary, individualized diagnostic approaches are recommended for safety and accuracy. Increased clinician training is needed to recognize atypical or less obvious presentations, especially those with prominent sensory issues[8][23].

## Coding and Reimbursement Practices in the US and EU

### United States

- **Coding**: ASD diagnoses in US clinical, insurance, and educational systems are mapped to ICD-10-CM code F84.0, irrespective of DSM-5/ICD-11 distinction[24][25]. DSM-5 guides clinical diagnosis, but all billing and coverage is currently under ICD-10-CM.
- **ICD-11 Adoption**: ICD-11 is not yet implemented in US billing or benefits determination. Transition planning is underway, but most payers anticipate a multi-year adjustment (expected in the early 2030s). Dual coding and system upgrades will be required[24][26].
- **Coverage Impact**: At present, transitioning to ICD-11 does not affect autism coverage, eligibility, or reimbursement. Service eligibility is tied to ICD-10-CM code and evidence of impairment as currently defined[24][25][26].

### European Union

- **Coding**: EU member states are transitioning to ICD-11 for health data and cross-border comparability, but national practice varies—some use ICD-10, some are implementing ICD-11, and some allow either[27][28].
- **Benefits and Support**: Services are generally needs-based, tied to evidence of documented functional impairment rather than ICD code alone. Multidisciplinary assessment is typically required for access[28].
- **ICD-11 Impact**: ICD-11 is expected to facilitate improved consistency in documentation and better data for health systems, but does not automatically alter coverage or benefit eligibility, which remains function- and need-based in most EU countries[27][28].

---

## Conclusion

DSM-5 and ICD-11 have converged in many aspects of ASD diagnosis, each including sensory processing as an explicit feature of diagnostic criteria—a significant step towards recognizing the diverse experiences of autistic individuals. DSM-5 is more detailed and prescriptive, supporting diagnosis with defined severity levels, while ICD-11 is broader, more flexible, and designed for international and multidisciplinary use. Empirical evidence suggests DSM-5 is more specific but may under-identify less typical or masked presentations; ICD-11’s inclusivity comes with increased risk of imprecision.

Clinical guidelines, advocacy organizations, and research all advocate for individualized, neurodiversity-affirming evaluation, considering both core and sensory symptoms, functional impairment, and lived experience. Transition to ICD-11 as a billing/coding standard is expected to be a long-term process, primarily affecting administrative but not necessarily clinical decisions in the near-term.

A multidisciplinary, culturally responsive, and strengths-based approach remains essential for accurate and effective autism diagnosis and support worldwide.
