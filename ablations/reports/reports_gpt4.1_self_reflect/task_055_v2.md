# Legal Foundations of Slavery and Trade in the 1730s British Empire: Statutory Language, Archival Access, Comparative Law, Modern Scholarship, and the Molasses Act Debate

## Overview

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the legal frameworks that shaped slavery and transatlantic trade in the 1730s British Empire. It synthesizes statutory texts, highlights where to access these laws, contrasts British parliamentary law with colonial slave codes, examines recent scholarship on the legal dehumanization of enslaved people, and summarizes modern scholarly debate regarding the Molasses Act of 1733. All findings are grounded in primary and peer-reviewed sources, with direct citations for further research.

## 1. Statutory Language: Molasses Act (1733) and Colonial Slave Laws

### Molasses Act of 1733

**Statutory Language**

The Molasses Act (6 Geo II c.13) was enacted by the British Parliament in May 1733. Its language is narrowly focused on trade and customs duties. Some representative passages:[1][2]

> “An act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty’s sugar colonies in America.”
>
> “There shall be raised ... upon all Rum or spirits ... imported ... from colonies not under His Majesty ... the Sum of Nine pence ... for every Gallon.”
>
> “Upon all Molasses or Syrups of such Foreign Produce ... the Sum of Six pence ... for every Gallon.”
>
> “All such Goods ... landed or put on Shore ... without due Entry and payment of Duties ... shall be forfeited.”

**Notably, the Act does not reference slavery directly, nor does it use language about chattel status or enslaved people as property. Instead, its legal impact is tightly tied to the trade in molasses and rum—core outputs of enslaved labor in the British Atlantic.**

### Barbados Slave Laws

**Key Statutory Texts (Especially 1661, As Re-Enacted in the 18th Century)**

- The foundational Barbados Slave Code of 1661, continuously reenacted and amended, was in active use through the 1730s. Its language set the paradigm for chattel slavery:

> “An Act for the better ordering and governing of Negroes ... to protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels.”[3][4]

- This code and its progeny defined Black slaves as “goods and chattels,” thus legally commodifying human beings and stripping them of rights and legal personhood. Owners had near-absolute authority over enslaved people, whose status was hereditary and permanent.

### Jamaica Slave Laws (1730–1739)

**Key Statutory Language**

- Jamaica’s first comprehensive slave code (1664, modeled on Barbados) was reenacted and amended throughout the 18th century. In the 1730s context, the underlying logic was untouched:

> “The code's preamble ... stated that the law's purpose was to 'protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels,' establishing that black slaves would be treated as chattel property.”[5][6][7]

- Provisions specified restrictions, punishments, and protocols for runaways, all while reaffirming slaves’ status as property, subject to violence and sale.

### South Carolina Slave Laws (1730–1740)

**Key Statutory Language**

- The landmark South Carolina Negro Act of 1740 (enacted just after the Stono Rebellion, but based on existing 1730s statutes) provides explicit passages:

> “All negroes, Indians (free Indians ... excepted), mulattoes or mestizoes ... shall be and remain forever hereafter, absolute slaves and ... chattels personal in the hands of their owners, and their executors, administrators and assigns, to all intents, constructions and purposes whatsoever.”[8][9]

- This code formalized permanent, inheritable chattel status, prescribed draconian punishments, and denied enslaved people legal rights.

## 2. Archival Location and Access to Statutory Laws

### Where Laws Are Archived

**British and Caribbean Statutes:**

- **UK National Archives (Kew, London):** Holds original Parliamentary statutes, Colonial Office records (CO series), and correspondence about Jamaica, Barbados, and other colonies. Access is primarily onsite. Catalog: [The National Archives - Caribbean Social and Cultural History][10]

- **Jamaica Archives and Records Department (JARD, Spanish Town):** Keeps colonial council minutes, legislative acts, and slave registers—many from the 17th–19th centuries. Most statutes must be consulted on site.[11]

- **Barbados Department of Archives (Black Rock, St. Michael):** Holds legislative records, deeds, manumission documents, and slave registers from the 17th–19th centuries. Some summaries and abstracts are digitally available.[12]

- **South Carolina Department of Archives and History (Columbia, SC):** Houses physical and microfilmed slave laws, manumission records, and legal dockets from the colonial period. FamilySearch and digitized collections provide partial online access.[13]

**Online and Digitized Collections:**

- **The Statutes Project:** Full English parliamentary statutes (including the Molasses Act), some colonial statutes, and links to Google Books/Archive.org for 19th-century law compilations. [The Statutes Project][2]

- **Laws of Enslavement and Freedom (University of New Brunswick):** Index of colonial slave laws (Barbados, Jamaica, South Carolina), sometimes with full statute texts or links. [UNB Laws Database][14]

- **Slavery, Law, and Power:** Provides digitized original and transcribed slave codes (esp. Barbados, Jamaica). [Barbados Slave Code][4]

- **Google Books and Internet Archive:** Later compilations (e.g., "The Statutes and Laws of Jamaica") are digitized, as are selected 17th–18th-century law books/gazettes.

**For earlier or full-text acts (pre-19th century), onsite research at relevant national and colonial archives remains essential.**

## 3. Distinctions Between British Parliamentary Law and Colonial Plantation Codes

### British Parliamentary (Metropolitan) Law

- **No Statutory Slavery in Britain:** There was no law in the 1730s explicitly providing for slavery within England or elsewhere in the metropole. Court precedent (notably Cartwright 1569 and, later, Somerset 1772) held that slavery was not recognized by English common law.

- **Legal Treatment and Property Rights:** While slaves could be insured and traded as valuables under commercial law (insurance, ship’s cargo manifests), their chattel status was recognized for imperial trade purposes, not as a matter of personhood within Britain.[15]

- **Absence of Statutory Punishments:** No explicit statutes in Britain allowed the summary punishment, sale, or execution of enslaved people.

### Colonial Plantation Codes: Jamaica and Barbados

- **Explicit Chattel Status:** Slavery was statutorily enshrined. Enslaved people were defined as “goods and chattels” or “chattels personal”—not human persons—and could be inherited, bought, or sold at the will of white owners.

- **Comprehensive Control and Punishment:**
    - Plantation codes specified:
        - Severe corporal punishments (whipping, mutilation, execution)
        - No right to trial by jury
        - No testimony allowed from enslaved people against whites
        - Summary justice by local “slave courts” comprising managers and magistrates[3][5][8]
    - Owners could inflict “wantonly cruel” punishments with little or no penalty; even murder was punished only financially, if at all.

- **Social Order:** Codes strictly policed movement, gatherings, religious practice, sexual relations, and even required the annual public reading of these laws.[3][6]

### Table: Key Differences

| Aspect                   | British Law (Metropole)                                       | Colonial Codes (Jamaica/Barbados)                         |
|--------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
| Status of Slavery        | Not recognized in law; ambiguous in cases of slaves in Britain| Explicit, hereditary, race-based, statutorily defined     |
| Property Status          | No statutory “chattel” status; recognized in trade only      | Slaves as “goods and chattels” or “chattels personal”     |
| Punishment/Control       | No statutory corporal punishment for unfree laborers         | Summary courts; brutal corporal punishment, execution      |
| Legal Rights             | In theory, all “subjects” had access to courts               | Enslaved excluded from courts, legal personhood denied     |
| Judicial Process         | Standard criminal/civil procedures and rights                | Summary justice, no jury, minimal owner accountability     |

- **Conclusion:** In the 1730s, British metropolitan law declined to define or regulate slavery domestically, while colonial codes made slavery a legal, permanent, racially defined condition with sweeping punishments and almost no protections for the enslaved person.[16][17]

## 4. Recent Scholarship (2015–2025): Legal Dehumanization of Enslaved People

### Representative Peer-Reviewed Scholarship

#### a. *Underwriting and Investing in Slavery: The London Assurance Company in the 1720s and 1730s* (Slavery & Abolition, 2025)

- Explores the financial and legal construction of slavery, highlighting how insurance and investment mechanisms fully adopted the language of enslaved people as cargo and chattel, reinforcing systemic legal dehumanization.[18]

#### b. “Negroes, Goods and Merchandizes: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700–1763”

- Analyzes how maritime and insurance law routinely classified enslaved people as property, not human beings—a process carried out in colonial courts with direct links to everyday slave trade realities.[19]

#### c. *General Average, Human Jettison, and the Status of Slaves in Early Modern Europe* (Cambridge University Press, 2023)

- Compares legal approaches to slaves as cargo in maritime disputes, highlighting the transition from ambiguous human status in the Mediterranean towards total codification as insurable property in the Atlantic context.[20]

#### d. “After Abolition: Cugoano on ‘Lawful Servitude’ and the Injustice of Slavery” (Journal of Modern Philosophy, 2023)

- Discusses 18th-century abolitionist arguments that saw the legal “chattelization” of Africans as the central injustice—contrasting biblical servitude with Atlantic slavery’s total commodification and violence.[21]

*While some articles focus on the legal-financial aspects, others directly problematize the stripping of personhood and the violence of the codes. Recent scholarship consistently underscores the centrality of statutory and legal dehumanization in both economic and everyday life in these societies.*

## 5. The Molasses Act Debate: Trade Regulation or Slave Control?

### Scholarly Consensus

- **Primary Aim: Trade Regulation**
    - Recent scholarship asserts that the Molasses Act (1733) was fundamentally a tool of British mercantilism—designed to protect the British West Indian plantation economy by reserving the North American sugar and rum markets for British (rather than French or Dutch) suppliers through high import duties.[1][2][22][23]
    - The statute’s language and all contemporary political debates centered on the economics of sugar, rum, and molasses, not the direct legal regulation of slavery.

- **Relation to Slavery**
    - Molasses production and trade were absolutely dependent on enslaved labor, and the triangular trade integrated enslaved people at every stage (molasses→rum→slaves→sugar).
    - However, the Act did not directly regulate or control enslaved populations, nor did it reference chattel status or slave management.
    - Its economic effect amplified the plantation slave system, but no modern historian finds evidence that slave control was a primary intention or design.[23]

- **Recent Scholarship**
    - Peer-reviewed articles and economic histories published from 2015–2025 uniformly frame the Molasses Act as a failed instrument of trade protectionism that contributed to colonial resentment—a critical step toward revolution but not an act of slave management or control per se.[18][22][23]

#### Representative Scholarship

- “The Act was designed to ‘force colonists to buy only British West Indian molasses, raising colonial tensions and resistance due to its economic impact.’ There is no evidence that the act’s primary purpose was slave regulation.”[22]
- “While deeply intertwined with the Atlantic slave economy, the Molasses Act’s legal effect was indirect; it sought neither to regulate nor to discipline enslaved people themselves.”[23]
