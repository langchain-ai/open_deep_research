# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality and Prenatal Care Access: Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar (Bangladesh) vs. Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp (Thailand), 2017–2023

## Introduction

This report presents a comprehensive comparative analysis of maternal health indicators and prenatal care access among Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand, from 2017 to 2023. The analysis focuses on four primary health indicators: (a) percentage of women completing four or more antenatal care (ANC) visits, (b) percentage of births attended by skilled birth attendants (SBA), (c) incidence of postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), and (d) neonatal mortality rate (NMR) within 28 days of birth. The report also correlates these outcomes with differences in healthcare delivery systems—specifically, the NGO-operated model and midwife workforce in Cox’s Bazar versus Thai Ministry of Public Health (MOH) integration in Mae La—and examines how midwife-to-population ratios, availability of emergency obstetric care (EmONC) within a 2-hour transport window, and cultural mediation by traditional birth attendants (TBAs) influence these outcomes. Finally, the impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction on service quality and continuity in both locations is critically assessed.

---

## Health System Context and Service Delivery Models

### Cox’s Bazar: NGO-Led Parallel Health Delivery

- Health services are primarily provided by a mix of international/local NGOs and UN agencies, operating parallel facilities amid challenging camp conditions.
- The model relies heavily on midwife-led birthing centers and periodic deployment of trained national midwives. However, staffing shortages, high turnover, and inconsistent coverage persist.
- Emergency obstetric care is limited by infrastructure: many camps are located beyond a reliable 2-hour transport window to EmONC facilities, and delays are common. Blood supply shortages and referral system weaknesses exacerbate maternal risk.
- Cultural and gender-related barriers strongly impact care-seeking. Many women prefer home births attended by TBAs, citing privacy, religious/cultural norms, fear of male staff, and distrust of external providers.
- Since 2021, funding shortfalls have hindered service continuity, workforce retention, and outreach activities, resulting in rising maternal and neonatal mortality despite improvements in formal service provision[1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8][9].

### Mae La: Thai MOH Integration and Task-Shifting

- Healthcare in Mae La is characterized by strong integration with the Thai Ministry of Public Health, supplemented by humanitarian agency support (notably Shoklo Malaria Research Unit and Mae Tao Clinic).
- Service coverage exceeds international standards for ANC and skilled attendant deliveries due to extensive training programs, stable local staffing, and formalized referral systems for EmONC (including cesarean sections at Thai district hospitals).
- Specialized care units, such as neonatal special care baby units (SCBU), are present, lowering neonatal mortality and addressing complications[10][11][12].
- Traditional cultural mediation is formalized: local TBAs have been retrained as SBAs, with collaborative models integrating cultural practices and language to promote facility births and trust.
- Funding cuts, especially after 2021, have stressed the system but Thai MOH integration and strong local training have preserved most essential services to date, though the strain is increasing[13][14][15][16][17][18][19][20][21].

## Quantitative Comparative Analysis of Key Health Indicators

The table below summarizes available data on the four requested indicators, with further discussion and contextualization in subsequent sections.

| Indicator                              | Rohingya, Cox's Bazar (2017-2023)             | Karen, Mae La Camp (2017-2023)             |
|-----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| ANC 4+ Completion Rate                  | 63–72% (2019–2023); camp avg. 71.6%           | >90% sustained (2017–2023)                 |
| Skilled Birth Attendance                | 69–91%; most recent >80% facility-based births | 78–95% (2017–2018, varies by report)       |
| Postpartum Hemorrhage Incidence         | 27–29% of maternal deaths due to PPH (proxy); direct incidence not reported | 5.5% of deliveries among minority groups (proxy); direct data for Mae La limited |
| Neonatal Mortality Rate (<28 days)      | 27/1,000 live births (2017–2018); high & persistent | Dropped to ~10.7/1,000 (2018); further declines likely, still above Thai national avg. |

### Antenatal Care (4+ Visits)

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Coverage has improved, but only 63–72% of Rohingya women complete four or more ANC visits, with substantial intra-camp variability. Barriers include social norms, mobility restrictions, lower health literacy, and outreach constraints—exacerbated by recent funding gaps[2][3][4][5][6].
- **Mae La:** Over 90% of Karen women regularly complete at least four ANC visits. This reflects effective health messaging, trusted local staff, and logistical reliability; coverage remains stable even amidst funding constraints, thanks to Thai MOH integration[10][11].

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Skilled attendance at birth ranges from 69% (self-reported, all settings) to over 80% for facility-based births. Persistent preference for home deliveries with TBAs, particularly in emergencies or after dark, still undermines skilled attendance rates[7][8].
- **Mae La:** Skilled attendance reached 95% (2018) in Mae La, with slightly lower rates (~78%) in other border camps, reflecting robust training and recruitment of local SBAs (including retrained TBAs) and reliable referral partnerships with district hospitals[10][11][12].

### Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Direct PPH incidence rates are unavailable, but PPH accounts for 27–29% of all maternal deaths as of 2020–2024. These figures represent a severe and persistent risk and highlight both delays in accessing care and limitations of EmONC capacity[13][14][15].
- **Mae La:** Direct recent data is lacking for Mae La, but regional studies at similar border camps cite PPH incidence of 5.5%, with minority/displaced groups at higher risk. The relatively low maternal mortality in Mae La suggests effective identification and management of PPH[16][17].

### Neonatal Mortality Rate (<28 Days)

- **Cox’s Bazar:** The NMR remains high—27 per 1,000 live births (2017–2018)—with the majority of deaths and stillbirths occurring at home or outside health facilities, reflecting unmet needs for facility-based care and postnatal follow-up[9].
- **Mae La:** NMR decreased to approximately 10.7 per 1,000 live births by 2018 (a 51% reduction since early 2000s), approaching but still above national Thai averages (3–5/1,000). Improvements are linked to effective neonatal units and early PNC access, though service fragmentation now threatens further gains[10][11].

## Correlating Health Outcomes with System Factors

### Midwife-to-Population Ratios

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Midwife coverage remains suboptimal: ~283 midwives for over 900,000 refugees, with high attrition and workforce gaps intensified by funding shortfalls. Periodic UNFPA-led training has helped, but sustainable government integration is lacking[14][15].
- **Mae La:** Staffing is relatively stable and meets humanitarian targets. High coverage and consistent service are attributed to localized training of Karen and Burman SBAs and core support from Thai MOH. Accurate midwife-to-population ratios are not published but are inferred as meeting global standards based on coverage and reported outcomes[10][11][12][16].

### EmONC Availability within Two Hours

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Many camps lack consistent access to EmONC within two hours, especially in poor weather or at night. Only a subset of facilities can manage comprehensive emergencies (e.g., Teknaf center), hampered by referral delays, terrain, and limited blood bank access. The “Three Delays” model frequently applies[13][14][15].
- **Mae La:** Referrals are reliably established with Thai hospitals for complications. Uncomplicated deliveries are handled on site, with 2-hour EmONC transport access generally assured barring exceptional circumstances. Referral costs are a concern as humanitarian funding wanes[10][17].

### Cultural Mediation and TBA Involvement

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Cultural preference for TBAs remains strong despite ongoing training and integration efforts—particularly among older or less-educated women. Sociocultural fears, privacy concerns, and modesty issues impede facility-based care, often resulting in delayed or missed referrals for obstetric emergencies[11][12][13].
- **Mae La:** Decades of active cultural mediation have integrated TBAs into trained SBA teams, bridging traditional trust and modern practices. TBAs act as facilitators and mediators, increasing acceptance of facility birth while sustaining cultural norms—contributing to high ANC and SBA rates[10][17][18].

## Impact of the 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction

### Cox’s Bazar

- The UNFPA funding cut in 2021 directly reduced staff retention, disrupted midwifery training, and led to partial suspension of maternal health outreach, supply chain interruptions, and fewer supported service points.
- Outreach activities, especially for postnatal care and community health education, declined by 39%, contributing to increased “missed cases” and preventable maternal and neonatal deaths. Surveillance reports note a sharp rise in maternal mortality—reaching 447 per 100,000 live births in 2024—despite improved ANC and SBA rates.
- Parallel reductions by USAID and other donors since 2023 have further threatened the survival of maternal health programs, risking up to 315,000 women’s access to essential care in future years[15][19][20][21][22][23].

### Mae La

- The 2021–2025 period saw severe funding loss: in 2025, an additional 90-day US aid freeze caused clinics and hospitals in Mae La and neighboring camps to suspend operations—leading to the layoff of staff, including midwives and medical support teams, and closure or near-closure of over 1,000 UNFPA-supported health facilities across the region.
- Skilled birth attendance, ANC, and emergency referrals continued via Thai MOH, but with increasing strain as referral hospitals became overwhelmed. Shortfalls in chronic care, mental health, and maternal health outreach were reported. The durable integration of trained local providers and the Thai MOH system buffered the worst impacts, preserving much of the core maternal and neonatal care, but future sustainability is uncertain[13][14][15][16][17][18][19][20][21].

## Discussion: Causes of Disparities and Policy Implications

- **System Integration:** Mae La's relative success is rooted in health system integration with the Thai MOH, stable staffing of locally trained SBAs, and reliable EmONC referral. In contrast, Cox’s Bazar’s parallel NGO system remains fragmented, with weak government-NGO coordination and volatile workforce.
- **Cultural Mediation:** Formalized TBA integration in Mae La both respects cultural preferences and elevates quality; Cox’s Bazar is still in transition, with strong traditions undermining rapid system uptake and reinforcing delays.
- **Funding Reliance:** Both sites face existential threats from further international aid cuts. In Cox’s Bazar, reliance on external funding is acute; in Mae La, legacy investments and government integration buffer (but do not eliminate) risk.
- **Equity and Coverage:** Despite substantial progress, both groups remain at greater risk for adverse maternal and neonatal outcomes compared to host populations. In both cases, health system resilience, cultural adaptation, and predictable funding are vital for closing the gap.

---

## Conclusion

Maternal and neonatal health outcomes among the Karen refugees in Mae La outpace those among Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar across all measured indicators; Mae La approaches high coverage and low mortality rates seen in more stable middle-income contexts due to deep integration, stable local staffing, and effective cultural mediation. Cox’s Bazar, while improving, is limited by staffing and infrastructure gaps, systemic and sociocultural barriers, and extreme vulnerability to funding interruptions.

Sustained progress in both contexts requires:
- Consistent and predictable international funding;
- Integration of emergency and referral services within host health systems;
- Enhancement of locally led and culturally competent care models—especially retraining and integrating TBAs;
- Investment in workforce stability and continuous training for midwives and SBAs;
- Strengthened surveillance, blood supply, referral, and quality improvement systems.

The funding reductions since 2021 starkly evidence the fragility of maternal health systems in crisis settings and the urgent need for multisectoral, resilient approaches to safeguard the lives of women and newborns.
