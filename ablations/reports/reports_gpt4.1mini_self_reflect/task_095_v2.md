# Tracing the Evolution of Feminist Legal Theory Across Four Legal Traditions: Comparative Study of Anglo-American Critical Legal Feminism, Islamic Feminism, African Feminism, and Chinese Feminist Legal Scholarship

This comprehensive report traces and compares the evolution of feminist legal theory within four distinct legal traditions, focusing on their conceptualizations and legal treatments of women’s property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation. The analysis integrates key feminist thinkers and movements—Catharine MacKinnon and Martha Fineman in Anglo-American feminism; Ziba Mir-Hosseini and Amina Wadud in Islamic feminism; African feminist critiques of customary law and human rights frameworks; and Chinese feminist legal scholarship under state socialism and marketization. The report examines doctrines, feminist critiques, reform strategies, socio-political influences, and recent developments up to 2026, highlighting how legal reforms, cultural traditions, religious interpretations, and state policies intersect within each tradition.

---

## 1. Anglo-American Critical Legal Feminism

### 1.1 Evolution and Theoretical Foundations

Anglo-American critical legal feminism emerged from the broader feminist movements of the 19th and 20th centuries, evolving beyond formal equality jurisprudence towards critical interrogations of law as a mechanism reinforcing patriarchal power. Central to this tradition are Catharine MacKinnon’s dominance theory and Martha Fineman’s vulnerability theory, both critiquing liberal legalism’s assumptions of neutrality and individual autonomy.

- **Catharine MacKinnon** situates feminist legal critique around sexualized power structures, viewing gender inequality as a form of systemic domination. She challenges legal doctrines that obscure or perpetuate male dominance, especially through the legal tolerance of sexual harassment, pornography, and sexual violence. MacKinnon emphasizes bodily autonomy as a locus of legal struggle, highlighting how control over women’s bodies is akin to property and central to subordination [2][24].

- **Martha Fineman** critiques autonomy-centered legal frameworks, arguing instead that universal human vulnerability and social dependency, especially in caregiving roles often fulfilled by women, should be the focus of feminist legal theory. Her work advocates state responsibility for caregiving and challenges existing property and family laws that ignore or marginalize women’s contributions outside formal economy [4][21].

This tradition built on second-wave feminism and is adaptive, incorporating intersectionality and critical race feminism, challenging the limitations of sameness and difference doctrines to reveal embedded patriarchal legal norms [7][9].

### 1.2 Legal Structures: Property Rights, Bodily Autonomy, Political Participation

- **Property Rights:** Fineman’s feminist legal scholarship emphasizes that legal systems fail to properly recognize and redistribute the economic value of caregiving labor to women, which is often excluded from property and inheritance laws. While formal legal equality in property ownership has expanded historically, persistent male normativity leads to economic disenfranchisement of many women [9][21].

- **Bodily Autonomy:** MacKinnon frames bodily autonomy within the sphere of sexual politics, identifying sexual violence, harassment, and the legal system’s tolerance of pornography as systemic barriers to women’s autonomy. Recent contentious legal developments—such as the 2022 Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization U.S. Supreme Court decision, rescinding federal abortion rights—have reinvigorated feminist legal debates focusing on reproductive justice and systemic inequalities in accessing bodily autonomy [2][6][9][11].

- **Political Participation:** Feminist critiques extend to the formal political structures, highlighting how sexual violence and patriarchal norms restrict substantive political agency. Fineman’s theorization ties women’s political participation to recognition of caregiving roles and demands for social policies that support gender justice beyond formal electoral equality [9].

### 1.3 Reform Strategies and Socio-Political Context

Reform strategies in this tradition include:

- Legislative and litigative advocacy recognizing gendered violence as a public legal issue.
- Expanding reproductive rights and reproductive justice frameworks.
- Restructuring family and economic laws to incorporate caregiving and dependency.
- Addressing workplace discrimination, sexual harassment, and economic inequalities with intersectional sensitivity.

This tradition’s development reflects its socio-political environment including neoliberal shifts, welfare state retrenchment, and ongoing backlash against feminist gains, necessitating resilient and theoretically nuanced feminist legal scholarship [4][21][23].

## 2. Islamic Feminism: Reinterpreting Sharia for Gender Justice

### 2.1 Theoretical Frameworks and Key Thinkers

Islamic feminism emerges as a distinct movement seeking gender justice within Islamic legal and ethical frameworks, advocating reinterpretations of Sharia (divine law) as distinguished from patriarchal fiqh (human jurisprudence). Scholars like Ziba Mir-Hosseini and Amina Wadud use feminist hermeneutics to reveal egalitarian principles rooted in the Qur’an while contesting patriarchal legal interpretations.

- Mir-Hosseini highlights the political and analytical complexity of Islamic feminism, emphasizing plurality and negotiation between Islamic ethics and universal human rights [1].
- Wadud promotes a hermeneutical method incorporating socio-historical and linguistic analysis to dismantle legal justifications for male authority, advocating mutual responsibility over male guardianship frameworks [18][19].

### 2.2 Women's Property Rights

Islamic legal texts grant women independent property and inheritance rights, yet patriarchal exegetical traditions have constrained these rights particularly under classical fiqh:

- Feminists challenge doctrines like *qiwamah* (men’s authority over women) and *wilayah* (guardianship), arguing these are exegetical constructs rather than Qur’anic mandates and distort women’s economic autonomy [20].
- Reform efforts advocate localized reinterpretations ensuring women’s rights to ownership, inheritance, and financial independence, often navigating resistance from conservative religious authorities [7][13].

### 2.3 Bodily Autonomy and Political Participation

- Bodily autonomy is reframed within Islamic feminist scholarship as women’s agency over their bodies, rejecting patriarchal readings of marital authority and advocating protections against violence, coercion, and discrimination [20].
- Political participation for women is advanced as consistent with Islamic justice, and feminists engage both Islamic ethics and international human rights to legitimate women’s public leadership, although gains often remain constrained by patriarchal state structures and Islamist political agendas [13].

### 2.4 Feminist Critiques, Strategies, and Socio-Political Influences

- Islamic feminists critique male-centric interpretations of sacred texts and challenge binaries between secular feminism and political Islam, advocating a pluralistic methodology that centers Islamic ethical principles with gender justice [1][23].
- Reform strategies include feminist *ijtihad* (independent reasoning), grassroots activism (e.g., the Musawah movement), and collaborations bridging Islamic jurisprudence and universal rights frameworks [7][10].
- Socio-political factors such as the rise of political Islam post-1979, authoritarian regimes, colonial legacies, and Western discourses affect the possibilities and constraints for feminist reforms within Muslim-majority contexts [1][14].

## 3. African Feminism: Navigating Customary Law and Human Rights Frameworks

### 3.1 Contextualizing African Feminist Legal Thought

African feminism critically interrogates the co-existence of customary laws rooted in patriarchal traditions and modern statutory and international human rights norms. Scholars emphasize the need for pluralistic and contextual approaches that avoid simplistic universalism or cultural relativism, recognizing women’s identities as both individual and communal within African societies [1][2][4].

- Matri-legal feminism foregrounds African women’s specific experiences shaped by colonialism, patriarchy, and cultural particularities, advocating an anti-essentialist, postcolonial critique of feminist legal theory [7].

### 3.2 Women’s Property Rights under Customary Law

- Customary law in many African contexts restricts women’s land and inheritance rights under patrilineal and communal governance, resulting in economic marginalization despite constitutional and statutory protections [5][12][15].
- Feminist activists and scholars advocate harmonizing customary law with constitutional guarantees, democratizing customary law, and employing strategic litigation and community education to secure women’s rights [11][13].
- Legal reforms, such as those inspired by the Maputo Protocol, provide substantive rights but face challenges in enforcement, social acceptance, and cooptation by traditional power holders [6][30].

### 3.3 Bodily Autonomy

- Bodily autonomy is constrained by cultural practices including forced marriage, female genital mutilation, and domestic violence. African feminists frame these within intersections of colonial legacies, customary practices, and patriarchal control, emphasizing grassroots activism and legal reforms to combat violence and enhance reproductive rights [17][19].
- Feminists emphasize bodily integrity as essential to gender justice and public health, calling for legal and social transformations [17][19].

### 3.4 Political Participation

- African women’s political participation has improved in some areas (e.g., Rwanda’s female-majority parliament) but systemic patriarchal and socio-economic barriers remain widespread [13][21][22].
- Feminist movements promote legal quotas, political education, and advocacy to dismantle barriers, although entrenched norms and political gatekeeping limit progress [22][23].

### 3.5 Socio-Political Dynamics and Legal Pluralism

- The legacy of colonial legal pluralism entrenched patriarchal customary systems that continue to coexist with state law, often to women’s detriment [33].
- Feminist legal strategies thus focus on negotiating legal pluralism, invoking constitutional equality over customary law, and fostering local community leadership for women’s rights [5][6].
- African feminism centers the cultural, historical, and political complexity of gender justice, combining local agency with global human rights discourses [1][45].

## 4. Chinese Feminist Legal Scholarship: Navigating State Socialism and Marketization

### 4.1 Historical and Institutional Context

Chinese feminist legal theory developed under socialist state feminism post-1949, emphasizing formal gender equality but within a patriarchal and authoritarian regime. Subsequent market reforms have introduced economic liberalization while intensifying gender inequalities.

- The Chinese Communist Party’s (CCP) state feminism operates through institutions like the All-China Women’s Federation (ACWF), balancing promotion of gender equality with strict state control and limited independent feminist spaces [2][24].
- Feminist activism and scholarship negotiate between cooperation with state structures and critiques of patriarchal authoritarianism, often facing repression and censorship [18][32].

### 4.2 Women’s Property Rights

- Although constitutional guarantees exist, rural women in particular face challenges due to patrilineal customs, administrative hurdles, and legal ambiguities that subvert formal equality [6][9].
- Recent provincial laws (e.g., Fujian 2023) attempt to improve women’s access to property information and enforcement, yet disparities remain in practice, especially regarding spousal property rights and land tenure security [10].
- Feminist legal analyses emphasize the legacy of traditional patriarchal norms, market-driven inequalities, and policy gaps undermining women’s economic autonomy [6][8].

### 4.3 Bodily Autonomy and Women’s Protection

- Bodily autonomy in China is shaped by state population policies—historically coercive (e.g., one-child policy)—and contemporary reproductive health inequalities, with governmental control blending with persisting patriarchal norms [14].
- Legal reforms have enhanced protections against workplace sexual harassment and discrimination, but enforcement is limited [12][13].
- Feminist activists adapt to censorship by employing “post-censorship feminism”—using coded language and digital networks to advocate for reproductive rights and gender equality amid repression [39].
- Heteronormative state feminism marginalizes queer and feminist voices, but limited spaces in social media allow bounded activism [37].

### 4.4 Political Participation

- Women’s political representation remains limited especially at grassroots levels, despite official rhetoric on equality. Quota systems and leadership training show partial success but do not disrupt systemic male dominance [20][22].
- The ACWF and similar bodies mediate women’s issues within the Party framework, limiting independent feminist political agency [26].
- Authoritarian governance constrains feminist movements while reinforcing traditional Confucian family values under Xi Jinping’s leadership [2][40].

### 4.5 Feminist Legal Scholarship and Movements

- Chinese feminist legal theory blends socialist feminism critiques with post-utopian feminist approaches, emphasizing localized, pluralistic feminist jurisprudence that challenges individualistic Western paradigms [25][42].
- Historic figures like He-Yin Zhen influence contemporary feminist thought, combining gender, class, and anti-capitalist perspectives [19].
- Feminist activism remains vibrant yet suppressed, using digital platforms creatively to sustain movements [18][32][38].

## 5. Comparative Analysis: Key Themes and Divergences

### 5.1 Women’s Property Rights

- **Anglo-American feminism** critiques legal property regimes for ignoring caregiving contributions and intersectional inequalities, advocating redistributive reforms [9][21].
- **Islamic feminism** focuses on reclaiming women’s Qur’anic property and inheritance rights through reinterpretation of patriarchal fiqh [7][20].
- **African feminism** confronts patriarchal customary law denying women land and inheritance rights, promoting contextual legal pluralism and human rights harmonization [5][6].
- **Chinese feminism** deals with formal equality shadowed by persistent local norms and marketization effects that disadvantage women, particularly in rural land tenure [6][9].

### 5.2 Bodily Autonomy

- **MacKinnon’s dominance theory** highlights sexual violence and legal violence against women’s bodily integrity [2].
- **Fineman** reframes autonomy within social vulnerability and caregiving dependencies [4].
- **Islamic feminists** challenge restrictive patriarchal religious norms, promoting agency rooted in religious ethics [18][20].
- **African feminists** prioritize combating cultural violence and harmful traditional practices through grassroots activism [17][19].
- **Chinese feminists** negotiate state reproductive control and patriarchal expectations, engaging in subtle resistance amid political repression [14][39].

### 5.3 Political Participation

- **Anglo-American feminism** focuses on removing systemic barriers from sexual violence and formal discrimination [9].
- **Islamic feminism** grounds women’s political rights in religious justice and human rights, facing state and Islamist constraints [13].
- **African feminism** combines legal quotas, advocacy, and community mobilization amidst patriarchal and socio-economic challenges [21][22].
- **Chinese feminism** encounters authoritarian limitations but pursues local leadership and digital activism within state-controlled frameworks [20][26].

### 5.4 Socio-Political Contexts and Legal Reform

- Each tradition reflects unique intersections of history, culture, religion, and politics:
  - Western liberal democracies allow feminist legal activism through courts and legislation but face neoliberal and backlash challenges.
  - Islamic feminism navigates tensions between religious doctrine, modernity, fundamentalism, and varied state regimes.
  - African feminism engages plural legal orders shaped by colonial legacies and diverse customary authorities, demanding context-sensitive reforms.
  - Chinese feminism operates within an authoritarian, marketized socialist state, with limited civil freedoms and state feminism’s contradictory role.

- Reform strategies resist imposition of singular universal frameworks, favoring pluralistic, locally grounded feminist legal projects.

---

## Conclusion

The evolution of feminist legal theory across Anglo-American, Islamic, African, and Chinese legal traditions reveals complex, context-specific engagements with women’s property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation. Each tradition reflects its distinct historical experiences, cultural meanings, religious teachings, and political regimes, producing varied legal critiques and reform strategies. Despite these differences, a shared commitment to gender justice emerges, advancing feminist legal thought through contestations of patriarchal power across multiple legal systems. These diverse traditions enrich global feminist jurisprudence, illustrating that effective feminist legal theory requires attentiveness to local contexts, plural methodologies, and ongoing resistance to gendered subordination within law and society.
