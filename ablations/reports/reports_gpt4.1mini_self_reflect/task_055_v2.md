## 1. Statutory Language and Legal Texts

### 1.1 The Molasses Act (1733)

The Molasses Act 1733 (6 Geo. II c. 13), formally titled “An Act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty’s sugar colonies in America,” legislated high duties on molasses, rum, and sugar imported into British American colonies from foreign (mostly French) colonies. Its language focuses exclusively on trade regulation and imposes penalties for illegal importation, with no mention or classification of enslaved people as property or chattels.

**Key statutory excerpts:**

- **Duties imposed:**  
  - Nine pence per gallon on rum/spirits from foreign plantations.  
  - Six pence per gallon on foreign molasses or syrups.  
  - Five shillings per hundredweight on foreign sugar and panela.

- **Entry and payment requirements:**  
  > "All such Goods as shall be so landed or put on Shore without due Entry made and before the Duties imposed shall be paid... shall be forfeited."

- **Restrictions on Irish imports:**  
  > "No Sugars, Paneles, Syrups, or Molasses... nor any Rum or Spirits... shall be imported... into the Kingdom of Ireland, but such only as shall be fairly and bona fide loaden and shipped in Great-Britain in Ships navigated according to the several Laws now in being."

- **Duration:** The act was to remain in force for five years from June 24, 1733.

The act centers on controlling trade flows and mercantile monopoly rather than any legal status of enslaved people.

### 1.2 Slave Laws: Barbados (1661)

Although enacted in 1661, the Barbados Slave Code profoundly shaped colonial slave laws throughout the 18th century, including the 1730s. This code is explicit in treating enslaved Africans as property or "goods and chattels," denying them legal personhood, and authorizing extreme measures for control.

**Representative language:**  
- Classification:  
  > "To protect them [slaves] as we do men’s other goods and Chattels."

- Description of enslaved people:  
  > "An heathenish, brutish and an uncertaine, dangerous kind of people."

- Punitive authority:  
  > Masters and overseers were authorized to impose severe punishments including flogging, mutilation, and even death for offenses such as rebellion or violence against whites.

- Restrictions on movement:  
  > Slaves required written permission (tickets) to leave plantations; unauthorized movement was punishable.

- Protection of owner property:  
  > Penalties for harboring runaways and attempt to persuade slaves to escape included heavy fines payable in sugar.

---

### 1.3 Slave Laws: Jamaica (circa 1739)

Exact statutory texts from the 1730s are sparse, but Jamaica’s slave laws were heavily derived from Barbados’ code. Key documents include the 1739 Maroon Treaty, which acknowledges the ongoing institution of slavery indirectly by mandating the capture and return of runaway slaves.

**Key treaty excerpts (1739):**

- Freedom and land granted to Maroons under colonial terms.  
- Obligation of Maroons to suppress rebellions and deliver runaway slaves:  
  > “If any negroes shall hereafter run away from their masters ... and fall into Captain Cudjoe's hands, they shall immediately be sent back to the Chief Magistrate...”

- This treaty institutionalized plantation control systems and the continuation of slavery as property, though not codifying enslaved people as property explicitly.

Previous Jamaican slave codes have classified slaves as property, authorizing corporal punishment and summary legal procedures denying them standard rights.

---

### 1.4 Slave Laws: South Carolina Slave Code (1740)

Following the 1739 Stono Rebellion, South Carolina enacted a comprehensive slave code in 1740. This is one of the clearest colonial statutes explicitly classifying enslaved people as property ("chattels personal") and instituting harsh controls.

**Key statutory excerpts:**

- **Classification as property:**  
  > “All negroes, Indians, mulattoes or mestizos... shall be and remain forever hereafter absolute slaves... and shall be deemed... chattels personal in the hands of their owners.”

- **Perpetual and hereditary slavery:**  
  Slave status inherited maternally; presumed enslaved unless proven otherwise.

- **Controls and punishments:**  
  - Written passes required for slaves to leave plantations.  
  - White persons authorized to apprehend, punish, or kill slaves assaulting whites.  
  - Summary trials without jury; capital offenses (rebellion, arson, murder against whites) punishable by death without benefit of clergy.  
  - Prohibitions on literacy, property ownership, and unauthorized trade by slaves, with heavy penalties.  
  - Penalties for owner cruelty, including large fines and loss of office.

## 2. Comparative Analysis: British Parliamentary Law vs. Colonial Plantation Slave Codes

### 2.1 British Parliamentary Law in the 1730s

- No explicit statute codified slavery or the legal status of enslaved people within the British Isles during the 1730s. The English common law tradition did not recognize slavery as legally valid without positive law.  
- Enslaved Africans in England occupied ambiguous legal ground; generally, they were not recognized as property by statute but were also denied the full rights available to English subjects.  
- Parliament’s legislative focus was on trade regulation (e.g., Molasses Act 1733), naval matters, and the transatlantic slave trade, not on detailed control or punishment of slaves themselves.  
- The Somerset v. Stewart case of 1772 would later rule slavery unsupported by English common law, but this postdates the 1730s.  
- English law stressed individual liberties for free subjects, clashing with the institution of slavery that was mainly maintained extralegally in colonies.

### 2.2 Colonial Plantation Slave Codes (Jamaica & Barbados)

- Explicit, codified statutes classified enslaved Africans as property or “goods and chattels,” denying legal personhood and human rights.  
- These slave codes instituted racialized, lifelong, hereditary slavery enforced through local colonial assemblies, not directly by British Parliament.  
- Severe punishments were authorized, including flogging, mutilation, branding, and death, without protections such as jury trials or habeas corpus.  
- Strict controls on movement, behavior, and communication were legally mandated through pass systems and curfews.  
- Laws criminalized rebellion, restricted literacy, and curtailed economic autonomy of slaves, aiming to maintain economic productivity and social order.  
- Enforcement involved militias, local magistrates, overseers, and often violent repression including martial law in times of unrest.

### 2.3 Summary of Differences

| Aspect                   | British Parliamentary Law (1730s)                         | Colonial Plantation Slave Codes (Barbados, Jamaica)            |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Legal Status of Slaves   | Ambiguous; no statute directly defining slaves as property; largely governed by common law with legal uncertainty. | Explicitly defined as property ("goods and chattels"), denied personhood. |
| Punishments             | No statutes regulating punishment of slaves; courts did not generally exercise jurisdiction over slaves as property. | Legalized brutal corporal and capital punishments without standard legal protections. |
| Rights and Protections  | English liberties recognized for free subjects; no positive law for slavery. | Denial of legal rights: no jury trials, no due process for slaves, ability to impose harsh penalties. |
| Movement Control        | No regulation of slave movement in Britain; no pass systems by law. | Pass systems mandated; travel without passes penalized severely. |
| Enforcement             | Focus on trade and slave trade oversight; no systematic regulation of enslaved individuals. | Local colonial authorities, militias, and magistrates aggressively enforced slave codes. |
| Legislative Authority   | Parliament sovereign but inactive on slavery issues; trade-focused legislation only. | Colonial assemblies enacted slave codes independently, creating legal dissonance with metropolitan law. |

## 3. Recent Peer-Reviewed Scholarship (2015–2025) on Legal Dehumanization of Enslaved People

Multiple recent academic studies analyze the legal dehumanization of enslaved people in the British Empire during the early 18th century, emphasizing the codification of enslaved Africans as property through legal language and judicial practices.

### Highlights:

- **Legal Commodification in Courts:**  
  “*Negroes Goods and Merchandizes*: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700–1763” explores how enslaved Africans were linguistically categorized as goods, facilitating their treatment as property in transatlantic commerce and legal frameworks [1].

- **Racialized Law and Denial of Subjecthood:**  
  Dana Y. Rabin’s study of English law demonstrates a consistent racialized legal regime denying enslaved people legal personhood, maintaining slavery through laws that privileged white subjects and enforced chattel status in the colonies [2].

- **Nuanced Views on Commodification:**  
  Nicholas T. Rinehart reflects on the complex interplay between commodification and humanity, arguing legal systems simultaneously dehumanized enslaved people while cultural and social realities preserved their humanity [3].

- **Ambiguous Status in Britain:**  
  S.P. Newman’s article reveals enslaved Africans residing in Britain during this period experienced ‘near slavery’ with uncertain legal status, highlighting the tensions between bondage and liberty [4].

- **Legal Formulation of English Slavery:**  
  The “*Bondage*” chapter in *Fragile Empire* shows English slavery’s legal institutions evolved chiefly through colonial slave codes that explicitly treated enslaved Africans as chattel, enshrining dehumanization [5].

- **Resistance and Legal Oppression:**  
  Michael Dickinson’s dissertation explores how enslaved peoples navigated and resisted the brutal legal structures while surviving under systemic dehumanization [6].

- **Educational Resources:**  
  Formal teaching resources highlight the legal frameworks that codified enslaved people as personal property and embedded commodification principles [7].

These works collectively emphasize that legal texts and court practices explicitly dehumanized enslaved people by classifying them as property, facilitating their exploitation and oppression.

## 4. Scholarly Debate on the Molasses Act’s Primary Purpose: Trade Regulation vs. Slave Control

The majority of recent scholarship agrees that:

- **The Molasses Act of 1733 was primarily a trade regulation statute** aimed at protecting British West Indies sugar producers by imposing duties on molasses, sugar, and rum imported from foreign colonies, especially French territories.  
- The Act targeted the colonial economies dependent on cheap foreign molasses that undermined British mercantile interests; it was chiefly an economic protectionist measure.  
- The law imposed import duties that were frequently evaded by smuggling and bribery, rendering enforcement difficult but increasing tensions between colonies and Britain.  
- Though the broader transatlantic economic system of sugar and slavery underpinned the molasses trade, the Molasses Act itself contained **no explicit provisions regulating enslaved people or functioning as a slave control statute.**  
- Some nuanced scholarship appreciates the Act’s indirect link to sustaining slavery economically, as it protected the markets reliant on enslaved labor but cautions against conflating trade legislation with legal control over enslaved populations.  
- No recent peers reviewed articles argue the Molasses Act was intended as an instrument of slave control. Its legal text and parliamentary debates affirm mercantile economic motivations.

## Conclusion

In the 1730s British Empire, enslaved people were legally classified as property or chattels within colonial slave codes (notably Barbados and Jamaica), with statutes and regulations rigorously defining their status, severely restricting rights, regulating movement, and authorizing brutal punishments. By contrast, British Parliamentary law did not explicitly codify slavery nor regulate enslaved people’s personal status or punishment; its legislative efforts focused on trade, including laws such as the Molasses Act (1733), which sought to protect commercial interests rather than managing the institution of slavery directly.

Recent scholarship (2015–2025) sheds light on how legal language and court practices dehumanized enslaved Africans within British imperial law, reinforcing their commodification and exclusion from legal protections. The scholarly consensus holds that the Molasses Act was a trade-control mechanism; its connection to the legal or physical control of enslaved people remains indirect and economic rather than statutory or punitive.

This compilation provides a foundational and nuanced lens for historically accurate representation of enslaved people’s legal status and the political economy of slavery and trade in the British Empire circa the 1730s.
