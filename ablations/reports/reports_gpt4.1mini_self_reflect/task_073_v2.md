# Comparative Research Report: Autism Diagnostic Criteria in DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019)

This report delivers a comprehensive, updated comparative analysis of autism diagnostic criteria from DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) and ICD-11 (World Health Organization, 2019). It focuses on key diagnostic elements such as core social-communication criteria, sensory processing differences and their diagnostic weighting, severity specifiers, age/onset requirements, and functional impairment thresholds. Incorporating a synthesis of relevant peer-reviewed studies, clinical practice guidelines, advocacy group perspectives, diagnostic safety risks, and healthcare reimbursement and coding implications in the US and EU, this report provides an evidence-based, balanced examination appropriate for clinicians, researchers, policymakers, and advocacy stakeholders.

---

## 1. Diagnostic Criteria: Side-by-Side Comparison

| **Diagnostic Element**                     | **DSM-5 (2013)**                                                                                                 | **ICD-11 (2019)**                                                                                                                   |
|--------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Core Social-Communication Criteria**    | Requires persistent deficits **across all three areas**:  
1. Social-emotional reciprocity (e.g., difficulties in back-and-forth interaction)  
2. Nonverbal communicative behaviors (e.g., eye contact, gestures)  
3. Developing, maintaining, understanding relationships  
Consolidates social communication and interaction deficits into one domain; symptom presence from early development but can manifest fully later | Requires persistent deficits in initiating and sustaining social communication and reciprocal interactions that are atypical for age, intellectual, and cultural context.  
Only **one clinical feature in this domain** required for diagnosis, increasing flexibility. Emphasizes clinical judgment over strict symptom counts. Symptoms usually begin in developmental period |
| **Sensory Processing Differences: Definition & Weighting** | Explicitly included in restricted, repetitive behaviors (RRB) (Criterion B): at least two of four RRBs needed, one can be hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input or unusual sensory interests. Sensory features have **equal diagnositic weight** with other RRB features. | Sensory sensitivities described under restricted/repetitive and inflexible behavioral patterns: lifelong, excessive, or persistent atypical sensory sensitivity or interests.  
Sensory features are recognized but **less explicitly weighted**, with broader, less operationalized descriptions. Sensory symptoms do not form a separate subcriterion and are not required. |
| **Severity Specifiers**                    | Three-level severity specifiers, **independently for two domains**:  
- Social Communication: Level 1 (support needed), Level 2 (substantial support), Level 3 (very substantial support)  
- Restricted/Repetitive Behaviors: same 3 levels  
Severity guides clinical support and intervention planning | No formal severity levels specified.  
Functional impact described qualitatively, based on clinician judgment.  
ICD-11 defines **five autism profiles** primarily based on intellectual ability and language use but does not include coded severity levels. |
| **Age / Onset Requirements**                | Symptoms must be present from **early developmental period** but may be masked or not fully manifest until social demands increase. No strict age cut-off. | Symptoms onset typically in **developmental period**, usually early childhood, but symptoms may be subtle or compensated and become evident when demands increase later. Includes recognition of **loss of previously acquired skills**, absent in DSM-5. |
| **Functional Impairment Thresholds**        | Symptoms must cause **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning. Operationally defined in criteria. | Symptoms cause **significant impairment** in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas. Less operationalized; relies on clinical judgment. Masking and compensation acknowledged as complicating impairment assessment. |
| **Additional Diagnostic Features**          | Combines social communication into one domain. Requires specific symptom thresholds (all 3 social communication criteria + at least 2 RRBs). Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder added to capture those with social communication deficits but no RRBs. | No strict symptom count required; diagnosis can be made with variable symptom combinations.  
Loss of previously acquired skills explicitly recognized. Profiles based on intellectual and language abilities guide clinical consideration. |

### Summary

- **DSM-5** presents **structured, quantitatively-defined criteria**, with mandatory symptom counts, explicit inclusion and weighting of sensory symptoms, and formal severity levels.  
- **ICD-11** offers a **broader, more flexible and clinically judgment-based framework**, without strict symptom thresholds or severity codes, highlighting sensory sensitivities but with less explicit diagnostic weight. Its flexible profiles aim at global applicability but may harbor less diagnostic specificity.

## 2. Evidence Synthesis: Peer-Reviewed Studies Comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 Criteria

### 2.1 Systematic Review / Meta-Analysis

**Ricci et al. (2025)** – *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*  
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of standardized ASD diagnostic instruments (e.g., ADOS-2, CARS) aligned primarily with DSM-5 criteria.  
- **Sample:** Aggregated data from 22 studies with tens of thousands of participants, children and adolescents.  
- **Key Statistics:**  
  - ADOS-2 sensitivity ~0.90  
  - Specificity ~0.69  
- **Findings:** Diagnostic tools support DSM-5 criteria with moderate to high diagnostic accuracy but heterogeneity in symptom presentation reduces consistency. Insufficient validated tools exist for ICD-11.  
- **GRADE Quality:** Moderate (due to study heterogeneity and lack of comparable ICD-11 instruments).  
- **Reference:** [3]

### 2.2 Empirical Study 1

**Blázquez Hinojosa et al. (2021)** – *Revista de Psiquiatría y Salud Mental*  
- **Design:** Retrospective clinical study comparing DSM-5 and DSM-IV-TR criteria in 118 clinical child/adolescent cases (no direct ICD-11).  
- **Key Stats:** DSM-5 sensitivity 0.69–1.00 (higher in females), specificity 0.64–0.73.  
- **Findings:** DSM-5 stricter criteria exclude some PDD-NOS cases; results in diagnostic narrowing, impacting treatment patterns.  
- **GRADE Quality:** Moderate (clinical retrospective sample).  
- **Reference:** [2]

### 2.3 Empirical Study 2

**McPartland et al. (2012)** – *JAMCAP*   
- **Design:** Secondary analysis of DSM-IV field trial (N=933), applying proposed DSM-5 criteria.  
- **Key Stats:** Overall sensitivity 60.6%, specificity 94.9%; subgroup sensitivities differ widely (Asperger’s: 25%, PDD-NOS: 28%).  
- **Findings:** DSM-5 improves specificity but reduces sensitivity, especially underdiagnosing higher-functioning and subthreshold ASD.  
- **GRADE Quality:** Moderate (large dataset, retrospective).  
- **Reference:** [10]

### 2.4 Empirical Study 3

**Mahajan et al. (2019)** – *PMC Article*  
- **Design:** Prospective cohort of 1,061 preschool children.  
- **Key Stats:** DSM-5 sensitivity 0.95, specificity 0.78; better identification than DSM-IV.  
- **Findings:** DSM-5 improved diagnostic accuracy, but females and mild cases underrepresented.  
- **GRADE Quality:** Moderate to high.  
- **Reference:** [7]

### 2.5 ICD-11 Diagnostic Utility Critique

**Schmitt et al. (2023)** – *Nature Neuroscience*  
- **Design:** Expert critical perspective on ICD-11 autism criteria.  
- **Findings:** ICD-11’s extensive symptom combinations (~300+) lead to high heterogeneity, risks false positives and difficulties with research replication. Sensory processing differences less explicitly weighted than DSM-5, potentially impairing clinical recognition.  
- **GRADE Quality:** Expert consensus.  
- **Reference:** [5]

## 3. Clinical Guidelines on ICD-11 Adoption and Sensory Processing Weighting

### 3.1 World Health Organization (WHO)  

- ICD-11 criteria officially endorsed by WHO (2019), to be implemented progressively across member states from 2022 onward.  
- Diagnosis recognizes social communication deficits and restricted/repetitive behaviors, including sensory sensitivities, but sensory features are subsumed within restricted behaviors without explicit separation or severity ratings.  
- Emphasizes clinical flexibility and profiles based on intellectual and language ability, aiming for global applicability.  
- WHO materials underline sensory differences as part of autism but do not elevate sensory symptoms to separate diagnostic domains or formal weighting.  
- [10]

### 3.2 American Psychiatric Association (APA)  

- APA maintains DSM-5 as the US clinical and research standard, with detailed operational criteria and severity specifiers.  
- Currently, APA does **not endorse ICD-11 ASD criteria** for routine clinical use in US.  
- APA recognizes DSM-5’s explicit inclusion of sensory processing differences as beneficial but flags ongoing clinical challenges in evaluating impairment and masking.  
- [5]

### 3.3 American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)  

- AAP recommends DSM-5 criteria for standardized screening, diagnosis, and management of ASD in children.  
- Sensory processing differences included under restricted behaviors in DSM-5; standalone diagnostic category of Sensory Processing Disorder discouraged.  
- AAP emphasizes early identification and evidence-based interventions.  
- ICD-11 criteria adoption not currently recommended pending further validation.  
- [8][9]

### 3.4 National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE)  

- NICE guidelines primarily reference DSM-5-based diagnostic frameworks.  
- Recognizes sensory sensitivities as common but integrated within broader ASD diagnostic features.  
- No formal guidance endorsing ICD-11 or specifying sensory processing weighting differences.  
- Recommends individualized assessments including sensory evaluation to guide intervention.  
- [9][13]

## 4. Autism Advocacy Organizations’ Positions on Neurodiversity and Diagnostic Manuals

### 4.1 Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)  

- Advocates for neurodiversity-affirming diagnostic frameworks that respect autistic strengths and experiences.  
- Critiques DSM-5's clinical, deficit-focused lens but supports inclusion of sensory symptoms explicitly in DSM-5 criteria.  
- Concerned that ICD-11’s broadness and inclusion of subjective/internal experience may dilute diagnostic clarity and exclude authentic sensory needs recognition.  
- Emphasizes validating sensory experiences and functional impact over rigid symptom counts.  
- [14]

### 4.2 National Autistic Society (NAS, UK)  

- Prefers DSM-5’s structured criteria for consistent access to services but acknowledges ICD-11’s advantage in global clinical flexibility.  
- Highlights the importance of sensory processing differences explicitly as in DSM-5 over ICD-11’s broader approach.  
- Promotes individualized, neurodiversity-affirming assessments respecting autistic identity and sensory profiles.  
- [13]

### 4.3 Autism Speaks  

- Uses DSM-5 criteria primarily for clinical and research purposes.  
- Highlights sensory processing differences as central to diagnosis in DSM-5 criteria and favorable in improving identification and services.  
- Warns that variability introduced by ICD-11's broad criteria might complicate service delivery and research harmonization globally.  
- Supports ongoing refinement of diagnostic frameworks to better represent sensory and functional differences within autism.  
- [15]

## 5. Diagnostic Safety Risks: Misdiagnosis, Over- and Under-Diagnosis, and Inappropriate Interventions

### 5.1 Misdiagnosis and Underdiagnosis

- DSM-5’s requirement for **all three social communication deficits** and at least two RRBs including sensory symptoms may lead to **underdiagnosis, especially in females, higher-functioning individuals, and those with mild or sensory-focused autism**, due to masking and subtle symptom presentations [10][7].  
- ICD-11’s **broader, more flexible criteria risks false positives and overdiagnosis**, potentially including individuals with social or sensory difficulties unrelated to ASD, complicating differential diagnosis with psychiatric or neurodevelopmental disorders [5][13].  
- Masking behaviors complicate both DSM-5 and ICD-11 assessments, but ICD-11 explicitly acknowledges compensation/camouflaging, which can increase sensitivity but reduce specificity [5].  
- Misdiagnosing ASD as personality disorders or anxiety disorders, especially in females with masking, leads to risks regarding prognosis and treatment. Careful developmental history and multi-dimensional assessment are essential [8].

### 5.2 Overdiagnosis

- ICD-11’s heterogeneous symptom combinations increase the risk of **overdiagnosis** and inflated ASD prevalence rates, with potential impact on research validity and allocation of services [5].  
- DSM-5 may allow overdiagnosis if clinicians do not apply criteria rigorously, particularly when external pressures for diagnosis exist or when comorbid conditions mimic ASD features.

### 5.3 Inappropriate Interventions

- Variability in diagnosis affects intervention decisions:  
  - Overbroad ICD-11 diagnoses may result in generic therapeutic approaches not tailored to ASD-specific profiles.  
  - DSM-5’s narrower criteria can restrict access, particularly for individuals with predominant sensory issues or mild social deficits but significant impairment.  
- Both risks highlight the importance of comprehensive, functional assessment alongside diagnosis.

## 6. Reimbursement and Coding Practices: United States and European Union

### 6.1 United States

- The US primarily uses **ICD-10-CM** coding system for billing ASD diagnoses, while DSM-5 provides clinical diagnostic framework.  
- ICD-10-CM codes (e.g., F84.0 for Autism Spectrum Disorder) correspond largely to DSM-5 diagnosis but do not capture ICD-11 nuanced profiles or severity specifiers.  
- **ICD-11 adoption is ongoing but not yet mandatory or fully integrated in US health systems**; projected implementation between 2025–2028.  
- Transition to ICD-11 promises improved coding specificity and electronic health record integration but entails significant training and infrastructure investments.  
- Insurance coverage and reimbursement policies currently **rely on ICD-10 coding aligned with DSM-5 diagnosis**, minimal immediate effect expected from ICD-11 adoption on coverage decisions.  
- ICD-11’s flexibility and absence of formal severity specifiers may complicate reimbursement authorization processes reliant on explicit functional impairment grades.  
- [1][23][24]

### 6.2 European Union

- Most European countries use **ICD-10** or are transitioning to **ICD-11** progressively for diagnosis coding; ASD coded under chapter 6A02 in ICD-11.  
- ICD-11 coding incorporates subtypes and profiles reflecting intellectual and language levels, improving diagnostic granularity and potentially aiding individualized care and resource allocation.  
- Reimbursement and social services eligibility rely heavily on ICD coding, so ICD-11 adoption may influence service distribution but does not fundamentally alter coverage frameworks, which depend on national health policies.  
- Variability in implementation speed and healthcare infrastructure across EU countries creates uneven impacts.  
- Use of multidisciplinary diagnosis including DSM-5 criteria remains common clinically, balancing research consistency and insurance coding needs.  
- [15][18][19]

## 7. Summary: Key Insights

- DSM-5 and ICD-11 both define autism spectrum disorder with overlapping but distinct conceptual and operational diagnostic criteria.  
- DSM-5 favors structured, symptom-count-based diagnosis, explicit sensory symptom inclusion, and formal severity levels, enhancing research consistency but risking exclusion of some presentations.  
- ICD-11 adopts a clinically flexible, globally-applicable framework with flexible symptom requirements, explicit sensory inclusion but less diagnostic weighting, and no formal severity coding, potentially increasing heterogeneity and diagnostic uncertainty.  
- Evidence indicates DSM-5 improves specificity but at sensitivity costs, particularly missing higher-functioning, female, or sensory-predominant cases; ICD-11’s broad scope may lead to overdiagnosis and diagnostic imprecision.  
- Major clinical guidelines (WHO, APA, AAP, NICE) presently endorse DSM-5 or DSM-based frameworks while cautiously monitoring ICD-11 adoption and impacts.  
- Advocacy organizations appreciate DSM-5’s explicit sensory recognition and tend to critique ICD-11’s vaguer criteria from neurodiversity-affirming perspectives.  
- Diagnostic safety risks include misdiagnosis (both over- and under-), complications from masking, and risks of inappropriate interventions due to criteria differences.  
- US reimbursement is anchored in ICD-10-CM codes linked to DSM-5 clinical diagnosis; ICD-11 implementation may improve coding precision but requires system upgrades.  
- EU countries are transitioning to ICD-11 coding with enhanced ASD profile granularity, but this does not yet radically alter reimbursement or coverage structures.
