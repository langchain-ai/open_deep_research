## 1. Overview of Current NCD IPO Landscape in India (April 24, 2026)

### 1.1 Market Context  
Non-Convertible Debentures are secured or unsecured fixed-income debt instruments that Indian corporates issue to raise medium to long term funds without diluting equity. These instruments are increasingly important in India’s ₹58+ lakh crore corporate debt market, serving both institutional and retail fixed income investors. As of early 2026, the market continues to be buoyed by RBI monetary easing (repo rate at 5.25%), increased retail participation supported by SEBI reforms, and steady credit demand particularly from NBFCs and infrastructure financing entities.

## 2. Credit Rating Comparison: CRISIL, ICRA, and Other Agencies

### 2.1 Comparative Rating Overview  
CRISIL and ICRA ratings are broadly aligned but independently assessed, providing a clear hierarchy of credit quality:

| Rating Level       | Indicative Risk Grade           |
|--------------------|--------------------------------|
| AAA / Aaa          | Highest safety, negligible default risk |
| AA / Aa            | Very high safety, very low credit risk |
| A / A              | Adequate safety with low credit risk |
| BBB / Baa          | Moderate credit risk, lowest investment grade |
| Below BBB          | Speculative, non-investment grade |

### 2.2 Rating Summary of Active NCD Issuers

- **Muthoot Fincorp Ltd:** CRISIL AA- Positive indicating strong safety with positive outlook; Brickwork AA Stable supports confidence but no ICRA rating publicly available.  
- **ICL Fincorp Ltd:** Acuité BBB- Stable denotes lower-medium investment grade; coupon rates above 11% imply higher risk premium; no CRISIL rating available.  
- **Capri Global Capital:** Fitch BB- (below investment grade) contrasts with Acuité AA, indicating rating agency divergence and highlighting caution; CRISIL details not disclosed.  
- **SBFC Finance Limited:** India Ratings IND AA- Stable indicates high safety and consistent credit profile.  
- **Nexus Select Trust and Oriental InfraTrust:** Both CRISIL and ICRA reaffirm AAA/Stable ratings, signaling the safest instruments in the current IPO tranche universe.  

### 2.3 Rating Stability and Historical Default Patterns  
- AAA-rated Indian NCDs have historically exhibited negligible default rates over 1-3 year bonds according to CRISIL’s FY 2025 Default Study.  
- Downgrades and defaults are typically preceded by deteriorations in asset quality, rising leverage, or sectoral downturns, especially among NBFCs or newly formed special purpose vehicles (SPVs).  
- Institutional investors emphasize AAA/AA bonds for safety, relegating BBB-rated NCDs to smaller portfolio holdings due to higher risk of credit migration.

## 3. Post-Tax Yield Calculations for Generic Investors vs. Senior Citizens

### 3.1 Taxation Overview Relevant to NCDs in FY 2025-26 (Assessment Year 2026-27)  
- Interest income from NCDs is fully taxable as **Income from Other Sources** at the marginal tax rate applicable to the investor’s income slab.  
- Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) is levied at 10% if interest payments exceed ₹10,000 per annum; senior citizens benefit from higher exemption thresholds and can submit Form 15H to avoid TDS deduction.  
- Senior citizens (aged 60+) can claim Section 80TTB deduction up to ₹50,000 of interest income from deposits and NCD interest, applicable only under the **Old Tax Regime**.  
- The New Regime, lacking most deductions including 80TTB, imposes direct slab rates on gross income but with lower slab thresholds and rebates.  
- Long-term capital gains tax on listed NCDs held >12 months is 12.5%, but fixed income portfolio managers generally prioritize interest income yields as primary return.  

### 3.2 Methodology for Post-Tax Yield Calculation  
- For **generic investors** (non-senior citizens or seniors on new regime):  
  \[
  \text{Post-tax yield} = \text{Gross coupon} \times (1 - \text{marginal tax rate including cess})
  \]
- For **senior citizens under old regime** (benefiting from Section 80TTB):  
  \[
  \text{Taxable interest} = \max(0, \text{Gross interest} - ₹50,000)
  \]
  
  Tax payable on taxable interest computed at marginal slab. Actual post-tax interest = Gross interest - Tax on taxable interest.  
- Investors filing under old regime with moderate to high incomes realize significant post-tax yield improvement from 80TTB.  
- TDS does not reduce tax liability; excess TDS is recoverable during filing.

## 4. Historical Precedents & Pattern Recognition in Indian NCD/Credit Markets

- Indian NCD markets over the past decade have evolved from niche instruments to widely accepted fixed income products, with NBFCs and PSU issuers dominating volume.  
- Major credit crises (IL&FS and DHFL defaults during 2018–2019) underscored liquidity and credit risk in mid-tier NBFC bonds, prompting institutional investors to emphasize investment-grade papers with strong credit enhancement.[33]  
- Rating transitions tend to cluster before defaults; deteriorating asset quality, rising NPAs, and expanding leverage commonly precede rating downgrades and crises in NBFCs.  
- Flat yield curves since 2024-2026 reflect RBI’s rate stability and market expectations of modest easing, compressing spreads on AAA-rated bonds and prompting investors to seek yield uplift with carefully selected A- or BBB-rated instruments.[26]  
- Segment differentiation:  
  - AAA-rated infrastructure trusts and PSU NCDs consistently offer lowest default risk and stable, if lower, coupons.[9][10]  
  - NBFCs offer higher yields but require careful credit scrutiny and diversification to mitigate idiosyncratic issuer risk.[14][16]

## 5. Competitive Positioning and Relative Value Analysis

- **AAA-rated NCDs (Nexus Select Trust, Oriental InfraTrust):**  
  - Positioned as safest credit instruments, suitable for core fixed income holdings, offering coupon yields around 6.5% to 7.5%, reflecting low risk and stable cash flows.[9][10]  
- **AA- / A-rated NBFCs (Muthoot Fincorp, SBFC Finance):**  
  - Provide yield pick-up (8.5%-9.25%) with acceptable credit risk for moderately risk-tolerant institutional retiree portfolios.  
  - Moderate leverage and stable asset quality metrics justify inclusion at prudent allocations.[16]  
- **BBB- or below-rated NBFCs (ICL Fincorp, Capri Global):**  
  - High coupons (up to 11.5%), but entail elevated credit risk and leverage concerns (Capri Global with 2.5x debt-to-equity).  
  - Recommended only for small "satellite" portfolio allocations subject to strict credit monitoring and potentially shorter maturities or staggered rollovers.[14][20]  
- **Coupon and Tenor Flexibility:**  
  - Monthly, annual, and cumulative interest payment options are extensively available, supporting retirees needing predictable cash flows or lump sum reinvestments.[1][5]  
- **Liquidity:**  
  - Secondary market trading volumes vary; AAA and AA issuers often enjoy better liquidity while small NBFCs and lower-rated issues carry liquidity risk, necessitating hold-to-maturity strategies.

## 6. Comprehensive Risk Assessment

### 6.1 Credit Risk  
- Defaults concentrated in BBB and below rated segments with weak financials, high leverage, and sectoral stress.[3][33]  
- Capri Global Capital’s 2.5x leverage and regulatory risk elevate its credit risk profile.  
- AA and AAA-rated issuers historically demonstrate negligible default. Continuous monitoring for rating migration is essential.

### 6.2 Interest Rate Risk  
- NCDs with longer maturities (>5 years) present higher sensitivity to interest rate moves; the currently flat corporate curve reduces premium for extending tenor but heightens valuation risk if rates rise unexpectedly.  
- RBI’s easing bias and stable inflation (CPI ~3.5%) support moderate interest rate risk as of April 2026.

### 6.3 Liquidity Risk  
- Secondary NCD market remains thinner relative to government securities.  
- Illiquid instruments necessitate planning for hold-to-maturity or staggered maturity laddering for retirees’ cash flow needs.

### 6.4 Regulatory & Policy Risk  
- SEBI’s regulatory reforms increase issuer transparency but ongoing changes in TDS, foreign investment limits, and reporting requirements may impact issuer behavior and investor compliance.[6][13]  
- Recent rules easing physical statement requirements and allowing smaller minimum investments benefit liquidity and investor participation.

### 6.5 Tail Risk  
- Global geopolitical tensions (Middle East conflict), currency depreciation risks, and macroeconomic shocks can impact credit spreads and issuer solvency, requiring diversified portfolio construction and active risk management.[31][38]

## 7. Regulatory Landscape and Policy Implications

- SEBI mandates stringent disclosure, rating requirements, involvement of debenture trustees, and creation of Debenture Redemption Reserves (DRR) for investor protection in NCD issuances.  
- Tax reforms harmonized TDS thresholds and significantly enhanced Section 80TTB benefits for senior citizens under the old regime, improving tax efficiency for retirement income portfolios.  
- RBI’s supportive monetary policy stance (repo rate at 5.25%) encourages issuers while cautioning on asset liability mismatches and credit quality.  
- Increased foreign participation in Indian debt markets via liberalized FPIs pathways enhances demand and secondary liquidity.  
- SEBI’s relaxation of paper statement requirements and digital subscription platforms improve transparency and accessibility for institutional and retail investors alike.[6][11][16]

## 8. Forward Yield Curve and Leading Economic Indicators (April 2026)

- India’s 10-year government bond yield ranges between 6.63% and 7.0%, signaling a relatively stable, moderately accommodative interest rate environment.[18]  
- Inflation maintained near RBI’s target band (~3.5%), supporting stable real yields and borrowing costs.  
- GDP growth projections remain robust at 6.7% to 7.8% for FY 2026-27, anchoring credit fundamentals.  
- Credit spreads for AAA-rated corporate bonds hover near historic lows; spreads widen cautiously in lower-rated bonds reflecting risk aversion.  
- Global risks (currency volatility, geopolitical tension) inject intermittent spread volatility, warranting constant monitoring.  
- Liquidity conditions remain moderate, with domestic institutional investor flows offsetting foreign selling pressures.[25][30]

## 9. Investment Thesis and Recommendations

- **Core Allocation:** AAA/AA-rated, well-capitalized issuers and infrastructure trusts (Nexus Select, Oriental InfraTrust, SBFC Finance) represent the safest fixed income holdings for retirees, delivering stable income with minimal credit risk.  
- **Yield Enhancement:** Moderate allocation to AA-/A-rated NBFCs such as Muthoot Fincorp can enhance portfolio yields to ~8.5%-9.2% with acceptable risk levels, balancing income and capital preservation.  
- **Selective Opportunistic Exposure:** Small allocations to BBB or below-rated issuers (ICL Fincorp, Capri Global) are viable only for credit-aware, risk-tolerant portfolios with strict monitoring and reduced tenure. Avoid issuers with debt-to-equity exceeding 1.5x unless credit compensations and covenants are strong.  
- **Tax Strategy:** Seniors should leverage Section 80TTB benefits under the old regime to enhance post-tax returns by 15%-20%, improving retirement income stability. Coordination with tax advisors for regime selection and return filing is essential.[15]  
- **Portfolio Construction:** Emphasize diversification across issuers, tenors, and sectors; consider tenure laddering to match retiree cash flow needs and mitigate reinvestment risk.  
- **Risk Monitoring:** Continually assess rating transitions, asset quality metrics, and macroeconomic indicators to proactively manage credit and liquidity risks.  
- **Liquidity Consideration:** Institutional investors should prefer NCDs listed on NSE/BSE with established secondary liquidity, particularly for retirement portfolios requiring greater cash flow predictability.

## 10. Conclusion

The current landscape of Indian NCD IPOs offers institutional investors and retirement-focused fixed income managers a spectrum of options balancing credit risk and return. AAA and AA-rated issuers provide robust capital preservation and predictable income, aligned with retirees’ risk tolerance. Moderate risk-taking with higher yielding NBFC NCDs can enhance portfolio income but necessitates rigorous due diligence, active monitoring, and conservative sizing, especially for issuers with high leverage or asset quality concerns. Tax efficiency strategies centered on senior citizen benefits considerably improve net returns, making these instruments particularly attractive for retiree income portfolios. Incorporating historical lessons, risk controls, diversification, and regulatory updates, the structured use of India’s NCD market can be a prudent cornerstone of retirement fixed income allocation against a stable macroeconomic and supportive policy backdrop.
