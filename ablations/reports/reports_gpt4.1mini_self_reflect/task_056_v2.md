## 1. Market Size and Growth Metrics (2018–2024)

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication industry, strictly within the construction and structural applications segment, exhibits a mature and evolving market landscape covering revenue, employment, and production tonnage from 2018 through 2024. These metrics are primarily sourced from authoritative government bodies such as Stats NZ, Ministry of Business Innovation and Employment (MBIE), Steel Construction New Zealand (SCNZ), and IBISWorld.

### 1.1 Revenue

- The national structural steel fabricating sector was valued at approximately **NZD 1.8 billion** by 2025/2026.  
- From 2018 to 2020, the industry saw steady revenues but experienced a contraction with a **compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of -6.0% between 2020 and 2025**, primarily caused by construction market slowdowns due to inflationary pressures, rising interest rates, and labor constraints.  
- Modest recovery is projected from 2021 to 2026, with a **CAGR of about 2.1%**, reflecting renewed infrastructure investment and technological adoption.  
- Global projections by Grand View Research estimate the New Zealand structural steel market revenue in 2024 to be approx. **USD 50.3 million (NZD 78 million)** with a forecasted 6% CAGR growth to 2030 for construction-related products [2].  
- The broader steel manufacturing sector, inclusive of structural steel, shows signs of growth stimulated by government infrastructure spending and private commercial projects.

### 1.2 Employment

- Approximately **4,100 workers** were employed in the structural steel fabrication sector by 2025, down from roughly 4,600 in 2018, evidencing an employment decline of nearly **9% over five years**, attributed to automation and workforce consolidation trends.  
- SCNZ indicates around **5,000 employees** across approximately **153 active structural steel fabrication companies** nationally, highlighting a concentration of SMEs employing typically 15 staff per firm.  
- Regional employment is heavily concentrated in Auckland (~1,950 workers), Canterbury (~630), Wellington (~520), and Waikato (~450) [16].  
- The total construction sector accounts for over 308,000 employees (2023), with structural steel fabricators comprising a niche but critical subset supporting overall sector activity.

### 1.3 Tonnage Fabricated

- Fabrication capacity nationally has grown from an estimated **120,000 tonnes in 2018 to about 150,000 tonnes by 2023**, with actual output running near **100,000 tonnes annually**, yielding a notable spare capacity estimated between **28–40%** depending on project cycles and seasonal demands.  
- Structural steel accounts for **over 50% market share in the multilevel construction market nationally** and exceeds **80% share in Christchurch** due to the city’s seismic risk and preference for steel for earthquake resilience [1].  
- New Zealand Steel’s Glenbrook mill produces around **650,000 tonnes annually** of steel products, but this figure extends beyond fabrication to include flat rolled steel and raw steel products.  
- Steel Construction New Zealand’s certification schemes ensure that more than **90% of structural steel output** complies with local and international fabrication standards [1].

### 1.4 Industry Characteristics and Trends

- The market remains competitive and sensitive to steel price volatility, labor market challenges, and weather-induced reconstruction demand.  
- Investment in modern fabrication technologies, including automation and digital fabrication workflows, has been key in productivity improvements and workforce safety gains.  
- Quality assurance is robust, with the Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC) covering the majority of output, fostering export potential and better alignment with international standards.  
- Structural steel is the preferred material in seismic-prone areas and large-scale infrastructure, supported by engineering excellence and innovation initiatives [1][14].

## 3. New Zealand Import Tariff Rates on Raw Steel (HS Codes 7207–7216) and Impact on Fabricator Margins

### 3.1 Current Tariff Rates

- According to the New Zealand Customs Working Tariff Document (effective 1 April 2026), raw steel imports under HS codes **7207 to 7216** are subject to a mix of **0%, 5%, and 10% tariff rates**, depending on steel types and forms.  
- Tariffs on most common steel categories (e.g., semi-finished products, bars, shapes, plates) generally range between **0–5%** where preferential trade agreements apply, including CPTPP (Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership), allowing **duty-free entry for qualifying products** [1][3][5].  
- The **Most Favored Nation (MFN) rates** can go up to **9.5%** but applied rates are often lower or zero due to FTAs.  
- Tariff concessions are in place for certain imports, notably for **electric arc furnace equipment** supporting domestic green steel production, easing capital costs for modernization and capacity expansion [6].

### 3.2 Impact on Fabricator Profit Margins

- Fabricators importing raw steel face **moderate additional costs due to tariffs**, but the impact is **limited given high domestic production**, especially by New Zealand Steel, which supplies over 90% of demand internally.  
- Imported steel is often required for specialized or higher-grade applications or where local production falls short, increasing input costs due to tariffs, freight, compliance testing (e.g., toughness standards), and customs levies [8].  
- Tariffs add **5-10% uplift** on imported steel costs in some categories, squeezing profit margins unless offset by efficiency gains or higher product pricing.  
- Strong domestic supply chains and vertical integration (e.g., Pacific Steel supported by New Zealand Steel billets) provide a **buffer against global price and tariff volatility** [14].  
- Global market shifts, including US tariffs on steel imports (up to 50%), indirectly affect New Zealand through price volatility and supply chain disruptions, though NZ’s own tariffs remain comparatively low [9][14].  
- Import tariff effects are further complicated by shifting levies on goods imports and compliance costs, highlighting important cost management challenges for fabricators dependent on global inputs [5].

## 4. Major Infrastructure Projects Driving Structural Steel Demand (2018–2024)

Three major projects distinctly shape the demand for construction structural steel fabrication across New Zealand, each with detailed timelines and estimated tonnages drawn from Infrastructure New Zealand, project portals, and official announcements.

### 4.1 City Rail Link (CRL), Auckland

- **Overview:** Largest urban rail infrastructure project in New Zealand, involving twin 3.45 km tunnels beneath Auckland’s central business district, linking major stations with two new underground stations for improved urban rail flow.  
- **Timeline:** Construction commenced 2016; civil construction completion expected by mid-2026; full operational opening planned late 2026.  
- **Steel Demand:** Approximately **25,000 tonnes** of reinforcing steel bars fabricated domestically (notably Pacific Steel using New Zealand Steel billets). Around **4,000 tonnes** of structural steel framing has been incorporated for key structural elements and heritage building supports [6][7][8][9].  
- **Project Value:** NZD 5.493 billion.  
- The project represents a peak demand phase for structural steel fabrication over multiple years, sustaining employment and capacity utilization.

### 4.2 National Green Steel Waikato Project, Hampton Downs

- **Overview:** A government-fast-tracked scrap steel recycling and processing facility employing electric arc furnace (EAF) technology to produce **up to 200,000 tonnes** per annum of high-quality structural steel from recycled scrap.  
- **Timeline:** Application lodged 2025; construction planned 2025–2027; operations expected post-2027.  
- **Steel Demand:** Targets reducing steel imports by increasing domestic structural steel availability; will supply major infrastructure projects directly from local production, boosting regional production capacity.  
- **Strategic Importance:** Aligns with New Zealand’s low-carbon transition, creating ~200 skilled jobs and supporting a circular economy [3][4][12][13].

### 4.3 Auckland Airport Expansion

- **Overview:** A $5.7 billion aeronautical infrastructure upgrade includes terminal expansions, taxiways, and airfield upgrades to accommodate future passenger growth.  
- **Timeline:** Ongoing from 2022, with significant structural steel fabrication phase running through 2024–2028.  
- **Steel Demand:** Over **1,600 tonnes of steel columns** fabricated with an estimated **33,000 tonnes** of associated structural steel used or planned for installation.  
- D&H Steel Construction and other local fabricators play a key role in supplying steel components [16][17][18][19][20].

## 5. Strategic Risks and Opportunities for Structural Steel Fabricators

### 5.1 Strategic Risks

#### a) Tariff Exposure and Trade Volatility

- While New Zealand applies relatively low tariffs, **global steel tariff volatility (e.g., US Section 232 tariffs)** and shifts in trade regimes impact supply costs indirectly.  
- The **complexity of tariff classifications and compliance** requirements add risk and cost to imported steel inputs [1][8][14].

#### b) Supply Chain Concentration and Disruption

- Heavy dependence on **few major suppliers**, including New Zealand Steel and selected overseas steel mills, increases vulnerability to raw material shortages, price spikes, and freight disruptions, exacerbated post-COVID and amid geopolitical tensions.  
- Shipping delays and port congestion remain operational risks, impacting just-in-time fabrication models [2][6][14].

#### c) Project Pipeline and Funding Uncertainty

- The **cyclic nature of construction**, with projects often deferred or cancelled due to funding constraints or regulatory delays, creates volatility in fabricator order books.  
- Workforce shortages and increased capital costs further tighten project delivery risk [3][19].

### 5.2 Strategic Opportunities

#### a) Growing Infrastructure Pipeline and Government Support

- Government infrastructure spending exceeding NZD 237 billion over the next decade ensures **sustained demand** for structural steel fabrication in transport, utilities, and public building sectors.  
- Fast-track approvals and PPP reforms incentivize private sector participation, expanding the project pipeline [1][22][23].

#### b) Technological Innovation and Digitalisation

- Adoption of **Building Information Modelling (BIM)**, automation, robotics, and modular prefabrication reduces costs, accelerates delivery, and improves quality.  
- Advanced fabrication technologies also mitigate labor shortages and support lean manufacturing principles within the sector [21][22].

#### c) Sustainability and Green Steel Expansion

- The **National Green Steel project** represents a major opportunity to **expand domestic capacity** for sustainable steel, reduce carbon footprint, and improve supply chain security.  
- Enhanced recycling, low-emission steelmaking (EAF), and environmental certifications are becoming differentiators for fabricators, opening market access and compliance advantages [13][25].

#### d) Regional Production Capacity Expansions

- Investments in regional fabrication hubs in Canterbury and Waikato diversify production risks and reduce logistical costs.  
- Regional expansions also mitigate skilled labor shortages by fostering localized workforce development [22][26].

## Summary and Outlook

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market in the construction sector remains a critical yet cautious industry. Market size reached NZD 1.8 billion with moderate growth outlooks tempered by economic pressures and employment shifts. The leading fabricators, ranging from large publicly traded entities like Steel & Tube Holdings Ltd to specialized family-owned firms such as Culham Engineering, demonstrate sector diversity and resilience.

Moderate steel import tariffs, largely mitigated by bilateral and multilateral FTAs, impose some cost pressure, particularly when importing specialized steel grades. However, strong domestic steel production and green steel initiatives provide competitive insulation.

Major infrastructure projects including the City Rail Link, green steel manufacturing developments in Waikato, and Auckland Airport expansion underpin demand for structural steel fabrication through mid to late 2020s. Risks tied to tariff volatility, supply chain fragility, and project funding uncertainties persist but are balanced by opportunities in technology adoption, sustainability initiatives, and expanding domestic production capacities.

Fabricators who leverage innovation, pursue sustainability leadership, and actively participate in the growing infrastructure pipeline are positioned for growth post-2026, supporting New Zealand’s economic development and low-carbon transition goals.
