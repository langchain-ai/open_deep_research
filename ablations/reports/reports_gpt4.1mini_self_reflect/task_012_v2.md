# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality Rates and Prenatal Care Access  
## Among Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh vs. Karen Refugee Communities in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)  

---

## Introduction  

This report provides a detailed comparative analysis of maternal mortality and prenatal care access among Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand, focusing on the period from 2017 to 2023. It compares critical maternal and neonatal health indicators—specifically antenatal care (ANC) completion rates (four or more visits), skilled birth attendance (SBA) percentages, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, and neonatal mortality within 28 days. The report investigates differences in healthcare delivery models, midwife-to-population ratios, availability of emergency obstetric care (EmOC) within two-hour transport, and cultural mediation involving traditional birth attendants (TBAs). It further assesses the impact of 2021 UNFPA funding reductions on maternal health services continuity and considers contextual sociocultural, security, and infrastructural factors that influence health outcomes in these refugee populations.

---

## Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicators (2017–2023)  

### 1. Maternal Mortality Rates (MMR)  

- **Rohingya in Cox’s Bazar:** The MMR remains critically high, estimated between 295 to 446 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births, significantly exceeding Bangladesh’s national rate and double that of Myanmar’s general population. Primary causes include obstetric hemorrhage (~29%), pregnancy-induced hypertension (~23%), and infections. Social-political factors, restricted movement, and poor health infrastructure exacerbate mortality [2][19][20][33].  
- **Karen in Mae La Camp:** The MMR stands at approximately 126.5 per 100,000 live births as of 2018, higher than Thailand’s national level but lower than Cox’s Bazar. This comparatively lower rate reflects longstanding, integrated health services and emergency care access despite ongoing displacement challenges [47][79].

### 2. Antenatal Care (ANC) Visit Completion Rates (≥4 Visits)  

- **Cox’s Bazar:** ANC coverage is variable. Some reports indicate 71.6% to 80.5% of Rohingya women complete four or more ANC visits, with about 80.5% initiating ANC in the first trimester among those who seek care. However, recent surveys suggest that nearly 47% of women had no ANC during pregnancy, highlighting disparities caused by cultural barriers, mobility restrictions, and misinformation [4][5][7][14].  
- **Mae La Camp:** ANC completion rates are consistently high, with over 90% of pregnant women regularly attending four or more visits. The availability of integrated services through the Thai Ministry of Public Health and NGOs ensures early and continued engagement in prenatal care [47][84].

### 3. Skilled Birth Attendance (SBA) Percentages  

- **Cox’s Bazar:** SBA has improved substantially but remains challenged by cultural preferences and fears. Facility births increased from around 22% in 2018 to over 80% in some supported areas by 2023, primarily due to NGO-led interventions and training of TBAs. Nevertheless, many births still occur at home under TBAs due to distrust of male health staff, privacy concerns, and family influence [5][13][40][41].  
- **Mae La Camp:** SBA coverage averages 78–80%, with the majority of births occurring in health facilities through skilled midwives or nurses. The health system integration and culturally sensitive training have maintained high SBA rates for many years [62][79].

### 4. Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence  

- **Cox’s Bazar:** PPH is the leading cause of maternal deaths, directly contributing to about 29% of fatalities. Weak referral systems, blood shortages, and delayed care seeking worsen outcomes. Community interventions targeting TBA training and emergency referrals attempt to mitigate PPH-related mortality [19][21][22].  
- **Mae La Camp:** Specific PPH incidence data are scarce; however, improved SBA training and availability of EmOC suggest lower relative incidence. The efficient referral and management protocols in the integrated health system contribute to better PPH outcomes [62][79].

### 5. Neonatal Mortality Within 28 Days  

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Neonatal mortality remains high at approximately 27 per 1,000 live births. Most neonatal deaths happen outside facilities, driven by inadequate neonatal care access, poor referral systems, and infrastructure constraints compounded by security challenges [1][24][25].  
- **Mae La Camp:** Neonatal mortality declined significantly—from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births between 2008 and 2011—following the introduction of Special Care Baby Units (SCBUs) staffed by trained refugee health workers. This decline underscores quality improvements in neonatal care [72][73][75].

## Healthcare Delivery Models  

### NGO-Operated Field Hospitals in Cox’s Bazar  

The health sector is dominated by multiple NGOs such as Save the Children, Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF), HOPE Midwifery Institute, MedGlobal, and IOM, each operating specialized facilities and community programs. The 2026 opening of a 59-bed Maternal and Child Hospital epitomizes recent investments in emergency obstetric and neonatal care, including 24/7 Caesarean section capability [23][26][36]. Services include mother-friendly spaces, mobile health teams, and referral hubs, but coordination challenges and a fragmented system remain.

High midwife turnover, security issues, and infrastructure limits impair sustainable service provision. The midwife-to-population ratio remains below ideal standards, despite training programs by BRAC and Hope Foundation increasing skilled birth attendance [30][31][32][34].

### Thai Ministry of Public Health-Integrated Services in Mae La Camp  

Mae La’s health services are integrated into Thailand’s public health system with close NGO coordination (e.g., Malteser International, Shoklo Malaria Research Unit). Comprehensive primary health care (PHC) includes maternal, neonatal, and general health services supported by trained refugee and government health personnel [47][79].

The midwife workforce is more stable and culturally adapted due to localized training programs producing skilled birth attendants from within the community. Emergency obstetric care is accessible within a two-hour radius, with facilitated referrals to Thai government hospitals reducing financial and geographic barriers. Financial subsidies for health workers introduced in 2025 further support sustainability [62][77].

## Workforce, Emergency Care Access, and Cultural Mediation  

| Factor                         | Cox’s Bazar Rohingya                          | Mae La Karen                                 |
|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| **Midwife-to-Population Ratio** | Low ratio, high turnover, scaling-up ongoing; midwife-led centers improve care and acceptability | More stable workforce through government and NGO collaboration; skilled local midwives trained via accelerated programs |
| **Emergency Obstetric Care Access** | NGO-operated CEmONC facilities, 24/7 transport via Referral Hubs but constrained by terrain and security | Accessible EmOC within 2 hours; referral networks involving provincial hospitals and camp clinics; government subsidies assist costs |
| **Cultural Mediation with TBAs** | TBAs influential; training promotes referrals and facility births, but cultural resistance persists | TBAs formally recognized, trained, supplied, and supported by Karen Women's Organisation; integrated into health promotion and care |

The interplay of workforce availability, EmOC access, and cultural engagement is pivotal. Mae La's model benefits from strong integration and cultural accommodation, resulting in better ANC uptake, skilled attendance, and neonatal outcomes. Cox’s Bazar faces more systemic and sociocultural challenges, though targeted interventions have improved indicators in recent years.

## Impact of the 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction  

### Cox’s Bazar  

Funding reductions have led to a decline in maternal health workforce recruitment and sustained service coverage. Despite UNFPA’s efforts to maintain critical services (safe deliveries, newborn care, psychosocial support), maternal mortality reportedly increased to approximately 446 per 100,000 live births by early 2024. MSF operational reports indicate ongoing maternity service provision (around 5,000 deliveries annually), though with increasing strain due to staffing shortages and deteriorating WASH conditions [44][45][26].

These financial constraints have hindered midwife retention and reduced the functionality of mobile teams and safe spaces, risking the gains achieved during 2017–2021 [45][94].

### Mae La Camp  

UNFPA funding cuts in 2021, exacerbated by a 2025 US aid freeze, caused closure or suspension of some NGO-run health facilities (including the International Rescue Committee’s programs). The Thai Ministry of Public Health stepped in with financial support for health workers to mitigate service disruption, but barriers to accessing emergency care outside camps increased due to cost and migrant status issues. Overall, the funding reductions undermined family planning, gender-based violence prevention, and reproductive health education programs, threatening maternal health service continuity [77][80][83].

## Contextual Factors Affecting Maternal and Neonatal Health Outcomes  

- **Cultural Norms and Gender Dynamics:** In both settings, women’s autonomy in health decisions is limited by patriarchal household structures. Female preferences for birth attendants and healthcare providers necessitate gender-sensitive approaches, especially in Cox’s Bazar where male staff presence deters utilization [20][40][42].  
- **Security and Mobility:** Cox’s Bazar faces geographic isolation, monsoon-related access bottlenecks, and camp violence, impeding timely EmOC access. Mae La residents, though more physically accessible to hospitals, experience legal movement restrictions that limit health-seeking options outside the camp [25][54][81].  
- **Health Workforce Turnover:** High attrition among midwives in Cox’s Bazar contrasts with relatively stable and locally trained cadres in Mae La, directly affecting care quality and continuity.  
- **Health Infrastructure:** Cox’s Bazar’s NGO-dependent, resource-constrained facilities contend with supply shortages and limited blood banks; Mae La benefits from integration into Thailand’s more robust health infrastructure, ensuring better resource availability [19][79].  
- **Information and Misinformation:** Distrust and misinformation about formal health care contribute to delayed ANC uptake and low facility birth acceptance in Cox’s Bazar, despite community outreach efforts. Mae La’s long-term health education programs foster community trust and informed maternal choices [7][40][42].

---

## Conclusion  

The maternal and neonatal health outcomes of Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar and Karen refugees in Mae La camp differ substantially due to variations in healthcare delivery models, workforce stability, emergency care access, cultural mediation, funding environments, and contextual barriers.  

- **Maternal Mortality:** Rohingya camps report double the maternal mortality rates of Mae La, reflecting systemic and sociocultural challenges exacerbated by recent funding cuts and complex camp conditions.  
- **Antenatal Care and Skilled Birth Attendance:** Karen refugees maintain higher and more consistent ANC visits and skilled birth attendance due to integrated government-NGO health services and stable workforce, while Rohingya refugees have improved but uneven ANC and SBA coverage amid cultural resistance and health infrastructure fragility.  
- **Postpartum Hemorrhage and Neonatal Mortality:** PPH remains a leading cause of death in Cox’s Bazar, whereas Mae La shows lower PPH risks due to better-trained providers and emergency services. Neonatal mortality is declining in Mae La but still unacceptably high in Cox’s Bazar, tied to facility access and newborn care quality.  
- **Healthcare Models and Cultural Practices:** Integration with national health systems and formal recognition of TBAs enhance Mae La’s maternal health outcomes. Cox’s Bazar relies heavily on NGO-run initiatives battling cultural barriers and logistical challenges.  
- **Funding and Sustainability:** The 2021 UNFPA funding reductions severely impacted both settings but were more disruptive in Cox’s Bazar, emphasizing the fragility of humanitarian maternal health services and the need for sustainable financing and government engagement.

Addressing these disparities necessitates continued investment in culturally adapted midwifery training, strengthening emergency obstetric care, community empowerment to overcome gender and cultural barriers, and securing sustainable funding to maintain and scale critical maternal and neonatal health services in both refugee populations.
