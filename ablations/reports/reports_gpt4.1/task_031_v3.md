# Evidence-Based, Guideline-Driven Protocol for Assessment and Management of a 68-Year-Old Man with Recurrent Near-Syncope, Symptomatic Sinus Bradycardia, and ECG Evidence of Dropped Beats with Variable PR Interval (Likely Mobitz I AV Block)

## Overview

This protocol addresses the comprehensive assessment and management of an elderly male patient presenting multiple times with recurrent near-syncope, symptomatic sinus bradycardia, and an ECG pattern characterized by dropped beats accompanied by a variable PR interval. The approach explicitly distinguishes Mobitz I (Wenckebach) AV block from other conduction pathologies, specifies ambulatory electrocardiographic monitoring and escalation protocols, and aligns all recommendations with current major guidelines (ACC/AHA/HRS 2017–2018, ESC 2017–2024) and recent literature.

---

## 1. Diagnostic Clarification: Distinguishing Mobitz I (Wenckebach) from Other AV Blocks and Arrhythmias

### ECG Pattern Interpretation

- **Mobitz I (Wenckebach) Second-Degree AV Block** is most likely given the described ECG (sinus bradycardia, occasional dropped beats, and a progressively varying PR interval between 180 and 240 ms).
    - **Classic diagnostic features:**
        - Progressive lengthening of the PR interval on consecutive beats, culminating in a non-conducted (dropped) QRS complex.
        - The cycle then resets, and the process repeats.
        - Most often associated with a narrow QRS complex, indicating an intranodal (AV node) origin[1][2][3][4].

- **Mobitz II Second-Degree AV Block** would present with constant PR intervals for conducted beats, with sudden dropped QRS complexes and a higher risk for progression to complete heart block.
    - Differentiation is crucial: an absence of PR prolongation preceding dropped beats supports Mobitz II[2][4][5].
    - Mobitz II is generally a more sinister finding, frequently associated with a wide QRS (His-Purkinje system disease) and mandates urgent pacing consideration[2][5].

- **Other Considerations:**
    - 2:1 AV block (alternating conducted and non-conducted P waves) cannot be reliably classified without additional ECG evidence, as PR interval trends are indeterminate[2][5].
    - Sinus bradycardia alone features slow but regular rhythms without dropped beats; the presence of dropped beats and variable PR essentially excludes isolated sinus bradycardia[3][6].
    - Vagal influences, medication effects (notably AV-nodal blockers), or underlying structural heart disease can provoke or worsen Mobitz I patterns[1][3][4][6].

**Conclusion:** The described ECG is most consistent with Mobitz I (Wenckebach) AV block, a typically intranodal conduction abnormality, distinct from Mobitz II both in clinical prognosis and required urgency of intervention[1][2][3][4][5].

## 2. Outpatient Monitoring Strategies: Operational Details, Modalities, and Escalation Parameters

### Minimum Monitoring Duration

- **At least 14 days** of ambulatory ECG monitoring is now guideline-supported to optimize detection of intermittent conduction abnormalities and correlate symptoms with arrhythmias, especially given the patient's recurrent, unpredictable episodes[7][8][9][10][11][12].

### Preferred ECG Monitoring Types

- **Patch Monitors (e.g., Zio Patch):**
    - Non-invasive, wire-free, and comfortable for up to 14 days continuous recording.
    - High diagnostic yield for infrequent symptoms[8][9][10].

- **Mobile Cardiac Outpatient Telemetry (MCOT):**
    - Real-time, continuous rhythm analysis with automated arrhythmia alerts.
    - Immediate clinician notification for actionable events[9][10][11].
    - Particularly recommended when near-syncope/syncope is recurrent and safety is a concern.

- **Extended Holter Monitors:**
    - Continuous ECG (3–14 days depending on device and insurance).
    - Can be limited by lack of real-time alerting[8][11].

- **Event (External Loop) Recorders:**
    - Patient or device-activated, good for less frequent events over weeks.
    - Lower yield for sudden/brief episodes without pre-warning but still reasonable if patch/MCOT unavailable[10][11].

### Actionable Alert Thresholds (for clinician response)

- **Auto-Flag or Urgent Escalation Alerts:**
    - **Pauses >3.0 seconds**, especially during waking or symptomatic periods[7][8][9][10][11].
    - **Episodes of symptomatic or profound bradycardia** (HR <40 bpm) not explained by medications/reversible causes[7][13].
    - **New or evolving high-grade AV block** (progression to Mobitz II, 2:1, high-grade block, or complete heart block)[4][5][9][10].
    - **Clustered or frequent non-conducted P waves** (e.g., multiple consecutive dropped beats).
    - **Arrhythmias temporally linked to symptoms** (syncope, near-syncope, presyncope, acute pallor, sudden confusion, or transient aphasia)[7][8][9][10][13].
    - **Other dangerous rhythms** (ventricular tachycardia, asystole).

- **Protocol for Alerts:**
    - Automatic alert to clinician/team within **24 hours** (preferably same-day) for any listed criteria.
    - If critical event occurs (prolonged pause, high-grade/complete AV block, severe symptomatic bradycardia), **instruct patient/family to seek immediate emergency care**.

### Patient Operational Instructions

- Wear the device continuously, removing only for bathing if required.
- Record and promptly report all symptoms (dizziness, syncope, confusion, pallor, inability to talk) using device diary or integrated app[8][11].
- Keep all contact information for monitoring provider and emergency team accessible.

## 3. Inpatient vs. Outpatient Pathways: Time-Based Escalation and Follow-up

### Inpatient Admission

**Criteria:**

- Persistent or recurrent near-syncope or syncope plus evidence of conduction abnormality (as in this case)[4][5][9][10].
- Unstable vitals or evidence of hemodynamic compromise.

**Monitoring:**
- Admit to monitored bed with continuous telemetry.

**Consultation:**
- **Same-day or within 24 hours**: Arrange cardiology/electrophysiology consultation for confirmatory diagnosis and individualized management plan[4][5].
    - If persistent, progressive, or high-grade block is documented, discussion for urgent pacing is mandatory.

**Further Testing:**
- Serial ECGs, bedside rhythm strip review, orthostatic vitals, laboratory re-assessment for reversible causes.
- Sleep apnea assessment if nocturnal bradycardia suspected.

### Outpatient (Discharge) Pathway—if Admission Not Feasible

**Strict Discharge Criteria:**
- No high-risk features (no persistent symptoms, stable ECG, reliable access to cardiology).
- Immediate setup of outpatient ambulatory ECG monitoring (see above).
- Explicit, written safety/return precautions.
- Education and support in place.

**Specialist Follow-Up:**
- **Cardiology/Electrophysiology appointment arranged to occur within 48–72 hours** (no later than 7 days) of discharge[13][14][15]. 
    - Expedite using rapid-access or urgent slots; remote/telehealth may be acceptable if immediate in-person capacity is unavailable.

**Criteria for Escalation:**
- Development of any persistent or repeated presyncope/syncope during monitoring.
- Device-detected actionable arrhythmias (see section above).
- Any new neurological or cardiac symptoms.

## 4. Discontinuation of AV-Nodal Blocking Agents and Addressing Reversible Causes

- **All AV-nodal blockers (e.g., beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, digoxin) should be immediately held/discontinued** in all patients with Mobitz I or symptomatic bradycardia/conduction abnormality until specialist review[3][4][5][12].
- Monitor for resolution or persistence of AV block; if the rhythm disturbance persists after discontinuation, urgent cardiology consultation and consideration for pacing are warranted.
- Reversible precipitants (electrolyte disturbance, acute ischemia, thyroid dysfunction, medication toxicity) should be systematically excluded and corrected before further intervention.

---

## 5. Safety and Return Precautions: Operational Clarity

### Patient Education and Precautions

- **Driving and Activities:** No driving or operation of heavy machinery until definitive diagnosis and specialist clearance, in accordance with road safety guidance and standard medico-legal recommendations[5][13][15].
- **Return to ED/Seek Emergency Care Immediately for:**
    - Any recurrence or worsening of syncope/presyncope.
    - Severe dizziness, pallor, confusion, inability to talk, or sustained bradycardia.
    - Chest pain, palpitations, or new-onset shortness of breath.
    - Any detected or reported device alert for concerning arrhythmias.
- **Written and verbal education** for both the patient and family regarding symptoms, action steps, and how to use monitoring device features/reporting tools.

### Family/Support Person Guidance

- Instruct to call emergency services for any witnessed loss of consciousness, collapse, or inability to rouse the patient.
- Provide contact info for monitoring provider and nearest ED.

## 7. Key Operational Recommendations Anchored in Guidelines

- **Mobitz I (Wenckebach) AV block should be explicitly distinguished from Mobitz II due to difference in clinical prognosis and urgency.**
- **Symptomatic Mobitz I requires urgent monitoring and expedited specialist evaluation due to risk of progression and recurrence.**
- **Minimum 14-day ambulatory monitoring (patch or MCOT) is guideline-concordant; MCOT preferred for real-time alerting in moderate-risk situations.**
- **Actionable event thresholds: pauses >3 seconds, high-degree AV block, symptomatic bradycardia, or device-patient symptom correlation.**
- **Admit if high-risk features present; arrange inpatient specialist consultation within 24 hours. If discharging, specialist follow-up should be within 48–72 hours (never more than 7 days).**
- **All AV-nodal blockers are to be held/discontinued until further evaluation.**
- **Provide operationally clear written and verbal instructions for safety, escalation, and device use.**
