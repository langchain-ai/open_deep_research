# Legal Classification of Enslaved People as Property in the 1730s British Empire: Statutes, Colonial Codes, and Economic Policy

## Introduction

The 1730s marked a crucial period in the legal and economic architecture of Atlantic slavery. The British Parliament and colonial assemblies in Jamaica, Barbados, and South Carolina constructed, defined, and reinforced the status of enslaved people as chattel property through detailed statutes and codes. These laws not only codified racialized dehumanization but also enabled the economic system of slavery, especially as it interfaced with imperial commercial regulation and trade laws such as the Molasses Act. The following report anchors its analysis in original statutory language, archival guidance, and recent legal-historical scholarship, providing an evidentiary foundation for historical novelists and researchers.

---

## I. Statutory Language: Defining Enslaved People as Chattel in Jamaica, Barbados, and South Carolina (1730s)

### Jamaica

**Key Statutes and Direct Language (1730s and Legacy Statutes)**

- Although Jamaica did not significantly revise its slave code in the 1730s, its legal regime was built upon foundational acts from the late 17th and early 18th centuries—amended and supplemented throughout the era. The enduring statutory definition, preserved in print in collections such as *An Abridgment of the Laws of Jamaica* (1756), identified enslaved persons as chattel:
  
  > “…for payment of debts and legacies all slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels are in the hands of executors or administrators”  
  [1]: (Jamaica, 1809/50 Geo. III; see also abridgment, 1756 [2]).

- The persistent language, largely unchanged in the 1730s, is found in the consolidated and abridged collections available from the *British Library* (call no. EAP148/3 & EAP148/4 digitized guides [3]), *Jamaica Archives and Records Department*, and *National Library of Jamaica*.

**How to Locate Statutes:**

- **Printed Collections:** 
  - *An abridgment of the laws of Jamaica* (1756), British Library, shelfmark: [BL 230.a.32]; also in databases such as ECCO (Eighteenth Century Collections Online) [2].
  - *The statutes and laws of the island of Jamaica* (1889, C. Ribton Curran, rev.), available at [British Library] and [Open Library][4].
- **Archives:** 
  - Jamaica Archives, *Principal Acts of the Jamaica Assembly, 1664–1800*, see jamcatalogue.org for modern bibliographic records [5].
  - National Library of Jamaica’s digital guide: [6]

### Barbados

**Barbados Slave Code of 1661 and Its Enduring Language**

- While no full verbatim statute from 1730–1739 is extant in the digital record, Barbados’ statutory framework from 1661 onward directly informed all subsequent legal practice. The 1661 code declared:

  > “…for the better ordering and governing of Negroes … we doe … protect them as we doe men’s other goods and chattels”  
  [7].

- Handler confirms that, by the late 17th and early 18th centuries, the formula “goods and chattels” consistently defined enslaved legal status in statute, probate, and court records [8].

**How to Locate Statutes:**

- **Printed and Manuscript Collections:** 
  - *The Laws of Barbados* (various 17th–18th c. compilations); notable in *Barbados Department of Archives* (Black Rock, St. Michael); for manuscript volumes: “Transcript Acts” [9].
  - Harvard Law Library Manuscript HLS MS 1047 covers documents ca. 1730–1778 [10].
- **Digital Collections:** Statutes.org.uk, Laws of Enslavement and Freedom [11].

### South Carolina

**Statutory Language: The 1740 Negro Act, Rooted in 1730s Practices**

- The first major explicit legislative definition is found in the 1740 act, but its content reflects and formalizes the governing assumptions of the 1730s:

  > “All negroes, Indians … mulatoes or mestizos … shall be and are hereby declared … absolute slaves, and shall follow the condition of the mother, and shall be deemed, held, taken, reputed, and adjudged in law to be chattels personal in the hands of their owners…”  
  [12].

- Earlier codes (1691, 1712) and practices established these terms, and court records from the 1730s invoked “chattel” and “property” regularly in probate, bills of sale, and dispute settlements [13].

**How to Locate Statutes:**

- **Archives:** 
  - *South Carolina Department of Archives and History* (SCDAH), Record Group RG 165000 (“Acts of the General Assembly 1691–”), 8301 Parklane Road, Columbia, SC [14].
- **Printed Collections & Digitized Volumes:** 
  - *Acts of the General Assembly of South-Carolina*, digitized in part at [Internet Archive][15].
  - *University of New Brunswick’s* Laws of Enslavement and Freedom [16].
  - Contact SCDAH or use their online records index for specific call numbers and microfilm references [14].

## II. Mechanisms: How Statutes Constructed Personhood and Property

**Key Mechanisms by Which Statutes Enforced Chattel Status:**

- **Asset for Debt Recovery:** Laws expressly permitted enslaved people to be seized and sold to satisfy creditor claims—equalizing their legal treatment with movable property and land (1732 Colonial Debts Act for imperial uniformity [17]).
- **Inheritance and Sale:** Statutes dictated that enslaved status followed the maternal line (partus sequitur ventrem) and that enslaved people could be transferred, bequeathed, or inventoried as “goods and chattels” in estate settlements [7][8][12].
- **Loss of Legal Personhood:** Slaves, by law, could not give testimony (except against other slaves), enter contracts, or initiate legal action. Summary procedures (e.g. no trial by jury for capital offenses in Jamaica and Carolina) rendered enslaved people legal nonentities outside property law [8][12][18].
- **Disciplinary Regimes:** Acts laid down explicit punishments, passes, patrol requirements, and prohibited literacy or independent economic activity for the enslaved; masters could inflict corporal punishment or even death, with minimal constraint [7][12].
- **Imperial Commercial Integration:** By standardizing the chattel principle, the British Parliament enabled slaves to serve as collateral and commodities underpinning Atlantic credit markets, directly linking plantation slavery to broader economic structures [17].

## III. British Metropolitan Statutes, the Molasses Act, and Legal-Imperial Doctrine

### The Molasses Act (1733)

- The Molasses Act (6 Geo. II, c. 13) regulated trade by taxing non-British (mostly French) West Indian molasses, rum, and sugar imported into North American colonies. While it did not contain direct chattel language, it was integral to the operation and profitability of the plantation system. It ensured the economic vitality of slave-based sugar economies and reinforced the triangular trade—sugar to Europe, slaves to the Americas, molasses/rum fueling colonial commerce [19][20].
- The act did not define enslaved people’s legal status but supported the broader regime by ensuring the flow of goods and profits dependent on slave labor.

### The Colonial Debts Act (1732)

- The Debt Recovery Act (5 Geo. II, c. 7) marked the most direct Parliamentary intervention:

  > “…houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates… shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts… as personal estates…”
  
  [17]

- By grouping “negroes” with other property, the act forced colonial courts to accept slaves as chattel assets, overruling protections and regional exceptions, and fortifying the commodification and liquidity of slave capital [17][21].

### Legal Opinions: The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729/1736)

- The Yorke-Talbot Opinion, issued by leading law officers, declared:

  > “We are of opinion, that a slave coming from the West-Indies to Great-Britain or Ireland, with or without his master, doth not become free, and that his master's property or right in him is not thereby determined or varied…”

  [22][23]

- Though not a statute, this legal doctrine powerfully reinforced the legitimacy of slaveholder property rights in both colonies and the metropole. It became a template for confidence in slave property rights across the empire, particularly in missionary and commercial circles [23].

### Distinctions Between Metropolitan and Colonial Law

- In the metropole (Britain itself), no statutory foundation for slavery existed, and common-law judges from time to time ruled in favor of liberty on English soil. But metropolitan Parliament and legal officers routinely “imported” colonial logic in matters relating to commerce and property abroad, as seen in the 1732 and 1733 acts, and in the Yorke-Talbot Opinion.
- Colonial assemblies, in contrast, enacted detailed slave codes defining property status, punishment, and racial hierarchy. These codes were grounded in the local needs of plantation societies and reflected, but went beyond, metropolitan legislative and legal doctrine [8][12][23].

## V. Interplay of Law and Economy: Statutory Effects on Trade and the Slave System

- **Triangular Trade Integration:** The chattel principle underpinned plantation credit and transfer mechanisms, while statutes like the Molasses Act protected the economic interests of British West Indian planters. Rum, sugar, and molasses, produced by enslaved labor, were foundational to the Atlantic economic system and fueled demand for forced labor [19][20].
- **Legal Architecture of Credit:** The Debt Recovery Act standardized enslaved people as assets, enabling planters to use them as collateral, thus fueling further borrowing and expansion. This fostered the growth of plantation economies and Atlantic commercial ties [17][21].
- **Parliamentary vs. Colonial Interests:** While Parliament acted to bolster economic transparency and creditor rights (for metropolitan merchants), colonial assemblies emphasized social control, punishment, and discipline via local statutes—yet both strands reinforced the commodification and dehumanization of enslaved populations [8][12][17].

## VI. Recent Scholarship (2015–2025) on Legal Dehumanization and Statutory Frameworks

- **Statutory Language and Dehumanization:** Lee B. Wilson (2020) analyzes how vice-admiralty and colonial courts explicitly used terms like "goods and merchandizes" to transform enslaved people’s legal identity into property, thus reinforcing dehumanization ([24]).
- **Comparative Atlantic Legal Studies:** Aaron Graham (2021) offers a comparative survey of Jamaican, Barbadian, and Irish penal and slave codes, demonstrating how statutes cemented subordination and commodification ([25]).
- **Economic Policy:** Scholarship (e.g., Claire Priest) illustrates how the Debt Recovery Act supported economic expansion and the plantation credit system by rendering enslaved people assets for debt settlement ([21]).
- **Modern Scholarship on Interpretation and Legacy:** Analyses in the *Journal of British Studies*, *Slavery & Abolition*, and *William and Mary Quarterly* discuss historical shifts in statutory purpose (Green, 2024), international legal legacies, and evolving interpretations of slavery and commerce ([26][27]).
- **Direct Access:** For in-depth analysis, see:
  - Lee B. Wilson, “‘Negroes Goods and Merchandizes’: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700–1763.” [24]
  - Aaron Graham, “Slave codes and penal laws in eighteenth-century Jamaica and Ireland…” [25]
  - Dyble, Jake, “General Average, Human Jettison, and the Status of Slaves in Early Modern Europe.” [28]
  - Edward Rugemer, *Slave Law and the Politics of Resistance in the Early Atlantic World* [29]
  - Reiss & Reagan, “Civilizing the Colonial Subject…” [30]

---

## Conclusion

The statutory regimes of the 1730s British Empire—across Parliament and in the colonies—explicitly, and repeatedly, defined enslaved people as chattel or property, centralizing this principle as the legal and economic foundation of Atlantic slavery. Parliamentary statutes such as the 1732 Debt Recovery Act and doctrines like the Yorke-Talbot Opinion explicitly reinforced colonial codes in Jamaica, Barbados, and South Carolina. These legal frameworks both reflected and structured the dehumanization of enslaved people and enabled the commercial and social order of the eighteenth-century British Atlantic. The mechanisms of commodification, legal non-personhood, and economic integration found in the statutes continued to shape the law, economy, and ideology of slavery until its abolition, and remain the subject of vital historical and legal scholarship.
