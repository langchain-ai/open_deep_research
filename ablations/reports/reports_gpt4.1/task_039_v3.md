# Comprehensive, Technically Precise Analysis of Independent Director Requirements Under NASDAQ Listing Standards (April 2026)

## 1. Overview

NASDAQ’s listing standards mandate rigorous and explicit requirements for the definition, eligibility, disqualification, and exceptions pertaining to independent directors. These rules are codified primarily in NASDAQ Rule 5605 and its interpretive guidance, with further overlay from SEC rules, most notably Rule 10A-3 relating to audit committees. Clarity, precision, and proper attribution to governing rules are crucial for compliance. This report delivers a granular analysis, explicitly distinguishing NASDAQ rules from NYSE where relevant, and meticulously referencing each cited requirement.

---

## 2. Definition of Independent Director Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 2.1 General Definition

Per NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2):

> “Independent director means a person other than an Executive Officer or employee of the company or any other individual having a relationship which, in the opinion of the company’s board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director. Ownership of company stock, by itself, does not preclude a finding of independence.”  
> [NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)][1]

Every director must first clear this subjective ("in the opinion of the board") test. However, bright-line disqualifications (detailed below) override board discretion.

---

## 3. Bright-Line Independence Disqualifiers Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 3.1 Enumerated Disqualifying Relationships

A director is **not independent** if any of the following apply during the **three years** preceding the determination ([Rule 5605(a)(2)][1], [3]):

1. **Employment**  
   The director is, or has been within the last three years, an employee of the company, its parent, or a subsidiary.

2. **Family Member Employment as Executive**  
   An immediate family member (“Family Member”) is, or was within the past three years, employed as an executive officer by the company, its parent, or a subsidiary.

3. **Compensation Threshold**  
   The director, or a Family Member, has received (other than for board/committee service or certain fixed retirement benefits) from the company, its parent, or a subsidiary, **more than $120,000** in direct compensation during any twelve-month period within the last three years.

4. **Interlocking Directorate (Compensation Committee Interlock)**  
   The director is, or has a Family Member who is, a partner in, or a controlling shareholder or executive officer of, any organization to which the company paid, or from which it received, payments for property or services in the most recent three fiscal years that exceed the greater of $200,000 or 5% of the recipient’s consolidated gross revenues, *other than* charitable contributions.

5. **Outside Auditor Affiliation**  
   The director or a Family Member is or was within the last three years a partner or employee of the company’s outside auditor and personally worked on the issuer’s audit during that time.

6. **Special Auditor Services Prohibition**  
   The director or a Family Member accepted directly or indirectly any consulting, advisory, or compensatory fee (other than standard fees for board or committee service or certain retirement payments) from the issuer or any subsidiary, as further extended for audit committee members under SEC Rule 10A-3 (see Section 6).

### 3.2 Family Member Definition

Under Rule 5605(a)(2):

- “Family Member” includes spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers- and fathers-in-law, sons- and daughters-in-law, brothers- and sisters-in-law, and anyone (other than domestic employees) sharing the director’s home.
- Stepchildren are only included if they share the director’s home.

### 3.3 Exclusions From $120,000 Threshold

The following are exempt from the $120,000 compensation calculation ([Rule 5605(a)(2)][1]):

- Director or committee fees
- Fixed retirement benefits from prior service (provided not contingent on continued service)
- Payments to a Family Member (other than an executive officer) for non-executive employment
- Reimbursements and de minimis perquisites generally available to all

### 3.4 Lookback Period

All the above disqualifiers are subject to a **three-year lookback**, calculated from the date the board makes its independence determination.

## 4. Eligibility and Board Process

### 4.1 Eligibility

To qualify, a director must:

- Satisfy Rule 5605(a)(2) bright-line tests
- Not have any other material relationship (factual circumstances are considered), in the *board’s opinion*, that would impair independence

Eligibility is assessed **at least annually** and upon any event that could affect independence status. Companies must document and disclose these determinations in proxy statements as required by SEC Regulation S-K Item 407(a) ([1][3][4]).

## 5. Exceptions, Exemptions, and Transitional Provisions

### 5.1 Controlled Company Exemption

#### 5.1.1 Definition

A “controlled company” is any company where more than 50% of the voting power is held by an individual, group, or another company ([Rule 5615(c)][4]; [7][9]).

#### 5.1.2 Exemption Scope

Controlled companies are **exempt from** ([Rule 5615(c)][4]):

- The requirement for a majority independent board (Rule 5605(b))
- The requirements of fully independent compensation and nominating committees (Rules 5605(d), 5605(e))

**However, controlled companies must:**

- Maintain an independent audit committee that meets both NASDAQ and SEC Rule 10A-3 standards ([Rule 5605(c)][3])
- Disclose in their annual proxy statement (or, if no proxy, in Form 10-K or 20-F):  
   - That they are a controlled company  
   - Which corporate governance requirements they do not comply with ([Rule 5615(c)][4]; [7][8])

#### 5.1.3 Phase-In and Transition Timing

When a company **ceases to qualify as a controlled company (i.e., loses majority voting control):**

- **Transition Period:**  
  - The company must comply with the non-exempt independence requirements **within six months after losing controlled status** for board/committee independence ([Rule 5615(b)(3)][4]; [6][10]).
  - Immediate compliance is **required for audit committee independence**; there is no phase-in or grace period for SEC Rule 10A-3 compliance ([14][19]).

- **During Transition:**  
  - If a director or committee member loses independence for reasons outside company control, or a vacancy arises, the company has until the earlier of the next annual meeting or one year to cure, unless this period extends beyond otherwise applicable transition periods ([11][12][13]).
  - No stacking: Companies using a phase-in period are not entitled to a cure period at the end of it unless they fall out of compliance while otherwise fully compliant ([10][11][13]).

- **Immediate Notice Requirement:**  
  - The company must notify NASDAQ promptly upon learning it is out of compliance ([3][4][6]).

### 5.2 Additional Transitional and Carve-Out Provisions

#### 5.2.1 IPOs, Spin-offs, and New Listings

Newly listed companies (including IPOs, spin-offs, and carve-outs) may phase in board and committee independence as follows ([Rule 5615(b)(1)][4]; [6][10]):

- **Audit Committee:**  
  - At least one member independent at listing  
  - Majority independent within 90 days  
  - All independent within one year
- **Other Committees and Board:**  
  - Majority independence within one year

#### 5.2.2 Exceptional and Limited Circumstances Exception

- Under Rule 5605(c)(2)(B) (audit and compensation committees):  
  - **One director** who does not meet the definition of “independent director” may serve on these committees for up to **two years** under “exceptional and limited circumstances” (e.g., unique expertise).
  - **Conditions:**
    - The committee must have at least three members.
    - The board must determine (and disclose) that the director’s service is in the best interests of the company and its shareholders.
    - The director may not chair the committee.
    - This exception **does not apply to affiliated persons or for audit committee financial expert exceptions** under SEC rules ([Rule 5605(c)(2)(B)][3], [8]).

- **Disclosure:** Use of this exception must be explained in the next proxy or SEC filing ([3][8]).

#### 5.2.3 Legacy/Contractual Rights Carve-Outs

- Where a company is contractually obligated (via shareholder agreements, etc.) to appoint certain directors or committee members, a transition period applies matching:
  - The life of the contractual right or any applicable phase-in period—**whichever is shorter**.
  - Upon expiry, **full compliance is required** ([11][13]).

#### 5.2.4 Foreign Private Issuer Exemption

- Foreign private issuers may follow **home-country governance practices** for board and committee independence (except for audit committee requirements under SEC Rule 10A-3) ([Rule 5615(a)(3)][4]).
- The issuer must:
  - Disclose the alternative practice in its annual Form 20-F and on its website.
  - **Audit committee independence per SEC Rule 10A-3 must always be met** ([14][19]).

#### 5.2.5 Investment Companies and BDCs

- Open-end and most closed-end funds: **Exempt** from NASDAQ committee independence rules, but must comply with SEC “interested person” standards ([9][14]).
- BDCs (Business Development Companies): **Subject to all NASDAQ governance rules** ([9]).
- All investment companies must comply with audit committee independence requirements under SEC Rule 10A-3 ([14][19]).

## 6. Audit Committee Composition: Enhanced Independence Standards Under SEC Rule 10A-3 as Incorporated in NASDAQ

### 6.1 SEC Rule 10A-3 Core Requirements

Adopted directly into NASDAQ Rule 5605(c), SEC Rule 10A-3 ([14][19]) requires:

- **Every audit committee member must be independent:**
  - No director may accept “any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee” from the company or its subsidiaries, except for board/committee fees [14][19].
  - No committee member may be an “affiliated person” of the company or subsidiary—this encompasses executive officers, significant shareholders, or those with material relationship influence [14][19].
- **Three-year lookback for disqualifying relationships**.
- **Additional requirements:**
  - Audit committee is solely responsible for the appointment, compensation, oversight, and retention of the external auditor.
  - Audit committee must establish procedures for confidential complaints.
  - At least one member must be financially sophisticated (i.e., “financial expert”) per NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2)(A).

### 6.2 Prohibitions and Exclusions

- **Prohibited:**  
  - Direct or indirect receipt of consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company (except board/committee fees) ([14][19]).
  - Affiliation with the company or subsidiaries.
- **Not Prohibited:**  
  - Payments for board or committee service.
  - Fixed retirement benefits for prior service, non-contingent on continued service.

### 6.3 Exceptions and Legacy Status

- **No phase-in flexibility for audit committees** (unlike other independence requirements): full audit committee independence **must be present at all times**, except as permitted during the initial public listing transition.
- **Small business or controlled company exceptions:** There are no audit committee independence exceptions for controlled companies, FPIs (other than home-country exemptions for structure/composition, not independence), or investment companies subject to Section 10A(m) of the Exchange Act ([14][19]).

## 7. Distinction Between NASDAQ and NYSE Independence Rules

- The NASDAQ independence standard is codified in **NASDAQ Rule 5605**.  
- NYSE’s independence standards are set forth in **NYSE Listed Company Manual Section 303A** (not detailed here, but key differences include: the NYSE uses a two-tiered approach with different financial thresholds and a slightly broader set of relationships, as well as distinction in subcommittee structures and transition rules) ([17][18]).
- **Do not assume harmonization:** Each exchange’s rules must be followed precisely as written. Where SEC rules (such as Rule 10A-3) mandate uniformity, both exchanges comply. Otherwise, only those NASDAQ provisions cited here apply.

## 9. Process, Documentation, and Disclosure

- Indicate annual independence determinations in board minutes, committee records, and the proxy or 10-K/20-F ([3][4]).
- Disclose use of any exemptions, phase-ins, or exceptional circumstances in the company’s proxy or required SEC filings ([3][4]).
- Notify NASDAQ immediately upon any independence deficiency ([3][4]).
