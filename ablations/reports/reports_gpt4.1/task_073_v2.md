# Systematic Comparison of Autism Diagnosis Criteria in DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (2019): Emphasis on Sensory Processing Differences, Core Social-Communication Deficits, Severity, and Policy

## Introduction

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5, 2013) and the International Classification of Diseases, Eleventh Revision (ICD-11, 2019) are the definitive diagnostic frameworks for autism spectrum disorder (ASD) worldwide. As autism prevalence, service eligibility, and intervention strategies often hinge on these criteria—particularly regarding sensory processing, social-communication, and severity—it is crucial to systematically clarify how these manuals differ, how they are implemented, and what is currently supported by evidence and policy in the US, UK, and globally.

## 1. Side-by-Side Comparison: DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019) Autism Diagnostic Criteria

The following table summarizes the key contrasts:

| **Diagnostic Aspect**           | **DSM-5 (2013)** | **ICD-11 (2019)** |
|---------------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| **Core social-communication criteria** | Requires all 3 specific deficits: social-emotional reciprocity; nonverbal communication; relationships. Must meet all subcriteria [1][2][3]. | Requires persistent deficits in initiating and sustaining reciprocal social communication/interaction; less rigid in sub-feature count, greater clinical flexibility [4][5][6]. |
| **Sensory processing (definition, operationalization, and weighting)** | Sensory processing differences appear as item B4 of restricted/repetitive behaviors (RRBs): “Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input, or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment.” Two RRBs (of four, including B4) are required, so sensory differences are *optional* but formally recognized. Precise language, standardized examples [1][2][3]. | Explicitly included within restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behavior (“unusual sensory sensitivities or interests”; “hypersensitivity/hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli”). At least one RRB type (including sensory features) is required, with sensory examples foregrounded as possible manifestations. Sensory differences are more directly highlighted, but not required [4][5][6]. |
| **Severity/Support specification** | Three severity levels (“requiring support,” “substantial support,” or “very substantial support”) specified separately for social-communication and RRB domains. Not tied to specific codes, but must select one level per domain [1][2][7]. | No direct severity levels. Instead, uses sub-codes reflecting the presence/absence of intellectual developmental disorder (IDD) and functional language impairment. Emphasizes functional description and support needs rather than standardized tiering [6][8][9]. |
| **Functional impairment requirement** | Deficits “cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning” [1][2][3]. | “Symptoms result in significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas of functioning” [5][6][10]. |
| **Age/onset requirements** | Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period; may be “masked” or first noted when social demands exceed capacities (e.g., adolescence/adulthood) [1][2][3]. | Onset in the developmental period, typically early childhood, but criteria clarify delayed or masked presentation due to compensation is recognized [5][6]. |
| **Subtyping/coding structure** | Unifies all subtypes (“autistic disorder,” “Asperger’s,” “PDD-NOS”) under “ASD” [1][2][3]. Severity and clinical specifiers added [7]. | ASD as a single diagnosis (6A02), with sub-codes specifying IDD and/or language impairment status (e.g., 6A02.0–6A02.3) [6][8][9]. |

## 2. Structural and Operational Differences: Detailed Contrast

### Sensory Processing Differences

- **DSM-5**:
  - Sensory reactivity (hyper/hypo, unusual interests) is listed as one of four possible RRBs (item B4). At least two RRB types are required, so sensory is weighted *as one of four*—not mandatory [1][2][3].
  - Language is explicit and includes concrete examples (“apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling/touching of objects, visual fascination with lights/movement”) [1][2][3].
  - Sensory processing can feature prominently, but diagnosis is possible without prominent sensory symptoms [1][2].
- **ICD-11**:
  - Sensory features (“unusual sensory sensitivities or interests,” hyper- or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli) are foregrounded as exemplars within the required domain of restricted, repetitive, or inflexible behaviors; at least one such manifestation (sensory or otherwise) is needed [6][11].
  - Sensory processing differences are thus explicitly recognized and may be sufficient, in context, to meet the RRB domain requirement [5][6].
  - ICD-11’s flexible criteria intentionally capture profiles with sensory-dominant manifestations, but do not require sensory features in all diagnosed cases [4][6].
  - Both systems represent a significant evolution from earlier versions, where sensory features were not included.

### Core Social-Communication Features

- **DSM-5**:
  - Requires all three social-communication sub-criteria, with detailed operationalization (e.g., atypical social-emotional reciprocity, deficits in nonverbal cues, trouble with relationships) [1][2][3].
  - Subcomponent failure is not optional: every ASD case must show persistent deficits in *all* three areas [1][2][3].
  - Designed to improve specificity and reliability by standardizing required aspects [1][7].
- **ICD-11**:
  - Requires persistent, developmentally inappropriate deficits in initiating/sustaining reciprocal social communication and interaction, but does not enumerate or require a fixed number of sub-features [5][6].
  - Focus is on clinical judgment of impairment and functional impact, allowing broader diagnostic flexibility (e.g., late-manifesting or camouflaged traits) [4][6].

### Severity and Coding

- **DSM-5**:
  - Severity levels (1–3) rated for each major domain, based on degree of support required. Intended for clinical description, not to determine access to services. Does not map to numeric or billing codes [7][8].
  - Application is highly subjective and inconsistent in clinical practice; no anchoring to standardized tools or thresholds [8].
- **ICD-11**:
  - No explicit "severity" levels. Instead, utilizes sub-coding to indicate the presence/absence of:
    - Intellectual developmental disorder (IDD)
    - Functional language impairment
  - Codes 6A02.0–6A02.3 cover the main specifier combinations [6][8][9].
  - Clinical guidance suggests describing support needs and functional impact individually, aligning with many service delivery/policy models [10][11].

### Functional Impairment and Age/Onset

- Both manuals require *clinically significant* functional impairment for ASD diagnosis [1][6].
- Both recognize that symptoms may be masked, especially in high-functioning individuals or those using compensatory strategies, and may only become evident when demands increase later in life (e.g., adolescence, workplace) [3][6].
- Both specify that diagnosis should be based on a developmental history confirming symptom onset in childhood, even if not recognized or first manifest until later [2][4][6].

## 3. Evidence Synthesis: Direct Comparative Studies and Expert Consensus

### Current State of Comparative Empirical Evidence

- **No direct, head-to-head studies** comparing the diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, or clinical utility of DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 for ASD exist as of April 2026. This absence is acknowledged repeatedly in the peer-reviewed literature [12][13][14].
- Most empirical studies compare **DSM-5 to DSM-IV-TR or ICD-10**, with findings that:
  - DSM-5 is more specific but less sensitive, potentially excluding subtle, high-functioning, or atypical cases [12][15].
  - The introduction of severity levels and Social Communication Disorder in DSM-5 led to diagnostic shifts [12][16].
  - There is only expert consensus and projection about the consequences of ICD-11’s broader, more flexible criteria: 
    - **Expert projections** (not empirically confirmed) indicate risk of greater diagnostic heterogeneity and possible over-diagnosis under ICD-11, due to the vast number of symptom combinations and less stringent sub-feature thresholds [13][14].
    - ICD-11 may better capture underdiagnosed or camouflaged populations (e.g., females, adults) but may also reduce diagnostic specificity [13][14].
- **Systematic reviews** echo the need for rigorous, cross-national DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 validation studies and highlight the ongoing reliance on clinical consensus rather than direct comparative outcomes [13][14].

### Key Distinction

All claims regarding the clinical impact of DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 (e.g., inclusivity, over-/under-diagnosis) **should be understood as expert hypotheses or projections**, not as verified empirical outcomes. There remains an urgent research gap for direct, prospective, cross-system studies [13][14].

## 5. Policy and Guideline Positions: Clinical Authority, Advocacy, and Coding

### Organizational Guidance

#### World Health Organization (WHO)

- **ICD-11 is the official international standard**, required for use in all WHO member states. Defines ASD with sensory processing differences as a core feature, and sub-coding for support needs [17][18].
- Emphasizes functional impact and individualized, context-sensitive assessment [6][17].

#### American Psychiatric Association (APA)

- Continues exclusive endorsement of DSM-5-TR for clinical use in the US. Sensory symptoms included as one of the core RRBs; no special weighting for sensory features beyond others [2][19].
- No current policy on ICD-11 use for ASD within the US [2][19].

#### American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

- Recommends recognition of sensory processing differences as critical for ASD diagnosis, aligns with both DSM-5 and ICD-11, but most US practice defaults to DSM-5 [20][21].
- Multidisciplinary evaluation and thorough documentation of sensory features encouraged [20].

#### NICE (UK)

- Endorses both DSM-5 and ICD-11 as valid frameworks for ASD diagnosis in clinical and educational practice [22][23].
- Sensory processing is recognized as important and should be assessed, but is not weighted above social-communication or RRBs for diagnosis [23].
- Shifts toward neurodiversity-affirming terminology in guidance [22].

### Advocacy Organizations

#### Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

- Pushed for the inclusion of sensory differences, masking, and a unified autism diagnosis in DSM-5. Critiques both manuals for possible underdiagnosis among females, minorities, and those with primarily sensory difficulties [24][25].
- Opposes using severity levels as care gatekeeping, and supports aligning criteria closely with lived experience [25].

#### National Autistic Society (NAS, UK)

- Supports the use of either DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria. Emphasizes need for formal recognition and systematic assessment of sensory processing, noting risks if not formally identified [26].
- Advocates for understanding the impact of sensory needs on support and access, calls for further research and improved pathways [26].

#### Autism Speaks

- Publicizes DSM-5 and ICD-11 as foundations for eligibility and assessment. Highlights sensory feature inclusion as important for identifying support needs and facilitating early diagnosis [27].
- Does not take an official stance on which manual is more neurodiversity-affirming, but states criteria should open service access [27].

### Coding and Reimbursement: US and UK

#### United States

- **Clinical diagnosis:** DSM-5/DSM-5-TR used by clinicians; **billing:** exclusively relies on ICD-10-CM code F84.0 for ASD [28][29][30].
- **ICD-11 is not yet implemented for billing or service eligibility**; transition for federal and private payers is anticipated late 2020s [28][31].
- Mapping from DSM-5 ASD criteria to ICD-10-CM for claims. When ICD-11 is adopted it will use 6A02 (with specifiers), but full implementation will require significant system transition and dual coding for a period [31][32].
- **No current change** in reimbursement or service eligibility regarding sensory processing until ICD-11 is adopted at the payment level.

#### United Kingdom

- National Health Service (NHS) and most regional authorities accept both DSM-5 and ICD-11 for clinical diagnosis. ICD-11 is being rolled out nationally; billing and official coding increasingly reference ICD-11 [17][32][33].
- Service eligibility and accommodations are legally “needs-based,” relying on functional impairment as documented by multidisciplinary assessment, not the code alone. Sensory symptoms can contribute to assessed needs, but do not guarantee eligibility unless they cause significant impairment [33].

## 6. Summary of Risks and Unresolved Gaps

- **Potential for over- or under-diagnosis:** ICD-11’s flexibility could, according to experts, lead to more inclusive but less precise (more heterogeneous) groupings, increasing risk of false positives [13][14]. DSM-5’s stricter checklists may miss atypical or masked cases, especially in females, minorities, or those with prominent sensory symptoms [13][15].
- **Both systems require functional impairment** for diagnosis, but implementation of severity/support specifiers remains inconsistent [8][12].
- **No direct comparative data** exists as to which system better identifies or serves individuals with sensory-predominant presentations or less typical ASD phenotypes. Observed differences are expert opinions or policy-level assessments, not empirical facts [12][13][14].
- **Coding and reimbursement** in the US is ICD-10-CM based; ICD-11 will increase descriptive precision and compatibility when adopted, but does not itself guarantee improved clinical outcomes or access [28][31][32].

## Conclusion

DSM-5 and ICD-11 offer harmonized but structurally distinct frameworks for ASD diagnosis. Both recognize sensory processing differences as core, but operationalize, weight, and specify diagnostic thresholds differently. DSM-5 is more rigid, requiring fixed social-communication deficits and offers subjective severity tiers; ICD-11 is broader, more flexible, emphasizes functional descriptors, and may better accommodate diverse or camouflaged presentations—though this may come at the cost of diagnostic precision. Policy, advocacy, and actual service access are evolving toward individualized, neurodiversity-affirming implementation, but empirical research directly contrasting DSM-5 and ICD-11 performance remains a pressing gap. In both the US and UK, practical implications for diagnosis, coding, and reimbursement reflect national context and the pace of ICD-11 adoption.
