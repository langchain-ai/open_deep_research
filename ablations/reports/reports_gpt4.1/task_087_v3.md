# Comparative Analysis of Women’s Labor Force Participation Rates (LFPR) 1970–2025: Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Iceland, and India

## Introduction

This report offers a rigorous, decade-by-decade, and comparative analysis of women’s labor force participation rates (LFPR) in Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Iceland, and India from 1970 to 2025. It systematically documents key statistics (clearly attributing sources—distinguishing between national, ILO/WDI modeled, and other datasets), the significance of varying definitions, detailed policy interventions with legislative specifics, as well as granular breakdowns (where available) by education, sector, employment type, and age. Methodological challenges and policy implications of international differences are critically addressed, and all data or reporting gaps are explicitly acknowledged.

---

## Saudi Arabia

### Decadal LFPR Trends and Data Sources

- **1970s–1980s:** No reliable continuous LFPR data. Qualitative sources agree that female LFPR was under 10%, driven mainly by restrictive social, legal, and religious norms. Most working women were teachers or healthcare workers, with formal employment rare for women[1][2].
- **1990s:** ILO/World Bank modeled LFPR estimates for women (15+) start at 14.5% in 1992[3]. Guardianship laws and sectoral prohibitions still limited women’s access. Informal and unpaid work largely undercounted, and sector/education splits not published[4].
- **2000s:** Gradual policy changes but limited legal reform. LFPR rises to ~17–19% by 2010 (modeled, ages 15+)[3][5].
- **2010s:** Substantial acceleration following Vision 2030 (2016). LFPR rises from 17% (2016) to 23% (2019) and 32% (2020)[6][7].
- **2020s:** Rapid transformation continues; GASTAT national survey places LFPR for Saudi women at 36.2% in Q3 2024, with ILO/WDI modeled estimates at 34.41%[8][9]. This exceeds Vision 2030’s 30% target years before deadline. Female share of total labor force rises above 18%[4].

**Definitions and Sources:**  
Main series use ages 15+. Some comparisons with ages 15–64 or 16–59. International series (ILO/WDI) are modeled for comparability and smooth over occasional survey gaps; GASTAT is the national survey series[3][10].

### Major Policy Interventions (with Legislative Detail)

- **2001:** Labor Law revision (Article 3) asserts gender-neutral right to work, but with continued gender-based restrictions in practice[4].
- **2012:** Royal Decree: Women admitted to Consultative Council (Shura)[11].
- **2016 (Cabinet Resolution No. 308):** Launch of Vision 2030, commits to raising LFPR for women from 17% to 30% with 26 dedicated empowerment projects—includes quotas, training, entrepreneurship, and anti-discrimination drives[6][7][12].
- **2017–2018:** Legal reforms:
  - **Driving ban lifted (2018).**
  - **Anti-harassment law passed (2018).**
  - **Guardianship reforms (2019)**—eliminated requirement for male guardian’s consent for business, travel, and work. ID and passport law amendments implemented[13].
  - Gender-mixed workplaces opened, retail/industry sectors liberalized for women[13].
- **2021:** Minimum wage raised from SAR 3,000 to SAR 4,000; female employment eligibility expanded in private sector[14].
- **2023–2025:** Extended maternity leave (12 weeks full pay), flexible/remote work, expansion of childcare/transport subsidies, active job matching, and public-private gender quotas[12][15].

**Eligibility and Implementation:**  
Most policies target Saudi nationals, generally women aged 15–59 or 15+, with legal work status. Major benefits (maternity, wage subsidies, transport) require formal sector employment; informal sector outreach is limited[14][15].

### Granular Breakdown: Education, Sector, Employment Type, Age

- **Education:**  
  University attainment by Saudi women (25+) rose from 15.2% in 2000 to 35.3% in 2024[16]. Paradoxically, unemployment is highest among university-educated women—public sector oversaturated and private demand lagging, despite anti-discrimination rules[17].
- **Sector:**  
  97.6% of Saudi employed women are in services (2022); industry (2.1%) and agriculture (<0.5%) remain negligible. Traditional strongholds are healthcare and education, but presence in ICT, retail, construction and finance is rising[15][18]. Women now hold 39% of private sector management roles; 28% of tech jobs[15].
- **Employment Type:**  
  Formal sector and full-time work dominate, with a rising share in part-time/gig/freelance (961,189 freelance certificates to women by 2021), but no consistent decade-long series[19]. Formal public sector employment is still disproportionate.
- **Age Band:**  
  Participation jumps at ages 20–34, peaks in this group, and tapers above 40. LFPR among young women (15–24) is rising fastest post-reforms, as education-to-work transitions become more possible[16].

### Methodological Caveats, Data Gaps, and Definition Effects

- Pre-1990 data are largely missing; all early numbers are ILO/WDI projections, not direct survey tabulations.
- Informal/unpaid work is undercounted, especially pre-2016[4][10].
- National and international series may diverge (up to 2–3 percentage points), depending on age bands or treatment of mixed-status employment[10].
- No consistently published detailed breakdowns of sector×education, region, or part/full-time for pre-2016 years[15][19].

## South Korea

### Decadal LFPR Trends and Data Sources

- **1970:** 39.3% (KOSIS, ages 15+)[20].
- **1980:** 42.5%.
- **1990:** 47%.
- **2000:** 48.6%.
- **2010:** 50.2%.
- **2024:** 56.8% (World Bank national estimate, ages 15+); 64% (ILO modeled, 15–64)[21][22].
- **2025 projection:** 64% for ages 15–64 (ILO modeled)[23].
- Gender gap: Male LFPR is 72.5% (2024)[22].

**Definitions and Sources:**  
Korean national data (KOSIS) typically use ages 15+, but OECD and ILO/World Bank modeled estimates use 15–64. National numbers and modeled data differ by up to 2 points (recent years); pre-1990s, detailed disaggregation is only available in Korean-language publications and select academic studies[20][21][22][23].

### Major Policy Interventions (with Legislative Detail)

- **1987:** Equal Employment Opportunity Act bans sex discrimination in work, launches enforcement for hiring, promotion, and wages[24].
- **2001:** Major amendment—adds work-family balance measures; triggers first coordinated parental leave expansion.
- **2018/2021:** Amendments lower eligibility thresholds (by company size), expand protection for non-regular and part-time workers, strengthen anti-harassment law, and promote flexible work[25].
- **2020:** Capital Market Act (articles 165-20, 165-21) requires women on corporate boards; targeted entrepreneurship schemes[25].
- **2025:** New labor law reforms: paternity leave doubled (to 20 days), IVF/infertility support, longer maternity and childcare leaves (to 18 months). Employer incentives and expanded legal aid implemented[26][27].
- **Parental leave:** Parents eligible after 1 year’s insured employment; wage replacement at 80% (subject to caps/exemptions), non-transferable family policies introduced for gender balance[28].

### Granular Breakdown: Education, Sector, Employment Type, Age

- **Education:**  
  Dramatic expansion in tertiary education for women—from 14.5% of school-leaver cohorts in 1985 to 76% (2024)[29]. Labor force participation among women graduates in their 20s now nears that of men, but career interruptions persist (childbearing).
- **Sector:**  
  Women are 70%+ of workers in services (retail, health, social care, education). STEM fields remain <20% female; management/board roles 5.6–22%, depending on metric[29][30].
- **Employment Type:**  
  Non-regular (temporary/part-time) work comprises 34–37% of female jobs (2020s). Full-time salary jobs common for early career, but “M-curve” effect means a substantial drop-off in the 30s partially offset by parental leave reforms[30].
- **Age Band (“M-curve”):**  
  Participation is high in 20s, dips sharply in the 30s (due to childbearing/childcare), and recovers by 40, with the “M” flattening somewhat post-2020 due to purposeful policy and cultural change[29][31].

### Methodological Caveats, Data Gaps, and Definition Effects

- Education, sector, and employment-type cross-tabs are only robust from early 2000s onward[29].
- Part-time, non-regular, and informality rates are sometimes redefined; caution needed for direct time-series comparison of these splits[30].
- ILO/OECD/KOSIS differences for identical years arise mainly due to age band (15+ vs 15–64) and inclusion/exclusion of military or micro-categories; data gaps for pre-1980s on fine breakdowns[21][22][23].

## Iceland

### Decadal LFPR Trends and Data Sources

- **1970–1975:** No official figure but multiple accounts indicate high “economic activity” despite underreporting (notably, 1975 “women’s day off” protest, where 90% withdrew all labor)[32][33]. No robust sectoral/education breakdown for this era.
- **1991:** 66.17% (World Bank/CEIC national estimate, ages 15+)[34].
- **2000:** 69% (ILO/World Bank, modeled)[35].
- **2010:** 72%.
- **2022/2023:** 76.7% (Statistics Iceland, ages 16–74)[36].
- **2024:** 71.33% (ILO/World Bank, ages 15+)[37].
- **2025:** 84% (ILO modeled, 15–64); 78.1% (Statistics Iceland, ages 16–74)[38][36].

**Definitions and Sources:**  
National definitions generally use 16–74 (Statistics Iceland), while ILO/OECD use 15+, 15–64, or 15–74. Differences of 1–2 points between datasets are common and should be noted for comparability[36][37][38].

### Major Policy Interventions (with Legislative Detail)

- **1975:** Women’s strike triggers Gender Equality Act (1976), banning wage discrimination[33].
- **1980–2000s:** Parental leave laws expanded successively—now at 12 months (6 months per parent, non-transferable), paid at 80% wage (max ISK 600,000/month), near-universal take-up[39][40][41].
- **2000:** Three months’ parental leave per parent; 2019 reform expanded to six. Rapid expansion of subsidized childcare for ages 1+; closing participation gap post-birth[39].
- **2013:** 40% board quotas mandatory in large companies[42].
- **2017:** Equal pay certification law requires all companies with 25+ employees to prove wage parity or face fines[43].
- **2015–2025:** Universal, state-supported parental benefits, pilot workweek reductions (to 36 hours), further expanded in collective agreements[41][44].
- **Benefits and Coverage:** Policies are universal for residents, with gender-neutral parental leave and enforceable legal mandates; high compliance due to robust labor market structure[40][43].

### Granular Breakdown: Education, Sector, Employment Type, Age

- **Education:**  
  Female tertiary attainment for ages 25–64 rose from 30.5% (2005) to 49% (2025); 70%+ of new PhDs female (2019)[45].
- **Sector:**  
  Services/health/education heavily female (e.g., 81.8% of health jobs, 76.8% of education jobs), men dominate construction and trades[46].
- **Employment Type:**  
  Full-time: 74.1% of women (2025); part-time: 25.9% (mostly for care/family); average weekly hours 32.7 (women), 38.4 (men)[47].
- **Age Band:**  
  Prime age (25–54) female LFPR: 88.19% (Q2 2025); youth (15–24): 81.8%; 55–74: lower but rising[48][49].
- **Informality:**  
  Essentially negligible in Iceland; no relevant official data[47].

### Methodological Caveats, Data Gaps, and Definition Effects

- Pre-1990: No granular data by sector or education; only aggregate participation rates.  
- Differences of up to 2 percentage points between age bands and data sources; recent surveys are harmonized with ILO/OECD practices[36][38].
- Non-binary participants included after 2024, making newest data non-strictly comparable to historical[50].

## India

### Decadal LFPR Trends and Data Sources

- **1970s:** 33% (1972), peaking at 33.4% (1977). Based on Census/National Sample Survey (NSS) “usual principal and subsidiary status” definitions, but underreporting likely[51][52].
- **1980s–1990s:** Steady decline; World Bank/ILO modeled estimates: 34% (1990) dropping to ~30% by 2000[53][54].
- **2000s:** NSSO 61st round (2004–05) records 34%, but falls sharply after[55].
- **2010s:** All-India FLFPR reaches historic low: ~17.5% (NSS/ILO/World Bank, 2017)[54].
- **2020s:** Rising again: PLFS records rural FLFPR of 41.5% (2023), urban at 25.4%; national average (ages 15+) at 32.8% (2024, modeled ILO; 41.7% as per government for 2023–24)[56][57].

**Definitions and Sources:**  
ILO/World Bank: ages 15+. Indian PLFS/NSSO: “Usual principal and subsidiary status,” “current weekly status.” Underreporting of informal, family, and agricultural work (especially for unpaid helpers). Note sizable differences between modeled and survey-based estimates[57][58].

### Major Policy Interventions (with Legislative Detail)

- **1976:** Equal Remuneration Act mandates equal pay regardless of gender; limited enforcement, weak in informal sector[59].
- **1961:** Maternity Benefit Act (extended 2017): 12-week paid leave, then to 26 weeks (formal sector and establishments >10 employees), and workplace crèche for companies with 50+ staff[60][61].
- **2013:** Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (POSH) Act: safety and redress mechanisms, formal/informal sectors included but implementation weak[62].
- **2020/2025:** Labour Codes (Social Security, Industrial Relations, Wage, Occupational Safety), effective from November 21, 2025, consolidate and expand coverage, allow women in all sectors (including night work with protections and consent), and extend maternity/social security to gig and platform workers[63].
- **Self-Help Groups/National Rural Livelihood Mission (2011–present):** Financial inclusion, entrepreneurship, skill-building for rural women; 9 crore women linked by 2024[64].
- **MGNREGA (2006):** Rural employment guarantee, 100 days, with one-third mandate for women and equal pay[65].
- **Skill India, Stand Up India, state-level schemes:** Vocational training; most effective for rural, less-educated women[66].

**Eligibility and Implementation:**  
Most protections and benefits (Maternity Benefit, social security, anti-harassment) legally cover the formal sector—where less than 10% of women work. Informal sector reach remains limited, despite code consolidation and SHG/NRLM/MBGNREGA advancements; actual enforcement is highly variable[61][62][63][64][65].

### Granular Breakdown: Education, Sector, Employment Type, Age

- **Education:**  
  U-shaped: Highest LFPR among illiterate/primary and postgraduate women; lowest among secondary level, due to social norms, reservation wages, and job mismatch[67]. 39.6% of postgraduate women worked in 2023; only 2.4% of all women aged 25+ were ever graduates, indicating low base rates[67][68].
- **Sector:**  
  Over 90% (2023) of employed women are in informal self-employment or unpaid family labor. Main sector: agriculture (~62% vs 42% for men); services major in cities[69][56].
- **Employment Type:**  
  Self-employed/unpaid: ~76% (2023). Wage/salaried: 18.2% in urban areas. Formal-sector jobs rare and have not grown proportionally[56].
- **Age Band:**  
  Participation peaks ages 40–44 (50.5% in 2023); steepest drop after 45. Childbearing and marriage deeply shape patterns, especially urban drop-off[70][67].
- **Informality:**  
  Over 90% of women are outside any formal labor protection, including maternity/social security; effect is even starker in rural areas[56][69].

### Methodological Caveats, Data Gaps, and Definition Effects

- All series underestimate participation, especially in unpaid agricultural/family activities and urban informal work due to survey design limitations[71].
- Pre-1990 sector/education/type splits are missing; most granularity available only post-2017 (PLFS)[56][72].
- Definitions are not uniform: NSS rounds, PLFS, and ILO/WDI differ in reference period, activity status, and even enumeration of “work” vs “labor force” (especially for part-time/casual or seasonal work)[71][72].
- Full part-time vs. full-time breakdowns not published centrally; regionally and semi-officially available for select populations only[72].

## Cross-National Comparative Analysis

### Patterns and Causes of Change

- **Iceland**: Led the world in female LFPR for most of the period, owing to continuous feminist activism, strict legal mandates, universal parental leave, and a highly supportive care infrastructure. Gender segregation by sector persists but overall participation is near parity; data quality and policy transparency are global exemplars.
- **South Korea**: Rapid progress from 1970s through industrialization, with plateauing in the mid-2010s and the gradual flattening of the “M-curve” thanks to parental leave, anti-discrimination, and flexible work reforms. Still, the highest OECD gender wage gap persists, and room remains for cultural and policy shifts to address mid-career retention.
- **Saudi Arabia**: Historic, government-led boom in the 2010s–2020s, with legal/driving reforms, quotas, and wage/parity laws integrated as national modernization policy—although significant sectoral, regional, and skills-job matching gaps persist.
- **India**: Chronic underemployment, measurement undercounting (especially informal/unpaid work), and a steep urban/rural split. Recent rebound in rural participation is mostly informal, with very little formal sector/female wage/salaried growth; law coverage mostly limited in scope and only slowly expanding via new labor codes.

### Methodological Comparability and Data Gaps

- **Definitional discrepancies** (age bands, inclusion of informal/self-employed/unpaid work, sector categorization) strongly affect cross-country and within-country trend interpretation, with Saudi Arabia and India most sensitive to these caveats.
- **Granular breakdowns** by sector, education, region, or employment type are consistently available for recent years only, with all countries lacking long, continuous, and fully-structured decade-by-decade cross-tabs. For Saudi Arabia and India, pre-1990s data are almost entirely aggregate or estimated.
- **Policy evaluation is strongest** where legal mandates are broad, enforcement is effective, and national datasets are open and granular (Iceland). Large informal sectors (India), limited data transparency (early Saudi Arabia), and evolving definitions (South Korea, India) remain obstacles.

### Policy Lessons and Implementation Realities

- **Universal, non-gendered parental leave, publicly supported childcare, and legal pay parity** are critical for sustained high female LFPR, as demonstrated by Iceland and, more recently, South Korea.
- **Sectoral bias and job matching programs**, as implemented in Saudi Arabia, accelerate rapid gains when coupled with legal liberalization.
- **Formal sector–centric policies** yield limited population-scale results in predominately informal economies (India).
- **Top-down reform alone is insufficient**—cultural and attitudinal shifts, enforcement mechanisms, and cross-sector policy integration are essential.
