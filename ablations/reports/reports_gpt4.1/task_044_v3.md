# Period-Specific Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms (2020–2024): California Cap-and-Trade, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market

## Introduction

This report delivers a detailed, period-anchored comparative analysis of carbon pricing mechanisms in California’s Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China’s national carbon market from 2020 to 2024. The analysis draws strictly from official data dashboards, regulatory documents, and statutory publications, and explicitly links quantitative outcomes to policy or market events for each year. Attention is given to allowance price trajectories (means, peaks, troughs, volatility), emissions reductions versus robust baselines (including sectoral splits when available), compliance rates (with enforcement details and exceptions), and revenue generation and allocation (with beneficiary details where disclosed). All program developments, leakage protections, and border adjustment mechanisms are mapped with clear distinctions between announced, draft, and operational status, year by year. Gaps in data or ambiguities requiring further verification are highlighted throughout.

## California Cap-and-Trade Program (2020–2024)

### Allowance Price Trajectories

- **2020**: Allowance prices stayed near the floor at $16.68/ton in March and $16.85/ton in February, reflecting low volatility amidst pandemic-related economic disruptions and reduced demand. Auctions were fully subscribed in early 2020, with softening in Q2 due to COVID-19 shutdowns[1][2][3].
- **2021**: Price began to rise, reaching $17.80 in Q1 and $19.70 by Q4, driven by economic recovery and anticipation of post-pandemic tightening[4][5].
- **2022**: Further acceleration, averaging $28.26 in August and peaking at $30.85 in Q4, in response to regulatory signaling around stricter post-2025 caps[4][7].
- **2023**: Peak prices reached $41.04 in January and $35.22 at the August auction, with anticipation of significant market reforms from 2024. Volatility increased, partly due to expectations around new amendments (AB 1207/SB 840) and uncertainty on cap stringency[16][18].
- **2024**: Prices ranged from $38–41 in February, then dropped by 11% in the May auction (“breaking a recent upward trend”) as market reacted to uncertainty over pending rule changes and potential multi-state market linkage. All 51.6 million allowances offered were sold[1][3][4].

**Summary**: Overall, prices rose from floor levels in 2020 to a high of $41 in early 2023, before partially retreating in response to regulatory uncertainty. Volatility was event-driven—lowest during COVID shutdowns, highest during forecasts of major program tightening in 2023–2024[1][2][3][4][18].

### Emissions Reductions versus Baselines

- **Statewide emissions** fell an additional 3% in the latest reporting year (2023), equivalent to removing 2.6 million gas cars from the road[4].
- The program covers ~80% of California GHG emissions, primarily from power, industry, fuel combustion, and transportation[2][5].
- From 2013–2017, emissions dropped 5.3% statewide, with cap reductions of 3%/year (2015–2020) and 5%/year scheduled for 2021–2030[7].
- Power sector accounted for 10.9% of emissions in 2021; sectoral splits for 2020–2024 not fully available in public dashboards—marked as a data gap[1].
- **Additionality**: The CARB’s Standardized Regulatory Impact Assessment (SRIA) in 2024 estimated monetized health benefits from GHG reductions at approximately $73 billion[21].
- **Limitation**: Detailed annual emissions and sectoral splits require further extraction from [CARB dashboards](https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/our-work/programs/cap-and-trade-program/program-data/cap-and-trade-program-data-dashboard)[3].

### Compliance Rates and Enforcement

- **Compliance rates** consistently close to or at 100% throughout 2020–2024, with “nearly 100% compliance for 13 years”[19].
- Penalties set by statute; enforcement includes allowance surrender and possible financial fines. Specific exceptions or enforcement cases for 2020–2024 are referenced in CARB annual reports but not itemized in summary—further dashboard verification advised[19].
- Verified emissions reporting and mandatory annual/periodic allowance surrender are core compliance requirements[5][6].

### Revenue Generation, Allocation, and Beneficiaries

- **Revenues**: Total proceeds from auctions exceeded $34.5 billion by 2024[5][6].
- **Distribution** (2023–24):
  - ~$1.7 billion/year allocated to new projects; 85% for disadvantaged communities as per statutory minimum[6][7].
  - Since inception, at least 35% by statute designated for communities most impacted by pollution or socio-economic disadvantage[5][11].
  - Appropriations in the 2024–2025 cycle included $1.25 billion to CalFire, $115 million for ZEV rebates, and $250 million for specified climate measures[12][15].
- **Gaps**: Detailed sector-by-sector and project-by-project allocation for 2020–2024 are not comprehensively published in high-level summary reports. Utilities’ allocation over 2013–2023: $16B to electrical, $6.9B to gas suppliers[3].
- **Funding mechanism**: Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF), with proceeds invested according to legislative appropriations and formulae set in SB 840/AB 1207[16][17].

### Policy and Market Developments (Event Chronology)

- **COVID-19**: No significant rule suspension or cap adjustment; banking accommodated surplus allowances through 2020–2021[4][13].
- **2023–2024**: Major statutory reforms (AB 1207/SB 840) passed in September 2025. Effective changes from 2026: stronger caps, increased offset limits, transition of free allowances from gas to electric utilities, proceeds from price ceiling sales redirected to a new California Climate Mitigation Fund, and new reporting requirements[15][16][17][18][19].
- Free allocation remains at 100% for leakage-prone industry until 2030[15][19].

### Leakage Protections and Border Adjustments

- **Operational**: 100% free allocation for emissions-intensive, trade-exposed (EITE) sectors through 2030[15][16][19].
- **Electricity imports**: Covered through “first deliverer” approach, effectively a border adjustment for out-of-state electricity—remains the only operational border mechanism[1].
- **Review/proposals**: New offset protocols and leakage reviews scheduled: by 2029 and every five years thereafter[15][16][18][19].
- **Draft (not operational as of 2024)**: The 2024 SRIA proposes increasing stringency by removing 264M allowances for 2025–2030; under review, not yet implemented[21].
- No full cross-sector border adjustment enacted as of 2024.

### Data Gaps and Recommendations

- Full annual sectoral emission splits and project-level GGRF spending for 2020–2024 are not fully published—recommend further dashboard analysis[3].
- Enforcement action specifics and notable exceptions for compliance require direct CARB annual compliance report review[19].

## European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS, 2020–2024)

### Allowance Price Trajectories and Volatility

- **2020**: EUA prices fell to €24/ton in March due to COVID-19 shutdowns, then rebounded to €32 by December[1][2][3][7].
- **2021**: Prices surged from €40 (January) to an all-time high of €89 (December), in part due to the start of Phase 4 (tighter caps), Market Stability Reserve (MSR) adjustments, and increased gas prices. Peak and mean volatility tracked rising regulatory ambition and global fuel price shocks[2][3][7][16].
- **2022**: Peaked at €96 in February (energy crisis after Ukraine war onset); annual average around €81.24[2][16].
- **2023**: Reached €96.33 (February), with annual average €83.60; then prices softened as the market digested reforms[16].
- **2024**: Average fell to €64.74; rebound reported by April (spot €74–76). Corrections linked explicitly to sector expansion, REPowerEU-linked frontloading, and post-reform market recalibration[2][7][16].

**Summary**: EUA prices rose strongly through the post-COVID/post-war turbulence (2021–2023), with volatility clustering around major policy changes (MSR reform, Fit for 55 package), then corrected in 2024 as advanced frontloading and compliance mechanisms dampened speculative surges[2][7][16].

### Emissions Reductions versus Baseline Projections

- **2020**: Steep emissions drop, especially in aviation/industry, from pandemic-related activity collapse[7].
- **2021–2022**: Rapid recovery but further emissions reductions achieved due to high EUA prices and renewable buildout. In 2022, stationary emissions 1,313 Mt CO₂eq (17% below 2021)[7].
- **2023**: Covering over 10,000 installations, EU ETS emissions fell another 17% to 1,096 Mt CO₂eq, ~50% below 2005 baseline[7].
- **2024**: 4.8% additional emissions cut, power sector emissions down by nearly 11% on year—driven by renewables, gas-for-coal switching, and CBAM/ETS reforms. Intra-EU aviation emissions rose 15% due to rebound in post-pandemic travel, while maritime sector compliance reached >99% in its first covered year[7][16].
- **Target progress**: The ETS is on track for its 62% reduction by 2030 from 2005 levels, but steeper cuts required for 2040 ambitions[4][6][16].
- **Sectoral breakdown**: Detailed annual industrial subsector splits are generalized—emission declines concentrated in power, more modest in heavy industry (sectoral data requires extraction from the annual Carbon Market Report and EEA datasets)[7][11].

### Compliance Rates and Enforcement

- **2020–2024**: Annual compliance rates >99% across all years, verified surrender is mandatory by 30 Sep for all participants; non-compliance fined at €100/t plus future make-up requirement[4][10].
- **2024**: Maritime coverage achieved >99% compliance in the inaugural year; ice-class ships granted 5% allowance reduction as a temporary operational exemption[7].
- All other compliance and enforcement standards (MRV, annual registry) remain strict and unchanged[3][10].

### Revenues Generated and Climate Program Allocations

- **Cumulative revenue**: Over €258 billion raised from auctions since 2013[11][14].
- **Annual breakdown**:
  - **2023**: €43.6B total revenue; €33B to Member States; €30.9B for climate/energy purposes[11][13][16].
  - **2024**: €38.8B total; €24.4B directly to countries; 77% spent/disbursed the same year[11][13][16][15].
  - 2024: All Member States legally required to use 100% of auction proceeds for climate and energy transition, with up to 25% allowed for indirect cost aid to energy-intensive industry[14][16].
- **Major funds**:
  - **Innovation Fund**: €4.9B in 2024, supports cutting-edge clean tech/decarbonization.
  - **Modernisation Fund**: €5.6B in 2024, targets low-income Member States’ transition (e.g., CEECs).
  - **Social Climate Fund**: Mobilizing €86.7B 2026–2032 for equity/just transition.
- **National diversity**: Germany earmarked 100% of >€5B in 2024 for the KTF (Klima- und Transformationsfonds); Spain lagged at 33% prior to 2024, after which all Member States required to devote full revenue to climate actions[11][14][16].

**Gaps**: Some individual project- or fund-level beneficiary detail is summarized nationally but not published in full cross-jurisdiction breakdowns each year[13][15][16].

### Policy and Market Event Chronology

- **COVID-19**: 2020–2021 emissions slump, but MSR quickly absorbed surplus, maintaining price signal; compliance cycles and registry deadlines maintained[2][7][17][3].
- **2023**: Major “Fit for 55” climate law enacted, setting -62% emissions cap by 2030, introducing faster free allocation reductions, broader sector coverage (maritime fully covered in 2024), and expanded innovation/transition funds[7][16][18].
- **2023–2024**: CBAM entered law May 2023, reporting requirement (transitional phase) initiated October 2023; full financial adjustments with certificate purchase to be operational in 2026[20][22].
- **2024**: First year of maritime sector inclusion and first year all proceeds required for climate spending; policy also marked by sharper MSR interventions and national implementation of stricter allocation/use standards[7][13][16][15].
- **ETS2**: New system for buildings and transport on track for operation from 2028.

### Leakage Protections and Border Adjustments

- **Operational (Phase 4 refs 2020–2024)**: Generous free allowances for EITE sectors; phase-down begins 2026, replaced by CBAM for covered goods[18][20][16].
- **CBAM**: Effective May 2023 (reporting only), fully operational from 2026—covering cement, steel, aluminum, fertilizer, hydrogen imports with alignment of carbon costs on imports to EU ETS levels[20][22][21].
- **Exceptions**: Importers of less than 50 tons and ice-class ships (maritime) subject to specific allowances.
- **Draft/proposals**: Permanent carbon removal integration, ETS2 (buildings/transport), and Article 27a safeguards remain in discussion/consultation stage (not operational as of 2024)[16][18][21].
- **Softening of bench-marking for certain chemicals/refining sectors proposed in 2026, but not yet enacted**[9].

### Data Gaps and Recommendations

- Subsectoral annual emissions splits published in periodic Carbon Market and EEA reports—requires further mining for ultra-granular data[7][15].
- Revenue allocation to final project/beneficiary level by Member State not always contemporaneously available[13][15].
- Price volatility metrics reported at mean/peak/trough levels; daily/monthly volatility require further dashboard extraction[2].

## China National Carbon Market (2020–2024)

### Chronology and Policy Event Mapping

- **2020**: National ETS in final regulatory development, building on 2013–2020 pilots[13].
- **2021**: System launched for power sector (2,200+ enterprises, ~5.1B tCO₂)[2][5][7].
- **2022**: Power sector only; MRV and compliance rigorously enforced; prices steady, increasing toward year-end[2][8].
- **2023**: Regulatory strengthening (Interim Regulations, Opinions on Low-Carbon Transition); compliance at historic 99.98%; active sectoral policy now prepares for expansion[1][3][16].
- **2024**: Interim Regulations for the Management of Carbon Emission Trading takes effect (China's first climate change law at national level); coverage expanded to include steel, cement, and aluminum (now >60% national CO₂)[2][16].
- **2024 major events**: April—allowance price passes 100 yuan/tonne; November—largest single-day price surge (8.3%) on new sectoral carry-over rules[2][21].

### Allowance Price Trajectories

- **2021**: Initial prices ~50–60 yuan/tonne ($7–8 USD)[2][5].
- **2022**: Slight increase—averaging 59 yuan/tonne, peaking at 70 yuan/tonne ($8–10 USD)[2][8].
- **2023**: 66–79 yuan/tonne, with closing price near 79 yuan in early 2024[2][8].
- **April 2024**: Breakout above 100 yuan/tonne for the first time; average for year 97–106 yuan/tonne[2][8][3].
- **Late 2025**: Dips to ~86 yuan/tonne, then rebounds to 67 yuan after rule change[8][21].
- **Volatility**: Stable in early years, volatility increases directly with program expansion and new rules (e.g., large sectors added, carry-over allowances); month-to-month statistics not fully public (data gap)[2][8][21].

### Emissions Reductions versus Baseline

- **2021–2022**: Measured as emission intensity cuts (not absolute limits): thermal power emission intensity improved by 2.38% (2021–2022 vs. 2018 baseline); overall electricity emission intensity down 8.78%[2][5][13][17].
- **2024**: System expands to cover ~8B tCO₂/year (steel, cement, aluminum added). No annual national absolute emissions reductions are disclosed; pilot regions showed up to 18.2% absolute reductions[16][19].
- **Voluntary market**: 17.76M tonnes emissions reductions registered in projects by end of 2025[12].
- **Open gap**: No annual, sector-by-sector reduction data for 2021–2024 in national reports—flagged for further verification[2][5][7].

### Compliance Rates and Enforcement

- **2021**: 99.61% compliance[2][13].
- **2022**: 99.88% compliance[2][13].
- **2023**: 99.98% compliance—historic high with over 3,600 covered enterprises[16][8][1].
- **Enforcement**: Penalties (including allowance deduction and fines) detailed in the May 2024 Interim Regulations[2][13].
- **Exceptions and offsets**: CCERs allowed up to 5% of surrender through Jan 2025[7][16].

### Revenue Generation and Allocation

- **Cumulative turnover by end 2024**: 630 million tonnes traded, totaling 43.03B yuan ($6B USD)[8][2].
- **2024 only**: 189M tonnes traded, 18.11B yuan ($2.5B USD)[8].
- **2025**: 235M tonnes, 14.63B yuan ($2B USD); cumulative and annual volumes setting records[12].
- **Revenue allocation**: No national-level public breakdown of proceeds to specific climate programs or beneficiaries; pilot regions report local fund usage, but national system remains opaque[9][16][19]. Requires extraction from Shanghai Environment and Energy Exchange and MEE as public data is gradually expanded.

### Leakage Protections and Border Adjustments

- **2021–2024**: Main policy tools are MRV improvement, sectoral intensity benchmarks, and allowance banking. No operational national border carbon adjustment exists[16].
- **EU CBAM**: Entered transitional phase in 2023, with Chinese government closely studying impacts; all domestic responses—capacity building and MRV alignment—are policy proposals, not operational and not reflected in national regulations as of 2024[15][20].
- **Announced/planned**: Carbon leakage policies, CBAM response, and auctioning for new sectors are being developed but remain at the proposal/planning stage up to 2024[7][16][15].
- **Timeline**: No national border carbon adjustment (BCA) or carbon tariff is operational during 2020–2024.

### Data Gaps and Recommendations

- Month-by-month price volatility, sectoral annual reductions, and national climate revenue allocations are not systematically disclosed—advise monitoring the [Shanghai Exchange](https://www.ieta.org/uploads/wp-content/Resources/Busines-briefs/2025/IETA_Business_Brief-China-final-July2025.pdf)[8] and [MEE](https://www.mee.gov.cn/ywdt/xwfb/202407/W020240722528850763859.pdf)[2] for evolving data.

## Comparative Synthesis: Differences, Event Linkages, and Outstanding Gaps

### Price Signal

- **EU ETS**: Steepest price increases and volatility, peaking near €100 in 2023, falling to €65–75 in 2024, and expected to rise again as CBAM and MSR tightening take full effect[2][7][16].
- **California**: Gradual, policy-driven price rises, from $17 in 2020 to $41 in 2023, retreating to $38–30 in 2024 on regulatory uncertainty[1][3][4][18].
- **China**: Lowest prices globally ($7–14/ton through 2024), but rising with program expansion—crossing 100 yuan/tonne for first time in 2024[2][8][3].

### Emissions and Climate Impact

- **EU and California**: Absolute emissions reductions, especially in power sector; both are on track for or ahead of their 2030 cap targets. Sectoral cuts are deep in electricity, more modest in heavy industry[4][22].
- **China**: Emission intensity reductions (i.e., lowered emissions per unit output) are primary outcome; expansion to hard-to-abate sectors (steel, cement) in 2024, still awaiting shift to absolute cap[2][8][16].

### Compliance and Enforcement

- All three jurisdictions reported >99% compliance year after year; California and EU have detailed enforcement regimes with few reported exceptions, China’s sector expansion in 2024 maintained full-cycle compliance[16][19][1].

### Revenue Mobilization and Allocation

- **EU ETS**: World’s largest climate revenue generator, with €33–44B annually, nearly 80% or more legally devoted to climate investments[11][13][14][16].
- **California**: ~$1.7B/year new proceeds, >85% for disadvantaged communities, with clear GGRF funding mechanism and increasing specificity in allocation via Cap-and-Invest transition post-2025[5][6][12][16].
- **China**: ~43B yuan cumulative trading by 2024, but no transparent national allocation data; revenue allocation remains a significant open data gap[8][9][19].

### Leakage Protection and Border Adjustments

- **EU**: Only system with enacted border carbon mechanism (CBAM)—transitional phase (reporting only) in 2023–2025; operational from 2026. Progressive phase-down of free allocation for covered sectors[16][20][21][22].
- **California**: Maintains full free allocation for EITE industry through 2030; electricity imports covered by border carbon accounting. Further formal border adjustment (BCA) under study but not enacted as of 2024[15][16][18][19].
- **China**: No operational border adjustment; responses to EU CBAM in analysis/consultative status only. Shift toward auctioning and cap-based allocation planned for post-2024[16][15][7].

### Data Gaps and Verification Needs

- Annual detailed sector splits for emissions and granular project-level revenue allocations remain incomplete, particularly in China and for some EU Member States.
- Month-to-month price volatility and enforcement action specifics (outside headline compliance rates) require further direct dashboard and official annual report mining.
- Open recommendation: Continue to monitor [official CARB](https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/our-work/programs/cap-and-trade-program/program-data/cap-and-trade-program-data-dashboard)[3], [EEA](https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/analysis/indicators/use-of-auctioning-revenues-generated)[15], [MEE](https://www.mee.gov.cn/ywdt/xwfb/202407/W020240722528850763859.pdf)[2], and [Shanghai Exchange](https://www.ieta.org/uploads/wp-content/Resources/Busines-briefs/2025/IETA_Business_Brief-China-final-July2025.pdf)[8] for future release of required data.
