# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality and Prenatal Care Access among Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Introduction

This report presents a comprehensive, year-by-year comparative analysis of maternal mortality rates (MMR), neonatal mortality within 28 days (NMR), access to and completion of prenatal care, skilled birth attendance, facility birth rates, health workforce counts, essential maternal health infrastructure, and the evolution of referral pathway models among Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh and Karen refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand from 2017 to 2023. All data are drawn from authoritative sources, primarily UNHCR iRHIS, SMRU, MSF, UNFPA, sector annual reports, and peer-reviewed analyses. All gaps in available data are explicitly acknowledged.

---

## Key Quantitative Health Indicators: Cox’s Bazar (Rohingya)

### Maternal Mortality Rates (MMR) and Counts

- **2017–2018:** 52 maternal deaths in the camps from Sept 2017–Aug 2018. MMR: 400 per 100,000 live births (retrospective review) [1].
- **2021:** 96 confirmed maternal deaths (aged 18–49); WRA deaths = 253 [8].
- **2023:** 57 maternal deaths reported; 75% occurred at facilities or during referral, 25% in the community [5],[4].
- **2024:** 46 maternal deaths reported, a 19.3% reduction from 2023 [18],[5].

### Neonatal Mortality within 28 Days (NMR) and Counts

- **2017–2018:** 27.0 (95% CI: 22.3–31.8) deaths per 1,000 live births (prospective surveillance for 811,543 individuals) [7],[8],[9].
- **2023 (Aug–Dec):** 304 perinatal deaths recorded, 78% in facilities [5].
- **2024:** 28% reduction in perinatal deaths observed after August 2023 [18].

### Facility Birth Rate

- **2017:** Data unavailable for actual facility birth rate; healthcare utilization among newly displaced estimated at ~46% [10].
- **2019–2020:** Study in Camp-4: 59.5% facility birth rate [2].
- **2023:** 33,854 reported facility-based deliveries, constituting 71% of all births; caesarean section rate: 5.7% [4].
- **2024 Q3:** 8,331 facility-based births recorded [6].

### Antenatal Care (ANC) Completion (4+ visits)

- **2023:** 71.6% of women in Camp-4 had at least 4 ANC visits [2],[3],[16].
- **2023–24:** Mean of 6.9 ANC visits per pregnancy in birth cohort tracked by iRRRd; only 19.2% delayed initiation until after first trimester [11],[15].
- **2023 (cross-sectional, 708 women):** 47% reported no ANC attendance for their most recent pregnancy—substantial inequality persists [4].

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- **2019–2020:** 69.4% of deliveries had skilled attendants (Camp-4 study) [2],[3],[16].
- **2023:** Exact annual figure sector-wide not consistently reported; studies and bulletins consistently cite skilled attendance rates between 69–85% [2],[3],[4],[5].
- **2023:** 283 midwives supporting 10,837 facility deliveries, accounting for 32% of sector deliveries [4].

### Health Workforce (Midwives, Skilled Birth Attendants, TBAs)

- **2023:** 283 midwives supported by UNFPA, 509 providers received SRH training, and more than 210 TBAs repurposed as referral agents [4],[18].
- **2017–2024:** No annualized breakout for SBA/TBA workforce, but qualitative documentation and program data consistently describe major scale-up of training for midwives, integration of TBAs into referral/education roles, and declining reliance on TBAs for primary deliveries [13],[18].

## Maternal Health Infrastructure and Referral Pathways: Cox’s Bazar

- **EmONC Facilities (2017–2023):**
  - Initial infrastructure in 2017–18: Field hospitals (MSF, IOM, Hope Foundation, Upazila health complexes); sparse availability of CEmONC [10].
  - **Aug 2025:** IOM launched the first in-camp CEmONC services at Camp-24, Teknaf [1].
  - By 2023: Multiple birthing centers, 24/7 EmONC via referral/hub-and-spoke, and midwife-led centers operating in several camps [4],[17].

- **Referral Systems:**
  - **Pre-2021:** Severe delays due to lack of SOPs, insufficient transport, and inconsistent blood supply [19],[21].
  - **2021:** Launch of improved SOP, WHO refurbishment of blood banks (Ukhiya, Teknaf), systematic expansion of emergency referral networks with 24/7 coverage (“Matri Sheba”) [19].
  - **2023–2024:** 10,492 emergency referrals recorded; ongoing challenges in achieving <2-hour transport for all patients, particularly at night [4],[5],[6],[18].

- **Critical Pathway Evolution:**
  - Major system overhaul began in 2021 (joint WHO-partner action) to streamline the referral process, optimize transport, and integrate blood banks; consistent annual reviews and community engagement included [19],[21].

## Causal and Contextual Analysis: Cox’s Bazar

- **Leading Causes of Maternal Death:**
  - Postpartum (obstetric) hemorrhage responsible for 29.2–40% of deaths (2017–2024) [5],[18],[8],[6].
  - Hypertensive disorders: 23–33% [5],[18],[8],[6].
  - Sepsis and indirect causes comprise the remainder (not annually proportioned) [5],[18].
- **Neonatal Deaths:** Prematurity, birth asphyxia, and intrauterine growth restriction are most frequently cited [5],[18].
- **Context of Deaths:**
  - In 2023, 75% of maternal deaths occurred in health facilities or en route (during referral), and 25% in the community [5].
  - 74% of 2024 maternal deaths involved home births attempted by TBAs, often with referral delays of up to 19 hours [18].
- **Role/Evolution of TBAs:**
  - 2017–2020: TBAs were the principal attendants for home deliveries; implicated in high first-delay maternal deaths [8],[18].
  - 2021–2024: More than 210 TBAs transitioned into referral and community support roles, aligned with a measurable increase in facility-based deliveries [18].

## Key Quantitative Health Indicators: Mae La Camp (Karen Refugees, Thailand)

**Important Note:** For Mae La, annual, camp-specific data disaggregated by year for all indicators are not available in the literature. Where unavailable, gaps are clearly stated.

### Maternal Mortality Rates (MMR) and Counts

- **1986–2024 (regional cohort including Mae La):** MMR: 176 per 100,000 live births [2],[3],[8].
- **2000–2018 (Mae Ra Ma Luang & Mae La Oon camps—closest aggregated comparators):** MMR: 126.5 per 100,000 [21].
- **2017–2023 (Mae La):** No annual or cumulative MMR or total deaths available for Mae La specifically.

### Neonatal Mortality within 28 Days (NMR) and Counts

- **2008–2011 (Mae La and similar sites):** NMR improved from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births after introduction of special care baby unit [9].
- **2017–2023:** No annual NMR available specifically for Mae La; cohort and regional averages suggest NMR remained below 11 per 1,000 [21].

### Facility Birth Rate

- **2017–2018 (adjacent camps):** >75% facility birth rates [21].
- **2000–2018:** Consistently 75–80% facility-based deliveries (aggregate data) [9].
- **2017–2023 (Mae La):** No direct annual or site-specific data; literature consistently reports >75% for the larger SMRU-supported border camps [21].

### Antenatal Care (ANC) Completion (4+ visits)

- **2000–2018:** >90% of women attended 4+ ANC visits (regional long-term average for Karen camps) [21],[9].
- **2017–2023:** No annualized or Mae La-specific data available; no known deviations from above.

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- **2000–2018 (regional):** ~80% skilled birth attendance at delivery; midwives completely replaced TBAs as of 2010 [9].
- **2017–2023:** No annual, disaggregated figures; ongoing program status reports and global health reviews cite 80–85% coverage [21]. No breakdown for Mae La alone.

### Health Workforce (Midwives, SBAs, TBAs)

- **Early–Mid 2010s:** 110 TBAs were active in SMRU/MLO/MRML camps collectively; as of 2010, midwives took over all maternal-child health [9].
- **2017–2023:** No annualized Mae La workforce counts published; region-wide reports note ongoing staff training and TBA integration into referral/community support roles [9],[21].

## Maternal Health Infrastructure and Referral Pathways: Mae La

- **Clinical & EmONC Infrastructure:**
  - SMRU-operated maternal health clinics provide ANC, delivery, basic EmONC; high-risk and complicated cases referred for CEmONC to Thai public hospitals [9],[19].
  - Exact number/timeline of EmONC facility launches in Mae La not available.
- **Referral Systems:**
  - Ambulance/maternity transport provided by SMRU and NGOs; two-hour referral window mostly achieved for critical cases [19].
  - **2017:** Launch of M-FUND, a voluntary insurance fund for migrants/refugees to access emergency obstetric care in Thai hospitals [19].
  - Challenges in timely access persist due to terrain, documentation, and insurance coverage; no annual or event-specific referral data for Mae La alone [19].

---

## Causal and Contextual Analysis: Mae La

- **Causes of Maternal Death:**
  - No Mae La annual forensic breakdowns available. Regional trends indicate postpartum hemorrhage and infection/sepsis as leading causes in recent decades (malaria was principal in 1980s/early 1990s) [8],[9].
- **Context of Deaths:**
  - No data by location (facility/community) for individual deaths in Mae La 2017–2023.
- **Role/Evolution of TBAs:**
  - Pre-2010: TBAs (about 110 active) were main birth attendants in Karen camps [9].
  - Since 2010: TBAs transitioned to referral, education, and community advocacy; all births attended by trained midwives/health staff, with continuous retraining programs [9],[21].
- **Facility/Skilled Attendance Impact:**
  - Introduction of special care baby units and referral networks correlated with sharp reduction in NMR and improvement in ANC and skilled birth attendance [9],[21].

## Structural Differences in Healthcare Systems

### Cox’s Bazar: NGO-Operated, Fragmented, Rapidly Evolving

- Multiple parallel service providers (MSF, IOM, UNFPA, WHO, local NGOs) [4],[19].
- Post-2021, concerted push towards facility-based delivery, emergency referral optimization, blood banking. Service capacity and resilience remain highly sensitive to external funding [4],[19],[5].
- Substantial achievements in expanding midwife coverage and facilities since 2018, but persistent infrastructure and workforce shortfalls (e.g., doctor/population ratio 1:40,000) [13].

### Mae La: Community Workforce, NGO–Government Integrated

- SMRU/NGO clinics embedded in refugee camp management; high synergy with Thai Ministry of Public Health [9],[19].
- Stable facility and skilled birth rates and robust ANC coverage, maintained via consistent nurse-midwife training, task-shifting, and strong host government support [21].
- Voluntary insurance (M-FUND), ambulance/referral system, and continuous retraining have buttressed service continuity despite funding volatilities [19],[20],[21].
- TBAs fully transitioned out of primary delivery roles by 2010, contributing to sustained outcome improvements [9].

---

## Documented Effects of 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction

### Cox’s Bazar

- Funding cuts in 2021–23 caused:
  - Staff layoffs,
  - Reduced midwife and outreach services,
  - Decline in postnatal and other key care coverage,
  - Slower expansion of EmONC and delays in facility launches [4],[6],[19].
- Direct association with increased maternal deaths and persistent high NMR in 2022–2023 [5],[4].

### Mae La

- Region-wide/global evidence of funding loss undermining staff recruitment, insurance coverage, and continuity of midwife support [24],[25],[26],[27],[28].
- No specific, annualized Mae La camp report on service breakdowns, but sector-wide resilience attributed to:
  - Integration with Thai public sector, 
  - Ongoing SMRU/midwife support and insurance models,
  - Task-shifting and flexibility at workforce/community level [21],[19],[20].

## Conclusion

From 2017–2023, maternal and neonatal outcomes for Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar steadily improved but remained below those seen across the Karen refugee sites in Thailand. Cox’s Bazar achieved major gains in facility births and skilled attendance, particularly as midwives and TBAs became integrated into coordinated referral and EmONC models. Persistent, documented challenges include infrastructure shortfalls, transport/referral delays, and heavy dependency on external funding—especially highlighted by service and outcome regression during and after the 2021–2023 UNFPA funding reductions.

In Mae La and other major Karen camps, maternal and neonatal health coverage exceeded 75–90% for key indicators over two decades, mainly due to a stable, integrated model drawing on host government systems, resilient NGO support, and community-based workforce development. The formal transition from TBA-led home birth to skilled facility delivery predated the assessment period and was maintained despite global funding instabilities.

The unmatched, year-by-year data from Cox’s Bazar showcase the power and limitations of direct measurement in humanitarian settings; for Mae La, the lack of granular annual data is itself a critical finding, highlighting systemic challenges in monitoring despite programmatic stability. Across both sites, stable, government-integrated service models, strong midwifery, EmONC expansion, and the careful cultural adaptation of TBA roles appear crucial for sustained reductions in maternal and neonatal mortality, particularly when paired with resilient funding and community trust.
