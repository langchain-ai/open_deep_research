# New Zealand Construction Metalworks (Structural Steel Fabrication) Market Analysis 2018–2024

## 1. Industry Definition, Scope Boundaries, and Data Methodology

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market for construction applications comprises companies mainly engaged in manufacturing structural steel components (e.g., girders, joists, rafters, reinforcing bars) for buildings, infrastructure, and large projects. The applicable industry classification is **ANZSIC 2221 Structural Steel Fabricating**, which excludes activities such as steel milling, pure distribution, erection/installation, and manufacture of prefabricated steel buildings. The accuracy of market measurement is influenced by these scope boundaries.

- **Stats NZ and MBIE Data Alignment:** Market revenue, employment, and capacity data were sourced under ANZSIC 2221 from [Stats NZ](https://www.stats.govt.nz/) and [MBIE](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/), with time series supplemented by [IBISWorld](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/industry/structural-steel-fabricating/235/), industry association publications (notably [SCNZ](https://scnz.org/)), and the Steel Fabrication Certification scheme.
- **Triangulation Approach:** Where full annual series were lacking, recent (2023–2026) industry snapshots from IBISWorld and SCNZ were interpolated along trendlines using anchor points from MBIE and official industry reports. All discrepancies are annotated and explained below.

**Discrepancies and Definitions:**

- **Official (Statistical) View:** Revenue, employment, and volume strictly cover firms classified under 2221—excludes upstream (mills), downstream (erection, project managers), and non-construction applications. Broader sector reports (e.g., some MBIE and PwC “total construction value-chain” studies) produce higher figures by including distribution, supply chain, and asset management but are not used for pure fabrication analysis[1][2].
- **Industry Association (SCNZ) View:** Focuses specifically on company-level fabrication for building and infrastructure projects, aligned to SFC-accredited companies responsible for >90% of output[3][4].

## 2. Market Size, Revenue, Employment, and Tonnage (2018–2024)

### Revenue and Market Size

- **2024 Market Revenue:** The structural steel fabrication market is valued at **NZD 1.8 billion in 2024**, stabilizing after a contraction period[5].
- **Growth Rate:** CAGR was slightly negative (–6% annualized during 2020–2025), reflecting the pandemic’s impact and subsequent recovery. Modest growth of 2.1% p.a. is forecast for 2021–2026, aligned with government infrastructure stimulus[5][6].
- **Annual Revenue per Employee:** Has increased, signaling productivity gains even as headcount slightly decreased[7].

### Employment

- **2024 Direct Workforce:** Approximately **4,100–5,000 people** directly employed in structural steel fabrication[7][8].
  - 2018: ~4,700
  - 2020: ~4,600
  - 2021: ~4,500
  - 2023: ~4,100
  - 2024: ~4,100 (projection)
- **Multipliers:** Sector supports ~28,000 additional FTEs across the supply chain[8][9].
- **Fabrication Companies:** 127–153 identified each year since 2018, with some consolidation (notably after COVID)[4][7].

### Tonnage Fabricated

- **Annual Output:** Around **100,000 tonnes** of structural steel fabricated for construction annually since 2018, with **installed sector capacity** reported at **120,000–150,000 tonnes** as of 2023–2024[3][4][10].
- **Spare Capacity:** Notably high (28–40%) during 2023–2024, providing resilience for project surges[4][10].

### Market Share by Application

- **Multilevel Construction:** Structural steel holds over 50% market share; in Christchurch, its use exceeds 80% due to seismic requirements[3][11].
- **Certification & Quality:** More than 90% of output from Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC)-certified fabricators; industry standards are among the highest globally[3][4].

#### Market Data Reconciliation

- **Revenue discrepancies** (e.g., lower USD figures in select global databases) are due to narrower segment definitions, currency conversion, and mismatched inclusion of fabrication vs. broader metalworks; preference is given to the NZD-based, ANZSIC-specific Stats NZ/MBIE/SCNZ approach for this analysis.
- **Tonnage data gaps** are triangulated between SCNZ capacity/utilization reports and IBISWorld volumes, consistent within ±10%[4][7][10].

## 4. Import Tariff Rates on Raw Steel (HS 7207–7216) and Margin Impact

### Tariff Rates as of 2024

- **Applicable Tariff Codes:** HS 7207–7216, covering semi-finished, flat, long, and structural steel inputs
- **NZ Tariff Rates:** For most structural steel inputs, **tariff is 0% (duty free)** for all FTA partners (Australia, China, ASEAN) and for most countries globally[22][23]. 
  - Non-preferential ("Normal Tariff") rate is 5% but rarely applies, as nearly all major steel import origins have FTA or Most-Favoured-Nation status[22][24].
- **Official Source:** [Working Tariff Document, NZ Customs](https://www.customs.govt.nz/business/tariffs/working-tariff-document)[23].

### Causal Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

- **Direct Impact:** As virtually all structural steel inputs are duty-free, **import tariffs do not significantly affect gross or net margins** for local fabricators[23][24].
- **Indirect Impact:** Fabricators face greater volatility from:
    - **Global steel cost fluctuations** (raw price swings)
    - **Freight/shipping charges** (exacerbated by recent Red Sea disruptions and supply chain bottlenecks)
    - **Exchange rate variations**
    - Changes in foreign government tariffs, which may affect cost competitiveness or supply chain reliability[25][26].
- **Conclusion:** Local policy settings (0% effective import duty) promote cost-competitiveness for fabricators, but real risks and opportunities stem from global macroeconomic factors and market access[24][26].

## 5. Major Infrastructure Projects: Project Profiles and Steel Demand

### 1. **City Rail Link (CRL), Auckland**
- **Description:** 3.45km twin-rail tunnel under Auckland CBD, NZ’s largest transport infrastructure build
- **Structural Steel Demand:** ~25,000 tonnes of locally produced reinforcing steel (the majority from Pacific Steel/NZ Steel), plus unique large-diameter bar products[27][28]
- **Completion Timeline:** Construction completed; operational launch scheduled for second half of 2026[29]
- **Official Sources:** [CRL Project](https://www.cityraillink.co.nz/)[29], [NZ Steel](https://www.nzsteel.co.nz/news-and-media/local-steel-for-aucklands-city-rail-link/)[28]

### 2. **Transmission Gully Motorway, Wellington Region**
- **Description:** 27km motorway, >25 bridges, delivered as PPP with major engineering works
- **Estimated Steel Demand:** Not precisely published; expert consensus implies **multiple thousands of tonnes** required (bridges, supports, retaining structures)[30][31]
- **Completion Timeline:** Project physically delivered, with final project review and benefits quantified in 2025–2026 analyses[32]
- **Official Sources:** [Infrastructure NZ](https://infrastructure.org.nz/)[31], [Transmission Gully Review](https://oag.parliament.nz/2020/transmission-gully)[32]

### 3. **Waikato Green Steel Manufacturing Project, Hampton Downs**
- **Description:** New domestic steel recycling and green steel plant (first of its kind in NZ)
- **Steel Demand (Proxy):** Designed to process **200,000 tonnes per year** of recycled steel, establishing a new supply base for the sector[33][34]
- **Completion Timeline:** Fast-tracked with regulatory approval in early 2026
- **Official Sources:** [Beehive announcement](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/waikato-steel-manufacturing-project-fast-tracked)[33], [Steel Times International](https://www.steeltimesint.com/news/new-zealand-fast-tracks-steel-manufacturing-project)[34]

**Note:** Projects are selected to represent major end-markets (urban transport, national highways, and essential steel manufacturing infrastructure). For CRL, tonnage includes all structural applications; for Transmission Gully and the Waikato plant, available proxies reflect project documentation and credible industry benchmarks.

## 6. Strategic Risks and Opportunities Facing Structural Steel Fabricators in 2024

### 1. Market Volatility, Construction Cycle, and Project Pipeline

- **Risk:** Weakening project pipeline in 2024–2025 due to softening construction consents, private investment delays, and elevated insolvency rates threaten utilization and cashflow[35][36].
- **Opportunity:** High spare capacity (28–40%), sectoral resilience, and forecast sector-wide rebound from 2026 as infrastructure cycles up again; pipeline tracking to recover to $63.7b (all segments) by 2029[37][38].

### 2. Supply Chain Concentration and Import Dependency

- **Risk:** Heavy dependence on imported steel exposes fabricators to:
    - International freight disruptions (e.g., Red Sea, pandemic backlogs)
    - Volatile global pricing 
    - Supplier concentration, e.g., from Chinese consolidation or Australian export shifts[39][40]
- **Opportunity:** Investments in domestic recycled steel (Waikato plant), enhanced EAF and green steel capacity, and tighter supply chain collaboration mitigate external shocks[33][41].

### 3. ESG, Decarbonization, and Compliance Pressures

- **Risk:** Growing demand for climate-aligned, “green” construction and ESG reporting increases compliance burdens, especially as international partners and local councils specify emissions criteria[42][43].
- **Opportunity:** NZ fabricators are global leaders in steel recycling (>95%), with rapid SFC certification adoption, the introduction of Sustainable Constructional Steel (SCS) certification, and emissions-reducing investments in EAF[41][43].

### 4. Skills, Productivity, and Digitalization

- **Risk:** Persistent skilled trade shortages and a slow pace of productivity growth (especially in smaller firms) threaten timely project delivery[44][45].
- **Opportunity:** Sector investment in staff training and prefabrication, supported by modern digital tools and project delivery models (e.g., Early Contractor Involvement)[3][44].
