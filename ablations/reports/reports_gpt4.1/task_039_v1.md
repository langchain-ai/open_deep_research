# NASDAQ Independent Director Requirements as of April 2026

## Overview

As of April 2026, the NASDAQ Stock Market requires most listed companies to have a majority of independent directors on their board of directors. The definition and requirements relating to independent directors are primarily governed by NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605, with additional details and exemptions provided in Rule 5615 and related SEC rules. These standards set out explicit eligibility and disqualification criteria for directors, as well as the categories of companies required to comply and allowable exceptions. Variations also exist for certain types of issuers, such as foreign private issuers, controlled companies, and funds.

This report comprehensively addresses:

1. The explicit eligibility criteria for independent directors under NASDAQ standards.
2. Disqualification criteria which prevent certain individuals from qualifying as independent.
3. Categories of NASDAQ-listed companies required to have independent directors, including all notable variations and exceptions.

## 1. Eligibility Criteria for Independent Directors

The core definition of an independent director is set out in NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2):

- **General Principle**: An independent director must be a person other than an executive officer or employee of the company or any of its subsidiaries.
- **No Relationship Impairing Independence**: The individual must not have any relationship which, in the opinion of the board, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director.
  - The board is required to make an "affirmative determination" as to each director’s independence. This assessment must consider all relevant facts and circumstances, beyond bright-line tests.
- **Ownership of Stock**: Merely owning company stock does not, in itself, disqualify a person from being considered independent[1][2][3].

### Affirmative Board Determination

- The company’s board has the responsibility to assess and record, on an annual basis, whether each director qualifies as independent, reviewing all relevant information and relationships that could affect objectivity[1][2][3].

## 2. Disqualification Criteria

NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2) lists six clear circumstances that automatically disqualify a person from being considered independent:

1. **Recent Employment**: The director (or any family member) is or was employed by the company or a subsidiary as an executive officer at any time within the past three years.
2. **Compensation Threshold**: The director or a family member has received, directly or indirectly, more than $120,000 in compensation from the company or its subsidiaries in any 12-month period within the past three years, except for certain permitted payments (such as director fees, deferred compensation for prior service, and non-compensatory payments)[1][2][3].
3. **Family Member Employment**: Any family member of the director is or was employed as an executive officer of the company or a subsidiary within the past three years.
4. **Inter-Company Business Relationships**: The director or a family member is a partner in, controlling shareholder of, or executive officer of any organization to which the company made, or from which the company received, payments for property or services in the current or any of the past three fiscal years exceeding the greater of $200,000 or 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues, except for charitable contributions[2][3].
5. **Compensation Committee Interlocks**: The director or a family member is employed as an executive officer of another entity where, at any time within the past three years, any of the company’s executive officers served on the compensation committee of that entity[1][2][3].
6. **Auditor Affiliation**: The director or a family member is a current partner of the company's outside auditor, or was within the last three years, or was an employee and personally worked on the company’s audit during that period[1][2][3].

### Additional Restrictions for Committee Members

- **Audit Committee**: Members must meet heightened independence standards under both NASDAQ Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3—they cannot accept, directly or indirectly, any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company (other than director’s fees or certain fixed compensation) and cannot be affiliated with the company or its subsidiaries[1][3][4].
- **Family Member Definition**: Includes a spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers-in-law, fathers-in-law, sons- and daughters-in-law, brothers- and sisters-in-law, and anyone residing in the director's home[1][3].

## 3. NASDAQ Companies Required to Have Independent Directors

### General Requirement

- **Majority Independent Board**: All domestic operating companies listed on NASDAQ’s three market tiers (Global Select, Global, and Capital Market) must have a board with a majority of independent directors[1][2][3][4].
- **Committee Independence**:
  - Audit and compensation committees must consist solely of independent directors.
  - Nominating/corporate governance committees must also be entirely independent; limited exceptions apply for controlled companies or during phase-in after listing[1][2].

### Variations and Exceptions

#### Phase-In Schedules

- **IPO and New Listings**: Companies newly listing securities may phase in compliance:
  - At least one independent audit committee member by listing date
  - A majority independent committee within 90 days
  - Full compliance (all members independent) within one year[2][3][5]
- No additional cure period if compliance failure occurs after the phase-in window closes[1][2][4].

#### Special Categories of Issuers

- **Foreign Private Issuers (FPIs)**:
  - FPIs may generally follow home-country board and committee composition practices, instead of NASDAQ’s corporate governance rules, if they disclose the differences and their alternative practice in their annual Form 20-F and on their website[4][5].
  - Despite this exemption, FPIs must have an audit committee that meets the requirements of SEC Rule 10A-3, including independence, even if the remainder of their board is not independent[4][5].
  - Effective March 18, 2026, FPIs are subject to U.S. Section 16(a) insider reporting unless their home jurisdiction has been granted an SEC exemption for "substantially similar" requirements[6][7][8].

- **Controlled Companies**:
  - Companies where more than 50% of voting power is held by a single entity, group, or individual.
  - Exempt from requirements for a majority of independent directors, and from independent compensation and nominating committees.
  - Audit committee independence is still required[3][5].
  - Controlled company status and basis must be publicly disclosed annually[3].

- **Investment Companies**:
  - Open-end funds and most closed-end funds are not subject to NASDAQ independent director requirements, though listed closed-end funds must have an audit committee complying with SEC Rule 10A-3[3][4].

- **Asset-Backed Issuers and ETFs**:
  - Asset-backed issuers and listed exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are generally exempt from most aspects of board independence requirements[3].

- **Smaller Reporting Companies**:
  - No broad exemption from director independence rules; standard requirements apply[5].

#### Special Exceptions

- In "exceptional and limited circumstances," a non-independent director may serve temporarily on compensation or nominating committees, but not the audit committee, with strict public disclosure and time limits[2][4].

### Ongoing Compliance Obligations

- The board must annually reaffirm director independence, maintain up-to-date documentation and robust due diligence, and disclose determinations in proxy or annual reports consistent with Regulation S-K Item 407(a)[1][4].

## 4. Committee-Specific Independence Requirements

NASDAQ imposes distinct requirements for board committees:

- **Audit Committee**: Every member must be independent under the strictest criteria of both NASDAQ rules and SEC Rule 10A-3. No exceptions apply—compensatory fees or affiliations trigger disqualification[1][3][4].
- **Compensation Committee**: Must be comprised solely of independent directors. A non-independent director may serve only under "exceptional and limited circumstances," subject to disclosure and restrictions[1][3][4][5].
- **Nominating/Corporate Governance Committee**: Also must be fully independent, with the same limited “exceptional circumstances” provision and controlled company exemption[1][4].

## 6. Key Points & Compliance Considerations

- The fundamental standard is that an independent director must have neither recent employment ties nor significant financial/familial conflicts with the company.
- All domestic operating companies must maintain a majority of independent directors and fully independent core committees, unless they fall into a special exempt category.
- Boards must perform annual independence determinations and make robust, well-documented judgments even when individuals pass the bright-line rules.
- Ongoing updates and public disclosures are required regarding board composition and the use of any exemptions or home-country practices.
