# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality and Prenatal Care Access Among Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Introduction

This analysis systematically compares maternal health outcomes and prenatal care access among Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar and Karen refugees in Mae La camp over 2017–2023. It traces four key health indicators—antenatal care (ANC) (4+ visits), skilled birth attendance, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, and neonatal mortality within 28 days—across multiple time points, reconciles data discrepancies, and differentiates in-period findings from post-period trends. The report also delves into facility/workforce details, referral pathways, and the evolving roles of traditional birth attendants (TBAs), critically evaluates service delivery models, and examines the impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction.

---

## Health Indicator Trajectories: Cox’s Bazar vs. Mae La (2017–2023)

### Antenatal Care (ANC) Visit Completion Rates (4+ Visits)

**Cox’s Bazar (Rohingya refugees):**

- **2017–2018:** No system-wide data available. Community-based prospective surveillance noted extremely low formal engagement at onset of crisis.
- **2019–2020 (Camp 4):** 71.6% of women had ≥4 ANC visits, based on cross-sectional survey[1].
- **2021:** System-wide facility data showed improvements, but camp-to-camp variation persisted.
- **2023:** Average of 6.9 ANC visits per mother, but almost 47% reported no ANC during pregnancy in some surveys; roughly 62–72% completion for 4+ visits depending on source/methodology[2][3][4]. There has been steady, if uneven, increase over the period.
- **Discrepancy:** Variation stems from differences in denominator (all pregnancies vs. facility users), population sampled (single camp vs. all camps), and method (survey vs. facility log).
- **Post-period:** Further improvements noted with investment in community outreach and transportation services.

**Mae La (Karen refugees):**

- **2017–2018:** Over 90% of pregnant women in Mae La Oon (adjacent camp) had four or more ANC visits, Mae Ra Ma Luang similar—Mae La is inferred to have parallel coverage[5].
- **2018:** 91–94% coverage for 4+ visits; SMRU clinics attended by majority of women[5][6].
- **2019–2023:** Specific annual rates not published; cohort studies and agency reports confirm sustained high coverage (>90%) across the period[5].
- **Discrepancy:** Most data aggregate Mae La with neighboring camps; precise annual breakdowns exclusively for Mae La are not available. Estimates are robust given program size/stability.

### Skilled Birth Attendance Percentages

**Cox’s Bazar:**

- **2017:** Facility-based/skilled attendance extremely low, <30%.
- **2019–2020 (Camp 4):** 69.4% of deliveries attended by skilled personnel; 59.5% facility delivery[1].
- **2021:** Facility-based delivery rate at 67%[7].
- **2023:** 82–85% facility-based deliveries, 80–85% skilled birth attendance reported system-wide (with 10,837 facility deliveries by UNFPA-supported midwives, 32% of sector total)[4][8].
- **Discrepancy:** Gaps between self-report and facility logs; variations in reporting “skilled attendant” definitions (e.g., inclusion/exclusion of trained TBAs).

**Mae La:**

- **2017–2018:** Skilled birth attendance 75–80%[5][9].
- **2018:** Mae Ra Ma Luang reported 79%, Mae La Oon 78%, consistent with Mae La[5].
- **2019–2023:** Peer-reviewed analyses and SMRU reports indicate continued >80% skilled attendance at birth, mostly by locally trained midwives[5][6].
- **Discrepancy:** Post-2018 single-year data not found, but multiple sources confirm stable skilled attendance rates despite staffing flux due to resettlement.

### Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence

**Cox’s Bazar:**

- **2019–2023:** No direct annual incidence of PPH per 1,000 births is published. Available data reports PPH as the leading cause of maternal death:
    - 2022: 44% of maternal deaths due to hemorrhage[10].
    - 2023: 35% due to hemorrhage[4].
- **Context:** Most PPH deaths occur at facility level; denominated "incidence" among all births is not systematically collected.

**Mae La:**

- **2017–2023:** No annual/aggregate PPH incidence publically available. Studies on maternal mortality and death surveillance in Thai-Myanmar border camps report persistent absence of such data, highlighting a global gap in humanitarian settings[11].
- **Proxy evidence:** Declining maternal mortality and perinatal outcomes following staff skill improvement and neonatal care may signal some reduction in PPH-related complications.

### Neonatal Mortality Within 28 Days

**Cox’s Bazar:**

- **2017–2018:** 27.0 neonatal deaths per 1,000 live births (prospective surveillance of 811,543 refugees)[12].
- **2019–2022:** No annual rates; sector bulletins cite continued high NMR; >70% of neonatal deaths still occur in the community, reflecting low early care-seeking.
- **2023:** Infant mortality 25.2/1,000; under-five mortality 28.4/1,000. Of 304 perinatal deaths (Aug–Dec), 81% at facility, main causes preterm, birth asphyxia, IUGR[4][13].

**Mae La:**

- **2008–2011:** Neonatal mortality fell from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births—51% decline after SCBU introduction[14].
- **2017–2018:** Rates remain <11/1,000 across Mae La and similar border camps; longitudinal studies show sustained improvements[5][14].
- **2019–2023:** Post-2018 rates not disaggregated by year; SMRU cohort studies confirm ongoing low NMR; data not open-access for precise annual rates[9].
- **Discrepancy:** Annual neonatal mortality within Mae La per year (2017–2023) not available, but stable low rates inferred from large, up-to-date cohort studies.

## Health System Elements: Facility and Workforce Structures (2017–2023)

### Cox’s Bazar

- **Facilities (2023):**
  - 45 primary health centers, 77 health posts, 3 CEmONC facilities, 8 secondary hospitals[8].
  - Specific emphasis on midwife-led birthing centers and expansion of emergency referral transport (ERTS); newly operational CEmONC in Camp-24 (first in-camp full EmONC capability by August 2025)[15].
  - At least 18 "homes of peace" (women-friendly spaces) established for counseling/referral[8].
- **Workforce:**
  - 283+ midwives supported by UNFPA; 509 health workers received SRH training in 2023; 1,400 community health workers active; 95 TBAs integrated as referral/support agents[8][16].
  - Midwife-to-population ratio improved from ~1:9,000 in 2017 to ~1:3,400 by end 2023[4].
  - Facility-based birth rate rose from <30% (2017) to 82–85% (2023)[8][4].
- **Referral Pathways:**
  - Multi-layered: CHW/TBA/community identification → health post (BEmONC) → 4 CEmONC facilities for complications.
  - ERTS (ambulance dispatch) and community shuttles for women established; >10,000 maternal emergency referrals handled in 2023[8][4].
  - Persistent gaps: insufficient ambulances, poor terrain, security/cultural barriers, communication delays[8].
- **Traditional Birth Attendants (TBA):**
  - 2017–2019: TBAs were major home birth providers.
  - 2021–2023: Shifted to “referral agents” (via targeted training/projects); home deliveries dramatically reduced in pilot areas (facility birth from 33% to 95% in one project area, 2020–2022)[17].
  - 2023: 95 TBAs formally integrated as core referral actors; ongoing community engagement essential for trust/cultural bridging[8][17].
  - **Policy shift:** Encouragement away from unsupervised home deliveries toward facility referral; TBAs retrained for danger sign recognition and community liaison[17].

### Mae La

- **Facilities:**
  - PHC model with at least one major maternal/newborn clinic (SMRU), supported by a range of NGOs in partnership with the Camp Committee and Thai MoI[5].
  - Services include antenatal, delivery (including basic EmONC), referral for CEmONC, special care baby unit (SCBU) for neonates[14][18].
  - Facilities serve both camp and surrounding populations (not just registered refugees)[19].
- **Workforce:**
  - Community-based skilled birth attendance—majority female, locally recruited, trained via accelerated/hybrid midwifery curricula (reflecting lack of formally registered nurses/midwives).
  - Ongoing intensive upskilling/replenishment due to resettlement/emigration; magnitude >80% of deliveries attended by trained staff[5][6].
  - No direct counts/year ratios available, but high stability confirmed in reviews[5][6].
- **Referral Pathways:**
  - Effective referral from clinic to Thai district/provincial hospitals, especially for CEmONC, is central; referral facilitated by M-FUND (insurance for migrants/refugees since 2017) and support from the Thai public health system[20].
  - Barriers: financial/insurance coverage, road and geopolitical constraints, administrative/legal challenges for undocumented refugees[20].
- **Traditional Birth Attendants (TBA):**
  - Historically primary birth attendants; formal policy since late 2000s directed TBAs into support, referral and education roles, discouraging unsupervised deliveries.
  - Ongoing, context-adaptive TBA training to serve as labor companions, educators, risk identifiers, and facilitators for skilled care-seeking[5][21].
  - Cultural and financial challenges in transition noted, but this model recognized as a global best practice in humanitarian maternal health[21].
  - Collaboration between TBAs and skilled attendants is institutionalized, with up-to-date refresher courses and community engagement[5][21].

## Healthcare Delivery Models, Referral Pathways, and EmONC Access

### Cox’s Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospitals & Multi-Partner Coordination

- Highly fragmented model: 40+ partners under SRH Working Group, delivering through field hospitals, birthing centers, health posts, and mobile clinics[8].
- Governance and quality strongly dependent on donor coordination (UNFPA, IOM, WHO). Parallel models lead to service overlaps, gaps, and variable integration of community actors[8].
- EmONC proximity: Historically limited; until 2023–25, no in-camp full CEmONC. Many deaths attributed to >2hr transport delay, especially in Teknaf region[15][8].
- Recent improvement: Teknaf CEmONC (in-camp full emergency coverage, 2025) promising access model and likely to drive mortality reduction[15].
- Referral pathways: ERTS and new community/CHW/TBA models reduce Three Delays (decision, transport, care), but persistent road/culture/gender/violence barriers[16].

### Mae La: Thai Ministry of Public Health Integration & Community-Led Model

- SMRU/NGO camps operate under Thai MoI and MOPH guidance; tight integration with national referral and inpatient system[5][18].
- EmONC: Onsite clinics provide basic and some advanced maternal care; severe complications efficiently referred to Thai hospitals, with costs/insurance subsidized for camp residents via UN and M-FUND institutional arrangements[20].
- Community training: Sustained investments in local (Karen/Burman) female health workers as skilled birth attendants, with repeated TBA training/refitting, are core to resilience during funding/policy shifts[5][21].
- Referral effectiveness: Barriers persist relating to documentation, financing for non-insured, and periods of border insecurity/pandemic closure, but system is generally robust and less vulnerable than in Bangladesh[20].
- EmONC proximity: Majority of critical obstetric cases reach Thai hospitals within 2 hours; SCBU and in-camp management for moderate/high-risk neonates is a key achievement[14].
- Cultural mediation: Deep engagement and adapted curricula led to sustained facility delivery/ANC results with strong community trust and high skilled attendance rates.

### Structural Differences and Outcome Links

- **Cox’s Bazar**'s parallel, donor-dependent, and less coordinated structure historically led to:
    - More rapid swings in outcome indicators with funding volatility
    - High coverage gains since 2017, but persistent maternal and neonatal mortality above accepted standards due to “Three Delays”
    - Dramatic impact of CEmONC facility openings and ERTS on trend inflections (2023–2025)
    - Ongoing struggle to fully integrate TBA/community actor network as trusted partners[8][17]
- **Mae La**'s Thai-led, community-trained, and insurance/NGO-supported approach:
    - Very high, sustained maternal and neonatal service coverage despite chronic resource constraints
    - Rapid outcome gains after new neonatal and referral interventions (SCBU, M-FUND)
    - Fewer disruptions in indicators with funding crises due to strong skills transfer and host-government alignment
    - Documented best practices in shifting TBA role with community buy-in and ongoing upskilling[5][21]

## Impact of the 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction

### Cox’s Bazar

- The 2021–2023 UNFPA funding reduction triggered staff layoffs, reduced outreach, temporary loss of SRH commodities, and a 39% decrease in postnatal care visits[8].
- Facility expansion and skills building for midwives slowed. Even with some improvements (EmONC scale-up), maternal and neonatal death rates rose: 84 maternal deaths and 304 perinatal deaths recorded in 2023; trend continued early 2024[4][8].
- Community service disruption exacerbated the "Three Delays," particularly in the context of security and gender norms. Capacity building programs and mentorship were paused or reduced[8].
- Attempts to mobilize domestic resources (Bangladesh government, local NGOs) have not matched losses.

### Mae La

- US foreign aid cuts (2023–2025) and loss of UNFPA/partner funding initiated clinic closures, staff layoffs, and transfer of case responsibility to overstretched Thai facilities[22].
- Disruption in supply chains (contraceptives, essential medications), reduction in outreach, but core maternity/newborn care largely preserved due to entrenched host-government and SMRU/NGO network[22].
- Skilled birth attendance, ANC, EmONC remained above international standards throughout 2023; yet, stress grew due to workforce shrinkage and uncertainty about funding or staff emigration[22].
- Long-term effects: incipient increases in unmet needs for non-emergency and chronic conditions; vulnerabilities exposed for uninsured, marginalized sub-groups[22].
- Data do not show catastrophic collapse of core maternal health services in Mae La, directly contradicting more alarmist narratives[22][5].

### Data Gaps, Contradictions, & Transparency

- Well-supported: Cox’s Bazar’s facility births, midwife ratios, PNC/facility delivery decline following funding drop; sharp impact in mortality trends; ERTS as an effective intervention.
- Contradicted narratives: Mae La's core maternal health outcomes did not collapse post-aid cut, though strain and risk of future decline increased; ongoing resilience due to government integration and skills transfer[22][5].
- Gaps: Year-on-year PPH incidence, granular TBAs’ quantitative impact in Mae La, facility/professional ratio specifics post-2020, Mae La neonatal mortality (2019–23), and detailed funding allocation per indicator.

## Mechanisms Linking System Changes to Outcomes

- **Health Workforce Expansion:** In both camps, increasing the number of midwives or skilled birth attendants aligned with rises in skilled attendance and facility birth rates, but only when paired with effective referral systems and community engagement.
- **TBA Role Shift:** Training and integrating TBAs as referral agents, rather than primary home-based attendants, led to marked increases in facility-based deliveries and prompt care-seeking[17][21].
- **Referral Pathways and EmONC Proximity:** ERTS and in-camp CEmONC facilities (in Cox’s Bazar) and robust referral/insurance systems (in Mae La) significantly reduced delays and facilitated complication management—directly reflected in decreasing mortality and morbidity rates after implementation[15][20].
- **Funding Volatility:** Loss of pooled donor/UNFPA resources quickly undermined outreach, staff retention, and some facility operations—especially in fragmented models (Cox’s Bazar). Integrated, government-supported models (Mae La) proved more resilient, sustaining outcomes even under financial stress.

---

## Conclusion

The comparative evidence from 2017–2023 underscores that Mae La’s integration with Thai governmental and NGO partners produced higher, sustained coverage of ANC, skilled birth attendance, and lower neonatal mortality, supported by deep community engagement and stable workforce investments. Cox’s Bazar demonstrated rapid improvements post-2017 but remained more vulnerable to funding shocks, staff turnover, and weak referral/transport, resulting in persistent gaps in maternal and neonatal outcomes. Recent expansion of in-camp EmONC and successful TBA-to-referral-agent transitions in Cox’s Bazar are promising but require stable, multi-year resourcing and further system integration.

Effective maternal health program design in humanitarian crises is inseparable from structural facility reach, skilled staff proximity, and cultural partnership with community actors. Governmental integration, continuous upskilling of community health workers/TBAs, insured referral linkages, and resilient funding are necessary for sustained gains and crisis flexibility.
