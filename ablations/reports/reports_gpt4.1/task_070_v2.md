# Enforceability of a Verbal Full Remote Work Promise under Promissory Estoppel and Texas Law: The Case of Jordan Hayes

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes whether Jordan Hayes, a software engineer, can enforce a hiring manager’s prior verbal promise of a fully remote work arrangement by invoking promissory estoppel under Texas law. The analysis focuses specifically on the legal implications of Hayes’s actions—selling a home and relocating—after receiving and executing a written offer letter that expressly contradicted the earlier oral promise and included a merger/integration clause. The report details why, under Texas law, such timing generally renders reliance on the prior verbal promise unreasonable as a matter of law, thus barring an estoppel claim. The report further explores the Texas summary judgment standard for promissory estoppel, the consequences of failing to secure a formal written exception, the correct limit on possible remedies, and provides citation to controlling Texas authority at each stage.

---

## Factual Background

- In April 2024, during hiring negotiations, a tech company's manager verbally promised Jordan Hayes a full remote work arrangement to induce Hayes to relocate from Seattle to Austin.
- On May 1, Hayes received and signed a written offer letter stating only “hybrid schedule per company policy” and containing a bolded merger clause: “This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations.”
- Hayes proceeded to sell a Seattle home at a $40,000 loss and signed a Texas lease on May 15, both after receiving the offer letter.
- In June, the manager (subsequently retired and lacking authority to modify standard terms) reiterated the remote promise via email.
- The company’s remote policy was formally updated on July 1, requiring three in-office days per week.
- Two comparator engineers hired simultaneously received formal written approvals for remote work; Hayes did not.

---

## Promissory Estoppel and the Effect of Subsequent Written Agreements Under Texas Law

### General Elements of Promissory Estoppel

Under Texas law, to establish promissory estoppel, the plaintiff must show:
1. A clear, definite promise;
2. Foreseeability of reliance on the promise;
3. Substantial, reasonable, and justified reliance by the promisee;
4. Injustice if the promise is not enforced[1].

### Actions Taken After Receiving a Written, Contradictory Offer Letter

**Critical Legal Principle:**  
When an individual takes reliance actions **after receiving a written offer letter that contradicts any prior oral promise and includes a merger/integration clause**, Texas law deems it unreasonable as a matter of law to rely on the prior promise.

- In *Sonny Ellen and Lynn C. Ellen v. F.H. Partners, LLC*, the Texas Court of Appeals held reliance on an oral promise unreasonable where a written contract explicitly covered the subject matter and disclaimed oral modifications:  
  *“It was not reasonable or justifiable for the Ellens to rely on the oral ‘promise’ because the Loan Agreement addressed the substance of the oral statement and specifically prohibited oral modification of its terms.”*[2]
- The *Barkley v. Connally* court dismissed a promissory estoppel claim, explaining: “Courts presume all prior oral and written agreements are merged into a subsequent written contract, and a merger clause embodies this doctrine.”[3]
- Thus, Texas courts uniformly find it is 'unreasonable and unjustified as a matter of law' to rely on any precontractual promise once a clear, directly contradictory written contract is signed[1][2][3].

**Application to Hayes:**
- Hayes sold the home and relocated *after* receiving a written offer letter that not only failed to guarantee remote work, but directly stated “hybrid schedule” and contained an express merger clause.
- This timing places all detrimental reliance *after* notice of the superseding written contract.
- As a matter of Texas law, such reliance cannot be considered reasonable or justified, and the promissory estoppel claim is therefore barred[2][3].

---

## Texas Summary Judgment Standard for Promissory Estoppel with Merger/Integration Clauses

### Summary Judgment Standards

- Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a allows summary judgment where “there is no genuine issue as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”[4]
- In contract cases involving prior oral promises contradicted by written agreements with integration clauses, Texas courts routinely grant summary judgment—finding the element of reasonable reliance defeated categorically—unless there is ambiguity or credible evidence of fraud (which is not present in Hayes’s facts)[2][3][4].
- In *Ellen* and *Barkley*, summary judgment was granted to the employer because the written agreement’s merger clause clearly superseded potential oral representations.

**Application to Hayes:**
- The direct and explicit contradiction between the prior verbal full remote promise and the written offer (“hybrid schedule per company policy”), together with a fully integrated merger clause, compels summary judgment *against* Hayes on the promissory estoppel claim under Texas law[2][3].
- The only theoretical exception would be the presence of ambiguity regarding “hybrid schedule.” However, even in that situation, the Texas cases cited find the merger/integration clause and written terms control absent a specific showing of fraud or lack of contract formation.

---

## Reasonableness of Reliance: Written Exceptions, Comparators, and Hayes’s Sophistication

### Comparator Employees and Company Exception Process

- Two other engineers hired contemporaneously with Hayes obtained formal, written company approvals for remote work.
- Texas law expects sophisticated employees—such as those in the tech industry familiar with employment negotiation—to recognize company process and know the significance of written agreements[5].
- The lack of a written remote work exception for Hayes, in contrast to peers who obtained such approval through the recognized formal process, further highlights the unreasonableness of reliance on a mere oral assurance for a non-standard arrangement (i.e., fully remote work)[2][3].
- As summarized by the Ellen court: “A sophisticated party cannot reasonably rely on an oral statement contradicted by a subsequent written agreement, especially where the exception sought is understood to require formal, written approval.”[2]

**Application to Hayes:**
- Hayes was aware that company policy generally required “hybrid” work, and had direct comparators who secured remote work by written approval.
- Given his sophistication and the existence of a transparent formal exception process, Hayes’s failure to obtain or even seek similar written confirmation renders any reliance on the prior oral promise especially unreasonable as a matter of law.
- This further defeats any estoppel theory and supports summary judgment for the employer[2][3][5].

---

## Limit on Remedies: Reliance Damages Only (No Specific Performance)

### Texas Law Limits Promissory Estoppel Remedies in Employment

- Should a promissory estoppel claim survive, Texas law **only allows recovery of reliance damages**: the out-of-pocket losses resulting directly from the detrimental reliance. Examples: costs of relocating, selling property at a loss, etc.[1][6]
- Texas courts consistently hold that **specific performance** (i.e., requiring enforcement of employment terms or continued remote work) is *not* an available remedy for promissory estoppel in the employment context[6].
- In *Frost Crushed Stone Company, Inc. v. Odell Geer Construction Co.*, the Texas Supreme Court expressly limited recovery to restoring the plaintiff to its original position[6].

**Application to Hayes:**
- Even if the promissory estoppel claim could survive, Hayes could, at most, recover financial losses directly resulting from reliance, such as the $40,000 home sale loss and relocation expenses.
- Hayes could not obtain a court order enforcing a fully remote work arrangement.

---

## Remaining Factual Ambiguities

Although the law is categorical under the described facts, some open issues remain:
- Whether “hybrid schedule per company policy” is sufficiently ambiguous to allow parol evidence about the parties’ intent.
- Whether the hiring manager had apparent (though not actual) authority to promise exceptions.
- Potential evidence of inconsistent or discriminatory application of remote work policy.

Absent ambiguity or evidence of fraud/authority, however, these will not overcome the bar established by the merger/integration clause and the controlling Texas law on reliance.

---

## Conclusion

Texas law clearly bars enforcement of prior oral promises by promissory estoppel where—**as here—all reliance acts occurred after the employee's acceptance of a written offer letter that specifically contradicts the prior promise and contains a merger/integration clause.** This is a categorical, matter-of-law determination, which supports granting summary judgment for the employer. Hayes's failure to obtain written approval, particularly given the established exception process and written approvals for similarly situated peers, and his sophistication as an employee, further undermines any claim of reasonable reliance. The only conceivable remedy—if the claim could survive—would be reliance (out-of-pocket) damages, not specific enforcement of the remote arrangement.

Any potential factual uncertainties regarding the definition of “hybrid schedule” or the authority of the individuals involved may require further factual development, but the controlling doctrine and case law in Texas overwhelmingly support the employer’s position.
