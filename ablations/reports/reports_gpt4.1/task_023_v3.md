# Comparative Analysis of Data Breach Insurance for Texas SMBs Handling PCI-DSS and HIPAA Data: Chubb vs. Coalition vs. Cowbell Cyber

## Executive Summary

This report provides a detailed, evidence-based comparison of Chubb, Coalition, and Cowbell Cyber data breach insurance products for a 15-employee Austin, Texas business handling both customer payment (PCI-DSS) and employee health (HIPAA) data. The analysis directly addresses the coverage of regulatory fines, sublimits and their customization, documented incident response service-level agreements (SLAs), scope and limits of credit monitoring/legal defense, explicit policy exclusions (e-commerce focus), and premium pricing drivers—referencing official specimen policies, endorsements, legal findings, and insurer/broker materials, specifically as they apply under Texas law.

---

## 1. Regulatory Fines & Penalties Coverage (PCI-DSS & HIPAA): Sublimits, Customization, and Texas Insurability

### Chubb

- **Coverage:** Chubb insures regulatory proceedings, PCI/health data-related fines, and penalties due to unintentional violations under the “Cyber, Privacy and Network Security Liability” insuring clause. Specimen policies confirm: “regulatory fines and penalties awarded as a result of a covered Claim, unless… deemed uninsurable under governing law.” HIPAA and PCI exposures are named in coverage guides and third-party claims are core to the product.[1]
- **Sublimits:** Fines/penalties are subject to sublimits lower than the main aggregate ($1M aggregate/$250K or $500K typical for PCI/Regulatory). Exact amounts are set and negotiable via the endorsement schedule and can be customized during application or renewal.[1]
- **Customization:** Chubb supports tailored adjustments to PCI and regulatory sublimits, subject to underwriting. Endorsements are available for higher or distinct sublimits if requested.[1]
- **Texas Law:** Policy specifies, coverage applies “unless uninsurable under the law.” Texas follows general U.S. principles: compensatory/regulatory fines are generally insurable; punitive, criminal, or intentional act penalties are not. No state-specific exclusion prohibits coverage for HIPAA/PCI fines, and Texas courts have enforced carrier sublimits per explicit wording.[2][3][4]
- **Specimen Wording:** “The Insurer will pay… regulatory fines and penalties awarded as a result of a covered Claim, unless such fines or penalties are deemed uninsurable under governing law.”[1]

### Coalition

- **Coverage:** Coalition policies explicitly name “regulatory actions, PCI fines, and penalties” in their Network Security and Privacy Liability section. Fines are paid unless uninsurable under applicable law.[5]
- **Sublimits:** PCI/Regulatory fines are commonly capped at $250K–$500K, but custom limits are available with strong security posture and negotiation.[5]
- **Customization:** Sublimits and even insured events themselves may be customized based on risk controls and company profile. Increased limits require documentation of compliance controls.[5]
- **Texas Law:** “Subject to insurability under the law of the jurisdiction.” Texas case law and regulatory guidance are in line with the U.S. standard (as above). No Coalition-specific Texas prohibition discovered.[2][3][6]
- **Policy Language:** “We will pay regulatory fines and penalties awarded as a result of a covered Claim, unless such fines… are uninsurable under the law of the jurisdiction imposing such fine or penalty.”[5]

### Cowbell Cyber

- **Coverage:** Cowbell’s “Security Breach Liability” insuring clause covers “liabilities related to Payment Card Industry (PCI) fines and penalties,” typically also encompassing HIPAA fines through coverage for “wrongful privacy acts”—confirm with broker for policy-specific language on HIPAA in Texas.[7]
- **Sublimits:** All regulatory exposures are subject to explicit sublimits distinct from the aggregate. Texas court has enforced as low as $250K as total per-year sublimit for all cybercrime/PCI claims regardless of incident count.[8][9]
- **Customization:** Endorsements and negotiation can adjust sublimits upward with risk controls.[8]
- **Texas Law:** Underwriting and courts enforce sublimits as written; Texas permits compensatory fines but bars insurance for punitive/criminal/intentional fines.[3][4][8][9]
- **Specimen Wording:** “We shall… defend the insured against any claim covered under the security breach liability including PCI fines and penalties… up to the sublimits in the Declarations.”[7]

## 2. Incident Response: Structure, Explicit Service-Level Agreements (SLAs), and Response Time Guarantees

### Chubb

- **Structure:** 24/7/365 incident response via hotline or Cyber Alert® app; immediate triage assigns a breach coach and retains pre-approved legal/technical vendors.[10]
- **Explicit SLA?:** No published time-based SLA for urgent events. Service guides state “immediate access” and 24/7 response but DO NOT guarantee acknowledgement or triage within a defined number of minutes/hours. Immediate support means same-day or as-fast-as-possible, not contractual time frames.[10][11][12]
- **Official Language:** “If you believe you’ve experienced a cyber incident, get immediate assistance… via 24/7/365 hotline or Cyber Alert® app.”[10]

### Coalition

- **Structure:** 24/7/365 access to Coalition Incident Response (CIR) for triage, legal, forensic, and PR support; active monitoring SaaS for policyholders.[13]
- **Explicit SLA?:** No public, official acknowledgement or triage time specified in policy or service guides. “Immediate access” only. While claim statistics (averages) are impressive, no benchmarked minute/hour guarantee for urgent event acknowledgement or escalation is stated.[13][14]
- **Official Language:** “Immediate access to expert incident response teams through CIR, available 24/7.”[13]

### Cowbell Cyber

- **Structure:** 24/7/365 incident triage, first call via hotline or claims email. Immediate review by claims/incident team and direct assignment to breach counsel and technical vendors.[15]
- **Explicit SLA?:** Yes—via partner IR vendor (CyberClan): “Guaranteed response within 15 minutes from global IR team, statement of work within one hour after scoping call.” Case studies confirm “Cowbell’s claims team was on the phone...within minutes.” This is the only carrier in this comparison with a quantified, published SLA for urgent events in U.S. service documentation.[16]
- **Official Language:** “CyberClan guarantees a response within 15 minutes... statement of work within 1 hour...”[16]

## 3. Credit Monitoring & Legal Defense: Scope, Duration, Aggregates, and Coverage Limits

### Chubb

- **Credit Monitoring:** Covered as required by law (typically 12–24 months in Texas), provided by pre-approved vendors. Exact durations specified in policy declarations, dependent on state law and material breach[1][17].
- **Legal Defense:** All defense costs for third-party/regulatory/consumer claims are covered but always erode policy and sublimits. Both in-house and panel breach counsel are used. No stand-alone defense outside of aggregate.[17]
- **Aggregation:** No explicit per-person or per-breach cap published in specimens—details outlined in the declarations or negotiated endorsements.[17]

### Coalition

- **Credit Monitoring:** Covered for affected parties post-breach “as required by law,” including all-state and federal notification/monitoring requirements. No claim of unlimited or universally fixed period; actual duration aligns with regulation/endorsement at bind.[14][18]
- **Legal Defense:** Third-party and regulatory defense—inclusive in policy/sub-limits, not in addition. All defense costs deplete aggregate; legal guidance and breach counsel included as part of CIR.[14]
- **Aggregation:** Durations and per-person aggregates defined in the declarations—no standalone universal sublimit/duration published for Texas; coverage is industry standard.[14][18]

### Cowbell Cyber

- **Credit Monitoring:** Explicit in the Security Breach Expense coverage: “costs associated with forensics, notification, call centers, credit monitoring...” Details of duration and per-person aggregate are in declarations; default duration aligns with legal/regulatory requirements, e.g., 12–24 months as typical.[7][19]
- **Legal Defense:** All expenses erode the applicable sublimit/aggregate. Policy and court documentation confirm that defense costs are paid solely up to the stated policy aggregate.[9][19]
- **Aggregation:** Endorsements can materially cap payout per type of breach (e.g. $250K social engineering sublimit enforced). Actual covered amount, per incident/individual, defined at bind; must be broker-confirmed.[9]

## 4. E-Commerce Relevant Policy Exclusions (Including Third-Party, Unpatched Systems, Business Interruption, Social Engineering Fraud)

### Chubb

- **General Exclusions:**  
   - Fraudulent/intentional acts by insured or insiders
   - Pre-existing or known incidents
   - War, terrorism, bodily injury, property damage 
   - Contractual liability, employee disputes, E&O unless endorsed
   - Losses after failure to patch known vulnerabilities within a “reasonable period” (Chubb: standard 45-day grace period via Neglected Software Exploit Endorsement)[20]
- **Third Party/Cloud:** Business interruption and extra expense for third-party service provider failure must be specifically endorsed—otherwise may be excluded.
- **Unpatched Systems:** 45-day window for CVE-published patching; post-window, coverage can be reduced or denied.[20]
- **Business Interruption:** First- and third-party coverage only for cyber-related downtime; pure system failure or future revenue loss not caused by attack generally excluded.[21]
- **Social Engineering:** Covered if Social Engineering Fraud Endorsement is added; base policies may exclude unless specifically scheduled.[22]

### Coalition

- **General Exclusions:**  
   - Fraud, insider/criminal acts, prior known events, bodily injury, property loss
   - Unpatched vulnerabilities without remediation—claims for failure to maintain “minimum security requirements” may be denied.[23]
   - Brand valuation, future loss of revenue, E&O liability
- **Third Party/Cloud:** Business interruption and dependent business interruption (cloud/server outage) are often covered in the base Coalition Active Policy, subject to sublimits.
- **Social Engineering:** Funds transfer fraud, impersonation attacks, and deepfake-enabled fraud explicitly covered if scheduled; however, these categories may be sublimited or require additional endorsements.[24]
- **AI/Automation:** Exclusion schedule adapted to clarify new risks but designed for clarity and ease of coverage expansion; more explicit inclusion of AI- and automation-related exposures in 2026 Active Policy.[24]

### Cowbell Cyber

- **General Exclusions:**  
   - Fraudulent/criminal/intentional acts by insureds
   - Prior known or pending claims/incidents
   - Coverage for system upgrades/enhancements—costs not restoring to pre-breach state are excluded
   - War, bodily injury, property damage, pollution
- **Third Party/Cloud:** Outage coverage is included if resulting directly from a covered cyber event; issues arising solely from vendor/subcontractor error outside a cyber event are typically excluded.[19]
- **Unpatched Systems:** Costs for upgrading/improving system post-breach are excluded; must maintain minimum security hygiene to remain eligible.[19]
- **Business Interruption:** Covered only if resulting from an actual insured cyber event; details set by endorsement and aggregate limit.[19]
- **Social Engineering:** Coverage available but almost always sublimited (as low as $250K total for the period); Texas courts strictly enforce sublimits on such claims.[9][25]

## 5. Premium Pricing Drivers for $2–5M Revenue, Dual PCI/HIPAA Texas Businesses

### Shared Core Drivers Across All Carriers

- **Business Revenue/Class:** Carrier uses revenue as a major base rating factor; higher revenue, higher risk, especially in e-commerce, retail, and healthcare.
- **Data Volume/Sensitivity:** Number of PCI transactions/year and PHI/PII records stored increases risk and premium.
- **Compliance Exposure:** Dual PCI/HIPAA exposure consistently raises estimated risk and rates by 25–50% vs. non-regulated businesses.
- **Security Posture:** Presence (and documented enforcement) of MFA, endpoint security (EDR/XDR), regular vulnerability management, secured/offline backups, employee cyber training, and an incident response plan are prerequisites for preferred pricing; deficiencies can result in declined coverage.
- **Requested Limits/Sublimits:** Higher main limits and higher sublimits (especially for regulatory, PCI, and social engineering) will directly increase premium, but are standard for dual-exposure SMBs.
- **Claims History:** Any recent claims, breaches, lawsuits, or fines will noticeably increase premiums and may trigger carrier underwriting restrictions.
- **Coverage Structure:** Zero-retention (first-dollar) incident response, selection of insurer-preferred vendors, and value-added risk monitoring can influence rates.
- **Deductibles:** Higher deductibles lower premiums; lower deductibles, especially for high-frequency events, increase them.
- **Texas/Carrier Specifics:** Texas market premiums for $1M–$2M coverage range from $1,200–$5,000 annually, usually at the lower end for clean-loss, well-controlled businesses and at the high end for high-volume, high-sensitivity data or prior claims.

### Carrier-Specific Premium & Underwriting Approaches

- **Chubb:** Manual risk engineering, control verification, multi-step application, and regular requests for evidence of PCI/HIPAA compliance. Emphasis on risk reduction, control validation, and documentation from broker/insured. Well-suited to businesses seeking higher limits or detailed regulatory defense.[5][26]
- **Coalition:** Algorithmic/preventive underwriting using Coalition Control® scanning, dynamic pricing updates, rewards for prompt incident reporting, risk remediation, and adoption of advanced controls. Ideal for tech-enabled or security-forward businesses. Dynamically adjusts pricing as exposures change.[13][14][27]
- **Cowbell Cyber:** Automated AI/ML risk scoring (Cowbell Factors™), premium credits for activating connectors/security tools, and a simple, SMB-forward quoting process. Fastest for tech-enabled underwriting and typically offers lowest barriers for SMBs, but applies strict sublimits for certain exposures (social engineering, PCI fines, etc.).[7][28][29]

## Conclusion

All three carriers—Chubb, Coalition, and Cowbell Cyber—can provide credible, regulatory-focused data breach insurance solutions for a Texas SMB with both PCI-DSS and HIPAA exposure. Policy structure and regulatory fine/penalty insurability are fundamentally similar: coverage is available up to sublimits, subject to Texas law (compensatory fines covered, punitive/criminal excluded), and all require careful broker engagement to set correct sublimits and endorsements.

Service-level guarantees for urgent cyber incidents are notably more explicit only with Cowbell/CyberClan (15-minute/1-hour SLA); neither Chubb nor Coalition publish official U.S. small business time-based benchmarks. All three provide comprehensive crisis management, credit monitoring, and legal defense—always within the stated aggregate or sublimit, and with scheduling and customization possible at the policy/endorsement level.

Coverage for business interruption, third-party risk (e.g., cloud vendor outage), vulnerabilities, and high-frequency social engineering or funds transfer fraud requires explicit confirmation and may be subject to sublimits or exclusions unless carefully endorsed. 

Premiums are driven by a combination of business profile, maintained security, requested levels and endorsements, and claims history, with all three carriers requiring increasingly robust documentation of controls and compliance for businesses handling regulated information.

**Recommendation:** Engage a specialist broker to assemble apples-to-apples quotes using a structured request for proposal (RFP) template, requiring full sublimit/exclusion breakdowns, schedule endorsements, and confirmation of specific urgent event SLAs and durations for credit monitoring/legal counsel to make an informed, comparable purchasing decision.
