# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms (2020–2024): California Cap-and-Trade, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market

## Introduction

This report systematically examines the design, implementation, and outcomes of carbon pricing in California’s Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China’s national carbon market from 2020 to 2024. The analysis provides annual, granular data on allowance price trajectories, emissions reductions versus baselines, compliance rates, revenue generation and allocation, program design evolution, market stability measures—especially responses to COVID-19—and policy interactions. The report also provides an in-depth assessment of carbon leakage and industrial competitiveness, including the development of border mechanisms like the EU CBAM and corresponding policy tools in California and China.

---

## 1. Program Design Overview and Yearly Evolution

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **Coverage**: Since 2013, the program has covered ~75–85% of California’s GHG emissions, including electricity, industrial, transport, fuel combustion, and more than 400–450 regulated facilities annually[1][4][10][23].
- **Allowance Allocation**: Mixed system. Around 50% of allowances are distributed for free to industry and utilities (to mitigate carbon leakage and ratepayer impact), the rest via quarterly auctions. From 2024, allocation methods and auction proceeds were further refined to align with sectoral priorities under SB 840/AB 1207 ("Cap-and-Invest")[4][10][18][24].
- **Administrative and Stability Features**: Declining multi-year cap, banking with holding limits, offset credit limits (4% of obligation through 2025, 6% thereafter; ≥50% in-state), no borrowing, price floor (2024: $22.74/ton), price ceiling (2024: $88.22, rising annually), Allowance Price Containment Reserve (APCR) as a cost collar[4][10][17].
- **COVID-19 Response**: Auction and compliance flexibility; no major rule suspension[4][10].
- **Recent Reforms (2023–2025)**: Extended to 2045 (SB 840/AB 1207), fixed allocation amounts for critical sectors, new Emissions Containment Reserve, offset stringency, further sector-specific cap trajectories[18][24][25].

### EU ETS
- **Coverage**: Spanning over 10,000 installations, covering ~40% of all EU emissions. Sectors include power/heat, heavy industry, intra-EU aviation, and—since 2024—maritime transport[1][5][12][18].
- **Allowance Allocation**: Primarily auction-based; free allocation for leakage-prone sectors, with annual reductions. Aviation free allocation is being phased out (due by 2025), and from 2026, CBAM sectors will see free allocation replaced by border adjustment[5][11][12].
- **Compliance & Stability**: Annual verified surrender, banking allowed but no borrowing, Market Stability Reserve (MSR) dynamically adjusts supply, with 24% of surplus allowances withdrawn annually above a set threshold. Regulatory amendments ensure rapid MSR responsiveness and cap tightening[7][12][17].
- **COVID-19 Response**: MSR absorbed allowance surplus from economic slowdown, no cap change, deadlines retained with operational oversight[12][17][20].
- **2023/24 Reforms**: “Fit for 55” reforms—62% reduction by 2030 (from 2005), tighter caps, maritime inclusion, sharper free allocation reduction roadmap[5][12][18][19].

### China National Carbon Market
- **Coverage**: Launched 2021, initially covering only thermal power (2,200+ entities; ~40% of national CO₂ emissions). 2024 saw expansion to steel, cement, and aluminum—now over 60% of national emissions[2][5][8][12][15][23].
- **Allowance Allocation**: Allowances are currently distributed for free based on output benchmarks. Pilot auctions and future plans for partial auctioning exist but no fixed timeline yet[4][12][17].
- **Compliance & Stability**: Annual obligation (from 2023), robust MRV and enforced compliance (heavy fines/penalties), limited banking, position/price limits, trading platform (SEEE)[5][14][15][12].
- **COVID-19 Response**: Reporting/verification flexibility, continued sectoral expansion. No major rule suspensions, but compliance cycles adapted to post-pandemic economic rebound[2][5].
- **Recent Developments**: 2024-2025—expansion to additional industrial sectors, annual performance-based allocation for new sectors, tightening of MRV, integration of CCER offsets[14][15][12].

## 3. Total Emissions Reductions vs. Baselines (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **2020**: GHG emissions fell sharply due to pandemic shutdowns[1][10][21].
- **2021**: 23 MtCO₂e below pre-pandemic (2019) baseline; 2022 saw resumption of long-term reduction trend[10][21][22].
- **2023–2024**: Still below the linear reduction pathway. The 2022 cap was 371.1 MtCO₂e; program on track for 48% below 1990 levels by 2030[8][10].
- **Sector analysis:** Power sector achieved 48% reduction in emissions relative to synthetic (no-policy) baseline; industrial sector less effective—actual emissions ~6% above synthetic control, showing room for complementary measures[22].
- **Program impact:** 113 MtCO₂e officially attributed to GGRF-funded projects since inception; most cost-effective abatement from a small subset of programs[9].

### EU ETS
- **2020**: COVID-19 caused emissions to plummet, especially in aviation/industry[6][12].
- **2021**: Rapid rebound, but cap continued to bite, driven by higher carbon prices and increased renewables[6].
- **2022**: Stationary emissions 1,313.2 Mt CO₂-eq, 17% lower than 2021, continuing sharp decline, 52% below 2005[12].
- **2023**: 1,095.9 Mt CO₂-eq (17% drop from 2022); in power sector, 10.7% reduction in 2024[11][6][9].
- **2024**: Overall, the ETS achieved a 4.8% sectoral reduction, with the power sector driving most of the cuts. Covered emissions ~47–50% below 2005 levels[6][9][12].
- **Forward look:** On pace for 62% reduction by 2030 (Fit for 55), but projections indicate further measures needed to meet 2040 targets[8][12].

### China National ETS
- **2021–2022**: Reduction primarily measured as intensity (CO₂ per kWh); 2.38% improvement in thermal power and 8.78% improvement in electricity generation intensity from 2018[5][6].
- **2023**: Absolute emissions in covered sectors remained steady; overall national emissions continue to rise, but peaking projected by 2025[8][6][9].
- **2024**: With inclusion of steel/cement/aluminum, ETS covers over 60% of national emissions (now ~8 billion tCO₂). Much of the reduction is in CO₂ intensity rather than absolute terms[2][4][5][12][15].
- **Pilot studies**: ETS regions saw 6–18% reduction in CO₂ intensity in regulated enterprises; modest leakage to neighboring cities found (see leakage section)[25].
- **Context:** Abatement outcomes heavily tied to power/industrial intensity measures and the pace of renewables rollout.

## 4. Compliance Rates and Enforcement (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **2020–2024**: Compliance rates consistently >96%, with most years close to or at 100%. CARB’s enforcement mechanism (including severe financial penalties and holding limits) ensures near-perfect compliance[3][14][21][23].
- **2023–2024**: Regulatory summary notes all covered entities met obligations; allowance banking monitored to avoid future non-compliance surges[10][14].

### EU ETS
- **2020–2024**: Compliance rates historically at or above 99% annually. Entities must surrender allowances by 30 September each year or face automatic penalties (set at €100/t, plus make-up in future allocation)[1][5][24][18].
- **2024**: Maritime sector joined and achieved >99% compliance by initial deadline[18].
- **Annual verification**: Strict MRV, full market registry, and automatic deduction for non-compliance.

### China National ETS
- **2020–2022**: First two annual cycles had compliance rates of 99.61% and 99.88%[5].
- **2023**: Record high compliance at 99.98%; 28 provincial regions reported 100% compliance[8].
- **Shanghai pilot**: 100% compliance since launch[13].
- **Enforcement**: Fines of up to 10x the average CEA price, exposure, and production suspension for violation[4][12][8].

## 5. Gross and Net Revenue Generation and Allocation (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **Total Revenues**: ~$28–31 billion cumulative auction proceeds by 2024[1][3][4][10].
- **Distribution**:
    - **2023**: $1.7 billion for new projects; 85% for priority/disadvantaged populations[6][15].
    - **2013–2023**: ~60% to the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF) for climate investment; ~40% to utility ratepayers as bill credits[6][15].
    - **2024–2026**: New legislation (SB 840/AB 1207) establishes fixed annual allocation for priority sectors—e.g., $800M/year for affordable housing, $400M for rail, $200M for transit, $250M for air quality—starting 2026[18][24].
- **Spending**: CCI programs implemented ~$11 billion; cost-effectiveness varies widely, with majority of abatement from a minority of program funding[9][10].

### EU ETS
- **Total Revenues**: Over €258 billion directly raised from ETS auctions since 2013; €43.6 billion in 2023, €38.8 billion in 2024[13][15].
- **Annual/Breakdown**:
    - **2023**: €33 billion to Member States; €30.9 billion for climate/energy purposes[13][15].
    - **2024**: €24.4 billion to Member States; 77% spent on climate.
    - **Funds**: Innovation Fund (€4.9B in 2024), Modernisation Fund (€5.6B in 2024), Social Climate Fund (to mobilize €86.7B 2026–2032)[7][14][16].
    - **National Allocation**: Germany, Poland nearly 100% of revenue for climate; Spain lagged behind at 33% until 2024, when 100% obligation came into force[14][16].
    - **Indirect cost relief**: Up to 25% of revenues can compensate energy-intensive sectors for indirect carbon costs[14][16][21].

### China National ETS
- **Total Turnover**: 2021–early 2025: 637 million tonnes traded, >43 billion yuan (US$6B) in turnover[4][8].
- **2024**: 189 million tonnes traded, total value 18.11 billion yuan (all-time record)[8].
- **Allocation**: Most allowances still distributed for free; revenue from trading used by some pilot regions for local clean energy/environment programs[4][12][10].
- **Auction income**: Not yet scaled nationally; auctioning planned with sectoral expansion[12].
- **Equity/Climate programs**: Revenue allocation frameworks are not yet systematized nationally, pending future auction scale-up and new distribution rules[4][12].

## 6. Program Stability: Banking, Borrowing, Market Reserves, and COVID-19 Adaptation

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **Banking**: Permitted within holding limits; as of 2024, banked supply estimated at 310 million allowances—potentially diluting near-term stringency[1][10].
- **Borrowing**: Not permitted[4][17][24].
- **Market Reserve**: APCR as multi-tier cost collar, with allowance price containment at escalating price stages; unsold allowances are withheld and may be retired to support price floor[4][10].
- **COVID-19**: Retail demand shock caused a shortfall in 2020 revenue and a slight surplus in allowances, but banking accommodated compliance; no structural rule waivers implemented[4][10][30].

### EU ETS
- **Banking**: Unrestricted between compliance cycles[5][1][11].
- **Borrowing**: Not permitted[5][11].
- **Market Stability Reserve (MSR)**: Withdraws 24% of surplus annually above 1,096 million, triggers invalidation of surplus to speed price recovery, and dynamically responds to economic shocks[17][7][12].
- **COVID-19**: MSR quickly absorbed surplus from demand shock; no change to annual cap, sectoral obligations remained[12][17].

### China National ETS
- **Banking**: Permitted under volume limits, designed to prevent future market distortion as sector scope grows[14][15].
- **Borrowing**: Exceptionally allowed at capped levels for specific compliance needs[17].
- **Market Stability**: Position/price limits on all trades; plans to introduce market stability mechanisms in step with coverage expansion and auctioning[2][4][12][14]. 
- **COVID-19**: Reporting and verification cycles adjusted for pandemic, including monthly reporting and flexibility in deadlines.

## 7. Policy Interactions, Sectoral Overlaps, and Timing

### California Cap-and-Trade
- Works as a backstop to rigorous complementary policies:
    - **Renewable Portfolio Standard**: 60% renewables by 2030 mandated, providing much of the emissions reduction (notably in power sector)[5][7][22][23].
    - **Low Carbon Fuel Standard**: 30% carbon intensity cut by 2030, 90% by 2045—credits for clean fuels/infrastructure; interacts strongly with transport emissions[7].
    - **Sectoral Rules**: Advanced Clean Cars/ZEV mandates, air quality measures targeting priority populations.
- **Timing**: These policies, combined with cap-and-trade, have kept California ahead of required reduction trajectory to 2024.
- **Complementarity**: Substantial share of reductions comes from direct sectoral policies, with cap-and-trade ensuring “no backsliding” and making up for gaps[22].

### EU ETS
- **Renewable Energy Directive**: Drives large share of power sector reduction, especially post-2020[6][7][9][12].
- **National Coal Phase-Outs**: Accelerate power sector cuts, amplified by rising carbon prices[25].
- **CBAM**: Boarders adjustment for cement, steel, aluminum, fertilizers, hydrogen. Transition phase 2023–2026, full operation from 2026. As CBAM phases in, free allocation proportionally falls for impacted sectors, creating strong decarbonization incentives[2][5][11][12][27].
- **ETS2/SCF**: New trading system for building and transport fuels to start 2027, with Social Climate Fund supporting equity objectives[1][11][14][19].

### China National ETS
- **Renewables Expansion**: National goal of 1,200 GW capacity reached by 2024, six years ahead of target; more than 80% of 2024’s new capacity is renewables[9][22].
- **Coal Policy**: Despite increased renewables, coal capacity also rose—though momentum shifted in 2024 toward renewables displacing incremental load growth[9][20][22].
- **CCER Offsets**: Complement the ETS; capped use (5%), incentivize renewables and conservation, mostly utilized by power sector[14][15][21].
- **Sectoral Expansion**: 2024–2025 brings steel, cement, aluminum under coverage; further industrial and petrochemicals to follow.
- **Interaction Effects**: ETS helps lower emission intensity, with policy support from renewables mandates and green certificates[21][12].

## 8. Carbon Leakage, Industrial Competitiveness, and Border Mechanisms

### California Cap-and-Trade
- **Leakage Mitigation**: 100% free allocation for leakage-prone industry until 2030; electricity imports managed via a "first deliverer" approach with border accounting[1][4][17][24][26].
- **Policy Evolution**: Continuous regulatory review of leakage risk, with new rules mandating analysis and considering border adjustments if needed post-2030[18][24][26].
- **Empirical Findings**: Modest evidence for electricity-sector leakage; rigorous rules have minimized broader industrial leakage to date[22][28][29].

### EU ETS
- **Leakage History**: Low leakage documented while carbon prices were low, mainly due to generous free allocation; indirect leakage via energy markets has occurred[29][31].
- **CBAM**: Enacted 2023, covers cement, steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen; gradually replaces free allocation (2026 onward) and ensures imports face equivalent carbon costs[2][5][11][12][27].
- **Effectiveness**: Studies find full CBAM could largely eliminate direct leakage in covered sectors, though only partial coverage (current design) leaves some risk. CBAM is driving global carbon pricing adoption via trade exposure[27][28][31].
- **Competitiveness**: State aid guidelines allow partial relief for indirect carbon costs (up to 25% of auction proceeds)[14][16][21].

### China National ETS
- **Leakage Outcomes**: Pilots saw ~6% direct emissions cut in covered cities, with ~1.7% leakage to neighboring areas mainly via industrial outsourcing[25].
- **National Projections**: Carbon leakage from national CN policies projected at 6.9–14.9% of reductions by 2060, partly offset by methane/N₂O mitigation and increased carbon sinks[24].
- **Mitigation Tools**: Strict MRV, steep fines, performance-based allocation, and rising market-based discipline as coverage expands; international border measures (CBAM) have driven China to accelerate MRV and coverage reforms[4][12][8][29].
- **Policy Response**: China lobbies for recognition of domestic climate efforts and argues for global coordination instead of unilateral border measures[29].

## 9. Comparative Assessment and Key Insights

- **Price Signal Strength**: EU ETS has had the steepest price rises and highest price volatility amid tightening and market expansion, with prices exceeding €100 in 2023, tapering to €65–75 in 2024. California’s prices are lower ($30–41), with strong cost containment; China’s prices remain the lowest ($11–14, CNY 80–100), but are rising rapidly.
- **Emissions Impact**: EU and California both deliver absolute reductions, led by the power sector; China’s ETS drives intensity reductions, with absolute peaking ahead of 2030. Direct emissions cuts are supported by complementary policy impacts (renewables, coal phase-out).
- **Compliance and Governance**: All three markets maintain near-perfect annual compliance rates, supported by strong MRV and enforcement.
- **Revenue and Investment**: EU and California ETSs generate very large and well-allocated climate revenues; China’s system, largely free allocation, is only now establishing nationwide revenue allocation models as it moves toward auctioning.
- **Market Stability**: EU’s MSR has shown success in managing market shocks. California’s cost collar (reserve, ceiling/floor) ensures predictability. China is evolving from pilot trading with price/position limits toward more flexible market design.
- **Policy Interactions**: Success in all jurisdictions depends on carbon pricing working alongside targeted renewables, efficiency, and industrial modernization policies.
- **Leakage and Competitiveness**: Free allocation, state aid, and sectoral “buffering” remain the first line of defense; only the EU has fully committed to border carbon pricing (CBAM) with global spillovers; California and China continue to monitor policy evolution and global trade trends to adjust their approaches.
