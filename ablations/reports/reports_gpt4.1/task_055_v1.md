# The Legal Status and Treatment of Enslaved People in the British Empire During the 1730s: Statutory Language, Legal Contexts, Comparative Law, Scholarship, and the Molasses Act

## Overview

This report provides an in-depth, historically rigorous overview of the legal status and treatment of enslaved people in the British Empire in the 1730s, focusing on statutory language from critical legislation, archival access, a comparative analysis of British and colonial law, recent scholarship on legal dehumanization, and the scholarly debate over the Molasses Act of 1733. Detailed primary excerpts, archival locations, and the current state of historiography are included.

## Statutory Language: The Molasses Act and Colonial Slave Laws (Jamaica, Barbados, South Carolina, 1730–1739)

### The Molasses Act (1733)

**Statutory Overview:**
- The Molasses Act (6 Geo. II c.13), enacted by the British Parliament in 1733, imposed duties on molasses, rum, and sugar from non-British colonies to protect British West Indian planters. Although not directly a slave code, it was deeply tied to the plantation and slave-based Atlantic economy.

**Selected Statutory Language:**
> "Whereas the Welfare and Prosperity of Your Majesty’s Sugar Colonies in America are of the greatest Consequence and Importance to the Trade, Navigation, and Strength of this Kingdom... From and after the Twenty-fifth Day of December, One thousand Seven hundred and thirty three, there shall be raised... upon all Rum or spirits... the Sum of Nine pence... for every Gallon thereof, and... upon all Molasses or Syrups... the Sum of Six pence... for every Gallon thereof; and... upon all Sugars and Paneles... a Duty after the Rate of Five shillings... for every Hundred Weight... Forfeiting all such Sugar, Paneles, Syrups, or Molasses, Rum, or Spirits, or the Value thereof, together with the Ship or Vessel..."  
[1]

**Archival Sources:**
- Full text found at [The Statutes Project](https://statutes.org.uk/site/the-statutes/eighteenth-century/1733-6-george-2-c-13-the-molasses-act/) and [American History Central](https://www.americanhistorycentral.com/documents/molasses-act-of-1733-text/) [1][2].

### Colonial Slave Laws

#### Barbados

**Legal Foundation:**
- The 1661 Barbados Slave Code and subsequent revisions, continuously reenacted into the 18th century, served as the template for slave codes across the British Caribbean and the southern mainland colonies.

**Representative Statutory Language:**
> "[Slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels."  
> "The Negroes and other Slaves... shall be held, taken, adjudged, and deemed in law to be Estates Real, and not Chattels."  
[3][4][5]

- The code explicitly described enslaved people as property, allowing complete owner power—including corporal punishment and severe penalties for resistance.

**Archival Access:**
- Original and reproduction statutes available at [Barbados Department of Archives](https://www.facebook.com/groups/269202760462518/posts/1324394411610009/) and academic digitizations such as [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/laws/barbados-1661) [6][3].

#### Jamaica

**Statutory Practice:**
- Jamaica largely replicated the Barbados template, explicitly defining enslaved people as "chattels or real property" and codifying heritability and owner power throughout the period (1730–1739). While direct statutory language from this exact decade is limited in digital repositories, contemporary scholarship attests to continuity.

> "[Jamaica statutes] defined slaves as chattels or real property, abolishing the legal distinctions between real property, chattel property, and slaves..."  
[7]

**Archival Access:**
- Jamaican slave laws are preserved in [The National Archives, UK](https://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/help-with-your-research/research-guides/british-transatlantic-slave-trade-records/) and university libraries; [Colonial Statutes at Large](https://libguides.rutgers.edu/c.php?g=336836&p=2267529) [8][9].

#### South Carolina (Negro Act 1740 and Predecessor Codes)

**Legal Language:**
- The 1740 Slave Code—codifying prior custom and practice—explicitly classified enslaved people as property.

> "All negroes, Indians (free Indians excepted), mulattoes or mestizoes... shall be and remain forever... absolute slaves... deemed... chattels personal."  
> "...chattels personal in the hands of their owners, and their executors, administrators and assigns..."  
[10][11]

- The Act also includes detailed regulations on travel, punishment, trial (by summary tribunal of justices and freeholders, denying trial by jury), and prohibits enslaved people from testifying or learning to write.

**Archival Access:**
- See [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/laws/south-carolina-1740), University of Wisconsin's [American Legal History to the 1860s](https://wisc.pb.unizin.org/ls261/chapter/ch-1-1-the-slave-code-of-south-carolina-1740/) [12][11].

## Comparative Analysis: British Parliamentary Law vs. Colonial Plantation Codes (Jamaica & Barbados, 1730s)

### British Parliamentary Law (Metropole)

- **Absence of Slave Statutes in England:**  
  In the 1730s, no statute in English law established or defined slavery on English soil. Court cases such as Cartwright (1569), and later Somerset v. Stewart (1772), established that "English law could not recognize slavery" within the realm [13][14]. There was legal ambiguity about the status of enslaved people brought to Britain—their presence did not automatically bring freedom, but there was no lawful foundation for chattel slavery.

- **Legal Status in Empire:**  
  However, in the colonies, Parliamentary law treated enslaved people as "goods and commodities within the Acts of Trade and Navigation" (Solicitor-General, 1677) and acknowledged their status as property, especially for the purposes of commerce (e.g., insurance, shipping, inheritance).

### Colonial Plantation Law (Jamaica and Barbados)

- **Explicit Chattel Status:**  
  Plantation codes—modelled on Barbados statutes—defined slavery as a hereditary, permanent, race-based condition. They explicitly rendered enslaved Africans "goods and chattels," stripped of legal personhood [3][4][6][10].

- **Owner Power and Control:**  
  These codes granted near-absolute power to slaveholders. Owners could whip, torture, mutilate, or execute enslaved people for a wide range of offenses, with minimal or no risk of legal punishment. Trials for enslaved people were summary, denied jury trial and, typically, testimonial rights.

- **Social Control and Racial Hierarchy:**  
  Slave codes also sought to suppress potential alliances between poor whites and Black people, policed interracial relations, and aimed to maintain the plantation hierarchy.

### Key Legal Differences

| Aspect                   | British Parliamentary Law          | Colonial Codes (Jamaica/Barbados)         |
|--------------------------|------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Status of Slavery        | Not legally defined, ambiguous, no statutory slavery; recognized only in colonial law | Explicit, statutory, race-based slavery with chattel status |
| Inheritance              | Not defined; no law of descent     | Maternal inheritance (partus sequitur ventrem): "condition of the mother" |
| Punishment/Control       | No legal basis for coercion or corporal punishment in Britain | Owners given broad powers: whipping, death, torture legal with little recourse |
| Legal Personhood         | Ambiguous; some court cases upheld petitions for freedom | Enslaved people denied legal rights; treated entirely as property |
| Administration of Justice| Judicial process in regular courts | Summary 'kangaroo' courts for enslaved people, barred from testifying    |

**Conclusion:** British law in the metropole avoided direct codification of slavery, leaving it formally undefined, while colonial law explicitly constructed an absolute, racialized regime of chattel slavery with comprehensive codes for control and dehumanization [13][14][15][16][17].

## Recent Peer-Reviewed Scholarship on Legal Dehumanization (2015–2025)

### 1. “‘Negroes Goods and Merchandizes’: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700–1763”  
**(Lee B. Wilson, 2020)**
- Examines how colonial Vice Admiralty Courts repurposed maritime law to treat enslaved people as chattel property, equating them to cargo.
- Legal procedures and language contributed centrally to a worldview where Africans were "not just like things, they were things."
- Shows the legal process itself was a vehicle of systemic dehumanization [18][19].

### 2. “General Average, Human Jettison, and the Status of Slaves in Early Modern Europe”  
**(Jake Dyble, 2023)**
- Examines the legal status of enslaved people as "cargo" versus "persons" under the principle of general average (maritime insurance).
- Contrasts Mediterranean/early modern legal practice (where slaves were not fully treated as merchandise) with the later Atlantic world, where they were rendered insurable property—marking a shift towards radical dehumanization and commodification [20].

### 3. “After Abolition: Cugoano on ‘Lawful Servitude’ and the Injustice of Slavery”  
**(Journal of Modern Philosophy, 2023)**
- Analyzes Ottobah Cugoano’s 18th-century antislavery thought, identifying the core injustice of colonial slavery as both the robbery of rights and dehumanization through commodification.
- Contrasts biblical "servitude" (bounded, waged, non-racialized) with colonial slavery (absolute, racial, violent commodification), illuminating how law both creates and legitimates dehumanization [21].

## Scholarly Debate: Was the Molasses Act of 1733 About Trade Regulation or Slave Control?

### Scholarly Consensus

- **Primary Intent: Trade Regulation**
  - The Molasses Act was designed to bolster British West Indian sugar planters by imposing high duties on foreign (primarily French) molasses, thereby attempting to direct colonial purchasing towards British-controlled sources.
  - It was another expression of mercantilist policy, seeking to secure Britain's commercial monopoly and raise revenue [1][2][22][23][24][25].
  - The Act was part of a web of regulations aimed at maintaining Britain's economic primacy in the Atlantic, closely tied to, but not directly aimed at, controlling enslaved populations.

- **Relation to Slavery**
  - Molasses and rum production were deeply intertwined with the slave economy. The triangular trade depended on inexpensive (often illegally imported) French molasses for New England rum—which was subsequently traded for enslaved Africans.
  - Despite this interdependence, no primary or secondary sources argue the 1733 Act was intended as a mechanism for slave control (in the sense of regulating or disciplining enslaved people). Rather, it was a policy of commercial protectionism with effects that deeply impacted the enslaved workforce and facilitated Atlantic slavery, but not by direct legal regulation of enslaved populations [22][24].

- **Scholarly Authority**
  - Economic historians (notably Eric Williams, “Capitalism and Slavery”) agree that the Act was fundamentally a trade measure—though one that both presupposed and perpetuated the Atlantic slave system [26][27].

### Representative Scholarly Statements

- "The Act aimed to force colonists to buy only British West Indian molasses, raising colonial tensions and resistance due to its economic impact" [23].
- "There is no evidence...that any major scholar argues the Act’s primary purpose was slave control or regulation of enslaved populations." [24][26].
- The act’s failure and subsequent colonial resistance became a critical economic precursor to revolutionary tensions, not a regulatory instrument over enslaved people per se [25].
