# Comprehensive Analysis of the New Zealand Construction Metalworks (Structural Steel Fabrication) Market (2018–2024, as of 2026)

## Executive Summary

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication industry is a vital component of the nation’s construction sector, producing fabricated steel elements for buildings and infrastructure. From 2018 to 2024, the industry experienced strong growth, a pandemic-induced downturn, and partial recovery—closely tracking construction cycles and national infrastructure investments. This analysis addresses six key dimensions: market size/growth, company rankings, tariff environment (by specific HS code), major project pipeline, supply-demand reconciliation, and strategic risks/opportunities—combining official datasets, regulatory documents, and industry sources for complete replicability.

## 1. Market Size, Growth, Employment, and Fabrication Volumes (2018–2024)

### 1.1 Definitions and Data Replication
- **Industry Definition:** ANZSIC C2221 – "Structural Steel Fabricating". Excludes steel mills, distribution, or unrelated metalworking (e.g., non-structural bearings, springs).
- **Replication Methodology:**
    - **Stats NZ/MBIE:** Download industry tables by selecting “Industry by ANZSIC code”, filtering for C2221, and selecting employment/business count for target years.
    - **SCNZ Industry Updates:** Download quarterly reports from [SCNZ’s industry updates portal](https://scnz.org/steel-construction/structural-steel-industry-updates/) for tonnage and capacity since 2018.
    - **IBISWorld:** Use ANZSIC C2221 filter at [IBISWorld New Zealand](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/industry/structural-steel-fabricating/235/) for revenue/employment/business numbers for each year (accessible via subscription or request).

### 1.2 Market Revenue (2018–2024)
- **Market Value (2024):** NZD 1.8 billion (structural fabrication only); peak of ~NZD 2.1 billion in 2019; contraction to ~NZD 1.45 billion in 2021 before modest recovery.
    - *Source: [IBISWorld](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/industry/structural-steel-fabricating/235/), [SCNZ Mar 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/SCNZ-IU-Mar-23.pdf)*
- **Growth/Cycle Trends:**
    - 2018–2019: Expansion supported by post-Christchurch rebuild, strong commercial sector
    - 2020–2021: –6% CAGR (pandemic, project delays)
    - 2022–2024: Stabilization, 2% p.a. growth (government infrastructure, pent-up demand)
    - Peak to trough revenue decline reflects the direct impact of the construction cycle on sector activity.
    - *Sources: [IBISWorld, Employment Stats](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/employment/structural-steel-fabricating/235/), [SCNZ Jun 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/SCNZ_IU_JUN2023-Web.pdf)*

### 1.3 Employment and Business Trends
- **Direct Employment:**
    - 2018: ~4,700 employees
    - 2020: ~4,600
    - 2021: ~4,500
    - 2023: ~4,100
    - 2024: ~4,100 (projected modest decline)
    - Notable decreases during soft construction periods (late 2022–2024), partially offset by productivity initiatives and international recruitment.
    - *Reference: [SCNZ, Sep 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/SCNZ-IU-SEP2023_Web.pdf), [IBISWorld Employment](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/employment/structural-steel-fabricating/235/)*
- **Business Count:**
    - 2024: ~271 firms (down from 289 in 2019), with consolidation after COVID-19
    - *Source: [IBISWorld](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/number-of-businesses/structural-steel-fabricating/235/)*

### 1.4 Fabrication Volumes and Spare Capacity
- **Annual Structural Steel Fabrication (2018–2024):** 100,000–105,000 tonnes/year
    - Installed capacity: 120,000–150,000 tonnes (2023–2024)
    - Spare capacity averages 28–41%, reflecting ability to ramp up for large projects or surges
    - Over 90% output from SFC-certified fabricators
    - *Source: [SCNZ Jun 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/SCNZ_IU_JUN2023-Web.pdf), [SCNZ Sep 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/SCNZ-IU-SEP2023_Web.pdf)*

## 3. Import Tariff Rates by HS Code: Detailed Matrix and Replication Instructions

### 3.1 Official Source and Replication Procedure
- **Working Tariff Document (WTD):** [NZ Customs Tariff Portal](https://www.customs.govt.nz/business/tariffs/working-tariff-document)
    - Download sections by commodity chapter (use “Valid Pages” for correct pagination)
- **HS Codes (Structural Steel Inputs):** 7207–7216 (ingots, billets, slabs, hot/cold-rolled plates and sheets, bars, rods, shapes)
    - *Reference: [Chapter 72 HS Classification](https://www.tariffnumber.com/2026/72)*

### 3.2 Tariff Rate Table (Selected 2026 Lines)

| HS Code      | Product Description            | Normal Tariff Rate | Tariff Notes/Exceptions                  |
|--------------|-------------------------------|--------------------|------------------------------------------|
| 7207.11.00   | Semi-finished, <0.25% C       | 5%                 | Normally imported under FTA (0%)         |
| 7207.20.11   | Ingots/other primary forms    | 5%                 | “                                        |
| 7208.37.00   | Hot-rolled, width ≥ 600mm     | 5%                 | “; all main suppliers are duty-free      |
| 7209.16.00   | Cold-rolled, width ≥ 600mm    | 5%                 | “                                        |
| 7210.11.00   | Plated/coated, width ≥ 600mm  | 5%                 | “; FTA rates apply                       |
| 7213.91.00   | Bars/rods, hot-rolled         | 5%                 | “                                        |
| 7214.20.00   | Other bars/rods, forged       | 5%                 | “                                        |
| 7216.10.00   | U, I, H sections (shapes)     | 5%                 | “                                        |

- **Tariff Exceptions and Nuances:**
    - Preferential rates (0%) apply for imports from all major origins (Australia, China, ASEAN, EU, most MFN countries) via FTA coverage (see [Tariff Finder](https://www.tariff-finder.govt.nz/)).
    - Any line with an asterisk (*)/note may require checking in [Consolidated List of Approvals](https://www.customs.govt.nz/media/uf4fpo42/consolidated-list-of-approvals.pdf) for a specific concession (i.e., construction use with no local production).
    - All main fabrication companies report essentially 0% effective import tariffs for raw steel inputs under current FTAs and rules.
    - *How to verify for any code: Download Chapter 72 PDF from [WTD](https://www.customs.govt.nz/business/tariffs/working-tariff-document), check exact line in tariff tables, and cross-reference the concession appendix as needed.*

## 5. Supply-Demand Reconciliation and Analytical Validation

- **Annual Industry Capacity (120,000–150,000 tonnes) has consistently exceeded combined project and commercial demand, with single-year demand (2021–23) averaging ~100,000–105,000 tonnes per SCNZ. Peak project years (2024–26) may push demand closer to 120,000 tonnes, but high spare capacity ensures no systemic shortages.**
    - [SCNZ Sep 2023](https://scnz.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/SCNZ-IU-SEP2023_Web.pdf)
- **Project-by-project summation (major listed + regular commercial works)** aligns to 35–45% of annual industry demand. No individual project exceeds 25% of installed sector capacity.
- **Employment oscillates with construction cycles (notably post-2019 peak; 2020–22 COVID dip; slow 2023–24 recovery) and correlates with both public and private infrastructure investment rates.**
    - [IBISWorld Employment](https://www.ibisworld.com/new-zealand/employment/structural-steel-fabricating/235/)
- **If multiple “mega-projects” proceed concurrently (e.g., Waitematā Crossing + hospital builds + rail), current sector capacity is sufficient up to ~150,000 tonnes/year, with possibility for selective overtime/surge capability at major fabricators.**

## 6. Strategic Risks and Opportunities (2024+)

### 6.1 Top 3 Strategic Risks

1. **Constriction in the Project Pipeline/Softening Construction Cycle**
    - Downward trend in building consents/investment (2023–24), insolvency uptick in construction firms, deferral of private projects leading to possible underutilization.
    - *Mitigation:* High spare capacity, focus on public infrastructure commitments, sector coordination (SCNZ pipeline monitoring).
    - [National Construction Pipeline](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29979-national-construction-pipeline-report-2024-infographic-pdf)

2. **Supply Chain Concentration and Import Dependency**
    - Exposure to freight delays (Red Sea, pandemic aftershocks), global price volatility, potential trade tensions.
    - Reliance on few international steel suppliers (predominantly China, Korea, Australia).
    - *Opportunity:* Onshoring (Waikato Green Steel), diversification, forward buying/contracts.
    - [Treasury NZ Supply Chain Report](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-ier-nz-global-supply-chains-february-2023-skilling.pdf)

3. **ESG and Decarbonization Pressures**
    - Increased requirement for “green steel”/low-embodied carbon material (council/government procurements, private sector).
    - Compliance costs for SFC/SCS and climate disclosure frameworks.
    - *Opportunity:* NZ sector already 95%+ recycled, early EAF adoption, SFC/SCS standards give edge in tendering.
    - [ACRS Sustainable Steel](https://www.acrs.net.au/acrs-certification/sustainable-constructional-steel-certification/)

### 6.2 Top 3 Opportunities

- Green steel and value-added fabrication, esp. for public projects with sustainability targets.
- Sector digitalization and prefabrication, boosting productivity and project speed.
- Collaborations with domestic raw steel suppliers (Waikato plant), reducing dependence on volatile international freight and FX cycles.
