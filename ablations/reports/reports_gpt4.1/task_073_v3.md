## 1. Introduction

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnosis is governed by two major frameworks: the DSM-5 (2013, further updated in the DSM-5-TR 2022) from the American Psychiatric Association and the WHO's ICD-11 (2019, implemented internationally from 2022 onward). Despite increased harmonization over previous decades, substantial technical and administrative differences persist. These differences—especially regarding explicit checklists versus clinically judged criteria, the recognition and operationalization of sensory issues, use of severity/support labels, onset/impairment requirements, and administrative coding—affect clinical practice, research, access to care, and outcomes.

---

## 2. Detailed Systematic Comparison: DSM-5 vs. ICD-11

### Core Social-Communication Requirements

**DSM-5 (2013, DSM-5-TR 2022):**

- Explicit *numerical thresholds*: Must meet *all* three social-communication criteria (“A1”–“A3”):
    1. Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
    2. Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors
    3. Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships  
- Each criterion operationalized with clear behavioral exemplars; all three must be evidenced for diagnosis.  
- Clinical judgment applies only as to whether each threshold is met, not as to the number required[1][2][3].

**ICD-11 (2019):**
- Broader, less prescriptive language:
    - “Persistent deficits in the ability to initiate and sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication”  
- No numerically required subcriteria; examples are offered, but deficits must be present globally, as judged by the clinician based on developmentally and culturally atypical functioning[4][5][6].
- Loss of the strict “three-subcriteria” model in favor of holistic, context-based evaluation.

**Technical Distinction:**  
DSM-5 employs a rigid checklist with exact required features; ICD-11 allows more flexible, contextually sensitive, judgment-based diagnosis.

---

### Sensory Processing Features

**DSM-5:**
- Sensory hyper/hyporeactivity or unusual sensory interests appears as one of *four* restricted, repetitive behaviors (RRBs; “B4”).
- Requirement: *At least two of four* (B1–B4) must be endorsed, so sensory processing may be, but is not required, for diagnosis.
- Examples are concrete: “apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds/textures, excessive smelling/touching, visual fascination with lights or movement”[1][2][3].

**ICD-11:**
- Sensory features are listed as examples in the required domain of “restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns.”  
- Only *one* RRB needed, which *may* be a sensory symptom.
- No specific threshold for sensory features—diagnosis can be made on sensory aspects alone (if they are functionally significant), but sensory symptoms are not mandatory in all cases[4][5][6].

**Technical Distinction:**  
DSM-5 provides strict numeric weighting within the RRB domain; ICD-11 foregrounds sensory features as core examples, grants greater latitude for diagnosis in “sensory-dominant” presentations, but does not make them compulsory.

---

### Severity/Specifiers and Coding/Labeling

**DSM-5:**
- *Three severity levels* (“Level 1: requires support” to “Level 3: requires very substantial support”), defined *separately* for social-communication and RRB domains; required as descriptive labels.
- Clinical judgment subjectivity acknowledged; severity labels guide, not dictate, service eligibility.
- Specifiers: intellectual or language impairment, genetic/medical conditions, neurodevelopmental/behavioral disorders, catatonia[2][3][6].

**ICD-11:**
- *No severity levels* per se.
- Instead, administrative specifiers (subcodes) for intellectual developmental disorder (IDD) and for language impairment (e.g., 6A02.0–6A02.3).
- Emphasis is on descriptive, functional assessment—clinician selects the most fitting code cluster, often with extensions for individualized clinical nuances[4][5][6].

**Technical Distinction:**  
DSM-5 codifies “severity” as a required but subjective label; ICD-11 encodes key functional differences as subtypes but does not grade support/severity in labeled tiers.

---

### Age and Onset Requirements (Including Masking/Delayed Presentation)

**DSM-5:**
- Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period, but may not become fully apparent until social demands exceed capacities (potential for masked, compensated, or late manifestation)[2][6][7].

**ICD-11:**
- Similar: Onset during developmental period—typically early childhood—but explicit recognition that symptoms “may not become fully manifest until later when social demands exceed limited capacities,” supporting diagnosis in “masked” or “delayed” cases[4][5][6].

**Both** explicitly affirm that late/unmasked presentation is valid, provided symptoms stem from development.

---

### Functional Impairment Requirements

**DSM-5:**
- Mandatory: “Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning.”
- Diagnosis is not permitted without this subjective but critical threshold[2][3][7].

**ICD-11:**
- Also mandatory: Deficits or symptoms “are sufficiently severe to cause impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.”
- No quantifiable threshold; left to clinical judgment[4][5][6].

---

### Administrative Coding Structure & Transition Details

**DSM-5:**
- Clinical diagnosis only; not used for billing.
- Labels: “Autism Spectrum Disorder”; levels and specifiers described as text.
- U.S. clinicians must crosswalk DSM-5 diagnoses to ICD-10-CM codes for reimbursement, most commonly F84.0 (“Autistic disorder” collapsed under ASD by default)[8][9][10].

**ICD-10:**
- *Codes used until country adopts ICD-11*: F84.0 (childhood autism), F84.1 (atypical autism), F84.5 (Asperger’s), F84.9 (PDD unspecified).
- U.S. has used ICD-10-CM since 2015, but ICD-10 codes still dominate U.S. claims/administration[8][9][11].

**ICD-11:**
- *Code*: 6A02 Autism Spectrum Disorder, with subcodes reflecting intellectual and/or functional language status:
    - 6A02.0: no IDD, mild/no language impairment
    - 6A02.1: with IDD, mild/no language impairment
    - 6A02.2: no IDD, absence of functional language
    - 6A02.3: with IDD, absence of functional language
    - ...plus “Y” (other specified) and “Z” (unspecified) modifiers[4][5][6][12][13].
- Digital-native, with extension clusters for clinical and administrative detail.
- *Effective global date for ICD-11*: January 2022, but national insurance/payor practices phase in at varying rates (U.S. transition projected late 2020s)[11][12][13][14].

**Technical Distinction**:  
DSM-5 is clinical-only and requires conversion for claims; ICD-11 merges administrative and clinical coding, with a broader, globally standardized taxonomy. DSM-oriented levels do not translate directly to ICD-11.

## 3. Structured Tables: Side-by-Side Criteria and Coding

### Diagnostic Criteria Summary Table

| Domain | DSM-5 (2013/DSM-5-TR) | ICD-11 (2019) |
|---|---|---|
| **Social-Communication** | Requires **all** 3 enumerated deficits: social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal behaviors, relationships. Numeric threshold enforced. | Requires persistent reciprocal deficits; no fixed subcriteria count. Clinical judgment, context-driven. |
| **Sensory Processing** | Sensory is *one of four* RRBs; at least two RRBs required, sensory feature optional but recognized; strict checklist. | Sensory features are one of several RRB examples; at least one RRB required. Sensory-dominant presentation is sufficient but not required; no numeric weighting. |
| **Severity/Support** | Levels 1–3 (by support need), recorded for both core domains; descriptive labels, not incoded for billing. | Subcodes for IDD and functional language (e.g., 6A02.0–6A02.3); functional description prioritized, no “levels.” |
| **Impairment** | “Clinically significant” functional impairment is mandatory. | “Impairment...sufficiently severe” in key daily domains is mandatory. |
| **Age/Onset** | Early developmental period necessary; can be masked/delayed. | As above, with explicit reference to delayed/masked. |
| **Administrative Coding** | DSM diagnosis for clinical use; crosswalk to ICD-10 for insurance (F84.0, etc.). | ICD-11 code 6A02 (with subcodes); digital transition starts 2022, rolling out internationally. |

## 4. Evidence Synthesis

### Diagnostic Accuracy: Sensitivity/Specificity

- **No direct head-to-head studies of DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 ASD diagnostic performance** have been published as of April 2026; this urgent evidence gap is widely acknowledged[11][15].
- **Closest adjacent evidence:**
    - **DSM-5 vs. DSM-IV-TR:** DSM-5 is more specific (fewer false positives) but less sensitive (misses some who would have met older criteria), especially in “higher functioning” or atypical cases, including some female and minority presentations. Reductions in ASD case identification rates seen up to 68% for certain subgroups under DSM-5 compared to DSM-IV-TR, especially among those previously diagnosed with PDD-NOS[6][16][17].
    - **ICD-11 vs. ICD-10:** ICD-11 relaxes age of onset, merges subtypes, and broadens inclusion (more similar to DSM-5 in structure, but with even greater clinical flexibility). Experts theorize ICD-11 may be more sensitive but potentially less specific due to the lack of symptom-number thresholds[11][18].
    - **Standardized tools (ADOS, ADI-R, CARS):** Pooled sensitivity and specificity data (not specific to DSM/ICD frameworks): Sensitivities generally 0.85–0.91; specificities 0.69–0.94; evidentiary certainty generally low-moderate[19][20].

---

### Test-Retest Reliability

- **No comparative direct studies** (DSM-5 vs. ICD-11) available.
- Test-retest and inter-rater reliabilities have been measured for major tools (ADOS, ADI-R) and are moderate to high in experienced hands, but not specifically linked to DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 diagnoses[19][20].

---

### Clinical Utility

- **Narrative consensus:** DSM-5's clarity and specifics improve communication for specialist clinicians; ICD-11's broader scope aids international standardization, public health, and administrative consistency. However, ICD-11’s greater flexibility increases the risk of diagnostic “overlap” with other conditions, and may decrease the reproducibility of research cohorts[11][21].

---

### GRADE Quality and Subgroup Effects

- **GRADE Summary:**
    - Tool accuracy studies: Moderate quality for ADOS, low for most others.
    - Direct comparison studies (DSM-5/ICD-11): Not available; adjacent data, mostly low-moderate due to retrospective design, secondary analysis, or small/biased samples.
    - Subgroups: Females, minorities, and those with sensory-dominant profiles are at particular risk for under- or overdiagnosis, depending on which set of criteria and assessment tools are used; evidence limited but suggestive[16][17][21].

---

### Evidence Gaps and Open Questions

- No DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 head-to-head diagnostic accuracy or reliability studies available as of April 2026[11][15][18][21].
- Effects of ICD-11 diagnostic flexibility on service access, safety, and accuracy remain theoretical/expert consensus[11][15].
- Impact of diagnostic criteria (both systems) on high-masking, adult, or culturally variant presentations is poorly characterized in empirical research.

## 5. Policy Statements and Organizational Stances

### World Health Organization (WHO)

- Defines ASD under ICD-11; expects member nations to conform by 2022+ for reporting and administrative use[22].
- No explicit language on DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 primacy in guidelines, but ICD-11 is the global reporting standard.
- “Once autism has been diagnosed, children, adolescents and adults with autism and their carers are offered relevant information, services, referrals, and practical support, in accordance with their individual and evolving needs and preferences”[22].

---

### American Psychiatric Association (APA)

- APA is the authorizing body for DSM-5/DSM-5-TR, which remains the primary US diagnostic standard.
- Diagnostic requirement: “all three” social-communication deficits and “at least two of four” RRBs (including sensory according to explicit examples).
- Severity levels required; direct quote: “The 2022 DSM-5-TR revision clarified the phrase ‘manifested by the following’ to ‘as manifested by all of the following’ to improve clarity in diagnostic criteria”[1][2][23][24].

---

### American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

- Emphasizes early and thorough screening, referral, and intervention.
- Supports evidence-based multidisciplinary evaluation, aligning with both DSM-5 and ICD-11 as framework options, but the bulk of clinical guidance references DSM-5 for operational details.
- No explicit policy stance on manual primacy in public material; sensory and severity specifier commentary not prominent post-2022[25].

---

### National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, UK)

- Endorses both DSM-5 and ICD-11 as valid: “An autism diagnosis should be based on criteria listed in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition (DSM-5) or the International Classification of Diseases 11th Revision (ICD-11)”[26][27].
- No primacy given; recommends use of multiple sources of information/clinical judgment.
- Sensory processing and support needs are expected to be assessed where relevant, but no weighting or prescriptive coding offered.

---

### Advocacy Organizations

#### Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

- “ASAN created two policy briefs discussing the proposed revisions to the definition of autism... in setting forth our recommendations for the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria...provided extensive research support for each recommended change”[28].
- Key values: reducing stigma, opposing restrictive/discriminatory diagnosis, and defending service access. No declared formal preference between DSM-5 and ICD-11; opposes “deficit” framing and restrictive or severity-tier-based service rationing[29].

#### National Autistic Society (NAS, UK)

- Accepts diagnoses under either DSM-5 or ICD-11 as valid; no declared primacy.
- Sensory processing “range from 53% to 95%, highlighting its commonality”; occupational therapy-based sensory assessment recommended if significant life impact reported[30].
- Focus is on inclusive access, recognition of lived sensory differences, and multidisciplinary support; no unique policy on severity or category labels.

#### Autism Speaks

- Relies primarily on DSM-5 as the public education and guidance tool (including for severity levels).
- “The DSM-5 revised the symptoms required to make a diagnosis... combining the social and communication domains into a single social-communication domain and adding sensory symptoms”[31][32].
- Offers clear public-facing explanations of all DSM-5 severity levels and sensory features; no explicit statements found about ICD-11 codes/specifications.

## 6. Diagnostic Safety Risks and Coding/Reimbursement Across US and EU

### United States

- **Diagnosis:** DSM-5/DSM-5-TR for clinical assessment and documentation.
- **Billing:** ICD-10-CM codes (primarily F84.0) for insurance reimbursement; DSM-5 severity/support specifiers do not directly translate to claims codes.
- **ICD-11 Not Yet Adopted:** U.S. payers plan for transition late 2020s; dual coding systems (ICD-10/ICD-11) may coexist in transition. Service access continues to rely on ICD-10-CM mappings from DSM diagnoses.
- **Sensory-Profile Impacts:** Sensory features recognized in DSM-5, but not required for claims or coverage. Claims processes remain unchanged until ICD-11 code adoption[9][10][11][13].
- **Confirmed Policy:** No new eligibility guarantees or coverage provisions for sensory-dominant or camouflaged presentations until ICD-11 implementation.

### European Union/UK

- **Diagnosis:** Both DSM-5 and ICD-11 accepted; ICD-11 codes gaining primacy for administrative/data/reporting functions post-2022.
- **Billing:** National Health Service (UK)/regional payers increasingly shift to ICD-11 codes (6A02.x). Functional needs-based eligibility remains standard—documentation of impairment, not diagnostic code alone, is decisive for supports.
- **Region-Specific Variations:** Each nation sets its own effective ICD-11 operational dates; code mapping guides available for service transition[13][14][35].
- **Sensory Features:** May inform functional needs assessment, but not currently weighted for direct entitlement or reimbursement[30][35].

## 7. Summary of Key Distinctions and Remaining Gaps

- **DSM-5:** Rigid numerical requirements (all 3 social-communication, 2 of 4 RRBs), explicit levels of severity, and descriptive specifiers; more precise for research and certain types of clinical communication, but may under-identify atypical or camouflaged individuals.
- **ICD-11:** More flexible, judgment-based approach (no fixed number of social-communication subcriteria, one or more RRBs by example rather than checklist), with administrative specifiers for functional status but no standardized severity levels. Enables broader, potentially more inclusive diagnosis but at the possible cost of diagnostic precision and research replication.
- **Major Empirical Gap:** No direct, prospective, head-to-head studies of DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 for ASD diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, or specificity exist as of 2026. Current projections about over- or underdiagnosis are based on parallel/adjacent evidence and expert opinion.
- **Policy Ambiguity:** Most organizations accept both frameworks. Formal manual “primacy” is rare, except APA (DSM-5 in US) and Autism Speaks (DSM-5 public guidance). Sensory processing is widely recognized in both systems but is not, in itself, a gatekeeper for billing or care access.
- **Administrative Coding:** US, UK, and Europe in transition from ICD-10 to ICD-11, with important region- and payer-specific variations in code recognition and effective dates; DSM codes remain clinical, not billing standards.
- **Diagnostic Safety Risks:** DSM-5 risks missing high-masking or sensory-dominant cases; ICD-11 risks diagnostically “broadening” the spectrum and increasing heterogeneity/false positives.
- **Open Questions:** Whether ICD-11’s greater flexibility will lead to net improvement (better inclusion, less missed diagnosis) or create new challenges (service access, specificity loss) is yet to be resolved by research.
