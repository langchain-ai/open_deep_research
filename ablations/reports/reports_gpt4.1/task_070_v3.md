# Enforceability of Verbal Remote Work Promises under Texas Law: Promissory Estoppel Analysis in the Case of Jordan Hayes

## Introduction

This report analyzes whether Jordan Hayes, a software engineer, can enforce an alleged verbal promise of a fully remote work arrangement made by a hiring manager during April 2024 negotiations with a tech company. The company subsequently issued a written offer letter on May 1, 2024, stipulating a "hybrid schedule per company policy" and including a bolded merger/integration clause. Hayes sold a Seattle home at a loss and relocated after signing and acting on the written offer. Key issues examined include:

- The enforceability of the verbal promise through promissory estoppel under Texas law, especially in light of the subsequent merger clause.
- The impact of the hiring manager's authority as demonstrated by HR records and company policies.
- The legal significance of a post-offer (June 2024) email from the retired manager, and comparator evidence from other engineers who secured formal, written remote work approvals.
- Application of the Texas summary judgment standard to these facts.
- The scope of remedies available under Texas promissory estoppel law in employment disputes.

## Factual Background

- **April 2024:** Hiring manager verbally promises Hayes a fully remote position to induce acceptance.
- **May 1, 2024:** Written offer letter provides for "hybrid schedule per company policy" and boldly states: “This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations.” (Merger/integration clause).
- **Post-May 1, 2024:** Hayes sells home at a $40,000 loss and signs a Texas lease after accepting the written offer.
- **June 2024:** Retired hiring manager sends Hayes an email confirming the earlier remote promise.
- **July 1, 2024:** Company policy is updated and clarified to require three in-office days weekly.
- **Comparators:** Other engineers hired at the same time as Hayes received formal written approvals for fully remote work, as documented in their personnel files.
- **Authority Question:** HR records and employee handbook state that work arrangement exceptions require written executive (not manager) approval.

## Promissory Estoppel Under Texas Law: Elements and Application

### Overview of Promissory Estoppel Elements

Texas courts require four elements to establish promissory estoppel:

1. **A Clear and Definite Promise:** The promise made must be specific and unambiguous.
2. **Foreseeability of Reliance:** The promisor should reasonably expect that the promisee will act in reliance.
3. **Substantial, Reasonable, and Justified Reliance:** The promisee must actually rely on the promise, and such reliance must be reasonable under the circumstances.
4. **Injustice if Not Enforced:** Injustice can only be avoided by enforcement of the promise[1][2][3].

Promissory estoppel is typically used as a defensive doctrine but is recognized as an affirmative theory of relief where detrimental reliance is shown[1].

### Effect of Written Offer Letter and Merger Clause

Texas law gives strong effect to written contracts containing a merger or integration clause, meaning that all prior and contemporaneous oral promises are "merged" into the final agreement. Such clauses generally bar any claim based on prior inconsistent oral representations, unless there is evidence of fraud or inequitable conduct[1][2][4]. 

- **Case Law:** In *Sonny Ellen v. F.H. Partners, LLC*, the court held, “It was not reasonable or justifiable...to rely on the oral ‘promise’ because the [contract] addressed the substance...and specifically prohibited oral modification”[2].
- Similarly, *Barkley v. Connally* warned, “Courts presume all prior oral and written agreements are merged into a subsequent written contract, and a merger clause embodies this doctrine”[4].

**Application to Hayes:**
- Hayes signed and acted upon a written offer explicitly contradicting any full-remote promise and including a bold integration clause.
- Texas courts routinely hold that reliance on a prior oral promise, after receipt of a written contract that addresses the same term and contains a merger clause, is unreasonable and unjustified as a matter of law[1][2][4].

### Authority of the Hiring Manager: HR Records and Company Policy Evidence

- The company's employee handbook and HR policy state: "Hybrid and remote work schedule exceptions must be approved in writing by executive leadership (Director-level or above). Managers may recommend such exceptions, but approval authority rests exclusively with Company Executive Leadership."
- HR onboarding checklists for 2024 indicate exception requests must be in writing and routed through HR, with signature lines for Director approval.
- The hiring manager is not on the list of individuals authorized to approve deviations from standard work schedules.

**Legal Effect:** Under Texas law, only promises made by an agent with actual or apparent authority bind the company[3][5]. Apparent authority requires not merely the representation by the employee, but also conduct by the company that leads the promisee to reasonably believe such authority exists. Here, Hayes had access to written company policy spelling out the authority structure and the offer letter required adherence to company policy, extinguishing any reasonable belief that a non-executive manager could unilaterally bind the company to a non-standard arrangement[3].

### Comparators: Other Engineers’ Employment Terms

- HR records from the same hiring tranche show that two other engineers received written exceptions, signed by a Director and logged in their personnel files.
- There is no record of Hayes seeking or being given written executive-level approval for remote work, nor did Hayes request "remote" language inserted into the final offer.

**Legal Implication:** Where others with similar job titles and circumstances secure remote work via formal written process, a sophisticated employee such as Hayes is chargeable with notice of the necessity for documented approval. This further undercuts any claim that Hayes could reasonably rely on a mere oral assurance, especially post-receipt of a contradicting, integrated contract[3][5].

### The June 2024 Manager Confirmation Email

- The retired manager sent Hayes an email—after Hayes had already signed and acted on the written offer—purporting to "confirm our prior agreement on full remote work."
- There is no record in company files, nor policy exception or approval, associated with this email.

**Texas Law:** A post-contract statement by a manager lacking actual or apparent authority cannot override an integrated contract[2][4][5]. Under Texas evidentiary rules, such a "confirmation" is generally inadmissible to contradict the final written agreement unless it evidences either:
  - Ongoing contract negotiations after execution (not present here), or
  - Fraud in procuring the written agreement (not alleged here).

The June email may be relevant as parol evidence of intent, but under Texas law, merger clauses bar such evidence unless ambiguity exists in the written terms[1][2][4]. Without evidence that Hayes was misled as to the manager’s authority, the email is not likely to affect the legal analysis.

### Ambiguity: "Hybrid Schedule Per Company Policy"

- The offer letter's phrase "hybrid schedule per company policy" is undefined, but company policies (as of May 2024) required office attendance for certain roles with exceptions available only by written Director approval.
- If ambiguity existed, Texas courts may allow parol evidence as to intent but—where company policy and contemporaneous exceptions for others required written approval and no such writing exists—summary judgment for the employer is generally appropriate[4][5]. In rare cases, clear and compelling evidence of ambiguity and reliance may create a factual issue for trial, but Hayes's admissions and the offer's terms make this outcome unlikely.

## Texas Summary Judgment Standards Applied to Promissory Estoppel in this Context

- **Procedural Standards:** Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a, summary judgment should issue where "there is no genuine issue as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law"[6][7].
- **Controlling Application:** In contract cases involving a merger clause and a written offer contradicting the oral promise, Texas courts grant summary judgment for the employer absent evidence of ambiguity or fraud[1][2][4][5].

**Result:** Hayes, having taken all reliance actions after clearly accepting a written offer letter that superseded all prior negotiations and deviated from the manager’s oral promise, is unable to present evidence of reasonable or justified reliance. This defeats an essential element of promissory estoppel as a matter of law and justifies summary judgment for the employer[1][2][4][5][6][7].

## Remedies for Promissory Estoppel in Texas Employment Law

- **Damages Recoverable:** Texas law allows promissory estoppel plaintiffs to recover only actual, out-of-pocket reliance damages—such as relocation costs, penalties from breaking leases, or direct losses from acting on the employer’s promise[8][9]. Future earnings or specific employment performance (such as an injunction compelling a remote assignment) are not available.
- **No Right to Job or Specific Performance:** Texas courts have emphasized that promissory estoppel "is not a vehicle to create or enforce employment rights otherwise unavailable at law." Damages are limited to those necessary to restore the plaintiff to their pre-reliance position[8][9].

**Application:** Even if Hayes could prevail on promissory estoppel (which is highly unlikely), these rules would bar any order compelling the employer to provide remote work. At best, Hayes could seek reimbursement for the $40,000 home sale loss and other direct moving-related damages, if such reliance were deemed reasonable (which, as explained, is very doubtful under these facts).

## Treatment of Ambiguous or Open Facts

- If evidence suggested discriminatory or inconsistent application of remote work policy based on protected characteristics, Hayes might have a separate claim under anti-discrimination law, not directly under promissory estoppel[3][5].
- If ambiguous language or company communications credibly suggested that “hybrid schedule” could include full remote work under the then-current policy, there may be a viable issue of fact for trial. However, the documentary evidence (written policy, comparator approvals, HR directives) weighs heavily against this argument in Hayes’s case.
- If Hayes could produce credible evidence that the company or its agents created a reasonable appearance of the hiring manager’s authority, this could weigh against summary judgment. Yet, clear policy and HR documentation undermine such a showing.

## Conclusion

Under controlling Texas law, Hayes cannot prevail on a promissory estoppel claim to enforce the verbal promise of full remote work. The key factors are:

- Hayes accepted a written offer letter that (1) contradicted the alleged oral promise, (2) explicitly included a broad merger/integration clause, and (3) was accompanied by company policies clearly requiring executive-level written approval for remote arrangements.
- All of Hayes’s detrimental reliance (sale of home, relocation) occurred after receiving and acting on the written offer, making such reliance unreasonable and unjustified as a matter of law.
- The hiring manager lacked actual or apparent authority to unilaterally bind the company to a remote exception, as shown by written company policies and HR practices.
- Comparator evidence (other engineers getting written approvals) underscores the necessity of following formal procedures.
- The June confirmation email post-dates the final contract and cannot override the written offer’s terms or company approval requirements.
- No remedy greater than actual out-of-pocket losses (not specific performance) is permitted under Texas law; but even reliance damages are unavailable where reliance is unreasonable or unjustified.

Accordingly, summary judgment for the employer is strongly supported by the record and by controlling Texas case law and statutes.
