# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Health Outcomes and Prenatal Care Access Among Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Introduction

This report compares maternal health outcomes and access to prenatal care among Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand, over the period 2017–2023. The focus is on four core indicators: completion rates of 4+ antenatal care (ANC) visits, skilled birth attendance percentages, incidence of postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), and neonatal mortality within the first 28 days of life. These outcomes are analyzed against the backdrop of varying healthcare delivery models—NGO-operated field hospitals in Cox’s Bazar versus Thai Ministry of Public Health integration in Mae La—and contextual factors such as midwife-to-population ratios, emergency obstetric care (EmONC) proximity, and systems for cultural mediation (especially support for traditional birth attendants, TBAs). The effects of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction on the continuity of maternal health services are also critically assessed.

---

## Maternal Health Indicators: Cox’s Bazar (Rohingya Refugees)

### Antenatal Care (4+ Visits)

- Recent data (2023) indicates that 63% of pregnant Rohingya refugees attended four or more ANC visits; a sharp improvement over previous years, but with variability across camps. For example, a 2019–2020 survey in Camp 4 found 71.6% completion of 4+ ANC visits[1][2][3].
- Average ANC visits per mother was 6.9 in 2023, reflecting increased engagement with health services, likely attributable to service expansion and outreach efforts[2][4].

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- In 2023, 82–85% of deliveries among Rohingya women took place in facilities, with more than 80% of births attended by skilled health professionals—an increase from prior years[1][2].
- Formal skilled birth attendance was reported at 69.4% in 2019–2020; more recent figures likely underrepresent home-based skilled attendance due to reporting challenges[3].

### Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence

- PPH is a significant contributor to maternal mortality: in 2021, 29.2% of maternal deaths among Rohingya women were due to obstetric hemorrhage[5][6].
- Facility-based mortality audits confirm PPH and hypertensive disorders as major direct causes of maternal deaths.

### Neonatal Mortality (<28 Days)

- Neonatal mortality rate (NMR) in 2017–18 stood at 27.0/1,000 live births. Most neonatal deaths (76.3%) and stillbirths (72.1%) occurred at home or in the community rather than health facilities[4].
- There has been little evidence of decline in NMR, underscoring persistent weaknesses in both care-seeking behavior and the health system.

### Maternal Mortality

- The maternal mortality rate (MMR) reached 295 per 100,000 live births in 2023, rising alarmingly to 446 per 100,000 by early 2024. Key drivers include delays in seeking care, transport, quality of facility care, and blood shortages[14][5].

## Maternal Health Indicators: Mae La Camp (Karen Refugees, Thailand)

### Antenatal Care (4+ Visits)

- ANC completion is well above international standards. More than 90% of pregnant women attended four or more ANC visits from 2017–2023; 91% coverage was specifically reported in Mae La in 2018[15][8].
- Sustained high coverage is linked to effective integration with the Thai public health system and continuous NGO engagement.

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- Skilled birth attendance in Mae La was reported at 95% in 2018, with c-section rates at 13%[15].
- Over 15,000 births (2008–2015) analyzed showed skilled birth attendance above 80%, attributed to effective training and professionalization of local Karen and Burman women[13][14].

### Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence

- Direct Mae La-specific PPH data is sparse. However, Thai hospital rates ranged from 1.4% to 10.6%[16].
- Higher PPH incidence is correlated with low nurse-midwife-to-patient ratios and lack of experienced staff—challenges that Mae La has partially overcome with focused staffing/training[16].

### Neonatal Mortality (<28 Days)

- NMR in Mae La dropped by 51% (from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births) between the early 2000s and 2018, reflecting significant improvement relative to Cox’s Bazar[12][8].
- Specialized care units (such as Special Care Baby Units) further reduced mortality in preterm infants[11][12].

### Maternal Mortality

- No maternal deaths were reported in Mae La in 2018; data for subsequent years remains favorable and well below Cox’s Bazar rates[15][8].

## Comparative Analysis: Service Models and Determinants

### Healthcare Delivery Models

**Cox’s Bazar**
- Services primarily delivered by a mix of international/local NGOs and UN agencies, with multiple parallel providers. Key modalities include midwife-led birthing centers, field hospitals, and a recently opened EmONC-capable maternal hospital[7][10].
- Referral systems exist, but coverage is uneven; many camps are beyond the 2-hour transport threshold for EmONC.
- Doctor-to-population ratio is low (~1:40,000); midwife shortages and high turnover persist[9].

**Mae La**
- Hybridized system with Thai MOH integration: clinics/nurses trained via NGOs (especially Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU) and Mae Tao Clinic) and formal referral agreements for obstetric emergencies[17][23].
- Consistently high nurse and community health worker coverage: 3 doctors, 72 nurses, 64 community health workers for ~39,000 refugees[15].
- EmONC available via referral; c-section rate at 13% and robust neonatal care through SCBU units[15][11].

### Midwife-to-Population Ratios

- **Cox’s Bazar:** 283 active midwives for >900,000 refugees—suboptimal, with periodic training and high attrition[2].
- **Mae La:** Nurse-midwife staffing more stable; training of locals as skilled birth attendants has led to high service coverage, minimizing gaps even under staffing pressure[13][15].

### Emergency Obstetric Care (EmONC) Proximity

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Many camps lack consistent 2-hour access. Efforts since 2023 expanded EmONC capacity (e.g., Teknaf CEmONC center), but transport insecurity and logistics remain severe constraints[8][7].
- **Mae La:** Reliable EmONC via established referral pipelines to local Thai hospitals for emergencies; logistical challenges persist but are less acute than in Cox’s Bazar, partly due to better infrastructure and the insurance innovations for undocumented patients[23][28].

### Cultural Mediation and Support for TBAs

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Low trust in formal systems—many women prefer home births with TBAs due to privacy, fears about male staff, cultural tradition, and security. Ongoing efforts to implement respectful, language-appropriate care and train TBAs are underway but uptake is variable[11].
- **Mae La:** Deep integration of traditional practices within modern care; training local TBAs as skilled birth attendants has led to increased facility births, high trust, and improved maternal/neonatal outcomes[13][14]. Culturally appropriate care is a referenced best practice.

## The Impact of the 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction

### Cox’s Bazar

- The 2021–2023 period saw a sharp funding decline, leading to phased reductions in outreach, staff layoffs, and decreased continuity of certain services (notably PNC—a 39% drop in visits in 2023)[2]. Maternal mortality rates rose sharply in early 2024.
- Capacity building for midwives and facility expansion slowed, compounding social and security barriers. Approximately 84 maternal deaths and 304 perinatal deaths were reported in 2023, with higher numbers in subsequent months as service gaps widened[14][2].
- The need for government integration and domestic resource mobilization has become acute as international aid continues to contract[13].

### Mae La

- Dramatic 2025 cuts (83% US aid reduction) and earlier UNFPA funding losses led to layoffs, clinic closures, and transition of care responsibility to under-resourced Thai facilities[22][25][26][1].
- At-risk populations lost access to specialized care; stockouts and cutbacks to chronic medications became common. Nonetheless, the legacy of Thai MOH integration and effective local training buffered the most catastrophic impacts, with core maternal and neonatal services (ANC, SBA, EmONC) largely preserved into late 2025—albeit with severe strains[1][22].

---

## Social and Structural Barriers

- **Cox’s Bazar:** High security risks, cultural conservatism restricting women’s mobility, and distrust of NGO staff combine with difficult terrain, hampering timely access to care, especially at night. Many deaths fall into the “Three Delays” model: delays in decision to seek, reach, and receive adequate care[5][6][15].
- **Mae La:** Structural poverty, legal uncertainty for residents, insurance barriers for the undocumented, and social exclusion persist. However, decades-long engagement and focused training/advocacy mitigated the worst access gaps.

---

## Conclusion

Across all measured maternal health outcomes, Mae La camp consistently outperforms Cox’s Bazar, with higher ANC completion, high rates of skilled birth attendance, lower PPH and neonatal mortality, and markedly lower maternal mortality rates. These differences are closely tied to delivery model structure: consistent integration with the host country’s public health system and successful cultural adaptation (particularly through training local women as skilled birth attendants and maintaining strong referral pathways) have dramatically improved outcomes in Mae La.

Cox’s Bazar, despite substantial improvements since 2017, continues to face systemic challenges: parallel NGO delivery models, workforce turnover, insufficient EmONC proximity, and cultural barriers limit progress. The 2021 UNFPA funding cut affected both sites, but the consequences were more severe in Bangladesh, given the heavier reliance on external aid for basic service continuity.

For both settings, expanding culturally sensitive, community-led approaches, strengthening government integration, protecting skilled midwife pipelines, and restoring predictable funding are essential to further close the maternal and neonatal mortality gap for these highly vulnerable populations.
