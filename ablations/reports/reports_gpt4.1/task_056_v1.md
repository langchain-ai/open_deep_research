# Comprehensive Analysis of the New Zealand Construction Metalworks (Structural Steel Fabrication) Market (2018–2024)

## Overview

This report delivers an in-depth analysis of New Zealand's construction metalworks market, focusing exclusively on structural steel fabrication for construction projects as of 2024. It covers market sizing and growth (2018–2024), leading fabricators’ rankings, applicable import tariffs, key infrastructure projects with steel demand, and the primary strategic risks and opportunities now shaping the market. All sections are supported by authoritative sources.

---

## 1. Market Size and Growth Metrics (2018–2024)

### Revenue and Industry Value

- The structural steel fabrication market size was approximately NZD 1.8 billion in both 2025 and 2026, having declined by 6.0% in 2025 but expected to stabilize thereafter. Growth over the 2021–2026 period is modest, with a projected CAGR of about 2.1%[1].
- Industry-specific studies put the 2024 market revenue at USD 50.3 million, and forecasted to reach USD 68.5 million by 2030 (CAGR ~6% from 2025 onward)[2].

### Employment Figures

- There were about 4,100–5,000 people directly employed in the sector as of 2025, across roughly 153 fabrication companies. Employment had been on a declining trend post-COVID, dropping by nearly 9% between 2020 and 2025, but is projected to stabilize near 4,000 by 2026[3][4].
- Broader estimates (including downstream effects) show over 10,000 direct FTE jobs, with linked industries adding up to 28,000 more[5].

### Tonnage of Structural Steel Fabricated

- Annual fabrication capacity was around 120,000 tonnes in 2023, with typical annual output for construction projects estimated at 100,000 tonnes. By late 2023, reported capacity reached 150,000 tonnes, indicating investment in new facilities and readiness for increased demand[1][4][6].
- Spare capacity varied year to year (spare: 31–47% between 2020–2024), underlining the industry’s resilience and ability to absorb project surges or delays[1][7].

### Market Characteristics

- Structural steel enjoys over 50% market share in multilevel construction nationwide; more than 80% in Christchurch, driven by seismic requirements[1][8].
- Sector quality is high, with 92.5% of output from SFC-certified fabricators and strong investments in sustainability and workforce development[1][9].

**Data Constraints:**  
While estimates for total employment, tonnage, and revenue are robust, year-on-year breakdowns are limited, with most sources providing annual snapshots rather than detailed historical time series.

## 3. New Zealand Import Tariff Rates for Raw Steel (HS 7207–7216) and Margin Impacts

### Tariff Rates (2024)

- For HS codes 7207–7216 (raw and semi-finished steel products), the *standard New Zealand import tariff rates are 0% (duty free) to 5%*, with the overwhelming majority of construction-grade inputs now duty free due to trade liberalization[16][17][18].
- Preferential tariff rates (often 0%) apply under FTAs with Australia, China, ASEAN, and others[19].
- The [Working Tariff Document][16] is the official reference, regularly updated. Section “C” of the WTD covers all active steel categories.

### Margin Impacts

- Tariff costs are *not a significant direct factor* for local fabricators’ margins in 2024. Most key inputs are imported at zero tariff, and nominal 5% tariffs are rare. However, fabricators remain indirectly exposed to global trade shifts, shipping costs, and occasional external tariff impositions (such as US Section 232 on exports)[20][21][22].
- Fluctuations in global freight rates and exchange rates have a *larger impact* than New Zealand’s import tariffs themselves[22].

**Key References:**  
[Tariffs - NZ Customs][16]; [MBIE - Tariffs in New Zealand][18]; [WTO Tariff Profile][19]; [IBISWorld Industry Analysis][20]; [RBNZ Analytical Note][22]

## 4. Major Infrastructure Projects: Steel Demand and Timelines

### 1. City Rail Link (CRL), Auckland

- **Description:** NZ’s largest-ever transport infrastructure project — 3.45km underground twin rail tunnels under Auckland CBD, two new stations, full line upgrade.
- **Steel Demand:** ~4,000 tonnes of structural steel (plus 25,000 tonnes of reinforcing/other steel)[23][24]
- **Timeline:** Construction completion targeted for December 2025; operational launch mid-2026[25][26]
- **Official Source:** [WSP Project Description][23], [City Rail Link Ltd][25], [Auckland Transport][26]

### 2. Transmission Gully Motorway, Wellington

- **Description:** $1.25 billion, 27km motorway with 25 major bridges; significant steel used for bridges (Te Ara a Toa: 230m span, 60m high)
- **Steel Demand:** Exact tonnage not formally published, but engineering accounts confirm a major structural steel component, likely in the low thousands of tonnes[27][28][29]
- **Timeline:** Construction 2016–2022; opened March 2022[30]

### 3. Drury and Paerātā Railway Stations, Auckland

- **Description:** New southern Auckland stations (Drury Central, Drury West, Paerātā) as part of electrification and rail network modernization; includes steel overbridges and platform canopies
- **Steel Demand:** Quantified steel not fully published; confirmed installation of large (up to 19m, 26-tonne) steel beams for overbridges; substantial steel built into station structures[31][32]
- **Timeline:** 50% complete as of August 2025; scheduled opening mid-2026[31]

## 5. Strategic Risks and Opportunities for Structural Steel Fabricators (2024)

### 1. Project Pipeline & Economic Uncertainty

- Consent volumes and project deferrals highlight a weak pipeline — residential consents down 22% YOY (to July 2024), non-residential down 12%. Insolvencies rose 37% (to May 2025); reliance on public sector investment is acute[33][34].
- Despite this, fabricators have ~28% spare capacity and the capability to fulfill new demand, but face “wait-and-see” sentiment and cautious private investment[35].

### 2. Supply Chain Concentration & Tariff/Import Exposure

- Heavy dependence on imported raw steel makes the sector *vulnerable to logistics shocks*. Shipping disruptions (e.g., the Red Sea crisis), rising freight, and global trade tensions continue to challenge procurement[36][37].
- Direct NZ import tariffs are low, but exporters are exposed to foreign tariffs. For example, US tariffs (Section 232) on NZ steel/iron exports reached 53% in 2025 (combined), cutting competitiveness by c. NZ$1.35 billion/yr for affected products[21][22].

### 3. Decarbonization & Green Steel

- Regulators and clients increasingly demand emissions reporting and lower-carbon steel. Investments in Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) technology by NZ Steel, and new capacity (e.g., National Green Steel plant at Hampton Downs), boost supply of low-emission steel and reduce import reliance[38][39][40].
- New Zealand's steel sector is a *global leader in recycling* (95% of steel waste), positioning it for forthcoming green construction mandates.

### 4. Technology and Skills

- Automation and digitalization underpin sector productivity; ongoing skills shortages still challenge growth, particularly as wage differentials encourage outmigration to Australia[41][42].
- 10%+ of workforce is in technical training/apprenticeships, but net staff numbers remain roughly flat.

### 5. Industry Collaboration & Certification

- Market cooperation among SFC-accredited fabricators, combined with early contractor involvement practices, have improved delivery, reduced defects, and bolstered local industry’s reputation[1][4][6].
- Opportunities exist in furthering prefabrication and offsite manufacturing for time/cost savings.
