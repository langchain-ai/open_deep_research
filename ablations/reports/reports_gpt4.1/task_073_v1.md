# Comprehensive Comparison of Autism Diagnosis Criteria in DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (2019)—Focus on Sensory Processing Differences

## Introduction

A clear, evidence-based understanding of how autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is diagnosed—particularly regarding the definition and weighting of sensory processing differences—is crucial for clinical practice, research, policy, and advocacy. The DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (2019) are the principal diagnostic manuals used worldwide. This analysis provides a detailed, structured comparison across clinical criteria, research evidence, guideline stances, advocacy perspectives, diagnostic safety risks, and coding/reimbursement realities, with comprehensive citations.

## 2. Evidence Synthesis: Peer-Reviewed Research Comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11

**A. Review-Level and Expert Commentaries**

- **Kamp-Becker (2024): Critical Review of ICD-11’s Impact**
  - Study Type: Expert narrative review
  - ICD-11’s broad and flexible criteria allow for a wide variety of symptom combinations, increasing inclusivity but at the risk of diagnosis imprecision and false positives.
  - Clinical utility is questioned due to vagueness and overlap with other disorders.
  - Risk: Reduced specificity may increase prevalence rates and non-specific treatments.
  - **GRADE: Narrative review (not assignable)**
  - [13]

- **Greaves-Lord, Skuse, Mandy (2023): Review of ICD-11 Innovations**
  - Broader behavioral criteria, explicit inclusion of sensory features, more emphasis on clinical flexibility.
  - ICD-11 designed to facilitate individualized and contextually sensitive diagnosis and care.
  - **GRADE: Narrative review**
  - [14]

- **Alvarez & Fernandez (2024): Clinical Comparative Review**
  - ICD-11 more inclusive, especially for underdiagnosed groups (females, later-life presentations).
  - Designed for broader healthcare system adaptability across cultures and resources.
  - **GRADE: Practice summary**
  - [15]

**B. Empirical Studies**

- **Blázquez Hinojosa et al. (2021): Sensitivity/Specificity of DSM-5**
  - Retrospective review, N=118 Spanish children/adolescents.
  - DSM-5 sensitivity: 0.69–1.00 (higher in females); specificity: 0.64–0.73.
  - 83% of “autistic disorder” cases retained ASD diagnosis, but only 35% for PDD-NOS (many shifted to social communication disorder).
  - Danger of some profiles (esp. milder or social-communication predominant) falling outside ASD but having significant needs.
  - **GRADE: Moderate** (methodological limitations: modest sample, retrospective)
  - [16]

- **McPartland, Reichow & Volkmar (2012): Sensitivity/Specificity of Proposed DSM-5**
  - Secondary analysis, N=933 (657 ASD, 276 non-ASD).
  - DSM-5: Sensitivity 60.6%, specificity 94.9%.
  - Sensitivity lower for Asperger’s (25%), PDD-NOS (28%), and those with higher IQ.
  - Implication: New criteria more specific but may exclude higher-functioning/milder autistic individuals.
  - **GRADE: Moderate-to-high** (robust dataset)
  - [17]

## 3. Official Clinical Guidelines: DSM-5 vs ICD-11 and Sensory Processing Weighting

### World Health Organization (WHO)
- ICD-11 is the official, globally endorsed autism diagnostic system.
- ASD diagnosis under ICD-11 requires persistent deficits in social communication AND restricted/repetitive behaviors, with explicit mention of sensory sensitivities as diagnostic.
- ICD-11 intended to enable consistent, inclusive diagnosis internationally and is designed to map easily onto service/resource planning.
- [18][19][20]

### American Psychiatric Association (APA)
- APA’s clinical recommendations use the DSM-5-TR framework.
- Sensory symptoms are included equally alongside motor/repetitive criteria; APA does not provide special weighting to sensory features, nor endorse ICD-11.
- No formal policy statement found on ICD-11 adoption for ASD diagnosis.
- [21]

### American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)
- Recommends recognizing sensory processing challenges as core ASD features in both DSM-5 and ICD-11.
- Advocates expert, multidisciplinary evaluation using current DSM/ICD standards.
- No explicit guidance on whether sensory processing should be weighted above other diagnostic features.
- [22][23]

### National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, UK)
- Endorses both ICD-11 and DSM-5 as valid frameworks for autism diagnosis in UK practice.
- Multidisciplinary, person-centered, and holistic assessment is best practice; unusual sensory sensitivities should be systematically assessed but are not weighted more strongly than core social/communicative and repetitive behavior deficits.
- Guideline language updated to use “autistic people,” reflecting neurodiversity-affirming trends.
- [24][25][26]

## 4. Advocacy Organization Positions: Neurodiversity-Affirming Evaluation

### Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)
- Advocated strongly for DSM-5 to feature a unified diagnosis (not subtypes), recognition of masking/compensation, and inclusion of sensory sensitivity.
- Successfully influenced DSM-5 language to be more inclusive of those masking traits, caution against severity scales as service gatekeeping, and highlight diversity of presentations.
- Still critiques both manuals for possible underdiagnosis of marginalized populations (esp. females, minorities) and for not lowering the social-communication threshold.
- Pushes for explicit alignment of diagnostic standards with lived autistic experiences and inclusivity.
- [27][28][29][30]

### National Autistic Society (NAS)
- Supports both DSM-5 and ICD-11’s explicit recognition of sensory processing differences.
- Emphasizes the real-world impact of sensory issues and the importance of assessment; calls for more clinical validation of sensory needs.
- Notes that Sensory Processing Disorder is not a standalone diagnosis, potentially leading to missed or misunderstood support for those with strong sensory features alone.
- [31][32]

### Autism Speaks
- Recognizes and publicizes the inclusion of sensory criteria in DSM-5, including its implications for early identification and service eligibility.
- Highlights DSM-5 (and now ICD-11) as authoritative guides but does not directly compare them for neurodiversity-affirming content.
- Notes that diagnosis should open doors to adaptive support, not limit it based on severity levels.
- Does not issue explicit position statement on which manual is more neurodiversity-affirming.
- [33][34]

## 5. Diagnostic Safety Risks: Misdiagnosis, Over-/Underdiagnosis, and Inappropriate Interventions

- **Over- and under-diagnosis are real concerns with both DSM-5 and ICD-11, especially in presentations dominated by sensory symptoms:**
  - Broad, flexible ICD-11 criteria may increase false positive rates due to overlapping and subjective symptom descriptions (“loss of specificity”).
  - DSM-5’s focus on fixed domain checklist and specificity may exclude those with less typical, later-manifesting, or masked presentations—especially in higher-IQ individuals, girls/women, and those from minority backgrounds.
  - Both manuals risk under-recognition of individuals whose most severe symptoms are sensory, because core diagnostic emphasis remains on social and behavioral features.
- **Diagnostic safety risks raised in research and major reviews:**
  - Systematic barriers in underserved groups result in delayed or missed diagnoses due to both provider and system-level factors (e.g., health literacy, cultural translation of symptom presentation, insurance barriers) [35].
  - Diagnostic overlap with conditions such as ADHD, social anxiety, and especially personality disorders (ICD-11 notes explicit overlap in adolescent females) can lead to mislabeling and inappropriate treatment pathways [36].
  - Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) is still not recognized as a distinct entity; thus, individuals with primarily sensory-driven presentations may be misdiagnosed or denied appropriate services [31][37].
  - Overdiagnosis concern: Broadened awareness and increased screening may pathologize normal variance (especially in children); however, underdiagnosis remains a more prominent risk for ‘less stereotypical’ or camouflaged autistic profiles [38].
- **Both recommendation and critique:**
  - Need for comprehensive, multidisciplinary assessment considering compensatory strategies, masking, sex/gender differences, non-verbal presentations, and cultural norms.
  - Strong need for enhanced clinician training and greater flexibility in recognizing ‘atypical’ but impactful autistic presentations, especially those centered on sensory distress [39][40].

## 6. Coding and Reimbursement: US and EU (ICD-10-CM, ICD-11)

### United States

- **Coding:**
  - As of 2026, ICD-10-CM code F84.0 is the mandatory code for autism spectrum disorder (including “childhood autism,” “atypical autism,” “Asperger’s”).
  - DSM-5 diagnosis (clinical) is usually mapped to ICD-10-CM code for billing/insurance purposes.
  - ICD-11 is not yet adopted for billing or eligibility determination; transition is anticipated to begin late 2020s, requiring multi-year preparation.
  - Only 24% of ICD-10-CM codes map directly to ICD-11, meaning future changes will require cluster-coding and adaptation in billing, EHRs, and payer systems [41][42][43][44][45][46].

- **Reimbursement/Impact:**
  - All insurance claims, federal/state mandates, and payer coverage practices remain ICD-10-CM based, including for ASD.
  - Major insurers (including Medicaid, Medicare, and private) require F84.0 for ASD-related service billing and may request documentation of DSM-5 criteria.
  - The transition to ICD-11 will eventually enable more granular coding and possibly streamlined claims, but requires major infrastructure and guideline overhaul; dual-coding is expected during the shift.
  - Until ICD-11 becomes official, there is no impact on coverage or reimbursement for autism services.

### European Union

- **Coding:**
  - ICD-11 is being promoted for adoption along with DSM-5 for standardized diagnosis and health data harmonization.
  - National policy varies: some EU countries are transitioning to ICD-11, others use ICD-10 or hybrid forms.
  - Financial qualification for benefits/supports relies on demonstration of functional impact and support needs, not solely ICD code.
  - EU-wide stance is for ASD service eligibility and accommodations to be “needs based,” often determined through multidisciplinary functional assessments [47][48][49][50][51][52].

- **Reimbursement/Impact:**
  - ICD-11 is expected to improve consistency in diagnosis documentation and facilitate access to cross-border and national supports, but national policies (not ICD code alone) continue to determine eligibility.
  - Consistency in data, claims, and health system comparability are main anticipated advantages of ICD-11.
  - Service/resource access in most EU countries depends on documented functional impairment, guide by team assessment, not just ICD/DSM label.

## Conclusion

DSM-5 and ICD-11 represent major evolutions in autism diagnosis, both including sensory processing as a critical diagnostic domain—a key neurodiversity-affirming step advocated by autistic-led organizations and clinical experts. They differ in detail, structure, flexibility, and system intent:

- **DSM-5** is checklist-driven, with rigid severity levels and high specificity, but may exclude subtle/atypical cases (notably women, minorities, masked traits, or those with primarily sensory features).
- **ICD-11** is broader and more flexible, designed for a range of global cultural and healthcare scenarios, explicitly lists sensory features, and intends to foster individualized, context-sensitive diagnosis—though this can risk over-inclusiveness or loss of diagnostic precision.

Both manuals require functional impairment for a clinical diagnosis, but the processes for assigning severity, recognition of intellectual/language impairment, and emphasis on lived experience can vary. There is strong consensus from advocacy and research communities for greater inclusivity, ongoing revision for diversity in presentations, and nuanced recognition of sensory processing differences. Coding and reimbursement remain ICD-10 based in the US; the EU is moving towards ICD-11 but maintains eligibility as function-based.

For best clinical and policy practice, multidisciplinary, neurodiversity-affirming assessment—integrating but not limited to current manual criteria—is strongly recommended.
