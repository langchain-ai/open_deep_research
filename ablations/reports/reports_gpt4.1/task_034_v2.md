# Comparative Analysis of Vertical Integration, Traceability, and EUDR Compliance Strategies: Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi), 2018–2024

## Executive Summary

Between 2018 and 2024, Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) solidified their positions as the top global cocoa processors through expanded vertical integration, origin-country investments, and major advances in supply chain traceability and sustainability. These developments were directly shaped by the emergence of the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), customer demands from premium chocolate manufacturers, and regulatory and social pressure to improve farmer incomes and environmental standards. This report provides a granular, year-on-year comparison of each company's vertical integration and compliance capabilities, with explicit reference to primary regulatory milestones and third-party research. Data gaps, definitional inconsistencies, and methodological differences are flagged to support a true like-for-like analysis.

---

## 1. EUDR Regulatory Milestones: Cocoa Sector Application and Company Compliance

### Entry-into-Force and Enforcement Timeline

- **Regulation (EU) 2023/1115** (“EUDR”) entered into force on **29 June 2023**, setting rules to ban products (including cocoa) linked to post–31 December 2020 deforestation or forest degradation from the EU market and requiring full traceability to farm plot level[1].
- **Original application date:** Most operator obligations, including due diligence, traceability, and geolocation requirements for cocoa, were to apply from **30 December 2024**[1][2].
- **Revised timeline:** Regulation (EU) 2024/3234, approved on 19 December 2024, postponed the majority of operator and trader obligations by one year:
  - **Main enforcement date:** **30 December 2025** for large and medium-size companies
  - **Small and micro-enterprises:** Additional six months postponement, to **30 June 2026**[2][3][4][5]
- Companies’ public compliance claims remain **provisional**: Per the European Commission and sector notices, legal obligations will not be fully in effect until these revised deadlines, though preparations, traceability build-out, and reporting systems are already being implemented in anticipation[3][4][6].
- No official additional amendments or postponements beyond these have been enacted as of April 2026; further delays have only been discussed at the proposal stage[3][7].

## 2. Vertical Integration, Sourcing, Investments, and Traceability (2018–2024)

### Measurement and Data Caveats
- **Direct Sourcing:** Companies vary in definitions (direct to farm/cooperative, to first local aggregator, or to licensed buying company—key in Ghana).
- **CapEx Allocations:** Public reporting rarely details year-by-year and country-level CapEx for processing/traceability in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana.
- **Traceability:** Most data aggregates “direct supply chain” mapping; “indirect” or “broker” supply chains have much lower traceability, and percentages are not always segment-specific.
- **Farmer Income:** Living income/poverty metrics diverge (World Bank $1.90, Fairtrade LIRP, company-specific, or national lines), and source of analysis (company or independent) varies.
- **Gaps:** Where data is incomplete or non-comparable, this is explicitly stated.

---

### 2.1 Barry Callebaut

#### (a) Direct Sourcing Ratios (Year-by-Year, by Geography)

- **2018/19:** 229,142 cocoa farmers in mapping/census (global)[8].
- **2019:** 167,000+ Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire farmers in sustainability programs; mapping 100% of direct supply chain farms in both countries declared by year-end[8][9][10].
- **2024/25:** Over 1.5 million cocoa farms mapped globally; 100% traceability from farm to warehouse for direct supply chain in Ghana, and farm mapping (barcode/RFID pilots) scaling in Côte d’Ivoire[11][12].
- Precise annual % of companywide cocoa direct-sourced from farm vs intermediary not published by geography or year, but direct programs dominate in Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana for sustainable sourcing[13].
- **Comparability note:** Definitions differ regionally—Ghana’s system routes all purchases through COCOBOD/LBCs vs more direct farm/co-op deals elsewhere[9][14].

#### (b) Capital Expenditures, Processing, and Traceability Technology

- **2023–2025:** BC Next Level program—net CHF 500 million investment (globally, not Africa-specific) in digital supply chain, processing agility, traceability[15].
- No public, annual, or country-level CapEx breakdown for Ivorian or Ghanaian factories or mapping tech[13][15][16]. Investments include barcode/RFID in Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire, and Katchilè digital platform[12][13][17].

#### (c) Supply Chain Traceability and Mapping Progress

- **2018/19:** 176,000 farms mapped globally[8].
- **2019/20:** 181,861 mapped[18].
- **2020/21:** 75,700 mapped in Ghana/Côte d’Ivoire direct supply[12][19].
- **2023/24:** Côte d’Ivoire: 188,566 cocoa farms mapped; Ghana: 71,945[20].  
  - Full direct supply chain mapping claimed in Ghana (100%) and Côte d’Ivoire (close to 100%) for at-risk farms[12][20].
- **2024/25:** All cocoa for EU and premium buyers in direct supply chains mapped to EUDR standard; pilots running for single-bag barcode traceability[12].
- **Comparability note:** “Traceable” means mapped to farm/warehouse level in direct supply; indirect flows less than 100%, not always reported.

#### (d) Farmer Income Improvement and Verified Outcomes

- **2024/25:** 557,739 cocoa farmers reported “lifted above the poverty line” (reference: $1.90/day)[21][15].  
  - In Côte d’Ivoire, ~2/3 of directly supported farmers above poverty line; <10% above living income benchmark (Fairtrade LIRP = €2.65/kg in 2025)[22][21][23].
  - Poverty and living income improvements validated by third parties—Agri-Logic, Impact Institute, Rainforest Alliance (sample: >700 Ivorian farmer households)[23][24].
  - From 2017 to 2024, share of Ivorian cocoa farmers in extreme poverty fell from 58% to 17%[24].
- **Comparability note:** Only 4–11% of Ghana/Ivory Coast farmers reach living income by independent benchmarks; living income ratios are higher in non-price-controlled origins (Vietnam, Cameroon)[23].
- Robust annual assurance by PwC, KPMG, AgriLogic, and third-party standards (certification compliance)[15][16][24].

### 2.2 Cargill

#### (a) Direct Sourcing Ratios (Year-by-Year, by Geography)

- **2017–18 (Côte d’Ivoire):** 35% of direct supply chain traceable[25].
- **2023:** 50% traceable in direct supply chain, Côte d’Ivoire; 100% barcode traceability in Ghana (27,000 farmers)[25][26].
- Cargill sources directly from six origins, with low direct share in brokered supply chains—full global direct vs indirect % not annually reported[27].
- **2024:** 92% of West African direct supply chain polygon-mapped, most volumes in Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana now fully covered in “Promise” supply chain[28].
- **Comparability note:** Definitions: “direct” = mapped farmer/co-op; “indirect” = trader or local aggregator, traceability currently lower (<14% is polygon-mapped)[25][29].

#### (b) Capital Expenditure on Processing Facilities and Traceability

- **2019–2021:** $100 million invested in Côte d’Ivoire (Yopougon—Africa’s largest plant, +50% capacity), $13 million in Ghana (Tema expansion), plus nearly $7 million on farmer training and digital traceability systems in West Africa[30][31].  
- Additional $69 million in broader ESG/sustainability investments globally in this period[32].
- No annual Africa-specific CapEx breakdown beyond these key projects[30][32].

#### (c) Supply Chain Traceability and Mapping Progress

- **2017–18 (Côte d’Ivoire):** 35% traceable (direct)[25].
- **2023/24:** 92% of direct supply mapped (“Promise” supply), 295,000+ ha monitored for deforestation risk, over 123,000 farms mapped; 99% of mapped cocoa plots deforestation-free since 2014[28][33].
- **Ghana:** 100% barcode traceability (27,000 farmers) in Cargill network; indirect supply chain traceability <14% globally[25][26].
- **Target:** 100% farm-to-factory traceability for all direct sourcing by 2030[26][27].
- Child labor monitoring via CLMRS: 101,100 farmers covered by 2024[32].
- **Comparability note:** Aggregate global numbers may mask regional differences; traceability much lower in non-direct channels.

#### (d) Farmer Income Improvement and Third-Party Verification

- **2024:** Cargill/Nestlé Income Accelerator Program (IAP) (West Africa pilots): Participants saw 32% increase in cocoa production and 38% higher net income (cocoa + off-farm) vs. controls; impact validated by independent assessment[28][32][34].
- Fairtrade study (2021): Only 7% of farmers earn a living income; 30–58% live below World Bank extreme poverty line in Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana; 23% average yield improvement = net income gain US$194/year[35][36].
- KPMG and independent KPMG/sustainability assurance confirm reporting accuracy for traceability, farmer support, and living income progress[32][37].
- **Comparability note:** Living income benchmarks (Fairtrade, IDH) differ from company poverty lines; farm size/yield heterogeneity significant.

### 2.3 Olam (ofi)

#### (a) Direct Sourcing Ratios (Year-by-Year, by Geography)

- **No published annual split** (2018–2024) showing % of Olam (ofi) cocoa sourced directly vs intermediaries in Côte d’Ivoire or Ghana.  
  - “100% traceability in direct supply chain” achieved since 2020 reported; applies to Olam’s programs only, not fully to all global or indirect procurement[38][39].
  - **Ghana caveat:** All companies, including ofi, must buy through Licensed Buying Companies (LBC), limiting direct farmer contracting[40].

#### (b) Capital Expenditures, Processing, and Traceability Investments

- **2020–24:** Olam Group global CapEx SG$6.03 billion in 2024, with ongoing cocoa factory and digital tech investments across Africa; no Ivorian/Ghanaian disaggregation published[41].
- “Investments in AI-powered traceability, partnership with Ghana Cocoa Board, and new AtSource digital platform” support EUDR-readiness across Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana supply chains[39][42][43].

#### (c) Traceability and Mapping Progress

- **2020:** 100% direct supply chain polygon-mapped and deforestation monitored globally; 79% of sustainability program farms polygon mapped as of 2022[38][44].
- By 2024, “traceability for 4.8 million MT and responsibly sourced 11.5 million MT (24% of all Olam purchased agri-commodities)”[41][45].
- Ofi’s CLMRS (Child Labour Monitoring & Remediation System) and AtSource platform employed for traceability and risk management, covering >320,000 cocoa farmers in Africa in 2024[46].
- Landscape projects with Ghana Cocoa Traceability System cover Kakum, Asunafo-Astutifi; Côte d’Ivoire’s Tai region[47].

#### (d) Farmer Income Improvement and Independent Analyses

- **2022/23:** 30,168 cocoa farmers supported to living income (ofi programs).  
- **2023:** 44,000; **2024:** 155,000 (exceeding internal 2030 target[48][49]). Represents 45% of sustainability program participants, attributed to high market prices, LID, and Olam direct interventions.
- Fairtrade/Impact Institute (Ivoire): From April 2023 to April 2025, % earning above living income rose from ~7% to 24%; extreme poverty fell from 26% to 7%[50].
- By 2024, 51% of Ivorian farmers in Olam programs above poverty line; those in extreme poverty fall from 36% (2020) to 17% (2024)[50].
- Investments in agroforestry (15 million trees 2023), 257,000 trained in GAP, 320,000 with livelihood support[49].
- Ghana REDD+/OFI partnership: $1M distributed for verified emissions reductions linked to cocoa[47].
- Independent verification: Fairtrade, Impact Institute, Rainforest Alliance[50][51].

#### Data Caveats and Non-Comparabilities

- No clear annual, country-level direct sourcing or CapEx breakdowns.
- Traceability and “living income” reporting refer only to direct or sustainability-program supply, not total volumes.
- Ghana’s national LBC structure means true “direct” sourcing is highly restricted[40].
- Living income/poverty lines vary by benchmark; see Fairtrade, World Bank, Olam definitions[50][51].

## 3. Disclosure Requirements and Evolving Buyer Demands under EUDR

### Core EUDR Due Diligence and Traceability Requirements

- **All exporters and first importers into the EU** must meet geolocation-to-plot traceability, deforestation/legality risk assessment, and maintain digital evidence of compliance for each cocoa shipment; due diligence statements (DDS) required from 30 December 2025 (main deadline)[1][2][3][4][52].
- Data granularity: Farm polygons to min. six decimal places of geocoordinates; batch identity; traceable segregation of compliant and non-compliant product[3][53][54].

### Anticipated Processor/Trader Disclosure Needs (2024–2026)

- Expanded **digital traceability reporting:** Real-time supplier geodata, satellite deforestation alerts, child labor risk reporting, batch-level DDS reference transmission to all downstream buyers.
- **Client/customer requirements:** Premium chocolate manufacturers (Lindt, Ferrero, Mondelēz) now require:
  - Full farm-level mapping & digital proof of origin for cocoa entering EU and select Asian markets[55][56][57]
  - EUDR-compliant risk assessment files maintained by cocoa suppliers; supplier transparency reviewed annually.
  - Segregation protocols for cocoa beans from mixed-compliance origins; direct collaboration with processors to minimize legal/market exclusion risks.
- **Supplier evaluation:** Programs such as EcoVadis assessment, “Source with Purpose” (Lindt), Cocoa Life (Mondelēz), and direct audit of processor due diligence systems[57][56].

### Unique Buyer Portfolio Needs

- **Premium buyers** demand:
  - End-to-end supply chain segregation, no blending/mixing with non-compliant origins.
  - Documentation for storytelling and customer-facing sustainability claims.
  - Child labor remediation systems, farmer income improvement evidence, and climate/deforestation performance data[57].
  - Certification no longer sufficient—direct EUDR compliance required.
- Failure to deliver compliant product on timeline risks contract loss to processors with advanced digital traceability and robust risk management capability[54][58].

## 4. Risk Management, Competitive Positioning, and Strategic Implications

- **Investment in traceability and risk systems** enables processors to (a) retain/grow supply contracts with EU/brand leaders, (b) secure price premiums, and (c) proactively reduce market exclusion risk under EUDR[59].
- **Barry Callebaut** is the most advanced in mapping, real-time digital traceability, and third-party–verified child labor/community impact interventions—cited as first-choice for premium CPG supply[11][15][60].
- **Cargill** leverages vertical scale, integrated digital/physical supply chains, and customer/third-party programs to deliver segregated, EUDR-ready beans to major chocolate makers[26][30][61].
- **Olam (ofi)** sets the pace in digital monitoring (AtSource), climate risk, and living income, but faces non-comparable data and unique challenges in Ghana due to statutory purchase structures—making compliance more difficult for full portfolio[38][40][49].
- **Risk of “two-tier market":** Smallholders outside main processor programs or certification at risk of exclusion; non-compliant supply likely to be discounted, leading to sector consolidation around the top three processors[62][63].

## 6. Conclusions

Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) are the only cocoa processors currently positioned to deliver the scale and compliance depth now demanded by the EUDR and by premium chocolate manufacturers. All three have made significant investments in digital traceability, community systems, and farmer support—but their ability to guarantee fully segregated, EUDR-compliant supply varies based on vertical integration, geography (especially Ghana), data infrastructure, and the granularity of their field operations. Company compliance claims are credible but must be seen as **provisional until after December 30, 2025**, when full enforcement begins. 

Strategically, advanced traceability confers contract advantage and premium margins; yet the sector faces risks of farm-level exclusion and a growing segmentation between compliant and non-compliant origins, with smallholder livelihoods in potential jeopardy. Transparent external measurement, public mapping, and standardized benchmarking are essential for credible, comparable progress.
