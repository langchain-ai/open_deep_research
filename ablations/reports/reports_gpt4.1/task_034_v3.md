# Comparative Analysis of Vertical Integration Strategies in Global Cocoa Processing: Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana (2018–2024)

## Executive Summary

This report provides an exhaustive, data-driven comparative analysis of the vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) in cocoa processing between 2018 and 2024, with an explicit focus on upstream investments, direct sourcing, sustainability premiums, and traceability solutions in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana. Emphasis is placed on company- and country-level facility data, sourcing ratios, financial flows, traceability milestones, impacts on farmer incomes (with full methodological disclosure), and detailed assessment of EUDR (EU Deforestation Regulation) compliance. The analysis also compares reporting and impact measurement methodologies, and explicitly references the sustainability sourcing practices of major premium buyers such as Lindt, Ferrero, and Mondelēz. All insights are substantiated with primary company and sector sources, with transparent acknowledgment of any data gaps.

## 1. Origin-Country Facility Investments & CapEx (2018–2024)

### Barry Callebaut

- **SACO (Société Africaine de Cacao), Abidjan, Côte d’Ivoire**
  - **Location:** Abidjan, Côte d’Ivoire
  - **Expansion Date:** March 29, 2019 (new state-of-the-art unit)
  - **CapEx:** CHF 55 million (FCFA 30 billion) investment; capacity increased by >40% to 210,000 MT by 2022[1].
  - **Operations:** Major cocoa liquor, butter, and powder production since 1964; important West African hub[1][2].

- **Processing in Ghana**
  - Operates major processing facilities in Tema and Kumasi. Key financial/expansion details are not publicly disaggregated, though Ghana is a core regional hub[3].

- **Traceability Infrastructure**
  - Digital platforms (Katchilè, barcode/RFID pilots) launched in 2022–2024; supports EUDR traceability requirements[4].

### Cargill

- **Yopougon Processing Plant, Côte d’Ivoire**
  - **Location:** Yopougon, outside Abidjan
  - **Expansion Date:** November 2021 (completed)
  - **CapEx:** $100 million for 50% capacity increase, now Africa’s largest cocoa-grinding plant[5][6].
  
- **Tema Plant, Ghana**
  - **Location:** Tema, Ghana
  - **Expansion Date:** November 2021 (completed)
  - **CapEx:** $13 million; expands grinding by 20% (to 90,000 MT/year)[7][5].
  
- **Traceability & Farmer Infrastructure**
  - $7.7m (Côte d’Ivoire) and $3.4m (Ghana) invested in GPS mapping, Cooperative Management Systems, digital payment/traceability platforms (2019–2023)[8].

### Olam (ofi)

- **San Pedro Processing Plant, Côte d’Ivoire**
  - **Location:** San Pedro, Côte d’Ivoire
  - **Opening:** 2012 (orig.), ongoing expansions
  - **CapEx:** €50 million total (including €33.6 million term loan from Proparco, DEG, FMO)[9].
  - **Expansion:** Additional $200 million investment in Côte d’Ivoire announced 2024 for further capacity increase[10].
  
- **Olam Cocoa Processing Ghana Limited**
  - **Location:** Kumasi, Ghana (Northern Sector’s only cocoa plant)
  - **CapEx & Opening:** Details undisclosed; plant operates with leading sustainable/traceable cocoa products[11].
  
- **Digital Traceability Platforms**
  - AtSource and OFIS (Olam Farmer Information System) platforms deployed 2018+, integrating national traceability, farm mapping, and ESG reporting[12][13].

## 2. Direct Sourcing Ratios and Traceability Progress (Annual, Cumulative, Country-Level)

### Barry Callebaut

- **Farm Mapping & Traceability**
  - Côte d’Ivoire: 188,566 farms mapped (2023/24)
  - Ghana: 71,945 farms mapped (2023/24)[4]
  - 100% direct supply chain traceability in both by 2020; all at-risk farms and warehouses mapped[4][14].
  - **Indirect chain:** As of 2021 pilot, 35,000 indirect supply chain farmers onboarded (Côte d’Ivoire); indirect mapping remains incomplete[15].
  - **Public direct sourcing ratios (%):** Explicit annual country-by-country tables are not published; full direct chain mapped, but non-direct volumes persist.

### Cargill

- **Farm Mapping & Traceability**
  - Côte d’Ivoire: By 2024, 123,432 farms polygon-mapped; 61% traceability in direct supply (up from 35% in 2017–18)[16][17].
  - Ghana: 100% barcode traceability and farm-to-factory tracking for 27,000 farmers by 2024[18].
  - **Global share:** ~60% of company’s cocoa sourced directly and traced (2023/24), with a 100% goal by 2030[17][19].
  - **Indirect chain:** Remains far less traceable; only 9-22% of indirect supply traceable across CFI participants[20].

### Olam (ofi)

- **Farm Mapping & Traceability**
  - Direct supply chain (Côte d’Ivoire, Ghana): 100% traced to mapped farm polygons by 2020[12][21].
  - Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana: In 2024, 91% of directly sourced cocoa reportedly traceable in each country (UN-backed estimate), showing catch-up from ~82–83% in 2023[20][22].
  - **Indirect supply:** Sectoral estimates show only ~35% of national exports are directly sourced (Trase, 2022)[23][20]; indirect coverage is much lower, similar to Barry Callebaut and Cargill.
  - **Methodological note:** In Ghana, all major companies must legally source via Licensed Buying Companies (LBCs), making direct-to-farmer arrangements structurally constrained[24].

## 3. Sustainability Programs, Financial Flows, and Farmer Support (2018–2024)

### Barry Callebaut

- **Cocoa Horizons Foundation**
  - 150,000+ farmers supported; CHF 17.7m in farmer premiums (2019/20)[25].
  - Payments for ecosystem services: CHF 325,000 (2023/24 snapshot); 24,000 farmers trained in agroforestry[3].
  - Living Income Differential (LID): Paid in line with Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire regulations since 2019 ($400/MT)[4][26].
  - 75% of VSLA membership are women; 256% increase in mid-2023/24[3].

### Cargill

- **Cargill Cocoa Promise**
  - $12.3 million (2019–2023) in sustainability/traceability in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana; $13m+ in Côte d’Ivoire (2023/24) for coaching, training, traceability[8].
  - 123,000+ farmers in Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana receive technical support; over 1 million agroforestry trees distributed[18].
  - Strong investment in digital payments, VSLAs, and women’s empowerment[17].

### Olam (ofi)

- **Cocoa Compass, AtSource/OFIS Systems**
  - $136 million in sustainability premiums (all countries, up to 2020); Living Income Differential (LID) paid per regulations[12][27].
  - 155,000 farmers earning a living income as of 2024 (surpassing original 2030 target)[5].
  - 320,000+ farmers receive livelihood support; 257,000 trained in GAP; 9.8 million trees distributed[5][21].
  - Over 95,000 cocoa farmers reached with Child Labour Monitoring & Remediation (2024)[13].

## 4. Traceability Milestones, EUDR Compliance, & Excluded Volumes

### Traceability Milestones & Platforms

- **Barry Callebaut:** 100% of direct supply chain in CIV/GHA traceable by 2020; public dashboard reporting; barcoded bag traceability in Ghana; RFID pilots; Katchilè (digital) launched 2022–2024; third-party verification by IDH, Rainforest Alliance, CFI[4][14][28][29].

- **Cargill:** 100% farm-to-factory traceability (direct chain) in Ghana; 61% in Côte d’Ivoire (2024); GPS polygon mapping; CocoaWise™/BeanTracker platform; limited third-party assurance by KPMG; targets 100% global direct supply mapping by 2030. Collaborates with Satelligence for deforestation risk monitoring[16][18][30].

- **Olam (ofi):** 100% direct supply chain mapped by 2020 in CIV/GHA; AtSource/OFIS systems integrate farm polygons, sustainability metrics, carbon/ESG data; blockchain and national platforms (Ghana Cocoa Traceability System, Côte d’Ivoire's CCC databases); FLA and ICI have conducted third-party verification until 2026[12][21][31].

### EUDR Compliance Readiness

- **Deadline:** Main obligations start December 30, 2025 (large/medium companies), June 30, 2026 (small/micro)[32].
- **Company status:** All three companies claim EUDR readiness for direct chains, with digital traceability, full geolocation records, and DDS protocols in place for CIV/GHA[14][18][21][30][32][33].
- **Mapping rates:** 100% for direct chains, approaching >90% for mapped volumes by 2024 (UN & CFI reports); indirect remains a significant compliance risk[20][22].

### Excluded Volumes & Indirect Supply Traceability

- **Barry Callebaut:** Excludes mapped cocoa farms overlapping protected forests; indirect chain mapping pilots (35,000 farmers, 18,000 mapped in CIV as of 2021). Public reporting clarifies exclusion threshold and area risk[15][4][14][28].
- **Cargill:** No public year-by-year excluded volume data, but non-mapped/unverified indirect volumes (at least 40%+ of global total) not EUDR compliant yet; goal for 100% by 2030[17][19][30].
- **Olam (ofi):** Indirect chain traceability is sectorally estimated at ~35% for CIV, less for GHA; non-mapped/unverified volumes will be excluded from EU supply as required[23][20][22][32].

## 5. Measured Impacts on Farmer Income & Poverty: Methodological Transparency

### Methodologies

- **Barry Callebaut:**  
  - Uses Farmer Field Book (561 farmers, 2,132 ha) with GPS-measured plots, median/mean household incomes compared to WB poverty line ($1.90/day) and Fairtrade Living Income Reference Price (GHA: €2.16, CIV: €2.65/kg in 2025); impact externally validated (Agri-Logic, Impact Institute, Rainforest Alliance)[28][34][35].

- **Cargill:**  
  - Adopts Fairtrade and IDH LIRPs (GHA: $5.81/day, CIV: $6.32/day, PPP 2018 USD); Farm Economic Model for household income, including diversification; impact statistics and targets (e.g., 25,000 households by 2025) externally assured by KPMG. Relies on yield, price, and diversification data to project impacts[17][36][37].

- **Olam (ofi):**  
  - Cocoa Compass targets closing “Living Income Gap” for 700,000+ farmers by 2030; uses automated/large-scale model and proprietary Living Income Gap Heuristic Tool; externally assessed by Fairtrade, Impact Institute, and International Cocoa Initiative. Focuses on real farm- and household-level data collected via AtSource[12][13][38].

### Outcomes by Company (Côte d’Ivoire & Ghana, c.2024)

- **Barry Callebaut**
  - 557,739 cocoa farmers reported “lifted above the poverty line” (global); in CIV, ~2/3 of directly supported farmers reached above poverty, but only 4–10% above LIRP (living income)[35][34].
  - Impact Institute: Premium payments increase household income by 31% (Côte d’Ivoire, 2021/22)[34].
  - Child labor prevalence dropped from 61% (2018/19) to 31% (2021/22) in programs[34].

- **Cargill**
  - 32% rise in cocoa production and 38% higher net income among direct participant farmers in joint programs (e.g., Nestlé Income Accelerator, independent evaluation)[17][36].
  - Only ~7% of farmers (Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana) meet LIRP (Fairtrade, 2021). 30–58% remain below WB poverty line[39][40].

- **Olam (ofi)**
  - 155,000 farmers in direct programs now earning a living income (2024); 45% of participants reached LIRP, with 24% of Côte d’Ivoire cohort and only 7% of non-program farmers passing living income lines (Fairtrade/Impact Institute, 2024)[5][38].
  - Olam reported above-expected improvement, attributed to higher cocoa prices, LID premium, and focused interventions[5][38].

### Benchmark Non-Comparability

- Definitions (direct/indirect, income thresholds), data collection techniques (field book, model, survey), reporting windows, and independence of verification differ between companies; country purchasing systems (esp. Ghana’s LBC/COCOBOD) constrain direct tracing and impact measurement[24][38][41].

## 7. Premium Contract Market Alignment: Customer Requirements (Lindt, Ferrero, Mondelēz)

- **Lindt**: Requires farm-to-bar traceability, launched blockchain-based line in 2025; mandates EUDR compliance and farm-level mapping from suppliers[42].

- **Ferrero**: Requires full supply chain traceability and robust third-party certification (Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade), documented farmer support evidence, and EUDR-aligned due diligence[43].

- **Mondelēz**: Cocoa Life program mandates EUDR-compliant sourcing, traceability to farm, and sustainability impact reporting; reviewed annually[44].

- **Supplier Practices**: All three processors supply these major brands and align with client codes through:
    - Mandatory farm/plot mapping for all EU-bound cocoa (especially for Lindt contracts in 2024–25)
    - Full auditability via digital platforms (e.g., AtSource, CocoaWise™, Katchilè) supplying data for regulatory and marketing claims
    - Requirement for payroll receipts of LID or sustainability premium payments
    - Detailed grievance mechanisms and third-party audit compliance reports[4][12][18][22][31][42][43][44]

- **Competitive Advantage**: Advanced digital traceability, fast-tracking of mapping across direct/indirect chains, and external assurance credentials are decisive in contract renewal/award for premium clients[31][43][44].

- **Contractual Transparency**: Individual contract clauses are proprietary, but contract compliance is evidenced by open adherence to customer sustainability and EUDR-readiness requirements as stipulated in public buyer-supplier codes and audit reports[42][43][44].

## Conclusion

Between 2018 and 2024, Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) each made substantial and well-documented investments in processing facilities, traceability infrastructure, and direct farmer support in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana, as they adapted to regulatory changes (notably the EUDR) and increasing customer scrutiny. While all have achieved 100% traceability in their direct supply chains in these two countries, significant cocoa volumes in the indirect chain remain challenging to trace, which will impact EUDR compliance after 2025.

Reporting methodologies and definitions are not fully harmonized: Olam stands out for its digital farm-level data systems, Barry Callebaut for rapid scale-up of mapping and transparent impact measurement, and Cargill for its systematic digitization and external limited assurance. Premium chocolate manufacturers are already requiring detailed digital traceability and confirmed EUDR compliance from their suppliers, with non-compliant volumes being excluded from lucrative contracts.

As enforcement dates approach, the primary risk for all three companies—and for the sector—is the proportion of national output in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana that remains outside mapped, direct supply chains. Sector benchmarks currently suggest 35–45% of national cocoa exports may not be EUDR ready. Ultimately, advanced vertical integration and traceability systems confer decisive competitive advantage, but harmonized, third-party validated impact measurement and complete national traceability coverage remain critical for both compliance and true sustainability gains.
