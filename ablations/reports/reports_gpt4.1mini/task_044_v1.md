# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms: California Cap-and-Trade, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market (2020–2024)

This report provides an in-depth comparative analysis of the implementation and outcomes of carbon pricing mechanisms under California’s Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China’s National Carbon Market from 2020 to 2024. It covers allowance price trajectories, emissions reductions, revenue generation and allocation, compliance rates, responses to COVID-19 economic disruptions, interactions with complementary climate policies, and the assessment of carbon leakage including border adjustment mechanisms.

---

## 1. Allowance Price Trajectories (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade Program  
- Prices generally increased over the period, starting near the auction floor price (which rose annually by inflation + 5%) and reaching approximately $38.35 per metric ton by May 2024.  
- Auction prices peaked close to $41 in early 2024 but experienced a dip due to regulatory uncertainties before rebounding with legislative clarity.  
- The program includes a multi-tiered price containment reserve (APCR) and a hard price ceiling, with the ceiling expected to rise to $94.92 by 2025.  
- Future proposals include removing 264 million allowances cumulatively (2025–2030) to increase stringency and push prices higher.

### European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)  
- EUA prices peaked around €100 per ton in early 2023, then declined to about €64.74 per ton in 2024 amid volatility linked to geopolitical and energy market factors.  
- The Market Stability Reserve (MSR) has actively intervened by adjusting auction volumes to reduce surplus allowances, thus stabilizing prices.  
- Forward projections estimate prices rising to around €75/tCO2 in 2025 and sharply increasing thereafter, particularly with the upcoming ETS2 system for buildings and transport starting in 2027.

### China National Carbon Market  
- Beginning with a price near 48 yuan (~$7) per ton in 2021, allowance prices steadily increased to historic highs of over 105 yuan (~$15) per ton by early 2024.  
- Market activity intensified significantly, with trading volumes up ~47% and turnover doubling in 2024, reflecting deepening market liquidity.  
- Projected prices anticipate growth toward 300–500 yuan (~$44–73) per ton by 2030 tied to sector coverage expansion and stricter benchmarks.

## 2. Total Emissions Reductions Compared to Baseline Projections

### California  
- Achieved a 14% reduction in overall GHG emissions since program inception. Official targets for 2020 (to reach 1990 levels) were met early, around 2016–2020.  
- Adjusted downward the 2020 emissions baseline following inventory corrections.  
- CARB’s 2024 Scoping Plan estimates about 98 million metric tonnes CO2e reduced through the program to date, while setting ambitious goals for 48% reduction below 1990 levels by 2030 and net-zero by 2045.  
- Reductions are partly attributable to complementary policies, complicating attributing causal impacts solely to cap-and-trade.

### European Union  
- EU ETS sectors have reduced emissions by approximately 46–50% since 2005, exceeding prior targets and on track for 62% reduction by 2030 compared to 2005.  
- 2024 saw a further 5.8% emissions drop in power and industrial sectors. Electricity sector emissions declined while renewable electricity capacity expanded to 47.3%.  
- Mixed trends in industrial subsectors, with some emission growth offset by declines elsewhere.  
- Aviation emissions increased post-pandemic, while maritime transport entered the ETS scope in 2024, leading to early reductions.

### China  
- National ETS power sector showed a 13% emissions reduction between 2013 and 2020, but overall national emissions plateaued or slightly declined only from 2024 onward.  
- China projected to miss its 2020–2025 carbon intensity reduction goal (achieved ~12% vs. target 18%), prompting pressure for steeper future cuts.  
- Coverage expansion to steel, cement, and aluminum sectors in 2024–2025 is expected to enhance reduction impact by driving production shifts and plant retirements.  
- Emissions reductions supported by rapid growth in renewables but partly offset by industrial demand rebounds and energy-intensive sectors.

## 3. Revenue Generation and Allocation to Climate Programs

### California  
- Auction revenues between $28 billion and $34.5 billion since program start, administered through the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF).  
- Legally mandated allocation: at least 35% of revenues to disadvantaged and low-income communities; over $12.8 billion invested to date in 590,000+ projects.  
- Funds support clean transportation, housing, wildfire prevention, agriculture, climate resilience, and energy innovation.  
- Recent legislative reforms (AB 1207, SB 840) improve strategic fund allocation and include programs for household energy cost relief from price ceiling proceeds.

### European Union  
- EU ETS auctions have generated more than €258 billion (~$280 billion) since 2013; 2024 revenue alone about €38.8 billion.  
- Member States are required to spend all ETS proceeds on climate and energy transition activities, such as renewable energy, energy efficiency, innovation, and social support via the Social Climate Fund (€65 billion earmarked for 2026–2032).  
- Allocation shares vary across Member States, with some countries (e.g., Germany, Poland) dedicating >90% of revenues to climate action, while others (e.g., Spain) allocate less than 40%.  
- Innovation and Modernisation Funds channel investments to low-carbon technologies and energy infrastructure upgrades.

### China  
- Trading turnover exceeded 18 billion yuan (~$2.6 billion) in 2024 with cumulative volumes surpassing 680 million tonnes CO2e by mid-2025.  
- Detailed public disclosure on revenue use is limited compared to California and the EU.  
- Green finance growth indicators suggest increased investment in low-carbon projects alongside carbon market revenues.  
- The voluntary carbon credit market (CCER) supports renewables, forestry, and efficiency projects but operates separately from compliance market revenue flows.

## 4. Compliance Rates Among Covered Entities

### California  
- Covers roughly 80–85% of state GHG emissions with about 400–450 entities regulated.  
- Compliance rates remain very high, supported by strict MRV protocols and significant penalties (four times missing allowances).  
- Offsets usage capped (4% rising to 6%), with rigorous review ensuring offset quality.  
- Market design includes self-ratcheting reserve mechanisms to maintain integrity.

### European Union  
- Compliance rates consistently high, facilitated by a comprehensive, transparent MRV framework via the Union Registry.  
- Maritime and aviation sectors now integrated with compliance rules since 2024.  
- Reporting deadlines aligned with verification procedures enhance compliance accuracy.  
- Strict penalties and regular audits help maintain adherence.

### China  
- Compliance rates exceeded 99.5% annually since launch, reaching 99.98% in 2023.  
- Transitioned to annual compliance cycles in 2023 for better market agility.  
- Strong MRV system and real-time monitoring reduce fraud risk and enhance data quality.  
- Enforcement backed by increasing penalties and joint review mechanisms.

## 5. Handling Economic Disruptions from COVID-19 and Market Rule Adjustments

### California  
- 2020 saw a COVID-19-related decline in emissions and allowance demand; 32 million allowances remained unsold in auctions.  
- The self-ratcheting auction reserve and withholding mechanisms removed surplus allowances, preventing price collapse.  
- No temporary banking rule relaxations observed, but accumulated banked allowances (~310 million) remain a key market factor.  
- Emission caps were recalibrated downward after 2020 inventory corrections.  
- Revenue dips prompted re-evaluation of investment strategies and resulted in proposals to tighten future caps.

### European Union  
- No temporary modifications to allowances or banking rules were adopted in response to COVID-19 within the ETS itself.  
- Emissions dropped in 2020 due to economic slowdown and fuel switching.  
- Broader EU economic recovery plans (~€800 billion NextGenerationEU) indirectly supported green investments.  
- Market Stability Reserve operation helped mitigate excess allowance surplus.  
- Extension of MRV scope to maritime and changes in surrender deadlines occurred post-pandemic to improve accuracy.

### China  
- COVID-19 led to an estimated 25% emissions drop during early 2020 lockdowns.  
- The carbon market experienced increased price and volume volatility due to economic disruption.  
- No detailed evidence of formal banking rule changes but regulatory oversight intensified to prevent market hoarding post-pandemic.  
- Stimulus measures subsequently fueled a rebound in carbon-intensive activity.  
- Experience underscored the need for integrated policies ensuring sustained low-carbon recovery.

## 6. Interactions with Complementary Policies (Renewable Energy Mandates, Coal Phase-Outs)

### California  
- Cap-and-Trade is embedded within a robust climate policy mix including the Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS), Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS), Zero Emission Vehicle mandates, and energy efficiency programs.  
- These complementary policies contribute substantially to emission reductions and ensure a diversified mitigation strategy.  
- Coal phase-out in power generation and refinery transitions are supported with allowance allocation adjustments and protections for ratepayers.  
- Legislative frameworks (AB 398, SB 32, AB 1207, SB 840) reinforce policy coherence.

### European Union  
- EU ETS tightly linked to renewable energy directives (e.g., Renewable Energy Directive Recast, Fit for 55 package) and national coal phase-out timelines.  
- ETS price signals incentivize utilities to retire coal plants or shift to cleaner fuels; combined effects accelerate decarbonization.  
- Complementary policies include energy efficiency standards and Just Transition Mechanisms for socially equitable shifts.  
- ETS2’s integration of buildings and transport sectors complements existing carbon pricing and renewables expansion.

### China  
- Coal phase-out policies target shutdown of inefficient plants, coordinated with ETS-driven incentives to reduce emissions.  
- Renewable energy deployment surged, with China accounting for >50% of global new capacity in recent years.  
- Policy frameworks incentivize clean technology adoption via green certificates and voluntary carbon credits (CCER), complementing ETS.  
- Combining mandatory coal retirements with ETS participation has increased reductions but also raised transition management challenges.

## 7. Carbon Leakage Concerns, Industrial Relocation, and Border Adjustment Mechanisms

### California  
- Free allowance allocations protect energy-intensive, trade-exposed sectors against leakage, covering cement, petroleum refining, and others.  
- Studies estimate short-run output declines around 17% at ~$10/ton CO2 compliance costs, with reduced longer-term impacts.  
- Legislated ongoing refinement of leakage prevention measures, balancing competitiveness with environmental goals.  
- Discussions underway concerning potential Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanisms (CBAM) linked to price offset protocols but no formal CBAM enacted to date.

### European Union  
- The EU maintains extensive free allocation to 63 sectors/subsectors judged at leakage risk.  
- Legislative adoption in May 2023 of the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), to debut in 2026, imposes tariffs on emissions embedded in imported goods in steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, and electricity.  
- CBAM phases out free allowances between 2026 and 2034 to maintain competitiveness while reducing leakage risk.  
- Empirical analyses indicate modest leakage reduction but highlight risks of welfare losses in exporting countries and trade tensions.  
- Proposals recommend revenue recycling to vulnerable sectors to improve equity.

### China  
- Faces carbon leakage pressure due to EU CBAM covering key export sectors like steel and aluminum representing ~$3.5 billion in exports currently.  
- No formal border adjustment or equivalent mechanism domestically but active efforts underway for international cooperation and CBAM impact mitigation.  
- Academic and policy analyses caution about CBAM’s limited unilateral efficiency and broader welfare impacts on developing economies.  
- Recommendations emphasize strengthening China’s carbon pricing instruments and improved emissions accounting for global leakage control.

---

## Conclusion

Between 2020 and 2024, California’s Cap-and-Trade Program, the EU ETS, and China’s National Carbon Market demonstrated distinct trajectories in carbon pricing, emission outcomes, and policy integration reflecting their differing economic contexts, policy architectures, and carbon market maturities. California and the EU manifest mature markets with high compliance, significant revenue use transparency, and advanced leakage mitigation, including the EU’s implementation of CBAM. China’s newer system is rapidly expanding coverage and market activity but faces challenges in reaching emissions goals and managing leakage pressures heightened by international carbon pricing policies.

All three systems adapted variably to COVID-19 disruptions, emphasizing market stability and integration with broader climate policies such as renewable energy mandates and coal phase-out initiatives to further drive decarbonization. Moving forward, each jurisdiction continues refining market rules and policy interactions to strengthen emissions reductions, economic resilience, and social equity while preventing carbon leakage.
