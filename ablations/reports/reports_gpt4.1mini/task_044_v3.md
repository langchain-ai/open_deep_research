# Comprehensive Research Report on the California Cap-and-Trade Program, EU ETS, and China’s National Carbon Market (2020–2024)

This report provides an exhaustive, data-driven comparison and analysis of three major carbon pricing mechanisms operating between 2020 and 2024: the California Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China’s National Carbon Market. It draws exclusively from primary, official governmental and regulatory sources, presenting detailed allowance price trajectories, sectoral emissions reductions relative to baseline projections, compliance rates, cumulative revenues, fund allocations, distinctions between implemented and planned policies, and explicit market mechanisms and COVID-19 response measures.

---

## 1. Overview and Scope of the Carbon Markets (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade Program  
- Introduced in 2013, the program covers ~80% of California’s GHG emissions, including electric power, industrial, transportation, buildings, mining, agriculture, and forestry sectors.  
- The emissions cap on allowances decreases approximately 4% annually (2021–2030 phase), aiming to meet legislated targets: return emissions to 1990 levels by 2020 (achieved in 2016), achieve 40% below 1990 levels by 2030, and carbon neutrality by 2045.  
- Approximately 400–600 entities are regulated, including fuel suppliers and industrial sources.  
- Allowances distributed through quarterly auctions and free allocation, with roughly half allocated freely to industrial and utility sectors to minimize carbon leakage and consumer impacts.

### European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)  
- Launched in 2005, currently in Phase IV (2021–2030), the EU ETS covers 35–40% of EU greenhouse gas emissions, including power generation, industry, aviation (intra-EEA flights), and maritime transport (phased in since 2024).  
- The annual linear reduction factor (LRF) increased from ~2.2% (2021-2023) to 4.3% (2024 onward), targeting a 62% emissions reduction from covered sectors by 2030 relative to 2005 levels.  
- Approximately 11,000 installations covered, spanning sectors subject to free allocation benchmarks, carbon leakage risk assessments, and phased sector expansion including maritime and building/road transport sectors slated for allowance surrender by 2027.  
- Auctioning combined with free allocation based on efficiency benchmarks protects vulnerable sectors; the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) initiated in 2023 supports leakage prevention and phases out free allocations in certain sectors by 2034.

### China National Carbon Market  
- Officially launched in July 2021, initially covering the thermal power sector (~5.1 billion tonnes CO2, about 40% of China's emissions), with staged expansion in 2024–2025 to include steel, cement, and aluminum smelting sectors, increasing coverage to about 8 billion tonnes CO2 (over 60% of national emissions).  
- The National ETS uses an intensity-based cap approach, allocating allowances free primarily based on output and sector-specific benchmarking, without a fixed absolute emissions cap.  
- Reports about 3,600 covered enterprises as of 2024, with annual emissions reporting and verification mandatory, as well as a legal framework enhanced by the 2024 Interim Regulations.  
- Voluntary China Certified Emissions Reduction (CCER) credit market revived in 2024, complementing the compliance market.

## 2. Allowance Price Trajectories (2020–2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade  
- 2020: Auctions started at ~$17.70 per metric ton CO2e, dampened by pandemic-induced demand declines and allowance surplus.  
- 2021: Incremental price growth to ~$19–21.  
- 2022: Further rise reaching $24–26 per ton.  
- 2023: Prices surged to $30–$38 owing to tightened caps and stronger industrial demand.  
- Q1-Q2 2024: Peak prices near $41, then a correction to ~$36 in May 2024.  
- Features a statutory price floor with annual 5%+inflation increase, an Auction Price Containment Reserve (APCR) releasing additional supply at price tiers to prevent price spikes, and a hard price ceiling of $88.22 in 2024 ([1][2][6][10][16]).

### EU ETS  
- Early 2020: Prices dipped to approximately €21–25/tCO2 during initial COVID-19 shocks.  
- 2021: Rapid recovery and increase to €55–60/t.  
- 2022: Continued climb to €75–85/t amid tighter caps and energy crises.  
- 2023: Price peak averaging €83.60/t with intra-year highs above €96/t driven by geopolitical tensions and gas prices.  
- 2024: Prices moderated to around €64.74/t with market stabilization and energy price normalization.  
- The Market Stability Reserve (MSR) actively withdraws surplus allowances to tighten supply, with the linear reduction factor increased to 4.3% (2024 onward) ([4][5][6][13][14]).

### China National Carbon Market  
- Mid-2021 (launch): Prices began near RMB 48 (~$7) per metric ton CO2e.  
- 2022: Increased to approx. RMB 55.3 (~$8).  
- 2023: Prices climbed steadily above RMB 90 (~$13.5).  
- By April 2024: Peaked above RMB 100 (~$15), reflecting market maturation and sector expansion expectations.  
- Early 2025: Market showed a mild price correction (~3.8% month-over-month decline).  
- Allowance auctioning remained minimal during 2020-2024; nearly all allowances allocated free ([2][4][7][15][19]).

## 3. Annual and Cumulative Emissions Reductions by Sector (2020–2024) Relative to Baseline Projections

### California  
- The Cap-and-Trade-covered sectors have achieved consistent reductions, aligning with the state’s GHG targets.  
- From 2019 through 2024: Emissions reductions in covered sectors cumulatively exceed approximately 98 million metric tons CO2e.  
- Sectors including electric power show steady decline: power sector emissions declined ~10.9% of total 2021 emissions, reflecting coal phase-out and renewable integration.  
- Annual declines ranged from 2.4% (2021-2022) to continued steady reductions afterward, consistent with the approximately 4% annual cap decline.  
- The program contributed significantly to the state’s achievement of 1990 emissions levels by 2016 and ongoing trajectory to 2045 carbon neutrality ([1][2][8]).

### EU ETS  
- From 2005 to 2023, emissions from covered stationary installations have dropped roughly 47%.  
- A notable 16.5% reduction occurred between 2022 and 2023 (post-pandemic rebound and policy tightening).  
- Power sector emissions declined 12% in 2024 compared to 2023, with renewables reaching 47.3% of electricity generation.  
- Sectoral variation exists: fertilizer sector emissions rose by 7% in 2024, cement sector emissions fell by 5%, and aviation emissions increased 15% post-pandemic (2024), with maritime transport regulated starting 2024 adding ~72 MtCO2 emissions.  
- The overall system targets 62% emissions reduction by 2030 relative to 2005 ([3][4][5][9][13]).

### China National Carbon Market  
- The market has achieved partial emission intensity reductions; thermal power generation intensity declined by 2.38% relative to previous baselines, and overall electricity generation intensity declined by about 8.78% compared to 2018 ([2][4][11]).  
- The 2020-2025 combined carbon intensity reduction target of 18% was not fully met; about 12% reduction realized, with further tightening planned.  
- Expansions to steel, cement, and aluminum sectors began in 2024-2025, adding ~3 billion tonnes to the regulatory scope.  
- Coal remains dominant (>60% in 2021 generation), limiting rapid decarbonization; however, policy synergies with large renewable capacity (>1,200 GW) underpin gradual reductions ([2][3][10][11]).

## 4. Compliance Rates and Covered Entities (2020–2024)

### California  
- Compliance rates maintained near 100% every year from 2020 through 2024 ([2][4][9]).  
- Program oversees ~350–400 regulated entities covering 76–80% of state emissions ([4][7]).  
- Offsets permitted up to 4% from 2021 to 2025, increasing to 6% starting in 2026; at least half must demonstrate direct California environmental co-benefits.  
- Enforcement includes mandatory third-party verification and penalties of up to four times missing allowances' value ([9][15][16]).

### EU ETS  
- Compliance rates exceed 99% across sectors including new maritime transport obligations (99% surrender in 2024 partial phase-in).  
- Approximately 11,000 installations covered in 2024, including industrial and aviation sectors.  
- Verified emissions must be matched with surrendered allowances annually; penalties of €100 per excess tonne for non-compliance.  
- MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification) system rigorously monitored through the Union Registry ([3][6][14][17]).

### China National Carbon Market  
- High compliance with 99.61% in 2021, rising to 99.88% in 2022, and 99.98% in 2023 ([2][7][15]).  
- Approximately 3,378 enterprises covered in power, steel, cement, and aluminum sectors by 2024.  
- Compliance cycles changed from two-year to annual from 2023, enhancing timeliness.  
- Penalties up to ten times the market value of non-compliant allowances, suspension of trading licenses, and public disclosures of violations ([1][19][23]).

## 5. Cumulative Revenues and Fund Allocations (2020–2024)

### California  
- Cumulative auction revenue from inception through 2024 estimated between $28 billion and $34.5 billion.  
- 2025 auctions projected to generate $3.13 billion.  
- By regulation, at least 35% of revenues must be invested in disadvantaged or low-income communities; actual investments often exceed this minimum with ~85% directed to priority populations in recent years.  
- The Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF) finances 90+ programs, including low-carbon transportation, affordable housing, forest health, wildfire prevention, and building decarbonization.  
- Since 2014, over $11 billion invested resulting in a projected lifetime carbon reduction over 109 million metric tons CO2e from funded projects.  
- Legislative updates in 2025 (AB 1207, SB 840) extended program through 2045, renamed “Cap-and-Invest,” and created a Climate Mitigation Fund to reinvest price ceiling revenues for energy cost relief programs ([6][9][12][15][17]).

### EU ETS  
- Total auction revenues since 2013 surpassed €265 billion, with 2024 revenues reaching €38.8 billion from 221 auctions.  
- In 2024, €24.4 billion was transferred directly to Member States; at least €21.2 billion (77% of obligated funds) dedicated to climate and energy investments per Directive 2003/87/EC Article 10(3).  
- Member States use revenues for building renovations, energy efficiency, clean transport, social support, and innovation funding.  
- The Social Climate Fund (SCF), established 2023, is earmarked to disburse €65–87 billion between 2026–2032 focusing on vulnerable households and micro/transport users under ETS2.  
- Allocation varies nationally: Germany channels >90% to climate action; Spain allocates <40%; the Modernisation Fund supports infrastructure in less wealthy Member States.  
- Consistency with statutory mandates regarding revenue recycling is high, though delays and fossil fuel subsidy risks persist in some cases ([6][9][22][23][24]).

### China National Carbon Market  
- Market turnover exceeded RMB 43 billion (~$6.7 billion) cumulatively by early 2025, mostly from free allowance allocations rather than auctioning.  
- Auctioning pilots are planned from 2025 onwards to increase auction revenue generation and market stability.  
- Transparency on carbon market revenues and fund allocations is limited; official financial disclosures on explicit revenues or fund deployment tied to carbon market proceeds are presently not publicly available.  
- The CCER voluntary credit market complemented the compliance market with approx. 9.48 million offset credits issued by mid-2025, supporting projects in renewable energy, forestry, and methane capture ([2][5][7][14]).

## 6. Market Mechanisms and Policy Implementation vs. Announcements (2020–2024)

### California  
- Fully operational market mechanisms 2020–2024 include:  
  - **Price floor**, **Allowance Price Containment Reserve (APCR)** tiers managing price spikes, and a **hard price ceiling** to limit costs.  
  - Free allowance allocations continue at 100% for energy-intensive trade-exposed industries through 2030, per regulatory mandates.  
  - Offset program with strict caps and quality standards and linkage with Québec’s program; ongoing linkage discussions with Washington State.  
- Legislative updates extending the program till 2045 (“Cap-and-Invest”) and introducing Climate Mitigation Fund implemented in 2025 ([1][4][13][15][16]).  
- No announced policies beyond that period affecting 2020-2024 outcomes are included.

### EU ETS  
- Active policies fully implemented during 2020–2024:  
  - Linear Reduction Factor increased to 4.3%, MSR withdrawals tightened supply by removing 2.9 billion allowances since 2023.  
  - Inclusion of maritime transport began in 2024 with partial allowance surrender.  
  - The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism initiated transitional reporting in 2023; full financial obligations and CBAM customs tariffs apply from 2026, gradually phasing out free allowances by 2034.  
  - ETS2 program for buildings, road transport, and small industry launched in 2025 with allowance surrender obligations starting 2027 ([3][6][14][16]).  
- Market mechanisms such as unlimited banking remain in force; no emergency COVID-19 banking rule changes were implemented.  
- Planned but not fully implemented policies beyond 2024 include gradual extension of ETS2, full CBAM tariff enforcement by 2026, and further MSR adjustments.

### China National Carbon Market  
- Implemented measures:  
  - Output-based benchmarking allowance allocation with ±3% adjustment caps introduced for steel, cement, aluminum sectors in 2024–25.  
  - Compliance reporting switched to annual cycles in 2023; strict enforcement with heavy penalties.  
  - Interim Regulations (effective May 2024) enhanced market oversight, penalties, and monitoring.  
  - Voluntary CCER market reforms effective 2024; allowed offset use capped at 5% compliance obligations (exclusion applying from 2025 onward in National ETS).  
- Planned but not implemented by 2024:  
  - Wider auctioning of allowances and market stability mechanisms scheduled for pilot introduction from 2025 onwards.  
  - Extension of sectoral scope to petrochemical and aviation not yet active ([2][4][7][15][19]).

## 7. COVID-19 Economic Disruptions: Timing, Magnitude, and Responses

### California  
- The pandemic caused a drop in emissions and demand in 2020, leading to an auction surplus of approximately 32 million unsold allowances.  
- No explicit emergency or ad-hoc rule changes to allowance banking or allocation were enacted.  
- Program flexibility, robust price containment measures (APCR), and steadily rising price floors mitigated price collapse risk.  
- Auction prices fell initially but stabilized by late 2021 and rose in subsequent years ([2][4][12]).  
- No statutory temporary relief or emergency compliance extensions were found in official CARB reports for the period 2020–2024.

### EU ETS  
- COVID-19 caused emissions and demand shocks leading to temporary allowance price declines in early 2020.  
- No modifications to banking or surrender rules related to COVID-19; the Market Stability Reserve absorbed surplus allowances coinciding with demand shocks.  
- Indirect support via broad EU recovery fiscal packages helped market and economic stabilization without direct ETS regulatory intervention.  
- Aviation and maritime sectors were added post-pandemic, but no COVID-specific relief for these sectors was implemented ([3][6][9][20]).  
- No official emergency suspension or amnesty records exist for EU ETS allowance compliance in 2020-2024.

### China National Carbon Market  
- Lockdowns in early 2020 temporarily decreased emissions by ~25%, with related price and volume market volatility during ETS start-up.  
- Regulatory tightening prevented allowance borrowing after 2022; annual compliance introduced to improve monitoring.  
- No documented ad-hoc regulatory or emergency adjustments to allowance allocations, banking, or surrender requirements during pandemic years in official MEE releases.  
- Economic stimulus and energy policies indirectly supported resilience but no direct carbon market pandemic-specific interventions are documented ([2][7][18][19]).

## 8. Statutory Mandates vs Actual Achievements (Allocations, Fund Use, Compliance)

### California  
- Mandated minimum of 35% auction revenues to disadvantaged and low-income communities; actual investments ~85% in priority populations since 2023 ([6][9][14]).  
- Free allowance allocation to industrial facilities and utilities preserved at 100% till 2030 per state regulations ([4][14]).  
- Offset use limits maintained as legislated (4% to 6%), with ongoing updates planned but no changes yet enacted in reporting period.  
- Compliance near 100% without exceptions; penalties enforced as per regulations ([9][15]).  
- Auction prices and revenues fluctuated within expected ranges under containment mechanisms ([10][11]).

### EU ETS  
- Member States obliged to spend 100% of auction revenues on climate or energy-related projects; in practice, ~77% of obligated revenues committed by end 2024, consistent with Directive 2003/87/EC Article 10(3) ([22][23]).  
- Free allocation benchmarks reduced progressively due to increased LRF and replaced incrementally by CBAM-linked tariffs with phase-out by 2034.  
- Compliance rates >99% with MRV and penalty mechanisms strictly applied ([14][16]).  
- The Social Climate Fund’s funding allocations by Member States are in varying stages of planning and implementation ([5][23]).  

### China National Carbon Market  
- Free allocation based on output benchmarks with performance adjustment limits ±3%; fully consistent with regulatory documents for 2024–2025 ([7][16]).  
- Offset credit use capped at 5% until end of 2024; excluded from National ETS starting 2025 ([15]).  
- Precise figures on cumulative revenues or mandated fund distributions are not publicly available; transparency remains limited ([2][4]).  
- Compliance rates and enforcement fully aligned with Interim Regulation mandates; legal framework strengthened as of 2024 ([1][19]).
