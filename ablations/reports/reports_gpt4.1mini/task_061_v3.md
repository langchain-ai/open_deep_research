This significantly improves the funded status by reducing the obligation without requiring immediate cash plan funding, thereby enhancing balance sheet stability.

### 1.2 Interpretation of Pension Settlement Activity

- The recorded settlement expense, while negatively impacting net income in FY2025 (contributing to a net loss of $5.1 million compared to net income of $46.6 million in FY2024), reflects a strategic shift toward **mitigating long-term pension risk** rather than increasing ongoing cash contributions.
- This approach reduces exposure to future pension liability volatility and related actuarial risks including discount rate fluctuations and mortality assumptions.
- The pension obligation reduction indirectly supports **capital allocation flexibility**, freeing resources for acquisitions and operational initiatives by lowering potential pension expense and liability volatility over time.
- Cash flow metrics support these actions without meaningful additional liquidity strain, consistent with management’s preservation of financial flexibility through FY2025.

### 1.3 Summary: Quantitative Pension Risk Management Conclusion

Columbus McKinnon’s pension management from March 2024 to March 2025 centers on a **quantitatively significant pension settlement charge of $22.1 million**, which reduces the pension liabilities and improves plan funded status in a non-cash manner. This aligns precisely with a risk management focus to hedge long-term financial volatility and preserve capital for strategic growth. Although exact funded status values are not explicitly tabulated in public filings, the magnitude and timing of settlement activities underpin a clear shift to **active pension liability reduction** without increasing immediate cash funding burdens.

## 2. Debt Reduction Patterns and Net Debt Analysis (March 2022 – March 2023)

| Date              | Total Debt (USD millions) | Cash & Equivalents (USD millions) | Net Debt (USD millions)                 |
|-------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| March 31, 2022    | $725.0                    | $329.2                           | $395.8                                 |
| March 31, 2023    | $471.6                    | $133.2                           | $338.4                                 |

- **Year-over-Year Net Debt Reduction** = $395.8M (2022) – $338.4M (2023) = **$57.4 million decrease (14.5% reduction)**

Debt reduction was funded primarily from operating cash flow and refinancing activities:

- **Debt repayments during FY2023:** Approx. $40.6 million
- **Operating cash flow FY2023:** $83.6 million
- **Free cash flow FY2023:** $71 million, enabling net debt paydown while investing in acquisitions

### 2.2 Acquisition-Related Debt and Refinancing

- The **montratec GmbH acquisition**, completed in May 2023 for approximately $115.7 million, was financed through an incremental Term Loan B facility increase (approx. $75 million) and a $45 million accounts receivable securitization facility.
- Despite such acquisition financing draws, the company’s debt reduction efforts prevailed, reflecting deliberate capital allocation discipline to balance leverage and growth financing.
- Amendments to the company’s credit agreements extended loan maturities to 2028 and revised covenants, affording operational flexibility amid active acquisition activity.
  
### 2.3 Financial Risk and Leverage Ratios

- Net leverage (Net Debt to Adjusted EBITDA) improved from roughly **2.4x at March 2022** to approximately **2.2x at March 2023**, moving closer to management’s strategic target near 2.0x.
- Strong adjusted EBITDA margins (~15.8%) and ongoing debt management initiatives support credit profile improvement.
- Management emphasized **deleverage as a critical capital allocation priority** throughout FY2023, maintaining a prudent balance between acquisition growth and leverage reduction.

### 2.4 Summary: Deleveraging Amid Growth Investments

Between March 2022 and March 2023, Columbus McKinnon successfully reduced net debt by about 14.5% or $57.4 million despite significant acquisition spending, including the montratec acquisition. This reduction was facilitated by strong cash flow generation and targeted debt repayments, confirming disciplined capital management focused on maintaining financial health and supporting strategic acquisition growth.

## 3. Employee Stock Options and Retention Risks as of March 31, 2025

### 3.1 Stock Option Counts, Pricing, and Underwater Option Estimation

- Official filings for FY2025, including the 2025 definitive proxy statement and 10-K annual report, do **not explicitly disclose** granular counts or percentages of underwater stock options.
- Available strike price data for outstanding stock options ranges from **approximately $15.16 to $54.26 per share**.
- Market price for CMCO common shares around March 31, 2025, generally ranged from **$14.40 to $16.00**, with recent trading closer to $14.40, indicating that options with strike prices above this range would be underwater.
  
### 3.2 Implication of Underwater Options

- Given strike price ranges and market prices, **it is estimated that over 50% of outstanding stock options were underwater** by March 31, 2025.
- "Underwater" means the exercise price exceeds the market price, resulting in zero intrinsic value for these options and diminished motivational effectiveness as a retention tool.
  
### 3.3 Employee Retention Risks and Compensation Strategy

- The lack of repricing or supplemental equity awards for underwater options in public disclosures suggests **potential increased retention risk, particularly among mid-to-senior management**, during a period of stock price pressure and operational challenges.
- The company terminated its Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP) in August 2025, which held approximately 131,903 shares, possibly reflecting adjustments in employee equity strategies.
- With a critical pending transformative acquisition (Kito Crosby), retention of key personnel is strategically vital; thus, the prevalence of underwater stock options could necessitate re-evaluation of long-term incentive design to mitigate talent attrition risks.

- **Net intangible assets** after accumulated amortization were approximately **$800 million to $850 million**, indicating accumulated amortization of ~$350 to $400 million.
- Goodwill totaled approximately **$710.8 million** prior to the Kito Crosby acquisition close.

### 4.2 Amortization Expense and Weighted-Average Amortization Rate

- Columbus McKinnon recorded approximately **$30 million** in intangible asset amortization expense for FY2025.
- The weighted-average amortization rate (amortization expense divided by gross intangible assets) calculates to:

  \[
  \text{Amortization Rate} = \frac{\$30M}{\$1,200M} = 2.5\%
  \]

  However, considering net intangible assets (approximately $825M average), the rate approximates closer to:

  \[
  \frac{\$30M}{\$825M} \approx 3.6\%

- Due to the timing of major acquisitions (e.g., Kito Crosby closed post FY2025), the amortization expense does not yet reflect the new acquisition-related assets.

- Industry norms and the company’s disclosures suggest:

  - Customer relationships amortize over approximately **14–15 years**.
  - Technology assets amortize over a shorter span (3 to 7 years).

- The lower overall amortization rate indicates **a portfolio weighted toward long-lived intangible assets**, primarily customer relationships and tradenames supporting recurring revenue and strategic positioning.

### 4.3 Strategic Assessment of Acquisition-Related Intangibles

- The acquired montratec GmbH intangible assets, including $33.5 million of customer relationships and $16.2 million of technology, have weighted average useful lives consistent with stated amortization rates.
- The Kito Crosby acquisition added approximately **$1.38 billion in intangible assets** (mainly customer relationships and trade names), not reflected in FY2025 numbers but pivotal for the company’s strategic scaling.
- The modest amortization rate relative to gross intangible asset balances reflects durable asset values extending income contributions and competitive differentiation beyond typical amortization cycles.
- This durable intangible asset base supports Columbus McKinnon’s capital allocation priority on **growth through strategic acquisitions** with earnings durability despite amortization expense.

## 5. Integrated Analysis: Linking Quantitative Findings to Capital Allocation and Risk Strategy

### 5.1 Capital Allocation and Financial Risk Management Cohesion

- **Pension Plan Management:** The $22.1 million non-cash pension settlement charge between March 2024 and March 2025 quantitatively evidences a risk mitigation approach that reduces long-term defined benefit plan liabilities, thereby lowering pension-related financial risk without increasing near-term cash outflows. This action conserves capital, maintaining flexibility for growth investments and acquisition funding.

- **Debt Reduction Discipline:** Despite significant acquisition activity (notably montratec acquisition), Columbus McKinnon reduced net debt by $57.4 million (14.5%) from March 2022 to March 2023 and improved net leverage closer to a 2.2x target. This balance of growth financing and deleveraging reflects disciplined capital stewardship to maintain credit health while pursuing transformative acquisitions.

- **Employee Retention and Stock Option Risks:** The estimated majority underwater stock option exposure as of March 31, 2025, suggests a measurable retention risk. The company’s equity incentive program lacks public disclosure of offsetting measures such as option repricing or new grants to address underwater status, potentially threatening key personnel stability during a pivotal growth and integration phase post-Kito Crosby acquisition. The termination of the ESOP also signals evolving compensation strategy considerations.

- **Acquisition-Related Intangible Asset Amortization:** The weighted-average amortization rate (~2.5–3.6%) and large intangible asset balances reflect durable, long-lived acquisition assets focused on customer relationships and technology. This intangible asset base underpins sustainable revenue streams and margin expansion, validating Columbus McKinnon’s growth-centric M&A strategy with manageable earnings amortization impacts.

### 5.2 Strategic Trade-Offs and Patterns

- The company balances **pension de-risking** and **debt deleveraging** efforts, maintaining capital for acquisitions without significant reliance on equity or dilutive financing.
- **Employee stock option underwater risk** flags compensation design as an area requiring strategic attention to safeguard human capital essential for integrating and scaling large acquisitions.
- The **intangible asset portfolio** resulting from acquisitions complements operational growth objectives with financially manageable amortization outlays, preserving earnings quality while expanding scale.
- Capital allocation reflects a **growth-then-deleveraging pattern**, backing large transformative acquisitions balanced by ongoing disciplined debt repayments and risk mitigation.

# Conclusion

Columbus McKinnon’s fiscal 2023–2025 capital allocation and financial risk strategy demonstrate a **quantified focus on balancing growth with prudent risk management**. Pension settlement activities actively reduce defined benefit plan liabilities, improving financial stability. Simultaneously, despite acquisition financing needs, net debt was significantly reduced, showcasing disciplined deleveraging. However, the prevalence of underwater stock options constrains retention incentives, posing a tangible risk during critical strategic integration phases. The company maintains a durable intangible assets base with moderate amortization rates, supporting sustained cash flows from recent acquisitions. Overall, the company’s capital allocation reflects a strategy to manage financial risk effectively while aggressively pursuing growth through acquisitions and operational investments, recognizing the need for ongoing compensation strategy recalibration to ensure employee retention and long-term value creation.
