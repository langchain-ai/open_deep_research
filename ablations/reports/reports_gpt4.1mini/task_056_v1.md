# New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market Analysis (2018–2024)

## Market Size and Growth (2018–2024)

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market, focusing strictly on construction and structural applications, has shown nuanced growth from 2018 to 2024. According to multiple authoritative sources including Steel Construction New Zealand (SCNZ) and IBISWorld:

- **Revenue:**  
  The structural steel fabricating sector’s revenue was estimated at around NZD 1.8 billion by 2025, though the industry witnessed some decline in the early 2020s, specifically a CAGR of approximately -6.0% between 2020 and 2025. However, forecasts indicate a modest recovery with a CAGR of about 2.1% projected from 2021 to 2026, underpinned by rising infrastructure spending and technological advances. Grand View Research placed the market revenue at roughly NZD 78 million (USD 50.3 million) in 2024 for structural steel, highlighting the niche but significant market size specifically for construction applications.  
- **Employment:**  
  Employment figures in the sector approximate 5,000 workers across roughly 153 fabrication companies nationally (SCNZ). IBISWorld data reports about 4,100 employees in 2025, with slight declines forecasted due to automation and workforce consolidation trends. The average firm employs around 15 people, underscoring the SME-dominated industry.  
- **Tonnage Fabricated:**  
  The structural steel industry’s annual fabrication volume has expanded from about 120,000 tonnes of capacity in 2018 to an estimated 150,000 tonnes by 2023, with actual production around 100,000 tonnes annually given spare capacity of about 28%. Notably, structural steel constitutes over 50% of the multi-level construction market nationally and dominates Christchurch’s market with over 80% share due to seismic performance needs.  
- **Industry Characteristics:**  
  Investment in modern fabrication facilities and quality schemes, such as the Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC), has been critical to industry resilience and capability. Geographic concentration is notable with Auckland hosting the largest cluster (99 units), followed by Canterbury (45) and Waikato (30) fabrication units.

Market dynamics are influenced by external factors such as inflation, labor market shortages, and weather-related rebuilding demand, impacting the overall construction sector which contributed approximately NZD 17.6 billion (6.3% of GDP) and employed over 308,000 people as of mid-2023 ([1],[2],[3],[4],[5],[6],[8],[12],[14]).

## Import Tariffs on Raw Steel (HS Codes 7207–7216) and Impact on Fabricators

- **Tariff Rates:**  
  New Zealand applies low to moderate import tariffs on raw steel under harmonized system codes 7207 to 7216. WTO data confirms bound tariffs average 9.5%, but MFN applied rates for 2024 average about 1.9%, with many steel items subject to 0%, 5%, or 10% tariffs depending on type. The customs tariff schedule maintains some tariffs at 5–10%, though trade agreements, including free trade agreements (FTAs) with major trading partners, reduce many duties significantly or to zero.  
- **Effective Impact on Margins:**  
  Tariffs modestly increase the input costs of imported steel-based products, potentially squeezing profit margins for local fabricators who partially rely on imported steel. However, due to New Zealand Steel’s significant domestic production (650,000 tonnes annually) and local fabrication capacity, dependence on imports is limited, which mitigates tariff impact locally. Fabricators benefit from having a strong domestic supply chain that buffers against import cost volatility.  
- **Broader Effects:**  
  Nonetheless, tariffs influence production economics, encourage diversification of suppliers, and complicate cost forecasting. With international steel price volatility persisting post-COVID and amid geopolitical tensions, tariff policy remains a notable risk factor.  
- **Sources:** Customs tariff documents, MBIE trade reports, WTO data, and industry commentary ([17],[18],[19],[20],[21],[22])

## Major Infrastructure Projects and Structural Steel Demand

### 1. City Rail Link (CRL), Auckland

- One of New Zealand’s largest infrastructure undertakings, the CRL consists of a 3.45 km twin tunnel under Auckland’s city center. Scheduled for completion around 2024–2026, it is expected to significantly increase rail capacity and support urban development with sustainability objectives.  
- **Steel Demand:** Approximately 25,000 tonnes of reinforcing and structural steel, much supplied and fabricated locally (Pacific Steel fabricated specialized 50mm bars with steel from New Zealand Steel).  
- **Project Value:** NZD 5.493 billion  
- **Sources:** City Rail Link official updates, New Zealand Steel news, and project reports [26],[27],[28],[30]

### 2. East West Link, Auckland

- A new four-lane road including cycleways, enhancing transport efficiency across Māngere Inlet. Early works commenced in 2024.  
- **Steel Demand:** Estimated in the thousands of tonnes for structural steel in bridges and roadway construction.  
- **Project Value:** NZD 1.85 billion  
- **Sources:** Infrastructure New Zealand, tender portals, and official announcements [33]

### 3. Takitimu North Link, Tauranga

- A 14 km highway enhancement project aimed at improving regional connectivity, with staged openings expected culminating around 2026.  
- **Steel Use:** Significant steel needed for bridges, retaining walls, and road structures.  
- **Sources:** Infrastructure project listings [33]

Additional large infrastructure projects supporting steel demand include the Ōtaki to North of Levin highway upgrades (NZD 1.5 billion), O Mahurangi Penlink toll road, and Te Ara o Te Ata – Mt Messenger Bypass with bridge and tunnel construction to 2027 ([1],[26],[33]).

## Strategic Risks and Opportunities (Construction and Structural Fabrication)

### Top Risks

1. **Tariff Exposure**  
   Even modest tariffs (5–10%) on imported raw steel can raise input costs and reduce margin flexibility, especially for fabricators relying on steel products not produced domestically. Fluctuating tariff policies globally add uncertainty. ([17],[20],[22])

2. **Supply Chain Concentration and Disruptions**  
   The dependence on limited raw steel suppliers, including New Zealand Steel and some overseas sources, exposes fabricators to supply delays, price volatility, and freight cost spikes. COVID-19 and geopolitical tensions have exacerbated these risks. ([1],[2],[25])

3. **Project Pipeline Volatility and Funding Risks**  
   The construction sector experiences cyclical risks tied to government infrastructure budgets, private sector investment confidence, and regulatory delays. Any deferment or cancellation impacts fabricator order books and cash flows. ([14],[36])

### Top Opportunities

1. **Growing Infrastructure Pipeline**  
   Government commitments to infrastructure, including projects like CRL and transport upgrades, ensure steady medium-term demand for structural steel. PPP (Public-Private Partnership) reforms are poised to increase private investment in construction. ([14],[29],[33])

2. **Technological and Sustainability Advances**  
   Adoption of advanced fabrication technologies, enhanced quality management, and sustainability initiatives (e.g., electric arc furnace facilities reducing carbon by up to 55%) position fabricators to secure competitive advantages amid increasing environmental regulation. ([2],[32],[34],[35])

3. **Expansion of Domestic Steelmaking Capacity**  
   New greenfield initiatives, such as National Green Steel Limited in Waikato that aims to produce 200,000 tonnes annually from recycled steel, will bolster local raw material availability, reduce carbon footprint, and improve supply reliability. ([31],[34])

Additional opportunities include close collaboration via Early Contractor Involvement (ECI) to reduce costs and timeframes, and workforce development strategies to address skilled labor shortages. ([1],[6],[37])

## Conclusion

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication sector serving the construction industry exhibits a mature yet evolving market from 2018 through 2024. While historically challenged by global commodity price pressures, employment shifts, and moderate tariff impacts, the sector is supported by:

- A sizeable domestic production capacity and a cluster of experienced fabricators  
- Strong government-driven infrastructure project pipelines demanding structural steel  
- Emerging sustainability and technology initiatives enhancing productivity and carbon footprint reduction  

Strategic risks remain from tariff volatility, supply chain concentration, and project pipeline uncertainties, but fabricators who invest in local capacity, innovation, and collaborative project delivery are well-positioned to capitalize on growth opportunities. Continued monitoring of tariff policies and supply chain resilience will be critical for industry stakeholders.
