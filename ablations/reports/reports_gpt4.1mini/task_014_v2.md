## 3. Post-Tax Yield Calculations with Legal and TDS Considerations  

### 3.1 Taxation Overview for NCD Interest Income (FY 2025-26 / AY 2026-27)

- **Taxation basis:** Interest income on both listed and unlisted NCDs is taxable under “Income from Other Sources” at the investor's slab rate (normal tax rules apply). No special exemption or rebate is available for this income [1][3][5].

- **Relevant legal provisions:**
  - **Section 193:** TDS on interest on securities (includes listed NCDs) at 10% threshold over ₹10,000 interest.  
  - **Section 194A:** TDS on other interest income, including unlisted NCDs, threshold ₹5,000 interest.  
  - **Section 80TTB:** Senior citizens can claim a deduction up to ₹1,00,000 on interest income from deposits, but NCD interest generally falls outside this deduction and is fully taxable [6][7][10][15].

- **TDS treatment:**
  - TDS at 10% applies when interest income exceeds thresholds, with higher rates (20%) if PAN is not furnished.
  - Senior citizens benefit from a higher TDS exemption limit of ₹1 lakh (as opposed to ₹10,000 for generic investors).  
  - Form 15G/15H filing can be used to avoid TDS where income is below taxable limits.  
  - Actual tax payable depends on the overall income slab and regime opted (old or new tax regime) [7][8][16][21].

## 6. Regulatory Discussion on Tax Provisions and Exemptions for NCDs vs Other Fixed Income Instruments  

- NCDs fall under domestic debt instruments regulated primarily by SEBI and Income Tax Act provisions. Interest income is always taxable at slab rates with TDS compliance mandatory for payers based on limits [4][6][23].

- **Distinctions from other instruments:**  
  - Tax-free bonds issued by government entities enjoy exemption under Section 10(15)(iv)(h) and do not attract income tax on interest.  
  - Bank deposits enjoy Section 80TTB benefits partially, but NCD interest generally does not [5][23].  
  - Capital gains taxation depends on holding period; listed NCDs qualify for long-term capital gains tax at 12.5% after 12 months, a tax advantage over deposits [3][24].

- **Investor segmentation:**  
  - Senior Citizens benefit from higher TDS thresholds and certain rebate provisions but must still pay tax on NCD interest.  
  - Institutional investors typically face withholding tax obligations at source; non-resident investors’ tax rates governed by Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA) [10][16].

## 7. Portfolio Construction Framework  

### 7.1 Credit Quality Allocation  

| Allocation Tier | Credit Rating Bucket      | Suggested Portfolio Weighting* | Rationale                                   |
|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Core            | AAA / AA+                | 40% - 60%                     | Capital preservation; lowest credit risk.  |
| Income Enhancers| A+ / A                   | 25% - 35%                     | Enhanced yield with moderate risk.          |
| Opportunistic   | BBB                      | 5% - 15%                      | Yield pick-up, higher risk exposure; active monitoring essential. |

\*Weights to be calibrated per client risk tolerance and regulatory limits [14][31].

### 7.2 Tenor Laddering Strategy  

- Ladder maturity across short (1-3 yrs), medium (3-5 yrs), and long (5-10 yrs) tenors to mitigate interest rate risk and ensure liquidity for reinvestments.  
- Favor shorter tenors for BBB-rated bonds to limit duration risk; longer tenors appropriate for AAA-rated bonds with price stability.  
- Align maturity schedule to anticipated cash flow needs in retirement portfolios, ensuring monthly or quarterly coupon payments matching liabilities [15][31].

### 7.3 Security Enforcement & Exposure Limits  

- Prefer **secured NCDs** (asset-backed with first charge) for retiree portfolios to enhance recovery prospects in default scenarios.  
- Limit exposure to issuers with debt/equity ratio >1.5x to minimize financial risk concentration unless balanced by strong credit enhancements or guarantees.  
- Monitor issuer financials continuously for signs of leverage escalation or asset quality deterioration.  
- Regulatory limits for institutional investments in NCDs apply: typically no more than 10% of the issuer’s aggregate debt securities outstanding or limits set by internal credit policy [6][14].

## 8. Qualitative Interpretation of Yield Spreads and Emerging Profitability/Leverage Trends  

- Wider credit spreads in BBB-rated NBFCs highlight market concerns over asset quality and leverage risk; spreads serve as leading indicators of credit stress and economic cycles [11][14].

- Narrowing spreads in AA and AAA segments, coupled with stable or improving issuer leverage ratios, indicate strong credit fundamentals and market appetite for high-quality assets even amidst moderate inflation and interest rate volatility [1][6].

- Yield compression observed in high-grade NCDs reflects competitive corporate borrowing costs and steady monetary policy, endorsing their role as defensive fixed income in institutional and retiree portfolios.

- Elevated leverage in several NBFC issuers, especially Hero FinCorp and Muthoot Mercantile, signals increased vigilance for credit deterioration despite strong current profitability and provisioning buffers. This dynamic underscores the need for stringent risk monitoring and conservative portfolio allocation to these issuers [13][16].
