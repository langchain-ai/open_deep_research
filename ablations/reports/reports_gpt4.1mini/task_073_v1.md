# Comparative Analysis of Autism Diagnostic Criteria: DSM-5 (2013) vs ICD-11 (2019)

This report provides a thorough, evidence-based comparison of the autism diagnostic criteria between the DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (2019). The analysis emphasizes core social-communication components, sensory processing definitions and their diagnostic weight, severity specifiers, age/onset requirements, functional impairment thresholds, and other relevant clinical and advocacy perspectives. It integrates peer-reviewed evidence, clinical guidelines, advocacy views, safety considerations, and reimbursement implications related to autism diagnosis in both manuals.

## A. Side-by-Side Table of Diagnostic Criteria: DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019)

| **Diagnostic Domain**                           | **DSM-5 (2013)**                                                                                                         | **ICD-11 (2019)**                                                                                                                    |
|------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Core Social-Communication Components**       | Requires **persistent deficits across 3 areas**:                                                                           
- Social-emotional reciprocity (e.g., difficulties in back-and-forth social interaction)  
- Nonverbal communicative behaviors (e.g., poor eye contact, body language)  
- Developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships (e.g., difficulty adjusting to social contexts)  
These deficits must be present from early developmental periods but sometimes manifest fully later when social demands exceed capacities. Criteria combine social communication and interaction into a unified domain. | Requires persistent deficits in initiating and sustaining social communication and reciprocal social interactions atypical for age, intellectual development, and sociocultural context.  
Emphasis on verbal and nonverbal communication abnormalities, social awareness, and difficulty maintaining peer relationships. Allows flexibility depending on intellectual and language profiles. Clinical judgment emphasized over strict symptom counts. |
| **Sensory Processing Differences: Definition & Weighting** | Explicitly included as part of the **restricted, repetitive behaviors (RRB)** criteria (Criterion B).  
At least two RRB symptoms required, including hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment.  
Sensory symptoms are given equal diagnostic weight with other RRBs, making them a core and explicit ASD diagnostic feature. | Sensory sensitivities noted under restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behavior but without detailed subcriteria.  
Described as lifelong, excessive, or persistent hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli atypical for age and sociocultural context.  
Sensory symptoms are recognized but less explicitly weighted; guidelines use broader, less operationalized language. |
| **Severity Specifiers**                          | Three levels of severity separately for 2 domains:  
- Social Communication: Level 1 (requiring support), Level 2 (substantial support), Level 3 (very substantial support)  
- Restricted/repetitive behaviors: same 3 levels   
Severity relates to required support and guides treatment planning. | No formal, standardized severity levels.  
Functional impact described qualitatively across life domains.  
Severity often inferred by clinician based on intellectual and language ability profiles and observed impairments, but no discrete severity coding system. |
| **Age / Onset Requirements**                    | Symptoms must be present in the **early developmental period** but may not be fully manifest until social demands exceed capacities.  
Onset is flexible but anchored developmentally. | Onset during **developmental period**, typically early childhood.  
Symptoms may be subtle or compensated early on and only manifest fully later when demands increase. Wording is broader and less prescriptive compared to DSM-5. |
| **Functional Impairment Thresholds**            | Symptoms must cause **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning.  
This impairment is essential and clearly operationalized in criteria. | Symptoms cause **significant impairment** in personal, social, educational, occupational, or other important functioning areas.  
Less operationalized; clinical judgment required.  
Masking and compensation acknowledged as complicating assessment of impairment. |
| **Additional Diagnostic Features**              | Combines social-communication deficits into one domain.  
Requires specific numbers of symptoms in each domain (all three social communication deficits plus at least two RRBs).  
Severity specifiers incorporated into diagnosis. | Emphasizes clinical judgment and assessment of profiles based on intellectual and language abilities.  
No strict symptom count thresholds; diagnosis allowed with varied symptom combinations.  
Profiles used to subtype based on intellectual/language capacities. |

### Summary

- **DSM-5** offers more structured, quantitatively defined criteria with explicit sensory symptom inclusion, mandatory symptom counts, and severity levels.
- **ICD-11** is broader and more flexible, relying on clinical judgment, with less explicit weighting of sensory symptoms and no formal severity specifiers. It reflects a more heterogeneous conceptualization of autism consistent with global applicability.

## B. Evidence Synthesis: Peer-Reviewed Studies Comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 Autism Diagnostic Criteria

### 1. Systematic Review / Meta-Analysis

**Ricci et al. (2025)** [Systematic Review, Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews]  
- **Objective**: Evaluate diagnostic accuracy (sensitivity, specificity) of ASD assessment tools aligned with DSM-5 criteria.  
- **Method**: Meta-analysis of standardized diagnostic instruments (e.g., ADOS-2, CARS) in children/adolescents.  
- **Sample**: Multiple studies aggregated, tens of thousands of participants.  
- **Findings**: ADOS-2 demonstrated moderate sensitivity (~0.90) and specificity (~0.69); overall, tools assist but don't replace clinical judgment. Diagnostic tools based on DSM-5 show variable accuracy; heterogeneous symptom presentations impact reliability.  
- **Quality**: Moderate GRADE quality; limitations due to heterogeneity of included studies and lack of validated ICD-11 aligned tools.  
- **Implication**: DSM-5 criteria supported by empirical tools; no equivalent extensive validation data for ICD-11 criteria available to date.  

### 2. Empirical Study 1

**Blázquez Hinojosa et al. (2021)** [Retrospective clinical study, Revista de Psiquiatría y Salud Mental]  
- **Design**: Retrospective analysis of DSM-5 vs DSM-IV-TR in 118 children/adolescents, using Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised (ADI-R) data.  
- **Key Statistics**: DSM-5 sensitivity 0.69–1.00 (higher in females), specificity 0.64–0.73 across subgroups.  
- **Findings**: DSM-5 excludes many PDD-NOS cases, reclassifying some into Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder; more pharmacological treatment seen with ASD diagnosis (DSM-5).  
- **Relevance**: Reflects stricter DSM-5 criteria impact on diagnosis; no ICD-11 direct comparison but highlights challenges with narrowing diagnosis.  
- **GRADE**: Moderate quality (retrospective design, clinical sample size).  

### 3. Empirical Study 2

**McPartland et al. (2012)** [Field Trial Secondary Analysis, J Am Acad Child Adolesc Psychiatry]  
- **Design**: Analysis of DSM-IV field trial data (N=933), applying proposed DSM-5 criteria to ASD diagnoses.  
- **Results**: DSM-5 sensitivity 60.6%, specificity 94.9%. Sensitivity higher in low IQ (70%) vs normative IQ (46%). Asperger’s and PDD-NOS sensitivity low (25% and 28%).  
- **Implications**: DSM-5 offers higher specificity at cost of lower sensitivity; concerns for underdiagnosing higher-functioning autism, milder presentations, or PDD subtypes.  
- **GRADE**: Moderate quality due to large sample, retrospective approach, and lack of longitudinal follow-up.  

### 4. Empirical Study 3

**Mahajan et al. (2019)** [Prospective cohort, PMC article]  
- **Design**: Evaluated DSM-5 vs DSM-IV criteria sensitivity and specificity in 1,061 preschool children.  
- **Findings**: DSM-5 criteria yielded sensitivity 0.95, specificity 0.78, better than DSM-IV PDD-NOS. Agreement κ=0.78 with autistic disorder but moderate for PDD-NOS. Females and milder cases more likely under-identified.  
- **GRADE**: Moderate to high quality, large prospective cohort.  

### 5. Comparative Clinical Critiques on ICD-11 Diagnostic Utility

**Schmitt et al. (2023)** [Nature Neuroscience Perspective]  
- **Focus**: Critical evaluation of ICD-11 ASD criteria, emphasizing extreme heterogeneity and broad symptom permutations (300+ combinations).  
- **Concerns**: ICD-11’s lack of symptom specificity reduces diagnostic precision, potentially elevates false positives, and complicates research reproducibility.  
- **Sensory Issues**: ICD-11 includes sensory processing differences but less clearly and formally than DSM-5, potentially under-recognizing these clinically.  
- **GRADE**: Expert consensus piece; calls for more empirical validation.

## C. Clinical Guidelines by WHO, APA, AAP, and NICE on ICD-11 Adoption and Sensory Processing Weighting

### World Health Organization (WHO)  
- ICD-11 ASD criteria officially endorsed in 2019 and intended for WHO member countries’ adoption starting 2022.  
- Diagnosis emphasizes social communication deficits and restricted, repetitive behaviors including sensory sensitivities as part of core features; however, sensory symptoms are integrated in RRB domain, not separated out.  
- ICD-11 highlights five autism profiles based on intellectual and language abilities, promoting clinical flexibility globally.  
- WHO underscores diagnosis during developmental periods with functional impairment, though operationalization is flexible to accommodate diverse cultural/healthcare settings.  
- WHO fact sheets and materials promote understanding sensory issues as part of autism but stop short of formal sensory disorder diagnoses or separate criterial domains.  

### American Psychiatric Association (APA)  
- APA’s DSM-5 remains the standard in the U.S., focusing on structured criteria including sensory symptoms in RRBs.  
- No formal endorsement of ICD-11 ASD criteria; APA acknowledges variability and ongoing comparisons but favors DSM-5 for clinical and research consistency.  
- APA recognizes challenges in functional impairment assessment and camouflaging, inviting consideration of these issues without revising ICD-11 adoption stances.  

### American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)  
- AAP guidelines and clinical reports recommend DSM-5 criteria exclusively, emphasizing early screening and diagnostic evaluation primarily shaped around DSM-5.  
- Sensory processing differences recognized clinically but categorized under RRBs rather than as separate disorders.  
- Caution urged around sensory integration therapies due to limited evidence.  
- No recommendation to transition to ICD-11 diagnostic criteria to date, awaiting clearer validation and implementation pathways.  

### National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE)  
- NICE guidelines (CG128, 142, 170) primarily use DSM-5-based frameworks; no formal recommendation for ICD-11 adoption at this time.  
- Guidelines discuss sensory sensitivities as common in ASD but embedded within broader clinical features, not isolated diagnostically.  
- NICE encourages diagnostic assessments integrating sensory processing evaluation to support individualized management.  
- Monitoring ongoing research into ICD-11 criteria and sensory diagnostic weighting for future guideline updates.

## D. Advocacy Organizations’ Positions on Neurodiversity-Affirming Manuals

### Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)  
- Prioritizes neurodiversity-affirming approaches recognizing autism as a natural variation rather than disorder.  
- Critiques DSM-5 for its medicalized, deficit-focused lens but acknowledges sensory symptom inclusion as beneficial.  
- Expresses reluctance about ICD-11’s broad, heterogenous framework potentially undermining clarity but appreciates its international accessibility.  
- Calls for diagnostic criteria to explicitly validate sensory experiences and sensory needs, favoring manuals that acknowledge strengths alongside challenges.  

### National Autistic Society (UK)  
- Supports diagnostic clarity to improve access to services, favoring DSM-5’s structured criteria but noting ICD-11’s clinical utility in global contexts.  
- Advocates for inclusion of sensory processing differences as core diagnostic features, aligning with DSM-5’s explicit criteria more than ICD-11’s broader approach.  
- Emphasizes individualized assessments that respect autistic identity and neurodiversity, including sensory profiles.  

### Autism Speaks  
- Uses DSM-5 criteria predominantly in clinical, research, and advocacy spheres.  
- Highlights sensory processing differences as a core diagnostic domain in DSM-5, which they view as improving diagnosis and services.  
- Notes ICD-11’s adoption by WHO but warns that inconsistent criteria across countries may impact research comparability.  
- Supports continued refinement of diagnostic systems to better capture sensory processing variations and functional impact.

## E. Diagnostic Safety Risks Related to DSM-5 vs ICD-11 Criteria Differences

- **Misdiagnosis and Overdiagnosis**: ICD-11’s broad symptom permutations risk increased false positives and overdiagnosis, especially in individuals with non-autistic neurodevelopmental or psychiatric traits overlapping with sensory sensitivities or social communication difficulties [Schmitt et al., 2023].  
- **Underdiagnosis**: DSM-5’s stricter social communication monothetic criteria and requirement of multiple RRB symptoms including sensory reactivity can exclude higher-functioning individuals, females, or those with mild sensory presentations, leading to under-recognition and limited service access [McPartland et al., 2012].  
- **Sensitivity to Sensory-Focused Presentations**: DSM-5 explicitly includes sensory hypo-/hyper-reactivity, increasing diagnostic accuracy for autistic individuals with predominant sensory symptoms. ICD-11 recognizes sensory sensitivities but less precisely, which may impair identification of sensory-driven ASD presentations or result in inconsistent diagnoses.  
- **Inappropriate Interventions**: Variability in diagnosis criteria affects treatment planning—overly broad ICD-11 criteria may lead to interventions without ASD-specific tailoring, while DSM-5’s narrower criteria may limit access for some needing support for sensory or social challenges.  
- **Masking and Compensation**: Both manuals complicate assessment, but ICD-11’s flexible criteria explicitly acknowledge camouflaging behaviors impacting functional impairment evaluation, possibly enhancing sensitivity but risking false positives.

## F. Reimbursement and Coding Practices in US and EU Payer Systems

- **United States**:  
  - The DSM-5 diagnoses are coded using ICD-10-CM codes (e.g., F84.0 for Autistic Disorder, F84.5 for Asperger’s formerly, now subsumed under F84.0 in DSM-5).  
  - ICD-11 has been approved by WHO, but US healthcare systems have yet to fully integrate ICD-11 coding; transitions are gradual and complex.  
  - DSM-5 ASD diagnoses retain primary billing relevance and insurance coverage; ICD-10-CM codes remain the standard for reimbursement despite DSM-5 diagnostic framework use clinically.  
  - Adoption of ICD-11 criteria has minimal immediate impact on US reimbursement; payers rely on DSM-5 aligned ICD-10-CM codes for claims and service authorization.  

- **European Union (EU)**:  
  - EU countries are progressively adopting ICD-11 coding in clinical and administrative reporting.  
  - ICD-11’s ASD codes (6A02) categorize autism with subtypes based on intellectual and language ability, aligning with clinical profiles.  
  - Reimbursement and social service eligibility in many European countries rely on ICD codes; ICD-11 adoption is expected to improve diagnostic uniformity but may introduce transitional challenges.  
  - ICD-11’s broader criteria may affect prevalence estimations and thus influence resource allocation but do not inherently change coverage policies, which depend on national health systems.  

- **Implications of ICD-11 Adoption**:  
  - Transition to ICD-11 coding requires updates in electronic health records, billing infrastructure, and clinician training.  
  - ICD-11’s flexible criteria may create variability in insurance authorization without clear severity specifiers like DSM-5’s, potentially complicating funding decisions for ASD-related interventions.  
  - Both systems acknowledge the importance of documenting functional impairment and support needs for reimbursement, irrespective of diagnostic manual used.

# Summary

DSM-5 and ICD-11 represent converging yet distinct conceptualizations of autism spectrum disorder. DSM-5 emphasizes structured, symptom-count-based criteria with explicit sensory symptom inclusion and severity specifiers, facilitating diagnostic reliability and clarity for clinical and research applications. ICD-11 offers broader, flexible, and globally applicable descriptions prioritizing clinical judgment and heterogeneous symptom profiles that recognize intellectual and language differences but provide less explicit sensory symptom criteria or severity coding.

Evidence suggests DSM-5 criteria yield higher specificity but risk underdiagnosing milder and sensory-predominant presentations, while ICD-11’s broader criteria may raise false positive risks and diagnostic heterogeneity. Clinical guidelines currently favor DSM-5 frameworks for diagnosis and management, with cautious monitoring of ICD-11 impacts. Autism advocacy groups generally view DSM-5’s sensory inclusions as favorable yet encourage further affirmation of neurodiversity principles and recognition of sensory processing differences.

Diagnostic safety concerns center around balancing sensitivity and specificity to minimize mis- and underdiagnosis, especially for sensory-focused presentations. Reimbursement systems remain largely contingent on ICD-10-CM coding in the US and evolving ICD-11 adoption in Europe, with practical implications tied to severity documentation and diagnostic clarity.
