# Comprehensive Report on the Definition and Eligibility Criteria of Independent Directors under NASDAQ Listing Standards (2026)

---

## I. Definition of Independent Director Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605

### A. Core Definition

Per NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2), an **independent director** is a director whom the board of directors has affirmatively determined has **no material relationship with the company**, either directly or as a related person, that could interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director. This definition embodies a **two-part test**:

1. **Bright-line objective criteria**, including specific disqualifying relationships and transactions, that presumptively negate independence (e.g., employment, compensation, significant business relationships).
2. An **affirmative subjective determination by the board**, annually assessing all relevant facts and circumstances and affirming independence — the ownership of company stock alone, even if significant, does not impair independence, as owning shares aligns the director’s interest with that of shareholders.

The independence determination must be **documented** in board resolutions and disclosed in proxy statements or annual reports according to SEC Regulation S-K Item 407(a) requirements, with transparency to investors.[1][2]

---

## II. Detailed Eligibility Criteria and Disqualification Mechanisms

The eligibility to qualify as an independent director hinges on avoiding **specified disqualifying relationships** during defined **lookback periods**, with **quantified thresholds** for compensation and business relationships that assess the materiality of ties.

### A. Employment Relationship

- A director is **not independent** if they or an immediate family member have been employed as an **executive officer** or other employee of the company or its subsidiaries within the **last three years**. The three-year lookback runs **backwards from the date of board determination**.[1][5]

- Temporary absence of employment (e.g., sabbaticals) does not alter this lookback.

### B. Compensation Thresholds

- A director or immediate family member is disqualified if they received more than **$120,000 in direct compensation** from the company or its subsidiaries during **any consecutive 12-month period within the last three years**, excluding director fees.

- Compensation categories **excluded** from this threshold calculation:

  - Fees received for board or committee service (including committee fees).
  - Pension or other forms of deferred compensation not contingent on continued service.
  - Payments under broad-based plans available generally to employees (e.g., 401(k) matching).
  - Compensation received by a family member who is a **non-executive employee** generally does not impair independence unless materially significant and subject to board review.

- This $120,000 threshold applies as a **bright-line rule but is supplemented by the board’s subjective judgment**, which may consider complex or borderline cases.

### C. Business Relationships and Financial Dealings

- A director or an entity with which the director is affiliated is presumed not independent if any of the following occur within the last **three fiscal years**:

  - Any business dealings (direct or indirect payments) between the company (including subsidiaries) and the director or the director’s affiliated entity exceeded the greater of **$200,000 or 5% of the recipient’s annual gross revenues** for any fiscal year within that period.

- This includes payments both **to and from** the director or their affiliated entities.

- The quantitative test applies to all types of business arrangements, such as customer/supplier relationships, consulting, joint ventures, or lending.

- Minor or negligible dealings not meeting thresholds are excluded; however, cumulative business dealings aggregating over thresholds in any single fiscal year trigger disqualification.[5][8]

### D. Auditor Affiliation

- Directors or immediate family members who are current partners, principals, or employees of the company’s independent registered public accounting firm, or who were so within the last **three years**, are disqualified.

- This rule also disqualifies any director receiving direct compensation from the auditor for services, other than for board-related work.

- This is intended to prevent conflicts of interest between the board’s oversight and the auditor’s independence.[1][5]

### E. Familial Relationships

The NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2) defines **“immediate family members”** with a wide but precise scope, as independence disqualifications extend to them:

- Included immediate family members:

  - Spouse or domestic partner.
  - Parents, including mother- and father-in-law.
  - Children, including sons- and daughters-in-law.
  - Siblings, including brothers- and sisters-in-law.
  - Anyone (other than domestic employees) who resides in the director’s household.

- **Explicit exclusions**:

  - Stepchildren who do **not** reside in the director’s household.
  - Domestic employees living with the director.

- An immediate family member who is currently, or was within the past three years, an **executive officer** of the company or its subsidiaries disqualifies the director.

- Similarly, an immediate family member receiving more than $120,000 compensation in any consecutive 12-month period triggers disqualification.

- Board judgment may consider borderline cases, but the default is strict inclusion.[10][11][13]

### F. Cross-Company Compensation Committee Conflicts

- Directors serving on a company’s compensation committee must not simultaneously serve on the compensation committee of another entity where any of the company’s executive officers serve as directors, to prevent conflicts of interest involving executive compensation.[5]

## IV. Independent Director Requirements by Company Type

### A. Regular Domestic Nasdaq-Listed Companies (“Regular Issuers”)

- Must have a **majority of independent directors** on the board.

- Audit, Compensation, and Nominating/Governance Committees must be composed **entirely of independent directors**, with exceptions only for limited cure periods or transitional arrangements.

- Audit Committee:

  - At least three members, all independent under NASDAQ and SEC rules.

  - At least one member must be **financially sophisticated**; at least one designated as an **audit committee financial expert** (per SEC).

  - Cannot receive consulting or advisory fees aside from board/committee fees.

  - Has authority over appointment, compensation, and oversight of auditors.

- Compensation Committee:

  - Entirely independent, following NASDAQ Rule 5605(d).

  - May hire consultants, disclosed with conflicts-of-interest considerations.

- Nominating/Governance Committee:

  - Entirely independent; responsible for director nominations, governance policies.

- The board must conduct an **annual independence determination** and disclose it as per SEC rules.[1][3][4][5]

### B. Controlled Companies (NASDAQ Rule 5615(c)(1))

- Defined as companies where an individual, group, or other company owns **more than 50% of voting power**.

- Exempt from:

  - Majority independent director requirement.

  - Independent Compensation Committee and Nominating Committee composition.

- Not exempt from:

  - Independent Audit Committee requirements.

  - Holding independent director sessions without management present.

- Must publicly disclose their controlled company status and the exemptions relied upon.

- Transition timeline when ceasing controlled company status: must achieve full compliance (majority independent board and independent committees) **within one year**.[18][30]

### C. Initial Public Offering (IPO) Companies (Rule 5615(b))

- IPO listing companies benefit from **phase-in schedules**:

  - At least one independent audit committee member at the time of listing.

  - Full audit committee independence (3 independent members) **within 12 months** post-IPO.

  - Majority independent board and independent compensation and nominating committees also phased in within **one year**.

- IPO companies must comply immediately with **SEC Rule 10A-3** audit committee independence — no phase-in or exemptions for these stricter audit committee independence requirements.

- Phased compliance allows a practical transition but with strict timelines and disclosure requirements.[16][17][19]

### D. Foreign Private Issuers (FPIs)

- May follow **home country governance practices** instead of most NASDAQ independence requirements except for **audit committee independence**, which must comply fully with SEC Rule 10A-3.

- Must disclose all NASDAQ corporate governance requirements not followed and provide detailed home country governance explanations in filings such as Form 20-F.

- Must have an audit committee with at least three independent directors under SEC and NASDAQ standards for audit committees.

- New SEC Section 16(a) insider reporting requirements apply to directors and officers of FPIs (effective March 18, 2026), except for issuers incorporated in qualifying jurisdictions with comparable insider reporting regimes.

- Compensation and nomination committee independence requirements are generally exempt or modified but must be transparently disclosed.[14][20][22][23]

### E. Registered Investment Companies and Business Development Companies (BDCs)

- Registered investment companies are generally **exempt** from NASDAQ Rule 5605 independence requirements due to their regulation under the Investment Company Act of 1940.

- Business Development Companies, however, **must comply fully** with NASDAQ independence and committee composition rules, including audit committee requirements.[18]

## V. Layered and Overlapping Independence Standards Including Committees

### A. Audit Committee Independence (NASDAQ Rule 5605(c) + SEC Rule 10A-3)

- **Stricter standards apply to audit committee members** than to general directors.

- Must be independent under both NASDAQ and SEC standards.

- Prohibited from receiving any compensation from the company other than director fees and fixed retirement benefits.

- Direct or indirect relationships with the company’s auditor are disqualifying, including employment within the last 3 years.

- Audit committees must have at least three members, all independent, with at least one financial expert.

- SEC Rule 10A-3, enforced for Nasdaq-listed companies, has **no phase-in period** for audit committee independence upon initial listing.

- These layered criteria ensure audit committee objectivity critical to financial reporting oversight.[1][4][5][26][27]

### B. Compensation Committee Independence (NASDAQ Rule 5605(d))

- Requires independent directors exclusively, with **limited exceptions**:

  - “Exceptional and limited circumstances” exemption allows non-independent director participation up to two years.

  - The board must disclose the exception and appoint non-independent members only on a temporary basis.

- Directors or immediate family members receiving over $120,000 (excluding director fees) during the last three years are disqualified.

- Cross-company compensation committee conflicts apply as noted above.

- The committee must adopt a written charter, with annual review.[18]

### C. Nominating/Corporate Governance Committee

- Must be composed entirely of independent directors, except in the absence of a formal committee, where the board’s independent directors perform these duties collectively.

- Responsible for director nominations and governance oversight.

- No phase-in or exemptions for regular issuers.

## VI. Major Exemptions, Special Cases, and Transition Timelines

### A. Controlled Company Exemptions and Transition

- Exempt from majority independent board, compensation and nominating committee independence.

- Must maintain independent audit committee and independent director sessions.

- Disclosures about controlled status and exemptions are mandatory.

- If a controlled company loses that status, full compliance with independence requirements, including majority independent board and independent committees (compensation/nominating committee), must be achieved **within 12 months**.

- Such companies are not eligible for cure periods immediately following phase-in.

### B. IPO and Emerging Growth Company Phase-ins

- IPO companies receive a **phase-in period up to 12 months** to comply with board and committee independence, except for audit committee independence required by SEC Rule 10A-3, which is effective immediately.

### C. Foreign Private Issuer (FPI) Exemptions

- Allowed to follow home country governance practices for board and committee independence (except audit committee).

- Must disclose deviations and maintain an SEC-compliant independent audit committee.

- New insider reporting obligations effective 2026 with exceptions based on qualifying jurisdictions.

### D. Business Development Company (BDC) and Registered Investment Company Distinctions

- Registered investment companies are exempt from NASDAQ independence rules.

- BDCs, regulated under the Investment Company Act and Securities Exchange Act, are subject to full compliance.

## VII. Summary of Source of Requirements: NASDAQ vs. SEC

| Requirement                     | Source                         | Notes                                                                                      |
|--------------------------------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Board Majority Independence    | NASDAQ Rule 5605(b)             | Applies to regular issuers; controlled companies exempt                                 |
| Director Bright-Line Tests     | NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)          | Includes employment, compensation, business relationships, family definitions            |
| Audit Committee Independence  | NASDAQ Rule 5605(c) + SEC Rule 10A-3 | SEC rules impose stricter requirements; no phase-in for SEC audit committee independence   |
| Compensation and Nominating Committees | NASDAQ Rule 5605(d)             | Must be independent, exceptions limited; no direct SEC rules but SEC enforces disclosure  |
| Disclosure and Annual Determinations | SEC Reg S-K Item 407(a), NASDAQ rules | Boards must publicly disclose independence determinations and relationships              |
| Foreign Private Issuer Governance | NASDAQ Rule 5615, SEC Rule 10A-3 | Exempt from some NASDAQ rules; must comply with SEC audit committee rules                  |
| Section 16 Reporting for FPIs  | SEC Rules (post 2026 amendments) | Expanded application to FPIs; NASDAQ reflects these requirements                          |

---

## VIII. Practical Guidance for Governance Officers, Legal Counsel, and Compliance Professionals

- Implement detailed **annual questionnaires** covering employment, compensation, financial dealings, family relationships, and auditor affiliations, referencing the three-year lookback and thresholds.

- Maintain detailed records of all business relationships exceeding the thresholds ($200,000 or 5% gross revenues).

- Monitor **immediate family members’ employment and compensation** closely.

- For IPO, controlled company, FPI, BDC, and registered investment company statuses, document and track phase-in schedules, exemptions, and disclosure obligations meticulously.

- Ensure audit committee members are in compliance with both NASDAQ and SEC independence standards with no prohibited compensation or relationships.

- For compensation committees, confirm no cross-company conflicts exist and monitor any exceptions rigorously, with required disclosures.

- For controlled companies losing that status, prepare timely transition plans to achieve full compliance within 12 months.

- Keep abreast of SEC rule updates affecting independence, particularly those impacting FPIs relating to Section 16 filings beginning in 2026.

- Foster clear communication with the NASDAQ Listing Compliance department for any status changes, exemptions reliance, or compliance issues.
