## 1. Statutory Language from the Molasses Act (1733)

### Overview

The Molasses Act (1733), formally cited as 6 George II c. 13, was enacted by the British Parliament to impose duties on molasses, rum, sugar, and related products imported into British American colonies from foreign (non-British) colonies. It principally aimed to protect British West Indies sugar planters from economic competition, particularly from French Caribbean colonies.

### Key Statutory Extracts

- Effective **from December 25, 1733**, the Act levied the following duties:  
  - **9 pence per gallon** on rum or spirits imported from foreign plantations.  
  - **6 pence per gallon** on molasses or syrups imported from foreign plantations.  
  - **5 shillings per hundredweight** on sugars and unrefined sugar blocks (“paneles”) imported from foreign colonies.

- Importers were required to **make due entry and pay duties before goods could be landed or put ashore**; failure to comply resulted in total **forfeiture of goods**.

- The Act forbade the importation of sugars and rum into the Kingdom of Ireland unless the goods were **loaded and shipped in Great Britain on British ships**, to ensure mercantile control.

- Penalties included forfeiture of goods, fines, and the empowerment of customs officials to enforce restrictions, with customs officers’ salaries linked to duties collected and penalties imposed.

- Exceptions were made for molasses and sugars from Spanish and Portuguese dominions (under certain treaties).

- The **Act would remain in force for five years**, from June 24, 1733.

### Representative Language from the Act

> "From and after the Twenty-fifth Day of December, One thousand Seven hundred and thirty three, there shall be raised... upon all Rum or spirits... the Sum of Nine pence for every Gallon thereof."

> "No Sugars, Paneles, Syrups, or Molasses... nor any Rum or Spirits... shall be imported... into the Kingdom of Ireland, but such only as shall be fairly and bona fide loaden and shipped in Great-Britain in Ships navigated according to the several Laws now in being."

> "All such Goods as shall be so landed or put on Shore... without due Entry... and before the Duties... shall be paid... shall be forfeited."

> "This present Act shall... continue and be in Force for the Space of Five Years..."

### Purpose and Context

- The **principal purpose** was trade regulation to protect British West Indies sugar plantations by **imposing high duties on cheaper foreign molasses and sugar**.  
- The Act was **not primarily intended to raise revenue** but to enforce mercantile trade monopoly.  
- Widespread colonial smuggling and bribery limited enforcement effectiveness.  
- The Act foreshadowed rising colonial tensions that contributed to the American Revolution.

## 2. Slave Laws from Jamaica, Barbados, and South Carolina (1730–1739)

### 2.1 Barbados Slave Code (1661—Applied in 1730s)

- Though enacted in 1661, the *Barbados Slave Code* was the **preeminent legal code governing enslaved people throughout the Caribbean in the 1730s** and heavily influenced other colonies.

- The code explicitly classified enslaved Africans as **“goods and chattels”** (property), depriving them of legal personhood.

- Key statutory language indicated the legal purpose was to:

  > “Protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels.”

- The code characterized enslaved people as a:

  > “Heathenish, brutish and an uncertaine, dangerous kind of people.”

- Allowed **masters to impose severe punishments** (flogging, mutilation, burning alive for severe offenses) without legal consequence.

- Required enslavers to provide slaves with a **yearly set of clothing** but no enforceable standards for food or shelter.

- Codified **hereditary slavery** (slave status inherited from mother) and legally entrenched chattel slavery in British Caribbean colonies.

- Reenacted with minor modifications periodically, it profoundly shaped Jamaican and South Carolinian slave statutes.

[Source: Barbados Slave Code (1661) and its continuing authority in the 1730s [6][7]]

### 2.2 Jamaican Slave Laws (1730–1739)

- Exact statutory texts from the 1730s Jamaica slave laws are scarce in easily accessible archives; however, colonial legislature maintained slave codes derived from the Barbados model.

- Jamaica’s laws similarly defined enslaved individuals as **property and chattels**, subject to the absolute control of owners.

- Laws authorized violent corporal punishment, prohibited literacy, restrained movement, and detailed punitive measures to stifle rebellion.

- Treaties with Maroon communities in 1739 imposed additional military and legal frameworks managing enslaved population control.

- Scholars recommend archival research at The National Archives UK and Jamaican records for exact texts.

[Sources: Archival guidance, Jamaican slave legislation overviews [8][9]]

### 2.3 South Carolina Slave Code (1740)

- Passed immediately following the **Stono Rebellion (1739)**, this code formalized brutal and controlling policies toward enslaved people as:

  > “Absolute slaves” and “chattels personal in the hands of their owners.”

- It legally cemented **heritable slavery** per the condition of the mother, confirmed enslaved people as property rather than persons.

- Established comprehensive restrictions including:

  - Ban on slaves’ free movement without written passes.
  - Permission to **kill slaves who assaulted whites**.
  - Swift summary justice with special courts lacking jury trials.
  - Prohibition on literacy, property ownership, and trade without licenses.
  - Brutal punishments including whipping, mutilation, branding, and death.
  - Community responsibility to capture escaped slaves.
  - Heavy fines on owners who cruelly mistreated slaves or caused death.

- The Act reflects intensified repression after slave uprisings, aiming to preempt revolts and sustain racial hierarchy and property rights of planters.

[Sources: South Carolina Slave Code 1740 full statutory excerpts and analysis [10][11][12][13][14]]

## 3. Comparison: British Parliamentary Law (1730s) vs. Colonial Plantation Codes (Jamaica and Barbados)

### British Parliamentary Law on Slavery (1730s)

- **No explicit Parliamentary statutes established slavery within Britain itself** at this time; slavery’s legality was ambiguous.

- Legal status of enslaved people in England was governed largely by **common law and royal court precedent, not statute**.

- Landmark cases such as **Somerset v. Stewart (1772)** would later hold that slavery had no legal foundation in England, though this ruling did not apply in colonies.

- The British Parliament primarily legislated on **trade and the slave trade**, rather than slavery directly.

- **English law emphasized rights of English subjects** and individual liberties, which technically conflicted with slavery’s chattel nature.

- Slavery was recognized and enforced through **extralegal colonial governance** and imperial policies, leaving an uneasy gap between metropolitan law and colonial realities.

### Colonial Plantation Codes in Jamaica and Barbados

- Slave codes **explicitly legalized enslaved people as chattel property**, denying any legal personality or rights.

- Codes institutionalized **racial slavery** with hereditary, lifelong servitude that was explicitly codified in statute.

- Codes allowed **extreme corporal and capital punishment** to control enslaved populations and prevent rebellions.

- Plantation laws removed legal protections common under English law, including due process and property rights, for enslaved persons.

- Enforced restrictions on mobility, communication, property, and social interaction to maintain planter domination.

- Slave codes operated **independently from metropolitan statute**, embodying a rigid racial caste system and dehumanization unprecedented in English law.

### Summary of Differences

- **British Parliamentary law in the 1730s was ambiguous and non-explicit on slavery**; it often operated by royal prerogative or common law, focusing legislation on trade, not direct slavery regulation.

- **Colonial plantation codes provided detailed, explicit, and brutal legal frameworks** categorizing enslaved people purely as chattels, authorized severe punitive controls, and denied any personal liberties.

- Parliament’s emphasis on individual rights contrasted sharply with colonial plantation societies’ legal racism institutionalizing slavery.

- Colonial codes were tools for securing the economic interests of the elite planter class and were enforced extralegally or with the Crown’s support, often in tension with English metropolitan law.

## 4. Recent Peer-Reviewed Articles (2015–2025) on Legal Dehumanization of Enslaved People

### Selected Scholarly Articles

1. **“Negroes Goods and Merchandizes: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700–1763”**  
   Examines how legal terminology in admiralty courts treated enslaved Africans as goods rather than humans, reinforcing their property status in transatlantic commerce. Highlights legal language's role in facilitating slavery's economic exploitation. [1]

2. **Chapter on “Bondage” in *Fragile Empire* (Cambridge University Press, 2024)**  
   Explores the legal evolution of chattel slavery in Caribbean colonies, emphasizing the denial of enslaved personhood and the racialization of bondage embedded in early English colonial law. [2]

3. **“Chattel” in *Bonds of Empire* (Cambridge University Press)**  
   Analyzes how English property law classified enslaved people as movable property, facilitating commercial transactions and credit, underpinning the economic system of plantation slavery. [3]

4. **Educational Resources on Slavery and Legal Dehumanization (NJ State Bar Foundation, 2020)**  
   Describes how enslaved individuals were legally regarded as personal property from the 17th to 19th centuries, accounting for widespread commodification and denial of human rights. [4]

5. **Article “Slavery, Law, and Race in England and its New World Empire” (Law and History Review, Cambridge University Press)**  
   Critiques the racialized legal construction of slavery in British imperial law, highlighting the interplay between race, law, and colonial governance that facilitated enslavement while maintaining legal ambiguity in Britain proper. [5]

### Key Insights

- Legal frameworks extensively **denied enslaved people legal personhood**, defining them as property (“chattel”), commodities, and economic assets.

- Language across legal systems and courts normalized dehumanization to sustain economic exploitation.

- Laws functioned both as **instruments of economic control and racial subordination**, embedding permanent bondage.

- Although metropolitan laws afforded rights to English subjects, colonial slave laws operated **outside these protections**, allowing brutal repression.

- Legal dehumanization was foundational both in commerce (especially admiralty law) and plantation control.

## 5. Scholarly Debate on the Molasses Act (1733): Trade Regulation vs. Slave Control

### Dominant Scholarly Interpretation (2015–2025)

- The **Molasses Act was chiefly a trade regulation law**, designed to limit foreign competition (notably from the French West Indies) by imposing high duties on molasses and related products entering British American colonies.

- The Act aimed to **protect British West Indies sugar planters and their economic interests** by promoting a mercantile monopoly.

- Colonial opposition stemmed primarily from **economic impact on the New England rum industry**, reliant on cheap molasses, leading to widespread smuggling and evasions.

- Enforcement was minimal, and the Act was replaced by the Sugar Act of 1764, which lowered duties but increased enforcement, markedly intensifying colonial tensions.

### Molasses Act and Slavery

- While **molasses production and the sugar economy were inseparably linked to the transatlantic slave trade and plantation slavery**, the Act itself did **not explicitly regulate enslaved populations or serve as a slave-control statute**.

- The Act functioned **within the broader economic system** reliant on enslaved labor, but its legislative intent and statutory language focus on trade duties and revenue protection rather than managing enslaved people.

- Recent dissertations and journal articles affirm that the **Molasses Act's role was economic protectionism**, framed within mercantilism, not direct slave management.

- Historic narratives acknowledge the **economic interdependence of molasses, rum, and slavery** but caution against conflating trade regulation with slave control legislation.

### Minority and Nuanced Views

- Some scholarship notes the **indirect role of the Molasses Act in sustaining the economic ecosystem of slavery**, by protecting a market essential to plantation profitability.

- However, no significant peer-reviewed recent research argues the Molasses Act was intended as or functioned as a slave population control measure.

[Sources: Recent historiography, economic history, and legal analyses [6][7][8][9][10]]

## Conclusion

The 1730s legal context for enslaved people in the British Empire was defined by a disjunction between British Parliamentary ambiguity and explicitly codified colonial plantation slave codes, which legally dehumanized enslaved Africans as property. The Molasses Act of 1733 served as a mercantile trade regulation to protect British economic interests connected ecologically to slavery but was not a legal instrument of slave control itself. Recent peer-reviewed scholarship from 2015 to 2025 consistently interprets the Molasses Act in this light, emphasizing its role in shaping colonial economic tensions but not direct legal regulation of enslaved people.
