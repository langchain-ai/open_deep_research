# Definition and Requirements of an Independent Director under NASDAQ Listing Standards

## Definition of an Independent Director

Under the most current NASDAQ listing standards, an **independent director** is a member of the company's board of directors who does **not** have any relationship with the company, either directly or indirectly, that would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment. Specifically, NASDAQ defines an independent director as a person other than:

- An executive officer or employee of the company or any of its subsidiaries; or
- Any individual who has a relationship that, in the opinion of the board of directors, would impair the director’s ability to act independently from management and the company.

This definition emphasizes the requirement for impartiality and independence from management influence, ensuring directors can provide objective oversight [1].

## Eligibility Criteria: Who Qualifies as an Independent Director

To qualify as an independent director under NASDAQ rules, a person must satisfy the following key eligibility criteria:

- **No Employment**: The individual cannot be, and cannot have been during the past three years, an employee (including executive officer) of the company or any of its subsidiaries.
  
- **Compensation Limits**: The director or any immediate family member must not have received more than $120,000 in compensation from the company or its subsidiaries in any consecutive 12-month period within the last three years, excluding director fees, non-executive family member payments, and certain retirement benefits.

- **No Material Business or Financial Relationships**: The director and their immediate family members cannot have had material business dealings with the company (e.g., as a customer, supplier, or lender) where payments exceeded $200,000 or 5% of the other's annual revenues in each of the last three fiscal years.

- **No Recent Auditor Connection**: The director cannot currently be, or have been in the past three years, a partner or employee of the company's outside auditor.

- **No Conflicting Committee Overlaps**: The director must not serve on the compensation committee of another company where any of the company’s executive officers serve on that company’s compensation committee.

- **Family Member Restrictions**: The director must not have an immediate family member who is, or was in the past three years, an executive officer of the company or its subsidiaries.

"Immediate family member" is broadly defined to include a spouse, parents, children, siblings (by blood, marriage, or adoption), and any person living in the director’s home [1][9].

## Disqualification Criteria: Who Cannot Serve as an Independent Director

Certain relationships or conditions explicitly disqualify a person from serving as an independent director. These include:

- **Employment**: Being employed by the company or any of its subsidiaries within the previous three years.
  
- **Excess Compensation**: Receiving compensation exceeding $120,000 during any consecutive 12-month period within the last three years in a capacity other than a director (subject to exclusions).

- **Family Executive Officer**: Having an immediate family member serving as an executive officer of the company or its subsidiaries currently or within the past three years.

- **Material Financial Transactions**: Having recent business dealings with the company exceeding materiality thresholds (payments > $200,000 or 5% of revenue over three fiscal years).

- **Auditor Relationship**: Being, or having been within three years, an employee or partner of the company’s external auditor.

- **Cross-Committee Service Conflicts**: Serving on another entity’s compensation committee where any executive officer of the company simultaneously serves on that entity's compensation committee.

Additionally, for directors serving specifically on the **Audit Committee**, NASDAQ imposes more stringent independence requirements in compliance with SEC Rule 10A-3. Audit Committee members:

- Must not directly or indirectly accept consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company, other than fixed retirement benefits.
- Must not be an “affiliate” of the company under SEC definitions.
- Must meet enhanced independence standards designed to assure the objective functioning of the Audit Committee [6][13].

## Process of Independence Determination

NASDAQ requires that each director’s independence be assessed annually by the board of directors, considering all relevant facts and circumstances. The board must exercise judgment and may consider relationships beyond the bright-line tests. Ownership of company stock alone does not disqualify a director [6][10].

Well-governed Nasdaq companies regularly conduct detailed questionnaires, maintain inventories of director relationships, and issue formal board resolutions confirming independence determinations.

## Types of Companies Required to Have Independent Directors

NASDAQ applies independent director requirements broadly across all its **listing tiers**:

- **Global Select Market**
- **Global Market**
- **Capital Market**

For all these tiers:

- A **majority of the board of directors must be independent**.
- The board must hold at least two executive sessions annually consisting solely of independent directors.
- The **Audit Committee** must be composed entirely of independent directors (at least three members), all financially literate, with at least one member possessing accounting or financial expertise.
- The **Compensation Committee** must also consist exclusively of independent directors.
- Boards must ensure independent director oversight of nomination processes, either through a nominating committee composed solely of independent directors or through another form of independent director supervision with formal charters or resolutions [2][3][7][8].

### Exemptions and Special Cases

- **Controlled Companies** (companies where a single shareholder or group controls the majority of voting power) are exempt from some or all independent director board composition requirements but must publicly disclose their controlled status and governance practices.

- Certain **initial public offerings (IPOs)** may receive temporary relief or phase-in periods for full compliance with independence requirements.

- **Foreign private issuers** listed on NASDAQ may follow home country governance standards but must maintain an Audit Committee with at least three independent directors meeting SEC independence rules, subject to phased transitions after IPO [12].

## Summary

NASDAQ’s standards for independent directors are designed to ensure objective oversight and protect shareholder interests by requiring:

- Directors to have no material relationships or recent employment/financial ties with the company.
- Strict compensation and family member restrictions.
- Enhanced independence for Audit and Compensation Committees.
- Majority independent boards across all Nasdaq listing tiers, with specific committee independence requirements.
- Disclosure and limited exemptions applied for controlled companies and IPOs.

Boards must annually evaluate independence, balancing objective bright-line tests with subjective analysis of relationships that could affect independent judgment.
