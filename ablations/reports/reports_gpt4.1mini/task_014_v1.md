## 1. Overview of the Current NCD IPO Landscape in India (December 2025)

### 1.1 Market Context  
Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs) are fixed-income instruments issued by corporates to raise debt capital without equity dilution. They have maturities typically ranging from 1 to 20 years and pay fixed or floating coupons. NCDs in India can be secured (backed by assets) or unsecured. They are actively traded on BSE and NSE, offering liquidity to investors. The Indian corporate bond market, including NCDs, has expanded substantially, growing from ₹10 lakh crore in 2010 to approximately ₹58 lakh crore by end-2025, supporting over 6,000 issuers across financial and non-financial sectors almost at parity.[1][15]

## 2. Credit Rating Comparison: CRISIL vs ICRA

### 2.1 Rating Scale Correlation  
CRISIL and ICRA use comparable but independent rating scales:  
- **AAA / Aaa:** Highest safety of timely payment.  
- **AA / Aa:** High safety, very low credit risk.  
- **A / A:** Adequate safety, low credit risk.  
- **BBB / Baa:** Lowest tier of investment-grade, moderate risk.  

Ratings below BBB are below investment grade (junk status). Institutional investors generally accept BBB-/Baa3 and above.

### 2.3 Historical Defaults and Rating Stability  
- CRISIL has observed zero defaults in the AAA category over 1-3 year durations up to FY25.[3]  
- India Ratings reports a historic low default rate of 0.5% in FY25, with improving upgrade/downgrade ratios and high stability of investment-grade ratings.[11]   
- Investment-grade NCDs have shown resilience even during volatile market cycles of 2008 and 2020, with downgrades often preceded by financial stress indicators such as rising NPAs and leverage surges, offering predictive warning signs for investors.[11][3]

## 3. Post-Tax Yield Calculations for Generic and Senior Citizen Investors

### 3.1 Taxation Environment FY 2025-26 (Assessment Year 2026-27)  
- Interest income from NCDs is taxable under 'Income from Other Sources' at the investor’s applicable slab rate. No special tax exemption is available for NCD interest.[8][11]  
- Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) applies at 10% if interest payment exceeds ₹10,000 p.a.; senior citizens benefit from higher TDS exemption thresholds and may submit Form 15H to avoid TDS in some cases.[16][21]  
- Senior citizens can claim deductions on interest income up to ₹1,00,000 under Section 80TTB. This reduces taxable income unlike generic investors.[13][18]  
- New Tax Regime slabs do not provide a senior citizen-specific basic exemption, but old regime slabs do; investors may elect whichever benefits them most.[13]  
- Section 87A rebate: ₹60,000 rebate applies for taxable income up to ₹12 lakh under the new regime.[15]

## 4. Historical Context and Pattern Recognition  

- Over the past 15 years, NCD markets in India have transitioned from niche instruments to mainstream fixed income components, particularly since 2015, with increasing retail participation and regulatory enhancements.[15]  
- Historic default rates for AAA-rated instruments remain negligible, supporting their role as core fixed-income building blocks in portfolios.[3][11]  
- Rating migrations exhibit ordinal patterns: upgrades/downgrades cluster moderately, with downgrades often preceded by stress in debt coverage and leverage metrics.[11]  
- Coupon rates have gradually compressed, particularly for higher-rated issuers, reflecting RBI’s moderate interest rate environment and competitive market dynamics for AAA and AA instruments.[13][15]  
- Retail investor shift toward structured, secured NCDs with robust disclosures has been aided by SEBI’s oversight and industry digitization of investment processes since 2023.[8][20]

## 5. Risk Assessment

### 5.1 Credit Risk  
- Defaults are rare at AAA and AA levels; however, risks amplify in BBB or below segments such as Prachay Capital. Defaults often coincide with sector-specific downturns or liquidity stress.[11][3]  
- Elevated leverage, as indicated by high debt-to-equity (>1.5x) and rising NPAs, flags caution. Among the current issuers, Capri Global’s 3.67x gearing implies increased vulnerability despite strong CRAR.[12]

### 5.2 Interest Rate Risk  
- NCDs, especially with long maturities, are exposed to interest rate fluctuations. The flat AAA yield curve seen in 2025-26 means limited upside from tenor extension but also limited rate risk for short-tenor issues.[15]  

### 5.3 Liquidity Risk  
- Secondary market in NCDs remains thin compared to government securities or equities, particularly for lower-rated or smaller issuers. Investors should plan for potential hold-to-maturity scenarios.[5][14]  

### 5.4 Regulatory & Policy Risks  
- SEBI mandates enhanced disclosure and rating transparency, reducing information asymmetry. However, regulatory shifts in tax treatment, foreign investment eligibility, or issuer compliance can impact market dynamics.[6][11][28]  

### 5.5 Tail Risks  
- Macro shocks (e.g., global credit crunches), geopolitical tensions affecting inflation, or sector-specific collapses (real estate slowdown, NBFC liquidity crises) pose tail risks. Historical experience from 2008 and 2020 crises highlights the utility of diversified, high-grade portfolios in mitigating tail losses.[11][15]

## 6. Regulatory Landscape and Policy Implications  

- SEBI regulates NCD issuance with requirements for issuer disclosures, credit ratings, debenture trusteeship, and investor protection mechanisms. The Securities and Exchange Board of India (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2026, introduced an abridged prospectus format to enhance transparency and investor understanding.[6]  
- Tax reforms in FY 2025-26 harmonize TDS structures and enable better compliance, with increased TDS exemption thresholds for senior citizens (up to ₹1,00,000 on interest income) facilitating post-tax income enhancement.[8][16][21]  
- The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) allows increased foreign participation in debt markets through FPIs and NRIs under liberalized frameworks, potentially enhancing liquidity and demand for NCDs in mid-to-large segments.[11]  
- Continued regulatory facilitation has supported record issuance volumes and deeper investor participation, particularly in retail channels via digital platforms.[5][20]

## 7. Forward Yield Curve and Economic Indicator Analysis  

- India's 10-year government securities yield hovered between 6.63% and 7.0% in early 2026, with inflation steady around 3.5%-4%, supporting stable borrowing costs.[15][18]  
- The credit curve for corporate bonds and NCDs, especially AAA-rated, remains flat, signaling that long-dated debt attracts yields close to medium/short tenure instruments.[15]  
- Economic growth forecasts remain solid (GDP growth around 6.7%-7.8% for FY 2026-27), providing fundamental support for credit demand and stable corporate performance in key sectors such as NBFCs, infrastructure, and manufacturing.[16][20]  
- Key risk factors include commodity price volatility, geopolitical concerns (e.g., Middle East tension), and potential inflation spikes, which could pressure yields upwards.[16]

## 8. Competitive Positioning and Relative Value  

- **AAA PSU Issuers (e.g., Power Finance Corporation, Samvardhana Motherson):** Lower yields (~6.8%-8.1%), superior credit quality, suitable as core defensive holdings within fixed income portfolios targeting retirees seeking capital preservation.  
- **High-Grade NBFCs (Edelweiss Financial Services):** Yields in 8.8%-10.1% range for A-rated instruments balance moderate credit risk with income needs, suited for moderately risk-tolerant retirees and institutional portfolios seeking yield pick-up.  
- **Mid-to-Lower Investment Grade NBFCs (Muthoot, Prachay, KLM Axiva):** Higher coupons (~9.5%-13.0%) compensate for higher credit and liquidity risks; recommended only for smaller portfolio allocations after rigorous due diligence.  
- **Coupon Structures:** Flexible options with monthly, annual, and cumulative interest payments cater to retirees’ cash flow needs; cumulative options preferable for those seeking retirement income lump sums.[5][14][21]

## 9. Investment Thesis for Institutional Fixed Income Portfolios for Retirees

- Current macroeconomic and regulatory conditions in India support continued growth and stability in the NCD market, especially for investment-grade instruments.  
- AAA-rated NCDs from PSUs and large corporates like Samvardhana Motherson provide the safest credit exposure, minimal default risk, and stable coupon income suitable for capital preservation and predictable cash flows required by retirees.  
- Select A and BBB-rated NBFCs like Edelweiss Financial Services offer attractive yield premiums but require vigilant credit risk monitoring and conservative position sizing to mitigate credit and liquidity risks.  
- Tax efficiency considerations favor senior citizens due to Section 80TTB and higher TDS exemption thresholds, improving net return profiles vis-à-vis generic investors. This makes NCDs with stable coupons a compelling fixed-income building block for retirement portfolios.  
- Investors should allocate primarily to secured, well-rated NCDs with manageable leverage and transparent risk disclosures, avoiding issuers with high debt-to-equity ratios exceeding ~1.0x unless yields sufficiently compensate for the risk with strong credit enhancement.  
- Established regulatory frameworks, improved investor disclosures, and digital subscription platforms have raised transparency and accessibility, reducing barriers for institutional and retiree investors.  
- Portfolio diversification across issuers, tenors, and sectors, combined with active monitoring of rating migrations and economic indicators, will optimize returns and protect principal in a rising or volatile interest rate environment.
