# Vertical Integration Strategies and Sustainability Compliance of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (2018–2024)

---

## Introduction

This report provides a rigorously evidenced analysis of the vertical integration strategies employed by Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam between 2018 and 2024, focusing on their upstream investments in West African cocoa cooperatives and sustainability certification programs. It thoroughly examines corporate capital expenditures in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana, premium payment amounts, farm polygon mapping, and monitoring percentages with precise temporal and geographic disaggregation. Additionally, the report elaborates on compliance with the European Union Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) citing primary legal sources, regulatory milestones, enforcement timelines, and highlights methodological caveats in reporting, data comparability, and compliance claims. Finally, it critically analyzes the processors' competitive positioning with premium chocolate manufacturers—Lindt, Ferrero, and Mondelēz—grounded in concrete buyer procurement requirements and evolving sustainability disclosure expectations.

## 1. Vertical Integration and Upstream Investments (2018–2024)

### 1.1 Barry Callebaut

Barry Callebaut AG operates as the global leader in cocoa processing and chocolate production, with an estimated CHF 8.1 billion revenue in FY 2021/22 and over 1.5 million cocoa farms mapped globally by 2024/25, including 188,566 farms in Côte d’Ivoire and 71,945 in Ghana during the 2023/24 period. Its **Forever Chocolate** initiative, launched in 2016, aims to lift over 500,000 cocoa farmers out of poverty by 2025 and achieve 100% certified or verified sustainable cocoa sourcing by 2030.

- **Upstream Investments**: To support direct sourcing relationships, Barry Callebaut engages cooperatives with:
  - Subsidized inputs, financial credit facilities (in partnership with IFC and IDH supporting ~100,000 farmers in Côte d’Ivoire),
  - Farmer training programs emphasizing agroforestry, soil fertility, and labor practices (targeting young and female farmers in over 80 communities),
  - Assisted natural regeneration pilot covering 1,200 hectares in Côte d’Ivoire,
  - Sustainable premium models routed entirely through the Cocoa Horizons Foundation to fund community initiatives [1][2][3][6].

- **Farm Polygon Mapping & Traceability**:  
  The company has completed farm polygon mapping for over 1.5 million cocoa farms globally to meet traceability requirements under forthcoming regulatory frameworks such as the EUDR. Mapping efforts focus on direct supply chains, achieving near 100% traceability down to the farm level in both Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana by 2024/25.

- **Capital Expenditures in West Africa**:  
  - Invested CHF 55 million (approx. US$60 million) in 2019 expanding Société Africaine de Cacao (SACO) grinding facilities in Abidjan, Côte d’Ivoire, increasing capacity by 40% by 2022.  
  - Earlier doubled Ghana processing capacity in Tema (2007) from 30,000 to 60,000 tonnes annually.  
  - Launched the BC Next Level program with CHF 500 million in net investments during 2023/24 focusing on operational agility, product innovation, and sustainability integration [43][44][24].

- **Sustainability Certification**:  
  As of 2024/25, 55.8% of cocoa supplied by Barry Callebaut is certified sustainable or verified (Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade, Cocoa Horizons) with a commitment to 100% by 2030. Certified cocoa volumes are supported by rigorous monitoring for child labor and deforestation risks. Over 51,420 cases of child labor remediation were active in 2024 [1][2][9][58].

- **Premium Payments & Farmer Incomes**:  
  Barry Callebaut channels 100% of premiums from sustainable cocoa sales to the Cocoa Horizons Foundation to fund improvements. Though exact premium payment amounts are undisclosed, IFC-supported credit facilities and yield improvements have driven income increases averaging 23% in supported regions, lifting 557,739 farmers out of poverty [2][4].

### 1.2 Cargill

Cargill is the second-largest cocoa processor globally, sourcing from over 6 direct origins and investing heavily in upstream farmer engagement through the **Cargill Cocoa Promise** program.

- **Upstream Investments**:  
  - Supported 211,000+ farmers certified and trained in Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) in the 2023/24 crop year.  
  - Operates 147 cooperative offices across Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana with extensive farmer coaching programs on child labor monitoring, cooperative management, and agroforestry.  
  - Distributed over 1 million agroforestry trees in West Africa since 2018; women farmer empowerment projects via Village Savings and Loan Associations increase incomes by as much as 87% in some cases [6][10][12][14].

- **Farm Polygon Mapping & Traceability**:  
  - Cargill reported polygon mapping of 123,432 farms in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana during 2023/24, covering 92% of direct sourcing volumes under Cocoa Promise.  
  - Employs GIS-enabled farm boundary digitization and real-time monitoring for deforestation risk in partnership with Satelligence and Meridia, achieving 99% of mapped farms free of primary forest loss post-2014.  
  - 100% traceability to first purchase points in Ghana; 61% in Côte d’Ivoire, with indirect sourcing less covered [10][60].

- **Capital Expenditures in West Africa**:  
  - Invested US$100 million in a new cocoa processing plant expansion in Yopougon, Côte d’Ivoire, completed in 2021, increasing capacity by 50%.  
  - Completed a US$13 million capacity increase of Tema cocoa processing plant in Ghana in November 2021, lifting annual throughput ~20% from 75,000 to 90,000 tonnes.  
  - Further investments include technology upgrades: US$6 million in farmer coaching and US$3.5 million in traceability systems in Côte d’Ivoire [15][29][47][59].

- **Sustainability Certification & Premium Payments**:  
  - Certified cocoa volumes represent over 54% of Cargill’s cocoa purchases, predominantly under Rainforest Alliance and Fairtrade.  
  - Premium payments were US$2.2 million to 26,500 farmers in Côte d’Ivoire (2011 data); more recent figures are embedded within multi-million-dollar investments in farmer development, agroforestry and social programs [6][28].

- **Farmer Income Improvements**:  
  - Income gains reported average 17% among cooperative cocoa farmers, with income improvements strongly correlated with sustained training, premium payments, and diversified activities under Cocoa Promise.  
  - Additional enhancements come from women’s empowerment initiatives, evidenced in VSLAs with income increases up to 87% in pilot communities [21][22].

### 1.3 Olam Group

Olam Group, a major integrated agri-business, has segmented since 2020 into Olam Food Ingredients (ofi) and Olam Agri, focusing on specialty cocoa processing and commodity trading, respectively. It emphasizes sustainability via its proprietary **Cocoa Compass** and **AtSource** platforms.

- **Upstream Investments**:  
  - Olam supports 760,000 farmers through sustainable sourcing programs in nine cocoa-producing countries, including Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana.  
  - Distributed 3.9 million cocoa tree seedlings and 20,000 tonnes of high-quality seed material in 2020.  
  - Achieved 100% child labor and deforestation monitoring certification in the direct cocoa supply chain as of April 2021, covering 183,000 households and 12,000 suppliers [11][21][22][34].

- **Farm Polygon Mapping & Traceability**:  
  - Attained full polygon mapping and digital traceability in direct supply chains by 2020, employing OFIS and AtSource for data management, integrating satellite monitoring (Global Forest Watch).  
  - Traceability coverage exceeds 85% of bought cocoa volumes, aiming for 100%, with continual improvement in indirect chain monitoring [18][20].

- **Capital Expenditures in West Africa**:  
  - Commissioned a US$43.5 million greenfield cocoa processing plant in Abidjan, Côte d’Ivoire, in early 2012 with 60,000 tonnes annual processing capacity.  
  - In 2026, invested US$40 million in Ghana’s first state-of-the-art pasta manufacturing facility in Kpone, which supports agro-industrial growth in cocoa origin countries, reflecting diversification.  
  - Ongoing infrastructure, logistics, and energy efficiency investments align with regional strategies and sustainability goals [52][54][55].

- **Sustainability Certification & Premium Payments**:  
  - Paid approximately US$26 million in quality premiums to farmers within sustainability programs as of 2020, embedded in Cocoa Compass initiatives.  
  - Engaged closely with certification schemes including Rainforest Alliance and proprietary standards, supported by third-party audits and extensive social and environmental monitoring [22][34].

- **Income Improvements**:  
  - Productivity improvements average a 9% increase in yields in direct farms; diversified income streams via livelihood programs such as beekeeping and grasscutter farming.  
  - Living income improvements traceable for 30,000 farmers achieving living income levels by 2022, aimed to reach 150,000 by 2030 [12][23].

## 2. EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) Compliance: Legal Framework and Corporate Implementation

### 2.1 Overview of EUDR (Regulation (EU) 2023/1115)

- **Legal Basis and Scope**:  
  EUDR, adopted on 31 May 2023 and entered into force 29 June 2023, expands beyond the earlier EU Timber Regulation by covering cocoa, coffee, soy, cattle, palm oil, rubber, wood, and derived products. It prohibits products linked to deforestation or forest degradation occurring after 31 December 2020 from being placed on the EU market, irrespective of national legality [4][15].

- **Regulatory Milestones and Deadlines**:  
  - Initially, enforcement was planned from 30 December 2024. Due to administrative and operational challenges, the EU Commission extended enforcement deadlines:  
    - Large and medium-sized operators: 30 December 2026  
    - Micro and small enterprises: 30 June 2027 [12][14][18].

- **Due Diligence Obligations**:  
  Operators placing cocoa on the EU market must conduct due diligence, including:  
  - Geolocation and farm polygon mapping at plot level, with precise GIS coordinates, particularly for land plots over 4 hectares, critical for cocoa origin verification.  
  - Risk assessments factoring supply chain and country-level forest risk, applying EU-designated risk classification.  
  - Risk mitigation including audits, supplier engagement, exclusion protocols, and corrective action plans.  
  - Submission of Due Diligence Statements (DDS) via the EUDR Information System, retaining documentation for at least five years [2][3][6][21].

- **Enforcement and Sanctions**:  
  Member States must designate competent authorities for oversight. Sanctions include administrative fines up to 4% of annual EU turnover, confiscation of products, and potential market access bans [10][15][20].

### 2.2 Cocoa Supply Chain Specific Challenges and Compliance Measures

- Cocoa production mainly involves smallholder farmers with complex supply chains, presenting traceability challenges, especially regarding farm ownership documentation limitations and informal transactions [8][9].

- To assist compliance, the **European Cocoa Association (ECA)** and **World Cocoa Foundation (WCF)** issued an official protocol aligned with EUDR Articles 9, 10, and 11, detailing traceability methods, risk assessments, and mitigation approaches [21].

- Satellite monitoring tools (e.g., Satelligence, Meridia) and digital traceability platforms (Barry Callebaut’s Cocoa Horizons, Cargill’s CocoaWise, Olam’s AtSource) facilitate farm polygon mapping (>90% coverage), deforestation risk detection, and exclusion of non-compliant volumes [6][10][20].

- Large cocoa processors publicly disclose high polygon mapping rates in their direct supply chain farms (>90% coverage), but possess limited data transparency on indirect sourcing, where risks remain [6][60].

### 2.3 Provisional Nature of Compliance Assessments

- Compliance status remains provisional given that:  
  - Enforcement mechanisms and due diligence system operations are still scaling up across EU Member States.  
  - The EUDR Information System was launched only in 2024, with staggered operator onboarding [18].  
  - Companies continue to refine data accuracy, integrate fragmented indirect supply chains, and reconcile varying national legal contexts [14][21].

- Auditor and NGO reports confirm ongoing gaps in full deforestation-free verification, especially in brokered cocoa volumes, urging for cautious representation of “compliance” claims [27][28].

## 3. Methodological Considerations and Data Comparability

### 3.1 Differences in Reporting Metrics Across Companies

- **Traceability Definitions**:  
  - Percentages vary depending on the unit measured (farms, cooperative offices, warehouses, total volume) and whether direct or indirect supply chains are included. Olam reports 100% traceability in direct supply chains by 2020; Barry Callebaut maps 1.5 million farms (direct); Cargill reports 92% farm polygon mapping in direct sourcing [6][9][18].

- **Farmer Income Reporting**:  
  - Barry Callebaut’s income improvements focus on poverty reduction measured by Living Income Benchmarks and yield increases with IFC credit facility impact.  
  - Cargill reports premium payments with regional income increases averaging 17% and promotes women’s empowerment metrics via VSLA income gains.  
  - Olam contextualizes income changes alongside livelihood diversification, using AtSource data integrating environmental and social indicators.  
  - Differences exist in farm size, price liberalization, yield gains, and payment mechanisms; income reporting often reflects estimations based on representative samples rather than exhaustive census [1][12][23].

- **Premium Payments**:  
  - Companies report premiums variably — Barry Callebaut discloses aggregated premiums funding Cocoa Horizons; Cargill cites discrete multimillion-dollar payments alongside investments in social programs; Olam specifies US$26 million in 2020.  
  - Transparency varies; timeframes and bases (per kg, per farmer, per cooperative) differ [1][6][22][28].

- **Data Gaps and Under-Reporting Risks**:  
  - Indirect supply chains remain under-digitized with incomplete data on sourcing ratios, deforestation risks, and premium circulation.  
  - Privacy and confidentiality restrict public disclosure of granular farm-level data.  
  - Temporal lags exist between data collection and reporting cycles.

### 3.2 Caveats on Regulatory Compliance and Sustainability Claims

- Traceability and polygon mapping are foundational but insufficient alone for regulatory EUDR compliance — due diligence mandates integrated risk and social impact assessments.

- Self-reported compliance statuses are provisional, pending official inspections and enforcement by EU Member States.

- NGOs and independent auditors have identified systemic risks especially related to indirect sourcing and brokered volumes, requiring ongoing improvements.

## 4. Competitive Positioning with Premium Chocolate Manufacturers

### 4.1 Buyer Requirements and Procurement Practices

- Premium chocolate manufacturers (Lindt, Ferrero, Mondelēz) increasingly mandate verified deforestation-free cocoa certified under high-standard schemes like Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade, and proprietary programs compliant with EUDR.

- Lindt & Sprüngli sources 72.3% of cocoa through sustainability programs targeting 100% by 2025; Ferrero commits to 100% sustainable cocoa by 2025; Mondelēz integrates similar commitments with enhanced transparency and traceability [24].

- Buyers require:
  - Farm-level traceability data with geocoordinates,
  - Third-party certification and audit reports,
  - Documented premium payments,
  - Supply chain disclosures as part of contractual sourcing agreements,
  - Compliance with human rights, child labor, and environmental regulations,
  - Food safety and quality specifications (moisture, pH, flavor profiles).

- Procurement increasingly involves digital platforms to verify supplier sustainability practices and engage with origin communities directly for impact validation, moving beyond generic claims [15][21][23].

### 4.2 Implications for Processors’ Competitive Positioning

- Vertical integration through direct sourcing, digital traceability, and sustainability certification improves credibility and attractiveness to premium manufacturers, enabling processors to secure long-term contracts and premium pricing.

- Processing expansions in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana facilitate local value addition, origin branding, and supply security aligning with buyers’ sustainability narratives.

- Sustainability reporting transparency and data granularity increasingly determine differentiation, favoring companies with advanced platforms (e.g., Olam’s AtSource, Cargill’s CocoaWise, Barry Callebaut’s Cocoa Horizons).

- Buyers favor processors demonstrating robust due diligence frameworks compatible with evolving regulatory frameworks such as EUDR, reflecting a shift to risk-based and impact-driven procurement.

## 5. Forward-Looking Analysis: Disclosure Expectations and Market Pressures (2024–2026+)

- **Enhanced Disclosure Requirements**:  
  The enforcement of EUDR post-2026 implies increased operator due diligence documentation submissions, transparency demands, and supply chain disclosures. Market pressures coalesce around comprehensive sustainability reporting extending beyond certifications to impact metrics on farmer livelihoods and forest conservation.

- **Impact on Competitive Positioning**:  
  Processors with ahead-of-curve digital traceability, third-party verified sustainability data, and demonstrable impact on deforestation risk mitigation will consolidate market share in premium segments. Suppliers lacking such capabilities risk exclusion or reduced contract volumes.

- **Regulatory Integration and Harmonization**:  
  Firms will need to comply with not only EUDR but also evolving frameworks like EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD), mandating deeper supply chain transparency and accountability [21][27].

- **Technological Adoption**:  
  AI, blockchain, and satellite monitoring innovations will be increasingly integrated into procurement and compliance processes, enhancing traceability reliability and lowering risk.

- **Procurement Shift**:  
  Increasing focus on living income verification, transparent premium payment flow, and social compliance audits signal a market moving from volume-based sourcing to value- and impact-based sourcing.

## 6. Sources of Methodological Uncertainty and Data Limitations Summary

- Differences in traceability scope and definitions among processors impede direct quantitative comparisons.

- Income changes are measured through varied methodologies (yield-based estimations, premium impact, survey data), impacted by farm size disparities, socio-economic contexts, and pricing systems.

- Premium payment reporting lacks standardization and temporal consistency.

- Data quality gaps persist mostly in indirect and brokered supply chains, limiting full compliance verification.

- Regulatory compliance statuses are provisional as EUDR enforcement rolls out progressively.

- Privacy and confidentiality considerations limit full public disclosure of granular supply chain data.

- Temporal mismatches between reported data collection and publication cycles introduce lag effects.

---

## Conclusion

Between 2018 and 2024, Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have solidified vertical integration in West Africa by deepening direct sourcing relationships with cocoa cooperatives, significantly increasing farm polygon mapping and traceability, investing major facility capital expenditures in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana, and advancing sustainability certification programs with broad socio-economic impact ambitions. All three demonstrate considerable preparation for EUDR compliance via comprehensive traceability systems and deforestation risk monitoring, though compliance remains provisional pending full regulatory enforcement. Their integrated upstream strategies underpin robust competitive positioning with premium chocolate manufacturers demanding certified, deforestation-free cocoa and rigorous supply chain transparency. Looking forward, evolving regulatory enforcement and enhanced disclosure expectations will drive deeper transparency, impact measurement, and technology adoption shaping sustainable cocoa procurement and processing.
