# Comparative Analysis of Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicators and Systems: Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh vs. Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

---

## Introduction

This comprehensive report systematically analyzes key maternal and neonatal health indicator trajectories from 2017 to 2023 for Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh, compared to Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand. The analysis provides year-specific data on antenatal care (ANC) 4+ visits completion rates, skilled birth attendance (SBA) percentages, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, and neonatal mortality within 28 days. Furthermore, it compares healthcare infrastructure, workforce composition, and referral system operations, emphasizing named facilities and program details. The effects of UNFPA funding reductions starting in 2021 on service continuity and quality are critically assessed. The role and integration of traditional birth attendants (TBAs) as cultural mediators are also evaluated. All data are rigorously anchored in primary datasets and official reports, with clear differentiation of data within and outside the 2017–2023 timeframe.

---

## 1. Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicator Trajectories (2017–2023)

### 1.1 Antenatal Care (ANC) 4+ Visits Completion Rates

#### Rohingya Refugees — Cox’s Bazar

- ANC 4+ completion among Rohingya women has exhibited fluctuating but generally improving coverage during 2017–2023:
  - Early years showed substantial gaps, with approximately 47% of women lacking any antenatal care in a 2024 cohort [8].
  - By 2023, about 70–71.5% of pregnant women reportedly completed four or more ANC visits [2,11,23].
  - The Sexual and Reproductive Health (SRH) Working Group's 2023 bulletin documented 25,946 mothers completing 4+ ANC visits, ~70% among Rohingya refugee populations [23].
  - Barriers include staff shortages, weak referral linkages, limited community knowledge, and cultural hesitance [5].

#### Karen Refugees — Mae La Camp

- ANC completion rates have remained consistently high:
  - Coverage sustaining over 90% of pregnant women receiving 4 or more ANC visits between 2017 and 2023 [41,42].
  - Stability attributed to integration within Thailand’s Ministry of Public Health system and longstanding community trust [41].
  
---

### 1.2 Skilled Birth Attendance (SBA) and Facility-Based Delivery

#### Rohingya Refugees — Cox’s Bazar

- SBA and facility deliveries have improved notably over the period, though with significant year-to-year variation:
  - In 2017–2018 surveys, SBA coverage was approximately 59.5%, with only that portion of births supported by skilled attendants or in facilities [2].
  - By 2023, facility births rose to 82%, supported by 283 midwives trained and deployed by UNFPA and partner organizations [11,23].
  - Midwife-led birthing centers (MLBC) report 89% of booked deliveries occurring onsite; only 21% require referral to higher-level facilities [26].
  - Cesarean section rates remain low but rose from 3.1% to approximately 5% by 2023, reflecting increased access to emergency obstetric care (EmOC) [11,26].
  
#### Karen Refugees — Mae La Camp

- SBA rates consistently around 80%, linked to delivery mostly taking place within camp clinics staffed by trained Karen women [42]:
  - Caesarean rates low (~3.7%), aligned with natural birth focus and limited surgical interventions [42].
  - Ongoing six-month SBA training courses for local Karen midwives bolstered service quality and uptake [63].
  - Approximately 75% of births occur in clinic settings, 20% at home with TBA assistance [41].

---

### 1.3 Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence and Maternal Mortality Causes

#### Rohingya Refugees — Cox’s Bazar

- PPH is the leading direct cause of maternal mortality:
  - MPMSR annual reports (2020–2024) consistently show obstetric hemorrhage responsible for 26.7% to 35% of maternal deaths, alongside hypertensive disorders (20–33%) [3,13,14,15].
  - Increased risk in home births and delayed referrals; early postpartum period particularly vulnerable [14].
  - The first delay in the “Three Delays Model” is heavily influenced by cultural preferences for home birth and fear of facilities [13,14].

#### Karen Refugees — Mae La Camp

- Explicit PPH incidence data limited, but maternal mortality remains elevated (~126.5 per 100,000 live births for 2000–2018):
  - Hemorrhage and hypertensive disorders major causes despite SBA training [42].
  - Training in evidence-based PPH prevention and management employed in camp clinics, but remoteness and physical barriers challenge outcomes [42].

---

### 1.4 Neonatal Mortality within 28 Days

#### Rohingya Refugees — Cox’s Bazar

- Neonatal mortality remains high:
  - Estimated at 27 per 1,000 live births between 2017 and 2018; persistently elevated through 2023 [14,39].
  - Most deaths (~72–76%) occur outside health facilities, primarily in the community or at home [14].
  - High causes include birth asphyxia, hypoxia, prematurity, and growth restrictions [14].
  - Scale-up of Essential Newborn Care (ENC) and Kangaroo Mother Care programs since 2022 initiated by NGOs aim to reduce these numbers [11].

#### Karen Refugees — Mae La Camp

- Significant reduction in neonatal mortality:
  - Declined by approximately 51% over four years with rate at ~10.7 per 1,000 live births by 2018 [39,42].
  - Special Care Baby Units (SCBU) operational since 2007 contributed to improved survival [23].
  - Neonatal intensive care and trained local midwives key to progress [41].

## 2. Healthcare Infrastructure and Delivery Models

### 2.1 Cox’s Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospitals and Fragmented System

- Facilities:
  - Approximately 45 primary health centers, 72 health posts, 1 Comprehensive Emergency Obstetric and Newborn Care (CEmONC) facility, and 8 secondary units [11,22].
  - **Friendship hospital** — only dedicated 24/7 referral center for emergency obstetric and newborn care [11].
  - **Save the Children Maternal and Child Hospital** opened January 2026 with 59 beds, offering 24/7 surgery (including cesarean), neonatal intensive care, mental health, and GBV services [21].
- Service Delivery:
  - Managed mainly by NGOs (MSF, Save the Children, BRAC) supported by UNFPA and local government [6,11].
  - Fragmented coordination challenges across agencies with overlapping roles [5].
  - Facilities often under-resourced relative to large camp population (~900,000 individuals among Rohingya) [11].
  
### 2.2 Mae La Camp: Integrated Thai Ministry of Public Health Model

- Facilities:
  - Primary health care clinics operated jointly by NGOs (Malteser International, SMRU) and Thai Ministry of Public Health [41,42].
  - Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) since 2007, with neonatal intensive care capacity [23].
  - Referral linkages to Thai government district and provincial hospitals for tertiary care within two-hour travel [42].
- Service Delivery:
  - Fully embedded within the public health system of Thailand, sustaining stable services [41].
  - Refugee self-governance and strong community participation facilitate health coordination [13,41].
  - Comprehensive maternal health services: antenatal, intrapartum, postnatal, and family planning delivered in a culturally sensitive manner [52].

## 3. Workforce Scale, Composition, and Mentorship

### 3.1 Rohingya Camps, Cox’s Bazar

- Staff Numbers and Ratios:
  - Approximately 283 midwives deployed in 2023, comprising over 60% of the Health Sector midwifery workforce [23].
  - Midwife-to-population ratio remains low, reflecting huge refugee numbers and ongoing recruitment challenges [11,14].
- Skill Mix and Roles:
  - Midwives provide full spectrum maternity care including ANC, delivery in midwife-led birthing centers (MLBC), postnatal, family planning, and psychosocial services [26].
- Mentorship and Training:
  - Structured mentorship programs under UNFPA, WHO, and partners (Midwifery Strategic Plan 2021–2023) deliver continuous clinical updates and leadership training [14,28].
- Integration of Traditional Birth Attendants:
  - TBAs have shifted from birth delivery to cultural mediation and referral facilitation roles since 2021, contributing to facility birth uptake improvements (from 33% in 2020 to >95% in some catchments by 2022) [34].
  
### 3.2 Karen Refugees, Mae La Camp

- Workforce Scale:
  - Smaller population (~40,000 refugees), skilled birth attendants predominantly locally trained Karen and Burman women [42].
  - Six-month accelerated SBA training programs have certified hundreds, enabling sustained SBA coverage (~80%) [63].
- Skill Mix and Sustainability:
  - SBA training aligned with international standards, including antenatal, delivery, postnatal, and family planning competencies [42,63].
- Mentorship:
  - Continuing education and refresher trainings embedded within community and NGO-supported models [75].
- TBAs’ Role:
  - Approximately 57 TBAs supported by Karen Women’s Organization with annual training, supplies, and manuals in Karen language [75].
  - TBAs function as cultural mediators, educators, and safe delivery promoters while supporting facility referrals [75].

## 4. Referral Systems and Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC)

### 4.1 Cox’s Bazar Referral System

- Infrastructure:
  - 24/7 community-based ambulance transport system (‘Referral Hub’) with free service coordinated by NGOs and community volunteers [29,30].
  - Referral pathways connect field hospitals (e.g., MSF’s 100-bed Facility, Friendship hospital) with Upazila Health Complexes [28].
- Challenges:
  - Average transport time varies widely; remote areas sometimes over 30 minutes away [12].
  - Obstacles include poor road infrastructure, seasonal weather, gender norms limiting female transport use, distrust of health facilities, and security concerns [12,17,30].
- Effectiveness:
  - Despite challenges, these referral systems have improved emergency access; however, underutilization persists due to cultural and logistical barriers [29].

### 4.2 Mae La Camp Referral System

- Structure:
  - Well-defined pathways to Thai district and provincial hospitals within two-hour travel [42,61].
  - Financial support from Thai authorities minimizes refugee out-of-pocket costs for emergency transport and care [61].
- Operations:
  - Referral protocols feature collaboration between camp clinics, NGOs, and Thai hospitals with community health volunteers and TBAs key to identifying emergencies [53].
- Challenges:
  - Funding reductions since 2021 (notably 2025 US aid suspension) strained logistics; some service gaps occur though local government mitigates impact via financial support to health workers [59,61].
  - Legal and financial obstacles for refugees seeking tertiary care outside camp have increased [15].

## 5. Impact of UNFPA Funding Reductions Beginning 2021

### 5.1 Cox’s Bazar

- Funding Context:
  - From 2021, global UNFPA funding reductions led to closures of over 1,000 health facilities and mobile health teams worldwide, with significant impact on Cox’s Bazar maternal health [31,32].
- Service Effects:
  - Midwife recruitment and retention slowed, with only ~47% of staffing targets achieved in some areas by 2023 [14,31].
  - MPMSR data reveal rising maternal mortality to an estimated 295 per 100,000 live births in 2023, up from 179 in 2018, correlating with funding and external stressors like disease outbreaks and environmental events [14,15].
  - Despite closures, some facilities like Save the Children’s maternal hospital (opened 2026) indicate partial service restoration using alternative donor partnerships [21].
- Programmatic Adjustments:
  - Ongoing community TBA engagement and Midwifery Strategic Plan helped partially sustain service quality amid constraints [34].
- Critical Assessment:
  - Evidence indicates funding cuts exacerbated resource shortages, stockouts (e.g., blood supplies), staff workload, and referral inefficiencies, threatening maternal and neonatal survival gains [14,17].

### 5.2 Mae La Camp

- Funding Context:
  - UNFPA and international donor reductions from 2021, compounded by US aid suspensions in 2025, led to closure/suspension of some NGO services, particularly the International Rescue Committee programs [15,59].
- Service Effects:
  - Disruptions in contraceptive availability, sexual and reproductive health education, and primary care observed [15,53].
  - Increasing financial/legal barriers limited refugee access to Thai hospitals for emergency care [61].
- Mitigation:
  - Thai provincial government increased financial support to health workers and coordinated with NGOs to maintain essential services [61].
  - UNFPA and partners focused on adolescent pregnancy prevention and gender-based violence programming continuity despite constraints [49,51].
- Critical Assessment:
  - While local adaptations mitigated some impacts, long-term funding shortfalls pose a risk to gains in ANC attendance, SBA coverage, and maternal health outcomes [15,53,59].

## 6. Role and Integration of Traditional Birth Attendants (TBAs)

### Rohingya Refugees (Cox’s Bazar)

- Cultural Context:
  - TBAs deeply embedded in Rohingya culture as primary birth attendants [34,36].
- Shift in Role:
  - Since 2021, TBAs have been systematically trained and transitioned from delivery providers to cultural mediators and referral facilitators [34].
  - Training focuses on birth preparedness, danger sign recognition, ANC promotion, and advocacy for facility births [34].
- Impact:
  - Facility births in areas with active TBA programs have increased from 33% in 2020 to over 95% by 2022 [34].
  - TBAs help bridge cultural mistrust and promote early care seeking [34].
- Challenges:
  - Gender norms restricting interaction with male providers and dependency on family decision-making limit full transition [34,36].

### Karen Refugees (Mae La Camp)

- TBA Program Structure:
  - TBAs supported by Karen Women’s Organization (KWO) with annual refresher training, provision of hygiene kits, and locally adapted manuals [75].
  - TBAs attend nearly 80% of births, working alongside professional SBAs [75].
- Roles:
  - Safe home deliveries, maternal education, birth preparedness counseling, cultural mediation, and facilitating referrals [75].
- Outcomes:
  - Strong community trust in TBAs fosters high SBA acceptance and facility-based deliveries [41,75].
- Sustainability:
  - Integration into healthcare teams enables culturally respectful maternal health promotion [75].

---

## Conclusion

Between 2017 and 2023, maternal and neonatal health outcomes among Rohingya refugees in Cox’s Bazar and Karen refugees in Mae La camp reflect marked differences shaped by healthcare systems, workforce capacity, cultural mediation, and funding environments.

- **Indicator Trends:** Mae La camp consistently demonstrates higher ANC and SBA rates (>90% and ~80% respectively) with marked neonatal mortality decline (~51% reduction), while Cox’s Bazar shows improvements but persistent gaps (70–72% ANC 4+ completion, SBA reaching ~82%, neonatal mortality ~27/1,000 live births).
  
- **Healthcare Models:** Cox’s Bazar relies on a fragmented, NGO-driven model with multiple actors and coordination challenges; Mae La benefits from integrated Thai public health system support with stable staffing and referral systems.

- **Workforce:** Mae La’s smaller, community-rooted midwife workforce is sustainably trained and mentored, while Cox’s Bazar confronts shortages despite scaling efforts and mentorship programs supported by UNFPA.

- **Referral Systems:** Accessibility and reliability of emergency referral and transport is stronger in Mae La, facilitated by government finance and infrastructure, compared to Cox’s Bazar’s terrain and cultural barriers hindering utilization.

- **Traditional Birth Attendants:** Both settings leverage TBAs as cultural mediators; Mae La’s formal integration model has longer standing and deeper health system embedding, whereas Cox’s Bazar initiated systematic TBA role transformation more recently with encouraging results.

- **UNFPA Funding Cuts (post-2021):** Funding reductions critically strained both contexts—leading to facility closures, reduced staffing, diminished service breadth, and rising mortality risks—though with differential impact depending on local adaptations and government support.

Addressing the complex interplay of funding stability, culturally sensitive community engagement, workforce strengthening, and accessible referral systems is vital to sustainably improving maternal and neonatal outcomes in these refugee settings.
