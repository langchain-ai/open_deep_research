# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality and Prenatal Care among Rohingya Refugees in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

---

## Introduction

This report analyzes and compares key maternal and neonatal health indicators among two refugee populations—the Rohingya in Cox’s Bazar, Bangladesh, and the Karen in Mae La camp, Thailand—between 2017 and 2023. It evaluates antenatal care (ANC) completion rates (specifically four or more visits), skilled birth attendance percentages, incidence of postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), and neonatal mortality within 28 days. The analysis further examines differences in healthcare delivery models, correlates them with midwife-to-population ratios, availability of emergency obstetric care (EmOC) within two hours transport radius, and cultural mediation involving traditional birth attendants (TBAs). Finally, it assesses the impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reductions on maternal health service continuity in both settings using data primarily from UNHCR health information systems and Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) reports.

---

## Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicators (2017–2023)

### Antenatal Care Visit Completion Rates

- **Rohingya Refugees, Cox’s Bazar:** ANC utilization is relatively high with 71.6% to 80.5% of women completing four or more ANC visits. Early ANC initiation within the first trimester reaches approximately 80.5% in some studies. Factors influencing utilization include education, parity, freedom of movement, and access to accurate maternal health information. However, some communication channels like radio were paradoxically linked to delayed initiation, suggesting complexity in health messaging effectiveness.  
- **Karen Refugees, Mae La Camp:** Maternal health services demonstrate consistent, high ANC uptake, with over 90% of pregnant women attending four or more visits. This outcome is supported by long-term integration of services with the Thai Ministry of Public Health and NGOs, contributing to sustained engagement in prenatal care many years before and during the study period.

### Skilled Birth Attendance

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Skilled birth attendance remains limited despite improvements. In 2018, only approximately 22% of births occurred in health facilities, with the majority taking place at home under the care of TBAs or family members. Barriers include cultural preferences for home births, mistrust of male health staff, privacy concerns, and fears regarding medical procedures like episiotomies. By 2024, facility births reportedly increased significantly within project areas, reaching above 80%, mainly due to NGO-driven programs targeting birth location decisions.  
- **Mae La Camp:** Skilled birth attendance is substantially higher, averaging around 78–80%. Facility births are common though cesarean section rates remain low (~3.7%). The relatively well-supported health infrastructure and sustained maternal health programs have led to more frequent use of skilled birth attendants and institutional deliveries.

### Incidence of Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

- **Cox’s Bazar:** PPH is identified as the leading cause of maternal deaths, accounting for approximately 49% of fatalities in the camps. Weak referral systems, delays in seeking and receiving care, and resource shortages exacerbate outcomes.  
- **Mae La Camp:** Specific incidence rates of PPH are not explicitly documented; however, maternal mortality rates, while lower than Cox’s Bazar, remain high (126.5 per 100,000 live births). Ongoing maternal deaths imply complications including hemorrhage, though advanced emergency obstetric care and neonatal units potentially contribute to better management.

### Neonatal Mortality within 28 Days

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Neonatal mortality is notably high at approximately 27 deaths per 1,000 live births (2017–2018). Most deaths occur out of facility (home/community) settings, especially in distant areas. Mortality rates are inversely related to proximity to health facilities with specialized neonatal care.  
- **Mae La Camp:** Neonatal mortality has decreased significantly over the long-term period (2008–2011 data) from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births, attributed to establishment of Special Care Baby Units (SCBU) staffed by trained refugee health workers, improved antenatal and neonatal care, and better overall camp health conditions.

## Healthcare Delivery Models and Service Comparisons

### Cox’s Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospitals and Community-Based Systems

- The health system is heavily NGO-driven and fragmented with entities like Save the Children operating specialized Maternal and Child Hospitals offering 24/7 emergency obstetric and neonatal care including caesarean sections. NGOs such as HOPE Midwifery Institute and MedGlobal provide midwifery training, ANC, delivery services, and community outreach (Mother’s Clubs, referral hotlines).  
- High midwife turnover and shortages persist despite advanced training programs. A midwife-to-population ratio remains suboptimal, with less than 10% of births attended by skilled providers in earlier years, improving only more recently.  
- Access to emergency obstetric care is supported by community referral systems (e.g., Referral Hub), enabling free 24/7 transport. However, delays caused by gender norms, fear of male healthcare staff, cultural preferences, terrain barriers, and security concerns affect timely utilization.  
- Traditional birth attendants hold strong influence culturally, often conducting home births. Since 2021, training programs for TBAs have sought to encourage referrals and promote facility births yet cultural barriers and mistrust persist.

### Mae La Camp: Thai Ministry of Public Health-Integrated Services

- Services are integrated within the Thai government health system and coordinated with NGOs like Malteser International. Maternal health is provided mainly through a Primary Health Care (PHC) model combining government hospitals’ capacity for emergency obstetric care with camp-level community outreach.  
- Midwifery staffing, including trained refugee assistant nurses and peer educators, is more stable and effectively integrated compared to Cox’s Bazar, though exact ratios are not precisely quantified.  
- Emergency obstetric care meets UN standards with multiple nearby facilities accessible within a two-hour radius. Financial subsidies and government-NGO cooperation reduce burden on refugees, though undocumented migrants may face cost and mobility constraints.  
- TBAs are incorporated through training and cultural mediation initiatives, supporting safe birth practices and active referrals while preserving valued cultural roles. Peer educators and refugee health workers contribute to culturally sensitive service delivery, increasing acceptance and uptake.

## Correlation of Health Outcomes with Workforce, Emergency Care, and Cultural Mediation

| Factor                                   | Cox’s Bazar Rohingya Camps                      | Mae La Camp Karen Refugees                       |
|-----------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Midwife-to-population ratio             | Low, with high turnover; ongoing capacity building; insufficient coverage in 2017–2023 | More stable workforce with trained refugee midwives and assistants; better coverage and integration |
| Emergency obstetric care availability   | Available via NGO facilities and Referral Hub; infrastructural and security challenges delay access; limited transport options | Multiple government and NGO facilities within 2-hour radius; subsidies ease financial burden; consistent transport access |
| Cultural mediation involving TBAs       | TBAs strong in community but mistrust of facilities; recent training improving referrals; home births culturally preferred | TBAs integrated formally; refugee peer educators facilitate acceptance of formal care; cultural norms balanced with modern practices |

The relatively better midwifery support, healthcare integration, and emergency care accessibility in Mae La camp correspond with higher skilled birth attendance and substantial neonatal mortality decline. In contrast, Cox’s Bazar’s resource constraints, cultural barriers, and fragmented NGO-based care initially contributed to lower facility birth rates and higher maternal/neonatal mortality, though recent interventions show marked improvements.

## Impact of 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction on Maternal Health Service Continuity

### Cox’s Bazar

- UNFPA funding cuts since 2021 have led to widespread global closures of over 1,000 health facilities and mobile health teams, affecting maternal health delivery. In Cox’s Bazar, although funding was constrained, UNFPA-supported programs sustained key maternal services such as 24/7 safe deliveries, newborn care, family planning, and psychosocial support through “women-friendly safe spaces.”  
- Despite these efforts, maternal mortality increased from around 179 per 100,000 live births in 2018 to 295 in 2023, indicating deteriorating outcomes amid resource gaps. Facility delivery rates paradoxically remain above 80% in targeted areas, suggesting uneven service continuity.  
- MSF activity reports reflect ongoing maternity care provision with approximately 5,000 deliveries annually supported across 8 health facilities, but also reveal increasing challenges due to deteriorated WASH conditions, disease outbreaks, and staffing shortages.  
- Funding shortfalls impede midwife recruitment and retention; UNFPA midwife coverage reportedly met only 47% of planned midwives in crisis settings in 2025, threatening further deterioration in maternal and newborn health.

### Mae La Camp

- Maternal health services rely on hybrid funding from Thai government, NGOs, and international aid, with UNFPA greatly contributing to SRH programming (family planning, adolescent pregnancy prevention, reproductive health education). Funding reductions starting 2021, further aggravated by US aid suspensions in 2025, led to closures or suspension of NGO-operated health facilities, notably those run by the International Rescue Committee (IRC).  
- Refugees faced increased barriers to accessing costly emergency care outside the camp, compounded by legal and mobility restrictions. This jeopardizes previously sustained high ANC attendance and skilled birth attendance rates.  
- UNFPA programs experienced reduced capacity to meet demand, especially for family planning and gender-based violence services, undermining progress achieved over decades.  
- The funding crisis underscores the fragile nature of humanitarian health interventions in protracted refugee settings dependent on external assistance.

## Additional Contextual Factors Influencing Maternal Health Outcomes

- **Cultural Norms and Gender Dynamics:** In both contexts, decision-making authority lies predominantly with husbands and family elders, limiting women’s autonomy over birth location and health service utilization. Strict gender norms, especially relating to preferences for female health providers, influence utilization patterns.  
- **Mobility and Security Concerns:** Cox’s Bazar refugees face geographic and security barriers, including impassable terrain during monsoons and risks to health workers, restricting access to EmOC even when available. Mae La refugees experience legal movement restrictions limiting options outside the camp.  
- **Health Workforce Turnover:** High turnover in Cox’s Bazar midwife workforce challenges consistency of care. Mae La benefits from longer-term staffing with refugee health workers and government-supported professionals, aiding service stability.  
- **Health Infrastructure:** Both settings face infrastructure constraints, but Mae La's integration within Thailand’s health system provides more stable access to comprehensive services. Cox’s Bazar depends on NGO field hospitals with variable capacity and supply chain challenges.

---

## Conclusion

The Rohingya refugee population in Cox’s Bazar and the Karen refugee population in Mae La camp exhibit distinct maternal and neonatal health outcomes shaped by differences in healthcare delivery models, cultural dynamics, workforce availability, and emergency obstetric care access.

- Mae La camp benefits from integrated Thai Ministry of Public Health services, a relatively stable midwifery workforce, and functional emergency care, resulting in higher antenatal care completion (>90%), skilled birth attendance (~80%), and declining neonatal mortality rates (down to ~10.7/1000).  
- Cox’s Bazar, relying primarily on NGO-operated field hospitals and challenged by cultural barriers, low skilled birth attendance (initially ~22%), and fragmented referral systems, shows higher maternal mortality (229 to 168/100,000 live births) and neonatal mortality (~27/1000), although improvements have occurred due to intensified midwifery training, community outreach, and referral innovations.  
- The 2021 UNFPA funding cuts significantly affected service continuity in both settings, more severely in Mae La camp where NGO service suspensions added financial and access barriers, and in Cox’s Bazar where constrained funding challenged midwife coverage and emergency care provision despite ongoing NGO engagement.  
- Addressing cultural mediation, empowering women’s decision-making, enhancing workforce stability, improving transport and referral systems, and securing sustainable funding are crucial to further reduce maternal and neonatal mortality in both refugee populations.
