# Comparative Analysis of Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicators among Rohingya and Karen Refugees (2017–2023)

---

## Introduction

This report presents a comprehensive, multi-year comparative analysis of maternal and neonatal health indicators among Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand, covering the period 2017 to 2023. It examines key indicators such as antenatal care (ANC) completion rates (with an emphasis on 4+ visits), skilled birth attendance (SBA) percentages, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, and neonatal mortality within 28 days. The analysis contextualizes these health outcomes within differing healthcare delivery models: the NGO-operated field hospital framework in Cox’s Bazar versus integration with the Thai Ministry of Public Health in Mae La. Further, it critically reviews midwife and skilled birth attendant workforce characteristics, emergency obstetric care (EmOC) availability, referral systems, and the evolving role of traditional birth attendants (TBAs). The impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reductions on maternal health service continuity is also evaluated.

---

## 1. Maternal and Neonatal Health Indicators: Trends and Comparison (2017–2023)

### 1.1 Antenatal Care Visit Completion Rates (4+ Visits)

- **Rohingya Refugees (Cox’s Bazar):** Antenatal care completion rates have shown steady improvement. Surveys indicate that between 71.5% and 80.8% of pregnant women completed four or more ANC visits from 2017 through 2023. Early ANC initiation (within the first trimester) was reported at 80.8% in recent cohorts. These rates represent a considerable increase compared to early post-influx data around 2017 [1][7][10][12]. Socio-demographic factors such as maternal education and freedom of movement positively influence ANC uptake, although some communication channels (e.g., radio) were paradoxically linked to delayed ANC initiation [7][10].

- **Karen Refugees (Mae La Camp):** ANC completion rates have remained consistently high and stable, exceeding 90% for 4+ visits throughout the 2017–2023 period. This consistency is attributed to integrated healthcare services embedded within the Thai Ministry of Public Health system and long-established NGO collaboration, enabling sustained engagement in maternal health care [24][28].

### 1.2 Skilled Birth Attendance and Facility-Based Deliveries

- **Rohingya Refugees:** Skilled birth attendance increased substantially over the period. Earlier in the studied years (circa 2017-2018), only about 59.5% of deliveries took place in adequately equipped health facilities, with a corresponding SBA coverage of roughly 69-70%. By late 2023, facility deliveries reached approximately 82%, with SBA attendance closely aligned. Cesarean section rates rose from about 3.1% to 5% in recent years, reflecting better access to emergency obstetric services although still below global averages [1][5][7][11].

- **Karen Refugees:** Skilled birth attendance remained high (~80%) with facility births predominating. Caesarean section rates were low (~3.7%), consistent with a primary care-focused delivery model emphasizing natural birth and limited surgical intervention. Stable long-term ANC and SBA rates reflect the continuity of integrated health services [24][28][35].

### 1.3 Incidence of Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

- **Rohingya Refugees:** PPH remains the leading cause of maternal mortality, accounting for approximately 29–35% of maternal deaths based on MPMSR data and field studies from 2017 to 2023. The highest risk is immediate postpartum hemorrhage, frequently occurring within 24 hours post-delivery, especially among home births attended by untrained TBAs [14][16][37]. Delays in decision to seek care, transport, and quality of care uptake compound the issue.

- **Karen Refugees:** Explicit incidence data on PPH are limited, but maternal mortality studies attribute hemorrhage as a major cause alongside hypertensive disorders. Reported maternal mortality in the camp remains elevated (~126.5 per 100,000 live births) despite low cesarean rates and high SBA coverage, indicating that PPH and related complications still challenge service delivery [24][28][35].

### 1.4 Neonatal Mortality within 28 Days

- **Rohingya Refugees:** Neonatal mortality was estimated at 27 deaths per 1,000 live births between 2017 and 2018, among the highest in refugee settings globally. Most neonatal deaths and stillbirths (72–76%) occurred outside health facilities, mainly at home or in the community. Longitudinal data through 2023 indicate persistence of these high rates, though improved facility care and specialized neonatal units (initiated by NGOs in 2023-2026) have begun reducing mortality risk [18][19][20][14].

- **Karen Refugees:** Neonatal mortality declined markedly over the past two decades, with a 62% reduction from 2000 to 2018 and rates reaching approximately 10.7 per 1,000 live births by 2011 after the establishment of Special Care Baby Units (SCBU). Infant mortality declines were attributed to improved neonatal intensive care and skilled refugee staff involvement [24][28][36][37].

## 2. Healthcare Delivery Models and Their Influence on Maternal and Neonatal Health

### 2.1 Cox's Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospitals and Fragmented Model

- Healthcare services are predominantly delivered by international NGOs such as Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF), Save the Children, and BRAC, complemented by UN agencies (UNFPA, UNHCR) and local government collaboration.

- Facility infrastructure includes field hospitals with emergency obstetric and neonatal care. Since 2018, MSF operates a 100-bed hospital with emergency rooms and ICUs; in 2026, Save the Children opened a dedicated Maternal and Child Hospital with 59 beds offering 24/7 services including cesarean sections and neonatal intensive care [6][7][16].

- Services are often fragmented with overlapping roles among NGOs, leading to coordination challenges in referral systems, supply chains, and data management.

- Referral hubs and transport systems have been established with NGO support, including free 24/7 emergency referral transport; however, terrain, security threats, and gender norms delay timely utilization [12][17][32].

- Community engagement programs train TBAs to act as cultural mediators and referral facilitators, aiming to shift births into health facilities. Transition is incomplete; strong cultural preferences for home births and mistrust of facilities persist [9][34].

### 2.2 Mae La Camp: Integrated Thai Ministry of Public Health Model

- Services are embedded within Thailand’s national public health system, managed in partnership with NGOs such as Malteser International and the Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU).

- The health infrastructure includes primary health care centers, a Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) established in 2007, and linkages to Thai government hospitals for advanced emergency obstetric care [23][24][33].

- Referral systems are well-established, financially supported by Thai health authorities, ensuring access to EmOC within a two-hour radius with minimal out-of-pocket expenditures for refugees [15][32].

- TBAs have been formally integrated as trained cultural mediators and peer educators, supporting smooth referral processes, community health promotion, and bridging cultural norms with biomedical practices [25][34].

- Strong refugee self-governance structures and coordination with Thai public health bodies enable sustained, culturally sensitive health service delivery and effective use of limited resources [13][14].

## 3. Midwife and Skilled Birth Attendant Workforce: Numbers, Skill Mix, Ratios, and Mentorship

### 3.1 Rohingya Camps, Cox's Bazar

- The midwifery workforce expanded amid a humanitarian emergency, with UNFPA supporting over 150 licensed national midwives and approximately 280 midwives across Cox’s Bazar and nearby sites by 2023. However, workforce coverage remains strained given the large refugee population exceeding 900,000 [1][11][30].

- Midwifery cadres operate in government and NGO facilities, midwife-led birthing centers (MLBCs), and outreach programs. Midwives provide antenatal, delivery, postnatal, family planning, and psychosocial care [4][5].

- Structured mentorship programs are provided via UNFPA, WHO, and partners, involving continuous supervision, clinical updates, and leadership development within a Midwifery Strategic Plan (2021-2023) [1][28][30].

- Recruitment is national, with licensed Bangladeshi midwives hired and trained on humanitarian protocols. Retention efforts include leadership opportunities, improved working conditions, security measures, and community engagement [1][5][6].

- Turnover remains a challenge due to high workload, cultural barriers, and camp insecurity. Training of TBAs as community health facilitators aids bridging workforce gaps and promotes facility births [9][34].

### 3.2 Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp

- The midwifery and skilled birth attendant workforce is composed primarily of locally trained Karen and Burman women who complete a six-month accelerated Skilled Birth Attendant training developed by SMRU, aligned with international standards [18][19][20].

- Precise midwife-to-population ratios are not explicitly documented; however, the program serves approximately 18,000 refugees, with high SBA coverage sustained over decades [20][21].

- Continuing education and mentorship are embedded within the training program to uphold care quality, supported by NGO and government collaboration [20][21].

- Recruitment focuses on community candidates with family and social support, facilitating retention and sustainability. Midwives gain social status and contribute to community resilience [20].

- Integration into the PHC model ensures midwives operate alongside Thai health staff, providing antenatal, delivery, postnatal, and family planning services with strong community participation [21][33].

## 4. Emergency Obstetric Care Availability, Referral Systems, and Role of Traditional Birth Attendants

### 4.1 Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC)

- **Cox’s Bazar:** EmOC is provided primarily through NGO field hospitals, including MSF’s 100-bed hospital and Save the Children’s maternal facility (opened 2026). Referral hubs coordinate emergency transport, but delays and coverage gaps exist due to geography, security, and cultural barriers. Approximately 8 health facilities support ~5,000 deliveries annually with capacity for emergency interventions [6][11][16][17].

- **Mae La Camp:** EmOC is integrated into Thailand’s health system with multiple nearby government hospitals accessible within two hours, supported by financial subsidies. The camp’s Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) provides neonatal intensive care, and emergency referrals to public hospitals are standard, with minimal payment barriers [15][23][32].

### 4.2 Referral Distances, Transport Times, and Referral Hubs

- **Cox’s Bazar:** Average referral distances vary widely, with some areas requiring over 30-minute transport, compounded by difficult terrain and seasonal weather. Referral hubs provide 24/7 ambulance services, but service utilisation is impeded by fear of male staff, cultural mistrust, and delayed decision-making by families [12][17][32].

- **Mae La Camp:** Referral distances are shorter and consistently within a two-hour radius to equipped hospitals. Transport is reliable, and financial support from Thai authorities reduces barriers. Referral hubs function with clear protocols and active cultural mediation [15][32].

### 4.3 Traditional Birth Attendants (TBAs): Transition and Cultural Mediation

- **Cox’s Bazar:** TBAs are culturally entrenched as primary birth attendants but have shifted from delivery providers to community health promoters and referral facilitators since 2021 through targeted training programs. Nonetheless, home births remain common due to persistent fears and cultural norms [9][34][41].

- **Mae La Camp:** TBAs are formally recognized and integrated as peer educators and cultural mediators, facilitating community trust and improved uptake of maternal health services. Their role includes educating pregnant women, advocating for facility births, and participating in referral networks [25][34].

## 5. Impact of 2021 UNFPA Funding Reductions on Maternal Health Services

### 5.1 Cox’s Bazar

- UNFPA funding cuts beginning in 2021 led to global closures and service suspensions but were partially mitigated in Cox’s Bazar via donor reprioritization and partnerships. Despite this, midwife recruitment and retention suffered, with coverage meeting only about 47% of planned staffing levels in crisis settings by 2023 [14][36].

- Maternal mortality increased from an estimated 179 per 100,000 live births in 2018 to approximately 295 in 2023, potentially reflecting funding constraints, increased demand, and external stressors such as disease outbreaks and environmental disasters [14][26].

- Facility delivery rates paradoxically improved (>80% in targeted areas), though unevenly, suggesting service continuity but reduced breadth and quality in some zones [14][36].

### 5.2 Mae La Camp

- Reduced UNFPA and international funding starting in 2021, compounded by US aid suspensions from 2025, resulted in closure or suspension of some NGO-operated services (notably the International Rescue Committee), affecting contraceptive availability and sexual/reproductive health education [15][33].

- Refugees faced increased financial and legal barriers accessing costly hospital care outside the camp, undermining previously high ANC attendance and SBA coverage [15].

- The fragile hybrid funding model, reliant on Thai government support and NGO contributions, was strained, diminishing family planning and gender-based violence services and risking reversals in maternal health gains [15][33].

## 6. Data Discrepancies, Limitations, and Reconciliation

- Differences in data collection methodologies and timing cause variations in reported indicators. For example, neonatal mortality data for Rohingya is derived from prospective surveillance (2017–2018) and continuous MPMSR reporting, while Karen camp neonatal mortality data primarily comes from retrospective cohort analyses ending in 2018 [18][24][36].

- Maternal mortality estimates vary by source and year, reflecting measurement challenges in humanitarian settings, but consistent patterns emerge linking PPH and hypertensive disorders as leading causes [14][24][37].

- Reporting on workforce ratios is limited; estimates indicate low midwife-to-population ratios in Cox’s Bazar due to large refugee numbers and staffing shortages vis-à-vis smaller, more stable Karen camp workforce [1][20].

- UNFPA and NGO reports supply the most current data, but publication lags and the dynamic crisis context require cautious interpretation of trends.

---

## Conclusion

Between 2017 and 2023, maternal and neonatal health outcomes among Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar and Karen refugees in Mae La camp reflected distinct trajectories shaped by healthcare delivery models, workforce capacity, cultural factors, and funding environments.

- **Healthcare delivery models** differ substantially: Cox’s Bazar’s NGO-driven fragmented model grapples with rapid population influx, resource constraints, and cultural barriers, whereas Mae La benefits from integrated Thai Ministry of Public Health coordination, stable workforce, and sustainable referral systems.

- **Maternal health indicators** show higher ANC and SBA coverage and greater neonatal mortality declines in Mae La (>90% ANC, ~80% SBA, neonatal mortality reduced by 62%) compared to Cox’s Bazar (ANC 71–81%, SBA improved to ~82%; neonatal mortality high at ~27/1000).

- **Midwife workforce** stability and localized training with ongoing mentorship underpin better maternal outcomes in Mae La, while Cox’s Bazar suffers from shortages and turnover despite UNFPA-supported scaling efforts.

- **Emergency obstetric care availability and referral systems** are more reliably accessible within Mae La, facilitated by government support and simplified refugee hospital access; Cox’s Bazar has improved facilities but faces delays and logistical challenges.

- **Traditional birth attendants’ transition** from delivery providers to cultural mediators is more formalized and effective in Mae La, contributing to facility birth acceptance; progress in Cox’s Bazar is ongoing but hampered by persistent cultural reluctance.

- **UNFPA funding reductions in 2021 and subsequently** exerted measurable negative impacts on both settings, influencing staffing, service continuity, and programmatic reach, though mitigated variably by local adaptations and partner support.

Addressing sustained funding, culturally sensitive community engagement, workforce strengthening, and integrated referral systems remains essential to advancing maternal and neonatal health in these refugee contexts.
