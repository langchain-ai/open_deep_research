# New Zealand Construction Metalworks Market Analysis: Structural Steel Fabrication (2018–2024)

---

## 1. Market Definition and Official Data Sources

### 1.1 Industry Classification: ANZSIC Code

The **New Zealand structural steel fabrication industry** is officially classified under the **ANZSIC code C2221NZ** (“Structural Steel Fabricating”). This classification specifically covers enterprises engaged in fabricating structural steel components for buildings or other structures, including steel frameworks, reinforcing bars, girders, joists, scaffolding, and reinforcing mesh [1][2][3].

This precise classification excludes steel mills, distributors, importers, and manufacturers of non-structural metal products, focusing solely on the fabrication stage where raw or processed steel is shaped and assembled into construction components.

---

### 1.2 Extracting Key Market Metrics from Official Sources

To analyse the structural steel fabricating sector’s **employment, revenue, and tonnage fabricated** for 2018–2024, the following primary New Zealand official data sources and methods are essential:

- **Employment and Business Counts**:  
  Use Stats NZ’s **Business Employment Data** and **Business Demography Statistics**, filtered by ANZSIC C2221NZ. These datasets provide quarterly and annual counts of jobs, filled positions, and number of active businesses, including geographic and demographic breakdowns ([5], [12], [14]).

- **Revenue and Financial Metrics**:  
  The **Annual Enterprise Survey (AES)** offers detailed financial information such as total income, gross profit ratios, and revenue per employee for structural steel fabricators. While AES covers a sub-set of businesses with turnover over NZD 60,000, it is the most comprehensive official revenue source available ([5], [11], [15]).

- **Tonnage Fabricated**:  
  Tonnage data is less directly available through official statistics. Instead, it is primarily sourced from industry reports by **Steel Construction New Zealand (SCNZ)**, which regularly publishes fabrication capacity and workload surveys, estimating actual output and spare capacity typically between 28–38%.  
  Additionally, triangulation with **MBIE and Stats NZ Overseas Merchandise Trade (OMT) data** on imports and exports of steel structural products helps estimate consumed volumes within New Zealand. Relevant HS codes for steel products (such as HS 7308, 7216, or 7207) are cross-referenced to infer fabrication demand ([4], [6], [10], [21], [24]).

---

### 1.3 Triangulation Methods for Missing or Incomplete Data

When official data series for tonnage fabricated are incomplete or lagging, triangulation adopts a multi-source approach:

- **Fabrication Capacity & Workload**:  
  Combine SCNZ’s quarterly fabrication capacity reports and workload surveys with employment and revenue data to model estimated annual tonnage fabricated.

- **Steel Trade Statistics**:  
  Use MBIE trade data on imports and exports of raw and processed steel (HS codes 7207–7216). Subtract known exports or intermediate uses to approximate domestic fabrication input demand.

- **Project Consumption Volumes**:  
  Aggregate estimated structural steel consumption from major construction infrastructure projects (detailed in Section 4) to validate or adjust production and market size estimates.

- **Business Demography and Firm-level Data**:  
  Amplify analysis with company-level data from annual reports, companies office filings, and IBISWorld reports for revenue and employment trends.

The integration of these data inputs allows holistic and consistent market estimation despite incomplete official series.

## 2. Market Size, Growth, and Trends (2018–2024)

### 2.1 Market Size and Revenue

- The New Zealand structural steel fabrication industry was valued at approximately **NZD 1.8 billion in 2025**, per IBISWorld estimates, with a slight contraction expected around 2025–2026 (CAGR approx. -6.0% from 2020 to 2025), followed by a modest recovery at a CAGR of +2.1% forecast between 2021 and 2026 ([1], [2], [9], [12]).

- Total businesses numbered around **267 firms in 2026**, predominantly SMEs, with medium to large firms making up a concentrated core.

- Revenue per employee increased over the period, evidencing productivity gains amidst employment decline. Median gross profit ratios vary by firm size but generally range from 50% to 88% ([5], [11], [15]).

---

### 2.2 Employment

- Employment data from IBISWorld and Stats NZ reports employment at about **4,100 workers in 2025**, declining slightly thereafter to around **4,000 in 2026**. This corresponds to an average firm size of about 15 employees.

- Industry employment contracted on average by about **-8.9% annually from 2020–2025** due to industry consolidation, automation, and efficiency improvements ([2], [7], [12]).

- Regional employment distribution aligns with business locations: Auckland leads with 99 businesses, Canterbury follows with 45, and Waikato with 30 ([5], [12]).

---

### 2.3 Tonnage Fabricated

- Structural steel fabrication capacity expanded from **~120,000 tonnes in 2018** to approximately **150,000 tonnes in 2023–2024**.

- Actual fabrication output is about **100,000 tonnes per annum**, with **28–38% spare capacity** indicating room for growth or slack amid variation in construction demand ([4], [6], [16]).

- Structural steel accounts for **more than 50% of the multi-level construction market nationally**, reaching **over 80% market share in Christchurch** due to seismic risk mitigation preferences ([16], [17]).

---

### 2.4 Market Dynamics and Construction Cycles

- The market closely tracks the broader **construction sector cycles**, as revealed in MBIE annual reports. Rising infrastructure spending and rebuilding (earthquake-prone building strengthening, hospital new builds) supported growth pre-2020.

- The COVID-19 pandemic and associated global disruptions induced short-term contraction in 2020–2021.

- Recovery from 2022 onwards ties to a strong government infrastructure pipeline, including large transport projects and public buildings, enabled by policy shifts such as strengthened seismic and sustainability regulations ([12], [14], [16], [36]).

- Employment declines are partly explained by automation and process innovations reducing labour intensity while maintaining or increasing output.

## 4. Major Infrastructure Projects Driving Structural Steel Demand (2018–2024)

This section includes the largest known projects where structural steel fabrication demand materially influences the New Zealand market.

---

### 4.1 City Rail Link (CRL), Auckland

- Description: New underground twin-tunnel railway (~3.45 km) beneath Auckland city centre, including four new stations.  
- Timeline: Construction began 2018, with expected passenger services from mid to late 2026.  
- Structural Steel Demand: Approximately **25,000 tonnes** of reinforcing and structural steel fabricated locally, primarily involving Pacific Steel as fabricator and steel supplied by New Zealand Steel.  
- Project Value: NZD 5.493 billion  
- Notes: Complex TBM tunnelling and station construction requiring major steel reinforcement works ([1], [4], [6], [9], [26], [27], [28], [30]).

---

### 4.2 Te Kaha (One New Zealand Stadium), Christchurch

- Description: Multi-use stadium with seating for 30,000–37,000 including sports and concerts.  
- Timeline: Steel superstructure largely completed by early 2025; opening expected April 2026.  
- Steel Demand: Estimated major radial steel truss modules of ~160 tonnes each installed; total tonnage undisclosed but understood to be significant for seating bowl and roof structures.  
- Project Value: NZD 683 million  
- Construction Highlights: Use of heavy-lift cranes for large steel modules; extensive fabrication and erection phases in 2023–2025 ([6], [7], [9], [11], [13], [14]).

---

### 4.3 New Dunedin Hospital Redevelopment

- Description: The largest health infrastructure project in Otago, involving outpatient and inpatient building construction.  
- Timeline: Early outpatient building phase completed 2026; inpatient building scheduled for completion 2029–2031.  
- Steel Demand: Approximately **490 tonnes** of reinforcing steel documented for outpatient phase alone, with upcoming large-scale structural steel erection planned for inpatient buildings.  
- Project Value: Initially NZD 1.88 billion, with cost escalations approaching NZD 3 billion  
- Notes: Heavy use of reinforcing and structural steel consistent with a 70,000 m² facility complexity ([16], [17], [19], [31], [32]).

---

### 4.4 Takitimu North Link, Tauranga

- Description: A 14 km highway upgrading project featuring multiple bridges and retaining walls.  
- Timeline: Construction commenced 2021, staged completion targeted in 2026.  
- Steel Demand: Structural steel required primarily for bridges with large girders (18 m length) lifted by 280t crawler cranes; tonnages substantial but no public totals available.  
- Project Cost: Estimated around NZD 400+ million ([16], [17], [20], [26], [30]).

---

### 4.5 Other Notable Infrastructure Projects

- Additional projects assisting steel demand include Ōtaki to North of Levin highway upgrades (~NZD 1.5 billion), Penlink toll road, and Mt Messenger Bypass; each features bridge and structural steel elements ([1], [26], [33]).

- The **East West Link** is excluded as it pertains to Australia, not New Zealand.

---

### 4.6 Aggregate Steel Demand vs. Market Capacity

- The combined structural steel demand from the highlighted projects accounts for approximately **30,000–40,000 tonnes**, principally from CRL and Te Kaha.

- When including other projects, total estimated project-driven steel demand for 2018–2024 aligns closely with official fabrication outputs (~100,000 tonnes annualised) and the total industry capacity (~120,000–150,000 tonnes).

- The aggregated project demand validates capacities and market size, confirming the sector’s ability to absorb major infrastructure pipeline requirements ([4], [12], [16], [26], [32]).

## 5. Explanation of Market Size Discrepancies and Trend Reconciliation

- **Scope Boundaries:**  
  Variations in reported market size arise from differing definitions and inclusions — some reports encompass only fabrication excluding distribution or raw steel production, while others aggregate these elements.

- **Employment vs. Revenue Variations:**  
  Employment contraction alongside stable or growing revenues reflects **productivity improvements, automation, and consolidation** in fabricating firms.

- **Construction Sector Cycles:**  
  Employment and capacity utilization correlated with broader construction cycles and infrastructure funding cycles. The COVID-19 period induced contraction, while recovery from 2022 aligned with increased infrastructure investments and large project starts.

- **Import Data and Supply Chain Factors:**  
  Steel trade volumes and fabrication outputs do not always match due to stockpiling, imports of semi-finished components, and export of fabricated steel.

- **Data Lags and Survey Differences:**  
  Official survey timing differences (quarterly vs. annual), thresholds for survey inclusion, and reporting conventions also contribute to observed discrepancies.

This nuanced understanding reconciles differences across IBISWorld, Stats NZ, SCNZ, and MBIE data sources.

## 6. Tariff Analysis on Raw Steel Imports (HS Codes 7207–7216)

### 6.1 Tariff Rates and Trade Regime

- Import tariffs on raw steel products classified under **HS codes 7207 to 7216** are generally **low or zero percent** in New Zealand, primarily due to multiple **free trade agreements (FTAs)** including AANZFTA, CPTPP, and RCEP.

- Where tariffs apply, rates range between **0% and 5%**, favouring duty-free access to qualifying origins. Tariff schedules have been stable and unchanged from 2018 through 2024 due to a government tariff freeze policy ([1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [8]).

- No significant tariff hikes or trade policy changes toward steel raw materials have occurred in this period.

---

### 6.2 Import Volumes and Economic Impact

- New Zealand imports substantial quantities of raw steel and semi-finished products annually, valued at approximately **NZD 700 million to over 1 billion** (2020-2024), primarily sourced from countries like China and Australia ([6], [8], [15]).

- The low tariff environment fosters competitive landed steel costs, ensuring fabricators access cost-effective global steel inputs.

- Margins for local fabricators are highly sensitive to **global steel commodity price fluctuations**, **foreign exchange rate movements (particularly NZD/USD)**, and **freight charges** rather than tariff-induced protection ([5], [15], [16]).

---

### 6.3 Analytical Context and Implications

- The effective **lack of border protection via tariffs implies a largely open market** for raw steel, enforcing strong global market price linkage.

- Local fabricators rely on a **robust domestic steel mill (New Zealand Steel)** producing ~650,000 tonnes annually, providing partial insulation from import volatility.

- Fabricators must manage raw material price volatility, logistics costs, and currency risk to sustain margins.

- Overstating tariff impact risks misunderstanding structural cost drivers. Tariffs are a **secondary cost factor**, with commodity price and supply chain dynamics dominant.

- Recent global geopolitical tensions and supply chain disruptions post-COVID present risks unrelated to tariff levels but affecting material availability and cost ([5], [15], [16], [23]).

## 7. Conclusion

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market for construction from 2018 to 2024 is a mature, well-defined sector anchored under ANZSIC code C2221NZ. Official data from Stats NZ combined with industry sources like SCNZ and MBIE provide robust insights into employment, revenue, and tonnage fabricated. Where direct data gaps exist, triangulation with capacity surveys, steel trade statistics, and project consumption volumes enables confident estimates of market size and dynamics.

The top fabricators are primarily SMEs, with notable larger firms including Brightwater Engineering, Steelworks New Zealand, and George Grant Engineering, each with proven capacity, certifications, and geographic diversity.

Major infrastructure projects—especially the City Rail Link, Te Kaha stadium, New Dunedin Hospital, and Takitimu North Link—drive significant structural steel demand, aggregating to volumes consistent with industry capacity.

Discrepancies between sources stem from scope definition, data timing, and differing survey methodologies but are reconcilable through comprehensive triangulation.

New Zealand’s low or zero tariffs on raw steel imports render border protection negligible, focusing fabricators’ margin risks on global steel prices, FX rates, and freight costs. This open market context incentivizes local capacity, technological advancement, and sustainability initiatives while requiring agile supply chain management.
