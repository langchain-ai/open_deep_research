# Comprehensive Comparative Analysis of Autism Diagnostic Criteria: DSM-5 (2013) vs ICD-11 (2019)

This report provides an in-depth comparative analysis of the autism diagnostic criteria in DSM-5 and ICD-11, emphasizing structural and operational differences, diagnostic accuracy, clinical and organizational guideline stances, coding and reimbursement frameworks, and the role of sensory processing criteria. The objective is to equip clinical, research, policy, and advocacy stakeholders with a detailed, evidence-based understanding of both systems, highlighting implications for diagnosis, healthcare delivery, and neurodiversity affirming approaches.

---

## 1. Comparative Analysis of Diagnostic Criteria

### 1.1 Structural and Operational Differences

| **Diagnostic Aspect**                             | **DSM-5 (2013)**                                                                                                       | **ICD-11 (2019)**                                                                                                              |
|--------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Core Social-Communication Domains**             | ASD diagnosis requires **persistent deficits across all three** specific subdomains:  
- Social-emotional reciprocity  
- Nonverbal communicative behaviors  
- Developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships  
These deficits must be present in the early developmental period but may manifest fully when social demands increase. Criteria are quantitatively defined and monothetic in this domain (all three subdomains must be met). | Diagnosis requires persistent deficits in **initiating and sustaining reciprocal social communication and interaction** characterized by impairments in verbal and nonverbal communication, social awareness, empathy, and relationship maintenance.  
These are described in **example-based, non-numeric terms** without strict symptom counts, allowing clinician judgment and flexibility based on intellectual and language variability. |
| **Restricted, Repetitive Behaviors (RRB) and Sensory Criteria** | Requires **at least 2 of 4** RRB criteria to be met (quantitative threshold):  
1. Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech  
2. Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines or ritualized patterns  
3. Highly restricted, fixated interests abnormal in intensity or focus  
4. Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects  
Sensory reactivity is explicitly required and weighted equally alongside other RRB symptoms. | RRB described broadly as persistent restrictive, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behavior and interests **atypical for age and sociocultural context**  
Includes **sensory sensitivities as non-mandatory examples** (e.g., hypersensitivity/hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli), embedded within this broader domain.  
No numeric thresholds or mandatory number of criteria to meet; diagnosis relies on qualitative clinician judgment. |
| **Sensory Reactivity**                             | Explicit, **mandatory** component of RRB criteria requiring at least two symptoms, with sensory hypo/hyper-reactivity as one criterion. | Mentioned **only as an example** of RRB symptoms, not a required or separately weighted diagnostic criterion. Sensory symptoms may be present but are not operationalized as mandatory. |
| **Severity Specifiers**                            | Three explicit severity levels coded separately for social communication and RRBs:  
- Level 1: Requiring support  
- Level 2: Requiring substantial support  
- Level 3: Requiring very substantial support  
These levels guide clinical planning and reflect support needs. | No formalized severity levels analogous to DSM-5 Levels 1-3.  
ICD-11 provides five **ASD subcategories combining intellectual and language impairments** with specific **diagnostic codes** (6A02.0 to 6A02.5), implicitly reflecting symptom profiles but without explicit severity grading.  
Severity is inferred via clinician’s assessment of functional impact, without distinct coded levels. |
| **Diagnostic Codes and Subcategories**              | DSM-5 itself is a diagnostic manual without billing codes; corresponding ICD-10-CM codes (e.g., F84.0) are used for reimbursement. | ASD consolidated under **6A02** code category with detailed subcodes:  
- 6A02.0: ASD without intellectual disability, mild/no language impairment  
- 6A02.1 to 6A02.5: varying intellectual disability and language profiles (e.g., with or without moderate/severe ID, language impairment)  
These code ranges represent distinct profiles aiding diagnosis and health data classification. |
| **Age and Onset Requirements**                      | Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period but may be fully apparent only when social demands exceed capacities. | Onset required **during the developmental period**, often early childhood; symptoms may be subtle initially and become fully manifest later. |
| **Functional Impairment Threshold**                  | Requires **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important functioning areas explicitly operationalized. | Requires significant impairment in personal, social, educational, or occupational domains. Operationalization is flexible with reliance on clinical judgment to accommodate cultural settings and masking. |

### 1.2 Summary of Key Differences

- **DSM-5 applies strict, numeric symptom thresholds**, particularly requiring at least two of four RRB symptoms (including sensory reactivity), making sensory processing a core, necessary diagnostic element.

- **ICD-11 adopts a broader, example-based, qualitative approach** without numeric RRB thresholds; sensory reactivity is cited only as an illustrative example within RRB, not a mandatory criterion.

- **Severity coding is explicit and standardized in DSM-5 but absent in ICD-11**, which instead uses diagnostic subcodes based on intellectual and language impairments to profile cases.

- **ICD-11 relies more on clinical judgment and accommodates diverse intellectual and language profiles globally**, reflecting a more flexible and heterogeneous conceptual model.

## 2. Evidence Synthesis: Diagnostic Accuracy and Implications

### 2.1 Central Limitation: Lack of Head-to-Head DSM-5 vs ICD-11 Diagnostic Accuracy Studies

- To date, **no direct diagnostic accuracy studies compare DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria in a head-to-head fashion**.

- This is a critical gap limiting robust empirical conclusions on differences in sensitivity, specificity, or clinical utility between the two diagnostic frameworks.

### 2.2 Empirical Findings on DSM-5 Diagnostic Performance

- Meta-analyses and large studies show DSM-5 diagnostic criteria tend to **reduce ASD diagnosis rates compared to DSM-IV(-TR)**, with an estimated **25–37% overall decrease** in diagnoses, particularly excluding individuals with previous PDD-NOS and Asperger’s presentations [Flinders University Meta-analysis (2022)].

- Sensitivity and specificity estimates for DSM-5 vary:  
  - Sensitivity ranges from **0.69 to 1.00** dependent on age, IQ, and sample characteristics.  
  - Specificity reported between **0.64 and 0.74**.  
  - Subgroups with higher IQ and females often show **lower sensitivity** (underdiagnosis risk).  
  - Sensory symptoms included explicitly enhance sensitivity in sensory-dominant phenotypes.

- Some research identifies risk of **underdiagnosing toddlers and individuals with mild sensory presentations** under DSM-5 due to strict RRB numeric thresholds [Mahajan et al., 2019].

### 2.3 ICD-11 Conceptual Considerations and Projections

- ICD-11’s **lack of quantitative symptom requirements** allows for over 300 symptom permutation combinations, which increases diagnostic heterogeneity and raises concerns about **false positives and reduced diagnostic specificity** [Schmitt et al., 2023].

- The **absence of explicit sensory criteria weighting** may reduce recognition of sensory-driven ASD presentations, leading to inconsistent diagnosis or under-identification of sensory-related impairment.

- Conversely, ICD-11's flexible criteria may **increase sensitivity in culturally and developmentally diverse populations**, but empirical data to confirm this remain lacking.

### 2.4 Distinction Between Test Accuracy and Clinical Utility

- Existing literature focuses predominantly on **test accuracy metrics (sensitivity, specificity)** for DSM-5 criteria, assessed mostly via retrospective or instrument-based studies.

- **Clinical utility, including functional outcomes, treatment planning, and access to services**, is less frequently evaluated but remains critical—DSM-5’s severity levels facilitate treatment orientation, whereas ICD-11's clinical flexibility may better capture heterogeneity but complicate uniformity in care pathways.

- Current GRADE quality ratings for DSM-5 test accuracy studies are **moderate**, limited by retrospective designs and sample biases; no GRADE assessments exist for ICD-11 diagnostic utility due to lacking empirical data.

## 3. Clinical Guidelines, Coding, and Reimbursement Policies

### 3.1 Guideline Adoption and Transition Timelines

| **Organization / Jurisdiction**         | **Current ASD Diagnostic Framework**                                        | **ICD-11 Adoption Status and Coding Transition**                                             |
|-----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **World Health Organization (WHO)**     | Endorses ICD-11 criteria since 2019; global member states encouraged to adopt. | ICD-11 effective January 1, 2022. Transition timelines vary by country (WHO member states). Some countries still using ICD-10 or dual coding. |
| **United States (US)**                   | DSM-5 ASD criteria remain clinical standard; ICD-10-CM codes currently used for billing/reimbursement. | ICD-11 official US adoption projected for 2025–2028 with 4–5 years transition. Dual ICD-10/ICD-11 claims processing anticipated during transition period. |
| **United Kingdom (UK)**                  | Clinical diagnosis primarily DSM-5 (NICE guidelines); ICD-10 codes still standard billing practice. | NHS and NHS Digital planning ICD-11 migration; use of dual coding systems ongoing. Coding shifts expected over several years post-2022 ICD-11 introduction. |
| **European Union (EU) General**          | Mixed adoption; many countries integrating ICD-11 coding alongside DSM-5 criteria use. | Gradual ICD-11 adoption underway; reimbursement systems based on national ICD coding; transition timelines variable. |

### 3.2 Organizational Positions on Diagnostic Criteria and Sensory Weighting

- **WHO:** Supports ICD-11 as global standard, emphasizing clinical flexibility and inclusive diagnostic profiles including sensory sensitivities (though not mandatory). Encourages cultural adaptation and clinical judgment. Neutral-to-supportive of ICD-11 broad approach [WHO Fact Sheet, 2022].

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA):** Favors DSM-5 for clinical and research purposes in the US, does **not formally endorse ICD-11 criteria** for ASD diagnosis. APA highlights DSM-5’s clarity and structured approach, especially the inclusion of sensory criteria [APA DSM-5 Manual, 2013].

- **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP):** Recommends DSM-5 criteria for early identification and diagnosis of ASD; recognizes sensory processing differences, aligned with DSM-5’s explicit inclusion. Advises caution with sensory-based therapies. Remains neutral or non-committal on ICD-11 adoption pending further validation [AAP Clinical Report, 2021].

- **National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, UK):** Uses DSM-5-based frameworks in guidelines; incorporates sensory issues as embedded clinical features rather than separate diagnostic domains. Monitoring ICD-11 developments without formal endorsement [NICE CG128, 2021].

### 3.3 Advocacy Organizations’ Positions

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN):** Critiques DSM-5’s medicalized framing but values explicit sensory inclusion; views ICD-11’s broad criteria skeptically due to lack of explicit sensory weighting and potential diagnostic dilution. Emphasizes neurodiversity affirmative diagnostics [ASAN Position Statements].

- **National Autistic Society (UK):** Supports DSM-5’s structured criteria and sensory inclusion but sees ICD-11 flexibility as potentially useful in diverse populations. Strong advocate for individualized sensory profiles [National Autistic Society Guidance].

- **Autism Speaks:** Predominantly supports DSM-5 criteria for diagnosis and research consistency; notes ICD-11 adoption by WHO but cautions about heterogeneity and research comparability. Supports sensory criteria importance [Autism Speaks ASD Criteria].

## 4. Sensory Processing Differences: Diagnostic Sensitivity, Specificity, and Risks

### 4.1 Role of Sensory Processing in DSM-5 and ICD-11

- **DSM-5** explicitly mandates the presence of sensory hypo- or hyper-reactivity as one of the RRB criteria; at least two of four RRB symptoms (including sensory) must be present for diagnosis.

- **ICD-11** includes sensory reactivity only as an illustrative example within a broad RRB domain and does **not require it** for diagnosis.

### 4.2 Diagnostic Sensitivity and Specificity of Sensory Features

- Sensory processing difficulties are reported in up to 90% of autistic individuals and can emerge early in development.

- Empirical evidence indicates sensory criteria have **moderate sensitivity and low-to-moderate specificity** for ASD diagnosis:  
  - Sensory features alone are insufficient to distinguish autism from other neurodevelopmental or psychiatric conditions.  
  - Social communication deficits remain the most sensitive and specific diagnostic pillar.

- Inclusion of sensory criteria enhances diagnostic sensitivity in sensory-dominant or “sensory-first” cases under DSM-5 but risks overdiagnosis in populations with sensory processing differences unrelated to ASD if not carefully assessed.

### 4.3 Risks of Misdiagnosis/Overdiagnosis/Underdiagnosis

- **Underdiagnosis Risk:**  
  - DSM-5’s numeric requirement for ≥2 RRB symptoms including sensory reactivity can exclude individuals with subtle or single sensory symptoms, notably higher-functioning females or those with mild presentations.

- **Overdiagnosis Risk:**  
  - ICD-11’s example-based sensory criteria within a broad RRB domain and flexible symptom threshold increase heterogeneity and risk of false positives, especially in individuals with non-autistic sensory processing disorders or psychiatric comorbidities bearing sensory features.

- Accurate differential diagnosis requires comprehensive assessment integrating sensory profiles, social communication, and functional impairment, sensitive to developmental and cultural contexts.

- Diagnostic ambiguity in sensory weighting between DSM-5 and ICD-11 demands clinicians prioritize **clinical judgment** supported by standardized tools to mitigate diagnostic errors.

---

## 5. Summary

- **DSM-5 provides structured, quantitatively defined autism diagnostic criteria with explicit inclusion of sensory processing differences within the RRB domain, and formal severity specifiers facilitating clarity and treatment guidance.**

- **ICD-11 offers a globally applicable, flexible, and example-based diagnostic framework emphasizing clinical judgment and accommodating diverse presentations, intellectual capacities, and language abilities. Sensory processing differences are acknowledged but not mandated diagnostically.**

- The **absence of direct head-to-head diagnostic accuracy data comparing both systems** limits empirical certainty; however, meta-analytic evidence suggests DSM-5 reduces diagnosis rates compared to DSM-IV and by extension may be more stringent than ICD-11.

- Clinical guidelines in major health systems predominantly endorse DSM-5-based criteria, with WHO advocating ICD-11 adoption globally but recognizing ongoing dual coding and transitional complexities.

- Sensory processing differences critically influence diagnostic sensitivity and specificity, with explicit DSM-5 criteria improving identification of sensory-driven autism but also posing underdiagnosis risks; ICD-11’s less defined sensory weight risks overdiagnosis or inconsistent identification.

- For clinical, research, policy, and advocacy stakeholders, understanding these nuanced differences and their practical implications is essential for balanced, equitable, and neurodiversity-affirming autism diagnosis and care.
