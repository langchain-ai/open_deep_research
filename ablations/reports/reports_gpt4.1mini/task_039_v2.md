# Comprehensive Report on the Definition and Requirements of an Independent Director under Current NASDAQ Listing Standards

---

## Introduction

The concept of an **independent director** under NASDAQ listing standards is foundational to ensuring robust corporate governance, protecting shareholder interests, and maintaining the integrity and objectivity of board oversight. NASDAQ Rule 5605 articulates independence through a combination of quantitative bright-line tests and qualitative board judgment. This report systematically details the definition, criteria, disqualifications, family definitions, committee-specific rules, and principal exemptions and special cases — all strictly aligned with current official NASDAQ Listing Rules and related authoritative guidance, avoiding any blending with standards from other stock exchanges.

---

## 1. Principle of Independence Determination

### 1.1 Definition under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

An **independent director** is one who is **not** an executive officer or employee of the company (or its subsidiaries), and who does **not** have any material relationship with the company that would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment. The NASDAQ listing standards establish that:

- Independence is **not only the absence of certain disqualifying relationships and transactions**, but also an **affirmative annual determination by the board** after considering all relevant facts and circumstances.

- This determination must be **documented in board resolutions** and disclosed in the company’s proxy statements or Form 10-K filings, reflecting transparency and accountability.[1][5][6]

### 1.2 Two-Part Test

The determination involves:

1. **Bright-Line Tests:** Objective, quantitative screens that exclude directors having specified types of relationships or transactions (employment, compensation, business dealings, family ties, auditor affiliation).

2. **Subjective Board Judgment:** The board evaluates facts and circumstances beyond these bright lines to confirm or disqualify a director’s independence.

### 1.3 Stock Ownership is Not a Disqualifier

- Sole ownership of company stock by the director or immediate family member **does not impair independence** under NASDAQ standards,[6] given the director’s interest aligns with shareholder value.

---

## 2. Eligibility Criteria and Disqualification Thresholds

### 2.1 Employment Restrictions

- **Employment Lookback:** A person is **not independent** if, during the **last three years**, they were employed by the company or any of its subsidiaries in an executive or other employee capacity.[5][8]

- Similarly, an immediate family member's employment as an executive officer within the past three years also disqualifies independence (see family section).

### 2.2 Compensation Thresholds

- A director or any immediate family member receiving **more than $120,000 in total compensation** from the company or its subsidiaries in any **consecutive 12-month period within the last three years** is disqualified.

#### Compensation Exclusions

Compensation that does **not** count toward the $120,000 limit includes:

- Fees received for service as a director (basic director fees).

- Pension or other forms of deferred compensation, provided they are **not contingent on continued service**.

- Payments under **broad-based plans** available generally to employees.

- Compensation received by a family member who is a non-executive employee, with certain exceptions.[5][8]

### 2.3 Material Business or Financial Relationships

- A director or any immediate family member who has or had a business relationship involving payments that exceed the **greater of $200,000 or 5% of the recipient’s annual gross revenues** in any of the last three fiscal years is considered to have a material relationship that disqualifies independence.

- This applies to both payments made **to** and **from** the company, including customers, suppliers, lenders, or other business dealings.

- The threshold is applied using the **greater-of formula** annually, for a rolling lookback of three fiscal years.[5][8]

### 2.4 Affiliation with the Auditor

- Any director or immediate family member who is **currently, or has been within the past three years**, a partner, principal, or employee of the company’s independent auditor is **not independent**.

- This restriction includes direct and indirect relationships impacting the auditor’s objectivity.[5][8]

### 2.5 Cross-Committee Service Relationships

- Directors serving on the compensation committee must not simultaneously serve on the compensation committee of another company where any of the company’s executive officers serve, to prevent conflicting interests.[5][8]

### 2.6 Immediate Family Member Employment and Compensation

- If an immediate family member is currently, or was within the last three years, an **executive officer** of the company or subsidiary, such relationship disqualifies the director.

- Similarly, if an immediate family member receives more than $120,000 in compensation in any consecutive 12-month period over the last three years (as defined above), independence is impaired.[5][8]

---

## 3. Comprehensive Definition of “Immediate Family Member”

NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2) provides an exhaustive definition to cover all key close relationships, critical for independence evaluation:

### 3.1 Included Immediate Family Members

- Spouse or domestic partner.

- Parents, including mother- and father-in-law.

- Children, including sons- and daughters-in-law.

- Siblings, including brothers- and sisters-in-law.

- Anyone (other than a domestic employee) who resides in the same household as the director.

### 3.2 Explicit Exclusions

- Stepchildren who **do not reside** in the director’s household are **excluded**.

- Domestic employees living in the director’s home are excluded.

- Other more distant relatives are not immediate family members under the rule.

### 3.3 Additional Considerations

- Boards may consider stepchild or other non-standard family relationships at their discretion when evaluating independence but these relationships are not automatically disqualifying.

This detailed family member definition is important because many disqualification rules mirror from direct director relationships onto immediate family members.[10][11][13]

## 4. Special and Distinct Independence Standards for Audit Committee Members

### 4.1 NASDAQ Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3 Integration

Audit committee members must meet **stricter independence criteria** due to the committee’s critical oversight role:

- Must be independent under stricter SEC and NASDAQ rules, including total severance of **any consulting, advisory, or compensatory fees** from the company other than director fees or fixed retirement benefits.

- Cannot be an “affiliate” of the company (i.e., no material relationship that could compromise objectivity beyond board service).

- All members must be **independent**, with **at least three members** on the committee.

- At least **one member must be financially sophisticated**, with at least one deemed an **audit committee financial expert**.[2][5][6][8]

### 4.2 Employment, Compensation, and Business Relationship Strictures

- Audit committee members cannot have recently been employed by the company or its auditor.

- Must not receive compensation or maintain business ties that could impair independence, evaluated under even more stringent tests than for general directors.

### 4.3 IPO and Phase-In Exceptions

- IPO companies can phase in full compliance for audit committee independence requirements:

  - Must have at least one independent audit committee member at listing.

  - Majority independent membership within 90 days.

  - Full compliance within one year post-IPO.[14][18][19]

---

## 5. Disqualifying Relationship Categories and Distinctions

### 5.1 Current vs Former Relationships with Auditor

- Directors or immediate family affiliated with the company’s independent auditor **currently or within the past 3 years** are disqualified.

- Past affiliations outside this period (over 3 years ago) do not automatically disqualify.

### 5.2 Director vs Family Member Restrictions

- The director’s independence is impaired not only by their direct relationships but also those of immediate family members defined above.

- Family relationships involving executive officer status or compensation may disqualify the director even if the director themself has no direct connection.

- Non-executive employees in immediate family generally do **not** disqualify independence, except where compensation is material and subject to board determination.[5][8]

### 5.3 Stock Ownership

- Owning company stock or options—without more—is **never a disqualifier** of independence.

- This aligns director incentives with shareholders and is explicitly permitted.

## 6. Principal Exemptions and Special Cases

### 6.1 Controlled Companies (Rule 5615(c)(1))

- Defined as companies where more than **50% of voting power** is held by an individual, group, or another corporation.

- Controlled companies are **exempt** from:

  - Majority independence requirements for the board.

  - Compensation and nominations committee independence.

- **Must retain:**

  - An independent audit committee.

  - Independent director executive sessions.[1][8][16][17]

- Must **publicly disclose** controlled status and governance practices.

### 6.2 Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) and Phase-In Periods

- IPOs benefit from **phased compliance timelines** for board and committee independence requirements (Rule 5615(b)):

  - Majority independent board required within 12 months post-IPO.

  - Certain committee independence requirements are phased in up to 1 year.

- Allows companies freshly listed on NASDAQ to ease into full compliance while maintaining investor protections.[14][18][19][20]

### 6.3 Foreign Private Issuers (FPIs)

- FPIs may follow **home country governance** in lieu of some NASDAQ requirements, except an **independent audit committee** in compliance with SEC Rule 10A-3 is still mandatory.

- Initial audits committees after IPO of FPIs may have limited phase-in (at least one independent member at listing, full compliance within 90 days).

- FPIs must **disclose** in SEC filings which NASDAQ requirements they do not follow and explain home country practices.[15][22][24][25]

### 6.4 Registered Investment Companies and Business Development Companies (BDCs)

- Such entities are subject to the Investment Company Act of 1940 and have **specialized, modified NASDAQ independence and committee rules** tailored to their unique regulatory environment.

- Ownership and compensation rules may be adjusted but still require independent oversight consistent with federal law.[15]

### 6.5 Legacy Contractual or Conditional Exemptions

- Certain companies may hold **contractual or historical waivers** from full independence compliance, subject to their specific terms.

- These are rare and require ongoing disclosure and board compliance to existing conditions.[1][8]

---

## 7. Additional Governance and Process Requirements

- The board must **annually evaluate each director’s independence**, documenting the determination in board resolutions.

- Boards conduct comprehensive questionnaires and maintain detailed inventories of director relationships.

- Independent directors must hold **at least two executive sessions annually** outside management presence.

- Corporate codes of conduct must be adopted and publicly available, with waivers disclosed.

- Notification regarding material non-compliance with NASDAQ rules must be promptly made by company executives.[1][2][8][17]

---

## Conclusion

NASDAQ's standards for defining and qualifying **independent directors** emphasize a rigorous, dual-pronged approach including bright-line quantitative screens and annual affirmative board determinations that consider all relevant facts. Independence hinges on the absence of certain material relationships such as recent employment, significant compensation, material business dealings, and familial executive connections, with precise lookback periods and quantitative thresholds applying uniformly.

Distinct, heightened independence standards protect the functioning of the audit committee, reflecting its essential oversight role. Exemptions and phase-in rules for controlled companies, IPOs, foreign issuers, investment and business development companies demonstrate NASDAQ’s flexibility while preserving core independence principles. All independent directors must meet NASDAQ’s comprehensive governance expectations, ensuring board objectivity and shareholder confidence.
