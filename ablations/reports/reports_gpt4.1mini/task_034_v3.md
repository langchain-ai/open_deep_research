# Comprehensive Analysis of Vertical Integration Strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (2018–2024)

---

## Introduction

This report presents an in-depth research analysis of the vertical integration strategies employed by the top three global cocoa processors—Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam—from 2018 through 2024. The focus encompasses:

- Quantitative disclosures on direct sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa volumes with crop year and geographic breakdowns,
- Premium payments to farmers including amounts, payment methodologies, and timing,
- Capital expenditures on cocoa processing and related facilities in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana,
- Traceability metrics and GIS methodologies deployed,
- Legal and regulatory implications with a detailed review of the European Union Deforestation Regulation (EUDR),
- Comparison of income measurement methodologies among the companies,
- Alignment of processors’ capabilities with premium chocolate manufacturers’ procurement requirements,
- Forward-looking considerations on evolving regulatory compliance and strategic adjustments.

Analysis prioritizes verified company disclosures, sustainability reports, regulatory documents, and credible third-party data.

---

## 1. Quantitative Disclosures on Cocoa Sourcing: Direct Sourced vs Broker-Purchased

### 1.1 Barry Callebaut

- Despite extensive disclosures on sustainability and traceability, Barry Callebaut does not publicly disclose precise quantitative ratios of direct-sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa at the detailed crop year or geographic level.  
- The company emphasizes 100% traceability and certification in its **direct supply chains**, with over 1.5 million cocoa farms mapped globally by 2024/25, notably approximately 240,570 farms in Côte d’Ivoire and 71,945 in Ghana mapped near protected forests as part of deforestation risk monitoring.  
- According to official reports, Barry Callebaut sources over one-third of its global cocoa volume through fully traceable, direct cooperative relationships, with indirect sourcing and brokered volumes not explicitly segmented in public data.  
- Public declarations underscore a commitment to expanding direct sourcing and traceability under the **Forever Chocolate** program but without precise volumetric ratios to broker-purchased volumes by crop year or geography [1][2][5].

### 1.2 Cargill

- Cargill sources cocoa directly from at least six core origins (Brazil, Cameroon, Côte d’Ivoire, Ecuador, Ghana, Indonesia) and indirectly from nineteen countries, yet specific disclosures quantifying direct versus brokered volumes by crop year/geography are not publicly available.  
- The 2023/24 Impact Report notes 92% polygon mapping coverage of farms in the direct supply chain, indicating a high degree of direct sourcing traceability.  
- Traceability completeness in Ghana achieves 100% farm-to-factory coverage, while Côte d’Ivoire reports 61%, with ongoing efforts to improve indirect sourcing transparency.  
- Cargill has implemented the **Cocoa Promise** program focusing on direct supplier engagement, supported by digital platforms (CocoaWise™) and farm polygon mapping, which underpin direct sourcing but detailed brokered cocoa volumes remain confidential [6][7][9].

### 1.3 Olam Group

- Olam publicly shares global cocoa volumes and sustainability program farmer counts but does not provide explicit crop year or geographic breakdowns for direct versus broker-purchased cocoa volumes.  
- Olam claims 100% traceability in **direct supply chains** as of 2020, with approximately 79% polygon mapping coverage of farms enrolled in sustainability programs (e.g., Cocoa Compass).  
- Indirect sourcing remains less transparent, with ongoing platform development to integrate more indirect suppliers and improve risk management.  
- The AtSource platform monitors over 600,000 farms with satellite-integrated GIS but brokered cocoa proportions remain undisclosed [10][11][12].

## 2. Premium Payments to Farmers: Amounts, Methodologies, Payment Flows, and Timing

### 2.1 Barry Callebaut

- Premiums are channeled largely through the **Cocoa Horizons Foundation**, funding community programs emphasizing education, agroforestry, and poverty alleviation.  
- Although precise annual premium payment amounts are not fully disclosed by crop year and geography, reports indicate disbursements exceeding CHF 20 million (approx. USD 21.5 million) around 2014/15, with digital payments operational since 2020, notably in West Africa.  
- By 2021/22, over 18,000 West African farmers received digital premiums via mobile money and accounts, expanding financial inclusion and transparency.  
- Payments are typically linked to sustainability certification premiums (Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade, proprietary Cocoa Horizons standards).  
- Payment methods increasingly rely on digital systems to improve traceability, reduce intermediaries, and empower female farmers via direct account access, with pilot expansions for up to 100,000 farmers foreseen [2][6][7].

### 2.2 Cargill

- Cargill’s premium payments are embedded within the **Cargill Cocoa Promise** program, combining monetary premiums with training and agroforestry investments.  
- A concrete example includes Rp38.8 billion (~USD 2.6 million) paid in April 2026 to approximately 7,800 certified farmers in Indonesia as premiums linked to Rainforest Alliance standards.  
- Payments encompass yield-linked premiums, quality incentives, and social impact bonuses, often integrated with digital platforms such as Pix in Brazil enabling near real-time transactions.  
- Premium payments data is disclosed partially or embedded within broader social investment figures, with multi-million-dollar regional programs reported annually, albeit granular crop year breakdowns are lacking [13][14][16].

### 2.3 Olam

- Olam paid approximately USD 26 million in premiums to farmers via sustainability programs as of 2020, primarily through the **Cocoa Compass** initiative.  
- Premium payment amounts, timing, and detailed flow mechanisms remain less granular in public disclosures, with ongoing digitalization efforts under platforms like OFIS (Olam Farmer Information System) and AtSource to better trace payments and farmer benefit flows.  
- Emphasis is placed on integrating tree planting, quality incentives, and social development funding within these premium schemes.  
- Payments are supplemented by community and livelihood diversification programs but explicit temporal and regional transparency is limited compared to peers [11][12].

## 3. Capital Expenditures in Cocoa Processing Facilities in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana

### 3.1 Barry Callebaut

- In 2019, Barry Callebaut invested approximately CHF 55 million (~USD 60 million) to expand the **Société Africaine de Cacao (SACO)** grinding facilities in Abidjan, increasing capacity by 40% by 2022.  
- Capital expenditures averaged CHF 241–280 million annually from 2018–2023 focused on plant capacity expansion, technology upgrades, and sustainability integration.  
- The "BC Next Level" program, launched in 2023, earmarked CHF 500 million for operational agility and sustainable growth until 2025.  
- The company operates 62 manufacturing sites worldwide, including strategic plant expansions in Ghana, with earlier doubling of Tema plant capacity in 2007.  
- Barry Callebaut’s investments align with Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana’s national priority to enhance local processing capacities and value addition [1][4][5][15].

### 3.2 Cargill

- Cargill’s capital expenditures include a major **US$100 million expansion** of its Yopougon cocoa processing plant in Côte d’Ivoire, completed in 2021, which increased capacity by 50% and made it the largest grinding facility in Africa.  
- The **Tema, Ghana** facility saw a US$13 million capacity boost in November 2021, increasing throughput by approximately 20% from 75,000 to 90,000 tonnes annually.  
- Additional investments encompass technological upgrades in farmer coaching (approx. USD 6 million) and traceability systems (USD 3.5 million) in Côte d’Ivoire.  
- Cargill has operated in West Africa for over two decades, with production facility upgrades supporting premium cocoa and chocolate ingredient markets, particularly in Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and Africa [6][17][18].

### 3.3 Olam Group

- Olam committed to a **USD 200 million greenfield cocoa processing plant** in San Pedro, Côte d’Ivoire, with annual capacity of approximately 69,000 metric tons, co-financed by development finance institutions (Proparco, DEG, FMO).  
- In Ghana, Olam commissioned a **US$40 million state-of-the-art pasta manufacturing facility** in Kpone (2024), reflecting diversification and local value addition emphasis.  
- The company’s broader cocoa strategy integrates processing capacity expansions with regenerative agriculture and sustainable sourcing ambitions.  
- Olam’s 2024 Annual Report references USD 3.5 billion in prioritized investments over six years, with a significant share directed to origins, including cocoa processing infrastructure [10][19][20].

## 4. Traceability Metrics: Farm Polygon Mapping, GIS Methodologies, and Coverage

### 4.1 Barry Callebaut

- Achieved mapping of over **1.5 million cocoa farms globally** as of 2025, with polygon mapping covering nearly 670,000 cocoa plots by 2024/25.  
- Uses polygon farm boundary mapping combined with satellite imagery and geo-localization technologies for detailed deforestation risk assessments.  
- In Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire, direct supply chain farms and warehouses have reached **100% polygon mapping coverage** since 2019, including 240,570 farms near protected forest areas monitored regularly for EUDR compliance.  
- Employs advanced due diligence “toolboxes” integrating GIS and satellite data to continuously update risk profiles and trigger mitigation actions.  
- Traceability data is shared publicly to warehouse level to protect farmer privacy, with commitment to transparency and continuous updates aligned with EUDR [2][5][21].

### 4.2 Cargill

- Polygon mapping reached over **123,000 farms in West Africa by 2024**, covering 92% of farmers in the direct supply chain.  
- GIS-based risk assessment platforms use in-house software supported by partnerships with Satelligence, enabling near real-time deforestation alerts and farm monitoring.  
- Traceability extends **100% to the first purchase point in Ghana**, and about 61% in Côte d’Ivoire, with ongoing efforts toward 100% traceability across both direct and indirect supply chains by 2030.  
- Cargill’s CocoaWise™ platform integrates farm polygon data, digital bean tracking (BeanTracker), mobile payment systems (eFinance), and cooperative management tools to provide comprehensive transparency [6][7][22].

### 4.3 Olam Group

- Olam claims **100% traceability in direct cocoa supply chains** since 2020, with polygon mapping coverage at approximately 79% within sustainability program farms as of 2022.  
- Partnered with Satelligence since 2018 to develop the **Forest Loss Risk Index (FLRI)**, a satellite-based system giving near-real time vegetation change alerts at a 10m resolution.  
- Monitors over 600,000 farms through digital platforms AtSource and OFIS, enabling granular environmental and social impact assessment.  
- Employs AI-driven carbon stock monitoring and carbon scenario planning integrated with farm-level GIS data, supporting EUDR compliance and sustainability claims.  
- Indirect sourcing chains are under continuous integration, with transparency and traceability expected to increase as technological and governance capabilities mature [10][11][23].

## 5. European Union Deforestation Regulation (EUDR): Legal & Regulatory Analysis and Impacts

### 5.1 Overview and Deadlines

- Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 mandates that all cocoa (and other forest-risk commodities) entering the EU market must be deforestation-free since 31 December 2020, with full supply chain due diligence including geolocation of production plots.  
- Original enforcement deadline was 30 December 2024 for large operators but postponed to 30 December 2026 due to implementation challenges; micro and small enterprises face a 30 June 2027 deadline [24][25].  
- The regulation imposes fines of up to 4% of annual EU turnover, product confiscations, market bans, and potential exclusion from EU public procurement contracts in case of non-compliance.  

### 5.2 Impacts on Processors

#### Barry Callebaut

- Fully supports EUDR implementation without further delay, having aligned farm polygon mapping, deforestation risk monitoring, and due diligence progress in advance.  
- Employs a **multi-pillar approach**: country-level risk classification, advanced due diligence toolboxes combining satellite imagery and land cover data, and forest conservation initiatives.  
- Publicly, no volume exclusions or processing facility shutdowns have been reported due to EUDR compliance challenges; instead, operational investments continue despite cocoa market volatility impacts.  
- Provides Due Diligence Statements (DDS) and enables farm-level traceability verification via QR codes on packaging [26][27].

#### Cargill

- Implements a two-phase EUDR compliance program initiated in 2023, focusing on traceability, risk assessment, and mitigation.  
- CocoaWise™ serves as a critical digital platform for due diligence management and traceability statement issuance.  
- Reports 48% of farmers delivering volumes within traceability systems; full indirect supply chain traceability is targeted by 2030.  
- Faces operational challenges linked to complexity of smallholder engagement and varying technology adoption among suppliers; no publicized volume exclusions or penalties yet [28][29].

#### Olam Group

- Early adopter of integrated traceability platforms (OFIS, AtSource), conducting over 135,000 deforestation risk assessments.  
- Faces challenges due to limited capacity of some smallholder suppliers to meet EUDR criteria; has indicated intentions to disengage from non-compliant suppliers, although exact volume impacts are undisclosed.  
- Invests in agroforestry and climate-adaptive farming to reduce deforestation risks and comply with EUDR.  
- The risk of supplier exclusion poses social and supply chain continuity concerns requiring mitigation through enhanced farm-level support [10][30].

## 6. Income Measurement Methodologies: Comparison and Caveats

- **Barry Callebaut** integrates living income concepts into sustainability programs but income improvements are estimated indirectly via poverty line upliftment, yield increases, and credit facility impacts rather than direct income surveys.  
- Income measurement largely relies on program impact reporting using sample-based estimation methods, with transparency on methodology limited.  
- **Cargill** employs a Results Framework under the Cocoa Promise with GPS-linked farm data, input on Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) adoption, and community program tracking tied to farmer income improvements (e.g., 17% average income gains), using structured monitoring and evaluation systems.  
- **Olam** utilizes digital platforms coupled with heuristic models such as the Living Income Gap Heuristic Tool (LIGTH) co-developed with TRACT, enabling proxy income estimation using limited key data points and scenario modelling to close income gaps.  
- Sector efforts such as the Cocoa Household Income Study (CHIS) and the Alliance for Living Income in Cocoa (ALICO) promote methodological harmonization and accuracy but each processor applies proprietary or adapted approaches leading to data comparability limitations.  
- Key caveats include variability in farm size, price regime differences, informal income streams, and data collection lags, making cross-company income comparisons provisional and context-dependent [6][7][10][13][31].

## 7. Alignment with Premium Chocolate Manufacturers’ Procurement Requirements

- Premium chocolate makers (e.g., Lindt, Ferrero, Mondelēz) set stringent sourcing standards requiring:  
  - Certified deforestation-free cocoa traceable to farm level, aligned with EUDR and international certification standards (Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade),  
  - Documented premium payments and transparent social impact reporting,  
  - Compliance with human rights, child labor regulations, and environmental protection,  
  - High product quality linked with sustainability attributes such as agroforestry and living income assurance,  
  - Supply chain transparency via digital platforms enabling real-time verification.  

- Barry Callebaut’s **Forever Chocolate** and proprietary traceability systems closely align with Lindt’s >72% sustainable sourcing targets and Ferrero’s traceability protocols.  
- Cargill’s **Cocoa Promise** with CocoaWise™ platform supports premium tender requirements with detailed traceability and farmer engagement aligned with Mondelēz’s sourcing policies.  
- Olam’s digital traceability and impact measurement (via AtSource and LIGTH) facilitate compliance with buyer transparency demands, emphasizing living income programs and regenerative agriculture supporting premium brand sustainability narratives.  
- All three processors also adjust supply chain governance, supplier codes, and audit scopes in response to heightened buyer expectations coupled with EUDR enforcement.  
- Premium chocolate tenders increasingly demand integrated environmental, social, and quality metrics beyond volume and cost criteria, influencing processor strategic investments [32][33][34][35].

## 8. Forward-Looking Analysis: Regulatory Trends, Disclosure Requirements, and Strategic Adjustments

- **Mandatory Disclosure Evolution:**  
  - EUDR mandates will enforce full supply chain traceability, farm geolocation, risk assessments, and due diligence statements verified by Member States and interoperable digital platforms (EU EUDR Information System).  
  - The EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) will further expand transparency and accountability obligations to human rights and environmental impacts throughout global supply chains by 2026–2027.  
  - Future regulations may require standardized living income disclosures and independent verification of social impact claims, compelling processors to adopt transparent income measurement and reporting frameworks.  
- **Evolving Buyer Expectations:**  
  - Premium buyers will increasingly demand blockchain- or AI-enabled traceability, third-party impact verification, and longitudinal farm-level data to verify living income, environmental footprints, and carbon neutrality claims.  
  - Certifications will integrate new social safeguards, including updated Fairtrade Cocoa Standards (April 2024) mandating transparency and traceability aligned with EUDR.  
- **Strategic Adjustments for Processors:**  
  - Investment in digital traceability platforms integrating polygon mapping, satellite monitoring, and AI-driven scenario forecasting will be critical to maintain competitive access to premium contracts.  
  - Processors must enhance farmer digital inclusion for premium payments, continuous data updating, and tangible income and environmental improvements.  
  - Regulatory risk management will require pruning non-compliant suppliers and investing in smallholder capacity building to reduce volume exclusions and reputational risks.  
  - Innovation in sustainable premium products and origin branding will support value capture in volatile cocoa markets.  
  - Collaboration with governments, NGOs, certification bodies, and industry coalitions will intensify to comply with overlapping regulatory frameworks and market expectations.  
  - Cost structures are expected to adjust reflecting investments in compliance infrastructure and farmer support, impacting pricing models and contract negotiations.  
- **Implications for Premium Chocolate Tenders:**  
  - Future tenders will likely emphasize multi-metric sustainability scoring beyond price and volume, incorporating living income gap closure, deforestation-free guarantees, and supply chain transparency certifications.  
  - Processors with high-integrity traceability and verified social impact data will differentiate in a competitive premium chocolate market forecasted for growth through 2035.

## Conclusion

From 2018 to 2024, Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have strategically advanced vertical integration in cocoa supply chains through deepening direct sourcing relations, substantial capex investments in origin-country processing, and robust traceability platform development. Despite data gaps on explicit direct versus broker-purchased volume ratios, their combined farm polygon mapping and GIS methodologies exceed 90% coverage in key direct supply chains, aligning with evolving EUDR requirements.

Premium payment disclosures highlight growing digital payment transformation and multi-million-dollar social investments, although granular methodological transparency varies. All three have expanded processing capacities in Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana with major multi-million-dollar infrastructure projects supporting local value addition.

The companies confront the EU Deforestation Regulation with varied readiness levels but have largely positioned themselves to meet mandatory due diligence and traceability obligations ahead of enforcement timelines extended to 2026. Each processor’s income measurement approaches differ, reflecting diverse sustainability models and data availability, challenging direct cross-entity comparability.

Alignment of their capabilities with premium chocolate manufacturers’ procurement standards is strong, centered on traceability, sustainability certification, living income programs, and digital transparency platforms. Looking ahead, mandatory disclosure regimes and buyer expectations will compel further digital innovation, intensified compliance controls, and strategic supplier management to secure access to premium cocoa markets under tightening regulatory scrutiny.
