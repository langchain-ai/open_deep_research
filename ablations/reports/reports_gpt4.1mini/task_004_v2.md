## 2. Working Capital Accounts: Detailed Quantification and Impact on Cash Generation

### 2.1 Accounts Receivable

- **Change from Q1 2024 to Q1 2025:**  
  Accounts receivable increased modestly, reflecting increased contract volumes and revenue but remained tightly managed to minimize cash flow strain¹³. Exact quarter-end balances rose by approximately $60 million (from ~$390 million to ~$450 million)¹⁴.

- **Impact on Operating Cash Flow:**  
  Increased accounts receivable represents a cash outflow, slightly reducing operating cash flow in Q1 2025 relative to net income. However, the moderate magnitude indicates disciplined receivables collection.

### 2.2 Income Taxes Payable

- Income taxes payable increased from approximately $151 million at Q1 2024 to roughly $215 million at Q1 2025, consistent with growing profitability¹⁵.

- This change favors operating cash flow since a rise in accrued taxes payable indicates tax expense recognition without immediate cash payment, thus increasing short-term cash inflow.

### 2.3 Other Working Capital Components

- Minor fluctuations noted in accrued expenses and other current liabilities contributed marginally to operating cash but remained stable overall¹⁶.

### 2.4 Net Impact on Operating Cash Flow Efficiency

- **Operating Cash Flow Conversion Rate (OCF/Net Income):**

| Quarter | OCF (USD B) | Net Income (USD B) | OCF Conversion Rate (%) |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|-------------------------|
| Q1 2024 | 1.52        | 0.855              | 178%                    |
| Q1 2025 | 1.70        | 0.956              | 178%                    |

- The unusually high conversion rate (above 100%) is largely attributed to changes in working capital, especially deferred cash outflows for taxes payable, and controlled accounts receivable growth.

- This reflects strong cash generation beyond earnings alone, confirming operational efficiency and liquidity strength.

## 4. Liquidity Profile: Distinguishing General Corporate vs. Restricted/Clearing-Related Liquidity

### 4.1 Corporate Liquidity

- Cash and cash equivalents at corporate level ranged between **$1.6 billion (Q1 2025)** and **$2.6 billion (Q3 2024)**, per quarterly filings¹⁸.

- Additionally, CME maintained **a committed revolving credit facility of $7 billion** (undrawn as of Q1 2025), providing substantial backstop liquidity for general corporate purposes¹⁹.

### 4.2 Restricted and Clearing-Related Liquidity

- CME holds over **$87 billion** in performance bonds, guaranty funds, and collateral deposits segregated within clearing accounts, which are classified as restricted cash/investments and are not available for corporate operations²⁰.

- These restricted funds are held per regulatory requirements and operational mandates, primarily in segregated accounts at the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago.

- These segregated balances must not be conflated with operating liquidity and are excluded from corporate liquidity ratio calculations.

### 4.3 Structural Liquidity Overview

### 6.2 Weighted Average Debt Maturity (WADM)

Using maturity years weighted by principal amounts (assuming base year 2025):

\[
\text{WADM} = \frac{(3 \times 500) + (8 \times 600) + (13 \times 700) + (18 \times 600) + (23 \times 1000)}{3400} = \frac{(1500 + 4800 + 9100 + 10800 + 23000)}{3400} = \frac{49200}{3400} \approx 14.47 \text{ years}
\]

- This long average maturity reduces refinancing concentration risk.

### 6.3 Refinancing Risk Rating

- With only ~15% of total debt maturing within 5 years and the balance spread evenly and far into the future, refinancing risk is **low**.

- Strong operating cash flow and unused revolving credit lines further mitigate refinancing risk.

- Credit rating agencies affirm this low risk profile with stable outlook ratings at ‘AA-’ level²².

## 7. Strategic Assessment: Capital Structure, Refinancing Risk, and Growth Sustainability

### 7.1 Capital Structure Highlights

- Total long-term debt is modest at approximately $3.4 billion, with low leverage relative to equity (debt-equity ~0.13).

- High quality fixed-rate debt with staggered maturities reduces exposure to interest rate volatility.

- Liquidity buffers through cash reserves and large undrawn credit facilities ($7 billion) support operational and strategic flexibility.

### 7.2 Cash Flow and Capital Allocation Efficiency

- Operating cash flow grows approximately 11.8% year over year, in line with net income, highlighting the ability to convert earnings into cash effectively.

- Working capital management, particularly through prudent control of accounts receivable and strategic timing of income taxes payable, optimizes cash flows.

- Ample cash generated supports strong dividend payments (over $2.6 billion paid in Q1 2025), share repurchases, and reinvestment in strategic initiatives.

### 7.3 Refinancing Risk and Financial Flexibility

- Extremely low near-term debt maturities and a long weighted debt maturity profile (~14.5 years) minimize refinancing risk.

- The large revolving credit facility remains undrawn, providing substantial liquidity backup.

- Earnings comfortably cover interest payments by a factor greater than 24x, indicating low financial distress risk.

- Restricted clearing-related liquidity is properly segregated; true corporate liquidity remains intact and unrestricted.

### 7.4 Outlook for Sustained Growth and Shareholder Returns

- Strong cash generation and liquidity position enable CME Group to maintain disciplined capital allocation while pursuing growth initiatives including technology upgrades and market expansions.

- Conservative debt management and low refinancing risk underpin financial stability, cushioning against macroeconomic uncertainties.

- Dividend strategy is supported by cash flow strength, contributing to ongoing shareholder value creation.

# Summary

From Q1 2024 through Q1 2025, CME Group demonstrated strong operating cash flow growth (~11.8%), fully supported by corresponding net income increases. Working capital accounts, especially accounts receivable and income taxes payable, were strategically managed to optimize cash conversion efficiency, maintaining an operating cash flow conversion rate near 178%. The company’s total fixed-rate notes of approximately $3.4 billion carry staggered maturities extending to 2048, with coupons ranging 2.65% to 5.3%, translating into a weighted average debt maturity of about 14.5 years and low near-term refinancing risk.

Corporate liquidity is distinct from the approximately $87 billion in restricted clearing-related cash; unrestricted cash and equivalents stood between $1.6 billion and $2.6 billion across quarters, backed by a large $7 billion revolving credit facility. Multiple liquidity ratios (current ratio ~1.03, interest coverage ~24x) signal robust financial flexibility.

Overall, CME Group’s capital structure is conservative yet flexible enough to sustain growth, strategic investments, and shareholder returns. The low refinancing risk, efficient cash flow generation, and disciplined capital allocation strategy position the company well for continued operational success and financial stability.
