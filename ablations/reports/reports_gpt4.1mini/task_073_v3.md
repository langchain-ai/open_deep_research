# Comparative Analysis of Autism Spectrum Disorder Diagnostic Criteria: DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019) with Focus on Sensory Processing Differences

---

## 1. Side-by-Side Detailed Comparison of DSM-5 and ICD-11 Diagnostic Criteria

| **Diagnostic Domain**                | **DSM-5 (2013)**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | **ICD-11 (2019)**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               |
|------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Core Social-Communication Criteria** | - Requires **all three persistent deficits**:  
  1. Social-emotional reciprocity (e.g., failure of normal back-and-forth conversation)  
  2. Nonverbal communicative behaviors (e.g., abnormalities in eye contact, body language)  
  3. Developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships (e.g., difficulties adjusting behavior to social context)  
- Deficits must be evident from early developmental period but may not fully manifest until increased social demands [1][4][5]. | - Requires persistent difficulties in **initiating and sustaining reciprocal social communication and interaction** beyond expectations for age and intellectual development.  
- Includes impairments in verbal and nonverbal communication, social awareness, empathy, and relationships.  
- Operationalized with **non-numeric, example-based descriptors**, allowing clinician judgment and cultural adaptation [6][7][10][26]. |
| **Sensory Processing Mentions and Weighting** | - Explicitly included as **one of four restricted/repetitive behavior (RRB) symptoms**:  
  - Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment.  
- At least **two of four RRB symptoms** (including sensory) must be met for diagnosis.  
- Sensory processing differences are weighted **equally among RRB criteria** and mandatory for diagnosis [1][4][13][15][25]. | - Included implicitly as part of RRB domain under **“atypical sensory responses”** (e.g., hyper/hyporeactivity).  
- Sensory processing is an **illustrative example**, not a mandatory or separately weighted criterion.  
- No numeric threshold related to sensory symptoms required for diagnosis.  
- Criteria favor **qualitative clinical judgment** over quantitative symptom counting [6][7][10][37]. |
| **Severity Specifiers**             | - Defined **3 discrete severity levels** assessing required support, separately for social communication and RRBs:  
  - Level 1: Requiring support  
  - Level 2: Requiring substantial support  
  - Level 3: Requiring very substantial support  
- Severity guides treatment and resource allocation [1][4][5][22]. | - No explicit severity levels analogous to DSM-5.  
- Severity inferred from **intellectual and language impairment subtypes** within five coded categories:  
  - ASD without intellectual disability and mild/no language impairment  
  - ASD with intellectual disability (mild/moderate/severe) and varying language profiles  
- Does not specify severity based on behavioral support needs [6][26][35][37]. |
| **Age/Onset Requirements**          | - Symptoms must be **present in early developmental period**, though feature expression may be delayed until social demands exceed capacities [1][4][5][28].                                                                                                          | - Onset required during **developmental period, typically early childhood**, but symptoms may only become fully apparent later (e.g., adolescence) when social demands increase [6][7][10][26][27].                                                                                                                                            |
| **Functional Impairment Thresholds** | - Requires **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important functioning areas, operationally defined [1][4][5].                                                                                                                                                                        | - Requires **significant impairment** in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important domains; threshold determined flexibly by clinical judgment [6][7][10][26][37].                                                                                                                                               |

---

### Summary of Key Diagnostic Differences Related to Sensory Processing

- **DSM-5 makes sensory processing differences an explicit, core, and mandatory diagnostic component** by requiring at least two RRB symptoms, one of which can be sensory hyper/hyporeactivity or unusual sensory interests. This ensures sensory processing is consistently assessed and weighted equivalently for diagnosis.

- **ICD-11 lists sensory processing differences only as an illustrative example within the broader RRB domain** without requiring specific sensory symptoms. This qualitative, flexible approach allows clinician discretion but may reduce consistency in weighting sensory symptoms diagnostically.

- DSM-5’s **structured severity levels** aid clinical planning, whereas ICD-11 uses intellectual and language subtyping without formal support-based severity levels.

- Age and impairment criteria are similar but DSM-5 operationalizes impairment and onset more explicitly.

### 2.2 Synthesis Highlights Regarding Sensory Processing and Diagnostic Accuracy

- **DSM-5 Criteria Sensitivity and Specificity**: Sensory symptoms inclusion enhances sensitivity in sensory-predominant autism but rigorous RRB thresholds lead to underdiagnosis of mild or atypical cases, particularly females and high-IQ individuals [5][18][1].

- **ICD-11 Diagnostic Flexibility**: Absence of strict sensory symptom thresholds and quantitative cutoffs increases heterogeneity and risk of false positives, but may better accommodate cultural, developmental, and intellectual diversity. Empirical diagnostic performance data for ICD-11 is lacking, reflecting a research gap [4][8].

- **Clinical Utility**: DSM-5 severity levels facilitate treatment planning and uniformity, whereas ICD-11’s subtype coding emphasizes functional language and intellectual abilities, focusing less on behavioral support levels [6][26].

- **Recommendation**: Clinicians should integrate DSM-5 structured criteria and ICD-11’s flexibility, combined with clinical judgment, especially when assessing sensory processing differences to improve diagnostic accuracy and personalize care.

## 3. Clinical Guideline Positions from Authoritative Bodies

### 3.1 World Health Organization (WHO)

- ICD-11 officially adopted by World Health Assembly in May 2019; global effective date January 1, 2022 [2][28].

- WHO endorses **ICD-11 as global standard** for autism diagnosis for public health reporting and clinical use, promoting gradual national implementation processes [2][7][28].

- Clinical Descriptions and Diagnostic Requirements (March 2024) guide ICD-11 ASD diagnosis globally, emphasizing **clinical judgment, inclusion of sensory processing differences as core but non-mandatory,** and allowance for developmental variability [1][7].

- Recognizes **masking and compensation behaviors**, urging clinician awareness especially for late- or masked-presenting individuals [27][34].

- National adoption timelines vary greatly; countries like India, China still using ICD-10, with multi-year transitions [8].

---

### 3.2 American Psychiatric Association (APA)

- DSM-5 (2013) is definitive diagnostic manual in U.S. clinical and research practice [6].

- APA favors DSM-5’s **structured criteria, explicit sensory symptom inclusion, and severity specifiers**. Does not formally endorse ICD-11 diagnostic criteria for ASD [6][9].

- Recognizes **masking and compensation**, especially in females, as complicating factors but maintains reliance on observable symptoms for diagnostic coding [6][34].

- DSM-5 remains the standard for clinical diagnosis, epidemiological studies, and treatment planning in the U.S. [6][36].

---

### 3.3 American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

- Recommends DSM-5 criteria for **early ASD diagnosis and screening at 18 and 24 months** [11][12].

- Emphasizes treatment initiation upon identification of developmental delays, not delayed until formal diagnosis [11][14].

- Addresses sensory processing as a **core ASD feature** but cautions against diagnosing standalone Sensory Processing Disorder; sensory-based therapies are recommended cautiously, supported by limited evidence [30][33].

- Not formally adopting ICD-11 criteria pending further validation; acknowledges **masking complicates diagnosis** in pediatric populations, especially girls [34][35].

---

### 3.4 National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, UK)

- NICE clinical guidelines (CG128, CG142, CG170) integrate DSM-5 and recognize ICD-11 criteria as roughly equivalent; no official preference but DSM-5 historically dominant [16][17][18].

- Sensory processing differences acknowledged as integral to ASD across clinical assessments and management [28][29].

- Aware of masking/camouflaging effects causing underdiagnosis, especially in females [18].

- ICD-11 adoption underway in NHS and EU with transitional dual coding systems; ICD-10 codes still widely used [22].

## 4. Autism Advocacy Organizations’ Positions on DSM-5 vs ICD-11 and Sensory Processing

### 4.1 Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

- Critiques DSM-5’s **medicalized and pathologizing framing**, yet values DSM-5’s explicit sensory inclusion as validation for sensory experiences [10].

- Views ICD-11’s **broad and less explicit sensory criteria** skeptically, concerned about dilution of recognition of sensory processing validity and potential impact on supports [10].

- Advocates strongly for **neurodiversity-affirming diagnostics** that respect individual variability and sensory processing profiles [10].

---

### 4.2 National Autistic Society (UK)

- Supports DSM-5’s structured criteria including sensory symptom inclusion but acknowledges ICD-11’s **flexibility may benefit diverse populations** [11].

- Calls for recognition of **individual sensory profiles** and tailored approaches within both manuals [11].

- Emphasizes that diagnostic manuals should center neurodiversity concepts and avoid pathologizing sensory differences [11].

---

### 4.3 Autism Speaks

- Supports DSM-5 as primary diagnostic criteria for clinical and research consistency [9].

- Notes WHO’s global ICD-11 adoption but cautions that ICD-11’s heterogeneity may challenge research comparability and clinical consistency [9].

- Strongly endorses sensory processing differences as critical features warranting careful diagnostic attention [9].

## 5. Diagnostic Safety Risks Related to Differences in Sensory Processing Weighting

- **Misdiagnosis / Underdiagnosis:**

  - DSM-5’s strict requirement of at least two RRB symptoms including sensory hyper/hyporeactivity may lead to **underdiagnosis in individuals with subtle or single sensory symptoms**, especially females, high-IQ, or mild presentations [5][18].

  - Masking or compensation behaviors may further obscure sensory difficulties, delaying diagnosis [27][34][35].

- **Overdiagnosis:**

  - ICD-11’s flexible criteria and absence of numeric thresholds may increase **false positives**, especially in patients with sensory processing disorders unrelated to ASD or with psychiatric comorbidities (e.g., anxiety with sensory sensitivity) [4].

- **Inappropriate Interventions:**

  - Differential weighting of sensory symptoms across manuals influences clinical decisions and treatment eligibility.

  - Over- or underemphasis on sensory features can lead to overuse or neglect of sensory-targeted interventions, with clinical consequences.

- **Safety Implications:**

  - Accurate differentiation between sensory-related ASD presentations and other neurodevelopmental/psychiatric conditions is vital to avoid inappropriate labeling.

  - Standardized tools and comprehensive multi-disciplinary evaluations are recommended to supplement manual criteria [2][6].

## 6. Reimbursement and Coding Summary: US and EU Contexts

### 6.1 Current Coding Systems

- **United States:**

  - DSM-5 criteria widely used clinically.

  - ICD-10-CM coding system currently required for billing and reimbursement.

  - Autism diagnoses coded via ICD-10-CM codes (e.g., F84.0 for autism).

  - ICD-11 adoption is planned; official adoption projected approximately between 2025–2028 pending system upgrades, training, and regulatory approval [1][4][8].

- **European Union / UK:**

  - Mixed use of DSM-5 clinically and ICD-10 legally for reimbursement.

  - UK’s NHS currently uses ICD-10 for coding; NICE guidelines align with DSM-5 but recognize ICD-11 equivalence [16][22].

  - ICD-11 gradually adopted post-2022 with dual coding systems during transition.

---

### 6.2 Cross-System Mapping and Transition

- Mapping between ICD-10-CM and ICD-11 codes for autism is available, but transition requires system adaptations including electronic health records and training [28][29].

- ICD-11 codes for ASD (6A02 and subcodes) provide more granular subtyping based on intellectual and language impairment, potentially affecting reimbursement if adopted in billing [26][35].

- Current payer policies in US and EU often require specific ICD codes for authorization of autism-related services, and changes in coding can impact coverage eligibility and service approval [15][16].

- The adoption of ICD-11 may enhance **data granularity and international comparability** but poses challenges requiring multi-year transition phases and consistent crosswalks.

## Summary

This comprehensive analysis reveals that:

- **DSM-5 (2013) provides structured, quantitatively defined ASD diagnostic criteria with explicit inclusion and equal weighting of sensory processing differences within restricted/repetitive behavior symptoms, formal severity specifiers, and defined functional impairment thresholds.**

- **ICD-11 (2019) offers a more flexible, qualitative, and clinically judgment-based framework, acknowledging but not mandating sensory processing differences, emphasizing developmental profiles, intellectual and language subtypes rather than behavioral severity levels.**

- **Empirical evidence on diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity favors DSM-5 for specificity and clarity but acknowledges underdiagnosis risks in mild and female presentations, particularly related to rigid RRB thresholds involving sensory symptoms.**

- **ICD-11's flexible criteria raise concerns about diagnostic heterogeneity and potential overdiagnosis, with limited direct empirical accuracy data available.**

- **Clinical guidelines from WHO, APA, AAP, and NICE each reflect national and organizational contexts, generally endorsing DSM-5 for clinical use in US and UK but supporting ICD-11 for global coding and reporting, with nuanced positions on sensory processing and masking.**

- **Autism advocacy organizations emphasize neurodiversity affirmation with varied preferences; ASAN critiques ICD-11’s implicit sensory criteria, whereas Autism Speaks supports DSM-5 consistency, and National Autistic Society recognizes benefits of both.**

- **Diagnostic safety risks from differences chiefly concern underdiagnosis or overdiagnosis related to sensory processing symptom criteria, further complicated by masking behaviors and compensation strategies.**

- **Reimbursement and coding processes are in transition, with ICD-10-CM dominant currently but ICD-11 adoption underway globally, necessitating careful mapping and training to maintain clinical and billing continuity.**

Clinicians, policymakers, researchers, and advocates should consider these nuanced distinctions and operational complexities to promote accurate, equitable, and neurodiversity-affirming autism diagnoses and supports.
