# Comprehensive Comparative Analysis of Autism Diagnosis Criteria: DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR) and ICD-11 (2019/2022)

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnostic criteria as defined in the DSM-5 (2013, with Text Revision in 2022) and the ICD-11 (2019, effective 2022), with a specific focus on how each manual defines and weights sensory processing differences. The analysis covers six distinct domains: a side-by-side criteria comparison, peer-reviewed evidence synthesis, clinical guideline positions, advocacy organization perspectives, diagnostic safety risks, and reimbursement/coding implications. The ICD-11, which came into effect on January 1, 2022, represents the first major revision to the WHO's classification system in 28 years and has been adopted by over 60 countries, while the DSM-5-TR (2022) remains the standard diagnostic framework in the United States. Both systems have converged significantly by abandoning the previous subcategories (Autistic Disorder, Asperger Syndrome, PDD-NOS) in favor of a unified autism spectrum diagnosis, yet important differences remain in their structure, specificity, and potential clinical impact. This report incorporates new evidence and developments since mid-2025, with an effective date of May 30, 2026.

## Section A: Side-by-Side Criteria Table

### A.1 Core Social-Communication Criteria

| **Dimension** | **DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR)** | **ICD-11 (2019/2022)** |
|---------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Criterion Structure** | Three sub-criteria (A1, A2, A3), **all three must be present** | Broad description with no fixed number of features required |
| **A1: Social-Emotional Reciprocity** | Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity, ranging from abnormal social approach and failure of normal back-and-forth conversation; to reduced sharing of interests, emotions, or affect; to failure to initiate or respond to social interactions | Persistent deficits in the ability to initiate and sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication |
| **A2: Nonverbal Communication** | Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors used for social interaction, ranging from poorly integrated verbal and nonverbal communication; to abnormalities in eye contact and body language or deficits in understanding and use of gestures; to total lack of facial expressions and nonverbal communication | Difficulties in understanding verbal and nonverbal social cues; integrating language with body language; initiating and maintaining conversations |
| **A3: Relationships** | Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships, ranging from difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts; to difficulties in sharing imaginative play or in making friends; to absence of interest in peers | Sustaining peer relationships; social awareness; empathy; sharing interests |
| **Minimum Features Required** | **All 3** social-communication criteria must be present (DSM-5-TR added the word "all": "as manifested by **all** of the following") | At least **1** social-communication feature documented alongside at least 1 repetitive behavior feature |
| **Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder** | Separate diagnosis available for those with social communication deficits not meeting full ASD criteria | No equivalent separate category |
| **Number of Possible Presentations** | Fixed polythetic approach with specific counting requirements | Approximately **304 different presentations** of autism described |

**Key Insight:** The DSM-5's requirement for deficits in *all three* social-communication areas creates a more restrictive threshold compared to the ICD-11's flexible approach. The DSM-5-TR (2022) added explicit language requiring "all of the following," strengthening this requirement. The ICD-11's allowance for diagnosis with only a single social-communication feature represents the most significant structural difference between the two systems. Research shows that this flexibility in ICD-11 yields up to 304 possible symptom combinations, whereas DSM-5 maintains a more constrained set of required features [10].

### A.2 Sensory Processing Mention and Weighting

| **Aspect** | **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|------------|-----------|------------|
| **Location in Criteria** | Criterion B4 (under Restricted, Repetitive Patterns of Behavior) — one of four sub-criteria | Under restricted, repetitive behaviors domain — integrated as a diagnostic requirement |
| **Exact Wording** | "Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interests in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)" | "Atypical responses to sensory stimuli, including hypersensitivity and/or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli" / "abnormal sensitivity or interest in sensory input" |
| **Required for Diagnosis?** | **No** — only 2 of 4 B criteria must be met, so B4 alone is insufficient | **No** — it is one potential manifestation of RRBs, not individually required |
| **Change from Previous Edition** | **New** in DSM-5 (2013) — not present in DSM-IV | **New** in ICD-11 — not present in ICD-10 |
| **Sensory Coverage** | Hyper-reactivity, hypo-reactivity, and unusual sensory interests explicitly named | Atypical responses to sensory stimuli including sounds, textures, lights, pain, temperature |
| **Diagnostic Utility** | Questioned by research — Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015) found only "failure to respond to noises" had sensitivity >0.75, but specificity was poor (0.34) | Broader inclusion may align better with clinical experience but raises similar specificity concerns |

**Key Insight:** Both systems now recognize sensory processing differences as diagnostically relevant, representing a major convergence from ICD-10, which did not consider sensory processing a core diagnostic feature [25]. However, the clinical utility of sensory features as diagnostic criteria has been questioned. The DSM-5's placement of sensory issues as one of four B-criteria (requiring two total) creates a higher threshold for individuals whose primary presentation involves sensory differences. The ICD-11 represents an important innovation by explicitly including "atypical responses to sensory stimuli" as part of diagnostic requirements, whereas ICD-10 lacked this entirely [25].

### A.3 Severity Specifiers

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **3 Levels of Support** rated separately for (a) social communication and (b) restricted, repetitive behaviors | **5 Profiles** based on intellectual development and functional language ability |
| **Level 1:** "Requiring Support" — difficulty initiating social interactions, rigid behavior limits functioning | **6A02.0:** Without intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 2:** "Requiring Substantial Support" — marked deficits in verbal and nonverbal communication; inflexible behavior obvious to casual observer | **6A02.1:** With intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 3:** "Requiring Very Substantial Support" — severe deficits in communication; extreme difficulty coping with change; behaviors interfere with all life aspects | **6A02.2:** Without intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| Severity should not be used to determine service eligibility; requires individualized assessment | **6A02.3:** With intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| Only 24.7% of participants showed concordant severe classifications across autism symptoms, cognitive, and adaptive measures in one study | **6A02.4:** With intellectual development disorder and with absence of functional language |
| | **6A02.5:** With other specified intellectual development disorder or language impairment |
| | Additional codes: 6A02.Y (Other specified) and 6A02.Z (Unspecified) |

**Key Insight:** The two systems take fundamentally different approaches to severity classification. The DSM-5 rates severity of core autism symptoms (social communication and RRBs separately), while the ICD-11 classifies based on co-occurring intellectual disability and language impairment. The ICD-11 approach may better capture functional outcomes but does not directly rate core autism symptom severity. The ICD-11 provides detailed guidelines for distinguishing autism with and without intellectual disability, unlike DSM-5, which merely notes their possible co-occurrence [8]. Research has questioned DSM-5 severity level reliability, finding that quantitative methods for differentiating between levels remain undetermined [21].

### A.4 Age of Onset and Developmental Period Requirements

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion C:** "Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period" | "The onset of the disorder occurs during the developmental period – typically in early childhood – but characteristic symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed limited capacities" |
| Removed previous age-of-onset cutoff of 3 years (from DSM-IV) | No specific age cutoff — onset "during the developmental period" |
| Symptoms "usually in the second year of life," though recognition can vary | Explicitly recognizes that some individuals experience distress and social challenges later in adolescence or adulthood |
| Symptoms "may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities" | Recognizes masking/camouflaging: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" |
| **No explicit mention of regression or loss of skills** in diagnostic criteria | **Explicitly includes "loss of previously acquired skills"** (regression) as a diagnostic consideration |
| Approximate age 8 or younger: "symptoms must be present in early childhood (age approximately 8 or younger)" | Onset "typically in early childhood" — more flexible timing |

**Key Insight:** Both systems recognize that symptoms may not fully manifest until social demands exceed capacities, enabling late diagnosis in higher-functioning individuals. The ICD-11's explicit inclusion of regression and its potentially broader interpretation of onset timing represent meaningful differences that could affect diagnostic capture of certain populations. The ICD-11 uniquely includes "loss of previously acquired competences" as a characteristic to be taken into account when making a diagnosis, whereas DSM-5 does not [8].

### A.5 Functional Impairment Thresholds

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion D:** "Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning" | "The symptoms result in significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational or other important areas of functioning" |
| **Treatment as standalone criterion** (must be met independently) | **Embedded** in overall diagnostic description |
| **Criterion E:** "Symptoms are not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay" — must be ruled out | Rett syndrome explicitly excluded (LD90.4) |
| Areas specified: social, occupational, or other important areas | Areas specified: personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas — broader range than DSM-5 |
| Masking/camouflaging implicitly recognized through onset flexibility | Explicitly recognizes masking: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" — impairment can still be present |
| Observable impairment typically required | Impairment "observable in all settings, albeit with contextual variations" |

**Key Insight:** The ICD-11 includes "educational" and "personal" and "family" as explicit impairment domains, which are absent from DSM-5's wording. The ICD-11's explicit recognition of compensation/masking may better capture individuals who maintain surface-level functioning but experience significant internal distress. The ICD-11 also explicitly states that "a diagnosis of ASD is still appropriate in such cases even when deficits may not be apparent to others due to exceptional effort" [17].

## Section B: Evidence Synthesis of Peer-Reviewed Sources Comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 Criteria

### Critical Note on Available Evidence

As of May 30, 2026, **no direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies** comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, or specificity for ASD have been published. The ICD-11 was released in 2019 and became effective on January 1, 2022, which has not allowed sufficient time for large-scale empirical validation studies comparing the two systems directly. The best available evidence consists of: (1) conceptual critiques comparing the two systems, (2) systematic reviews comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria (DSM-IV-TR), (3) organization-level comparisons of ICD-11 and DSM-5 for all mental disorders, and (4) reliability studies of DSM-5 criteria alone. These are synthesized below.

### B.1 Kamp-Becker, I. (2024). "Autism spectrum disorder in ICD-11—a critical reflection of its possible impact on clinical practice and research." *Molecular Psychiatry*.

- **Study Design:** Perspective article / critical reflection
- **Sample Size:** Not applicable (conceptual analysis)
- **Key Statistics:** No empirical data; provides conceptual analysis
- **Key Findings:**
  - ICD-11 allows a "high variety in symptom combinations, resulting in an extremely diverse picture, possibly at the expense of precision"
  - "This conceptualization can hardly be differentiated from other mental disorders and autism-like traits"
  - ICD-11 "moves away from an observable, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorder to a disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively"
  - "This bears a **large danger of false positive diagnoses**, of further increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments"
  - ICD-11 allows diagnosis with only 1 social-communication feature + 1 repetitive behavior feature, with onset "at any time in life"
  - For research, the increased heterogeneity "will hamper replication, limit understanding of ASD etiology, and delay the realization of precision medicine"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Very Low** (perspective/opinion article; no empirical data collection; not a systematic review)

*Relevance:* This is the most directly relevant source comparing the conceptual underpinnings of DSM-5 and ICD-11 for autism. While not an empirical study, it provides expert critique of ICD-11's potential impact from a leading German psychiatrist.

### B.2 First, M.B., et al. (2021). "An organization- and category-level comparison of diagnostic requirements for mental disorders in ICD-11 and DSM-5." *World Psychiatry*.

- **Study Design:** Structured systematic comparison of ICD-11 and DSM-5 classification systems
- **Sample Size:** 103 disorders common to both systems were compared
- **Key Statistics:**
  - 20 disorders (19.4%) had **major differences** between ICD-11 and DSM-5 — including Autism Spectrum Disorder
  - 42 disorders (40.8%) had **minor definitional differences**
  - 10 disorders (9.7%) had minor differences due to greater specification in DSM-5
  - 31 disorders (30.1%) were **essentially identical** between the two systems
  - A total of 26 disorders appear in one system but not the other
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (structured systematic comparison with explicit methodology; however, this is not a systematic review of diagnostic accuracy studies; it is a classification-level comparison)
- **Key Findings:**
  - "The ICD and DSM are now closer than at any time since the ICD-8 and DSM-II"
  - Differences are "largely based on the differing priorities and uses of the two diagnostic systems and on differing interpretations of the evidence"
  - WHO prioritized public health needs; APA required substantial empirical evidence for new diagnoses
  - Specific differences for ASD: ICD-11 provides broader diagnostic concepts with less prescriptive criteria aimed at global applicability, while DSM-5 favors detailed criteria sets with stricter symptom counts [8]

*Relevance:* Provides the most comprehensive organization-level comparison of all mental disorders across both systems, with ASD identified among the 19.4% with major differences.

### B.3 McPartland, J.C., Reichow, B., & Volkmar, F.R. (2012). "Sensitivity and specificity of proposed DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for autism spectrum disorder." *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*.

- **Study Design:** Cross-sectional reanalysis of DSM-IV field trial data
- **Sample Size:** N = 933 participants total (657 clinically diagnosed with ASD; 276 diagnosed with non-autistic disorders)
- **Key Statistics:**
  - 60.6% of cases with clinical ASD diagnosis met proposed DSM-5 criteria
  - Specificity: **94.9%** — high rate of accurate exclusion
  - Sensitivity by subgroup: Autistic Disorder **76%**; Asperger's Disorder **25%**; PDD-NOS **28%**
  - IQ < 70: **70% met criteria**; IQ ≥ 70: **46% met criteria**
  - Relaxing the social-communication requirement improved sensitivity to **.75** but reduced specificity to **.85**
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (well-designed retrospective analysis of field trial data; large sample; however, it applies proposed DSM-5 criteria to DSM-IV-era data; no ICD-11 comparison)

*Relevance:* Provides critical evidence that DSM-5 criteria reduce ASD diagnoses by approximately 40% compared to DSM-IV-TR, particularly affecting higher-functioning individuals. This is indirect evidence for understanding how ICD-11's broader criteria might differ.

### B.4 Kulage, K.M., et al. (2020). "How has DSM-5 Affected Autism Diagnosis? A 5-Year Follow-Up Systematic Literature Review and Meta-analysis." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*.

- **Study Design:** Systematic literature review and meta-analysis (5-year follow-up)
- **Sample Size:** 33 studies comparing DSM-IV-TR and DSM-5 criteria across various settings
- **Key Statistics:**
  - Approximately **20%** of individuals previously diagnosed with ASD under DSM-IV-TR no longer met DSM-5 criteria
  - Only about **30%** of those losing the ASD label qualified for Social Communication Disorder (SCD)
  - This reduction (~20%) is **smaller than the earlier predicted one-third drop** from the 2014 study
  - Least discrepancy found when diagnoses were made by **interdisciplinary teams**
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (systematic review and meta-analysis with explicit methodology; comprehensive searching; however, it only addresses DSM-5 vs. DSM-IV-TR — no ICD-11 comparison; indirect evidence only)
- **Key Findings:**
  - "About one in five people who had an ASD diagnosis under the old rules no longer qualified under DSM-5"
  - "Only three out of ten of those who lost ASD could switch to Social Communication Disorder"
  - "The drop was smaller than the 2014 review had first shown"
  - "Least discrepancy was noted when applying DSM-5 criteria by interdisciplinary teams of physicians and psychologists"

*Relevance:* This is the most comprehensive meta-analysis comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria, providing essential context for understanding how criteria changes affect diagnostic rates. The 20% reduction under DSM-5 provides a baseline for comparing with ICD-11's more inclusive approach.

### B.5 Doernberg, E. & Hollander, E. (2016). "Neurodevelopmental Disorders (ASD and ADHD): DSM-5, ICD-10, and ICD-11." *CNS Spectrums*.

- **Study Design:** Narrative review comparing diagnostic evolution of neurodevelopmental disorders across DSM-5, ICD-10, and anticipated ICD-11
- **Sample Size:** N/A (review article — no original data)
- **Key Statistics:** None (no quantitative diagnostic accuracy data)
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Low** (narrative review, not systematic; compares DSM-5 to ICD-10, not ICD-11; the comparison with ICD-11 is anticipatory only)
- **Key Findings:**
  - Neurodevelopmental disorders "have undergone considerable diagnostic evolution in the past decade"
  - Important to highlight "the importance of progress towards complementary diagnostic classification systems, keeping in mind the difference in tradition and purpose of the DSM and the ICD"
  - "These systems are dynamic and changing as more is learned about neurodevelopmental disorders and their underlying etiology"

*Relevance:* Provides historical context for the evolution of both diagnostic systems and highlights the different traditions and purposes of DSM and ICD.

### Summary of Evidence Gaps

The most critical finding is the **absence of direct DSM-5 vs ICD-11 empirical comparisons**. The five sources above provide indirect evidence: DSM-5 significantly reduces ASD diagnoses compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R (by 20-40% depending on population), particularly for higher-functioning individuals. The Kamp-Becker (2024) critique raises concerns that ICD-11's much broader criteria may swing the pendulum toward over-diagnosis. A direct head-to-head comparison applying both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria to the same clinical population remains an urgent research priority.

## Section C: Clinical Guideline Positions

### C.1 World Health Organization (WHO)

The WHO designed and released the ICD-11 on June 18, 2018, after a decade of development and over 10,000 revision proposals. It was **unanimously adopted by the 72nd World Health Assembly on May 25, 2019**, and came into effect on January 1, 2022 [28].

**Implementation Status:**
- As of 2026, over **60 countries** are at different implementation stages of ICD-11 [28]
- The WHO officially stopped maintaining ICD-10 in 2018; future enhancements will only appear in ICD-11 [28]
- ICD-11 covers approximately **85,000 entities** and is fully digital and available online free of charge [17]

**Design Philosophy:**
- The WHO designed the ICD-11 "to be broadly applicable across cultures worldwide"
- Committee member David Skuse stated: "What we are trying to do with ICD-11 is to create a set of criteria that are so broad that they could be applied anywhere in the world" [8]
- WHO tended to prioritize public health needs: if convincing empirical evidence existed for a condition's legitimacy as a focus of health care services, it was consistent with ICD-11's purpose to include it [8]

**Autism-Specific Changes:**
- The ICD-11 aligns closely with DSM-5 by consolidating Asperger's Syndrome, Childhood Disintegrative Disorder, and other pervasive developmental disorders under the umbrella term "Autism Spectrum Disorder" [24]
- The ICD-11 includes atypical sensory responses as a diagnostic requirement for the first time, whereas ICD-10 did not consider sensory processing as a core diagnostic feature [25]
- The ICD-11 provides detailed guidelines for distinguishing autism with and without intellectual disability, unlike DSM-5 which merely notes their possible co-occurrence [8]

### C.2 American Psychiatric Association (APA)

The APA publishes the **DSM-5 (2013) and DSM-5-TR (2022)** as the standard diagnostic manual for mental disorders in the United States.

**Key Position:**
- The APA has **not issued a formal comparative position statement** endorsing one system over the other for autism specifically
- The APA maintains the DSM as the US clinical standard while acknowledging that ICD codes are required for billing and insurance
- An ICD-DSM Harmonization Group was appointed to harmonize the two systems' organization [8]

**Degree of Alignment:**
- Among 103 disorders common to both ICD-11 and DSM-5: 19.4% have major diagnostic differences, 40.8% have minor definitional differences, 9.7% differ due to greater specification in DSM-5, and 30.1% are essentially identical [8]
- "The ICD and DSM are now closer than at any time since the ICD-8 and DSM-II" [8]
- From the APA's perspective, concerns about proliferation of new diagnoses required considerable empirical evidence for adding diagnoses in DSM-5 [8]

**Expert Commentary:**
- David Skuse: "DSM-5 was conceptually correct and ICD-11 is following a similar view" [8]
- Fred Volkmar: "[ICD-11] is taking some of the better parts of DSM without falling into the same pitfalls" [8]
- Catherine Lord: "It will make life simpler for people making diagnoses" [8]

### C.3 American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

The AAP published its key clinical report **"Identification, Evaluation, and Management of Children With Autism Spectrum Disorder"** (updated January 2020) [13].

**Screening Recommendations:**
- The AAP recommends "general developmental screening at 9, 18, and 30 months and autism screening at 18 and 24 months" [13]
- The AAP does not endorse any specific screening test exclusively

**DSM-5 as Reference Framework:**
- The AAP's clinical report uses **DSM-5 as the reference diagnostic framework** throughout
- The AAP does not explicitly endorse ICD-11 over DSM-5, but aligns with DSM-5 as the US diagnostic standard

**Position on Diagnostic Categories:**
- "DSM-5 established a single category of ASD to replace the subtypes of autistic disorder, Asperger syndrome, and pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified" [13]

**Sensory Processing:**
- The AAP report highlights the inclusion of sensory processing issues in DSM-5 diagnostic criteria
- In 2012, the AAP issued a policy statement advising pediatricians not to diagnose sensory processing disorder as a standalone condition but to evaluate for autism, ADHD, or anxiety instead [16]
- Dr. Susan Hyman, representing AAP: "While sensory issues exist and are biologically based, we cannot disentangle sensory from behavior... the main component should be behavior therapy" [16]

**Key Recommendation:**
- "Pediatricians should not wait for an autism evaluation or diagnosis for children to get the care they need" [13]

### C.4 NICE (UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence)

**Guideline Title:** "Autism spectrum disorder in under 19s: recognition, referral and diagnosis" (NICE Guideline CG128) [20]

**Publication and Review Status:**
- Originally published: September 2011
- Last updated: December 20, 2017
- Last reviewed: June 14, 2021 — "We found no new evidence that affects the recommendations" [22]

**Position on Diagnostic Criteria:**
- NICE explicitly states that diagnostic decisions should **"use ICD-11 or DSM-5 criteria"** — endorsing both as equally acceptable diagnostic frameworks [21]
- "Do not rely on any autism-specific diagnostic tool alone to diagnose autism; use clinical judgment based on ICD-11 or DSM-5 criteria" [21]
- Diagnosis should be "informed by information from a range of sources, together with clinical judgement"

**Timeliness Standard:**
- Diagnostic assessments should **start within 3 months of referral** [21]

**Sensory Processing:**
- NICE has **not issued specific guidance weighting sensory processing criteria differently** between ICD-11 and DSM-5
- The adult guideline (CG142) explicitly recognizes sensory sensitivities as a core feature of autism [24]

**Terminology Updates:**
- The 2021 surveillance added editorial amendments including updating language to **"autistic people"** instead of "people with autism" [22]

**Monitoring ICD-11 Adoption:**
- NICE is monitoring ICD-11 adoption as part of ongoing initiatives that may impact future service delivery [22]

### Summary of Clinical Guideline Positions

| **Organization** | **Primary Framework Endorsed** | **Position on ICD-11** | **Sensory Processing Stance** |
|------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **WHO** | ICD-11 (its own publication) | Full endorsement; 60+ countries implementing | Included as diagnostic requirement for 1st time |
| **APA** | DSM-5/DSM-5-TR (its own publication) | Acknowledges ICD-11 alignment; no formal comparison statement | Criterion B4; part of RRBs |
| **AAP** | DSM-5 (as US reference) | No explicit endorsement of ICD-11; uses DSM-5 framework | Recognized as diagnostic feature; advises against SPD as standalone diagnosis |
| **NICE** | Both DSM-5 and ICD-11 equally | Full dual endorsement; use either | No differential weighting between systems |

## Section D: Advocacy Organization Positions on Neurodiversity-Affirming Diagnostic Criteria

### D.1 Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

**Organizational Background:** ASAN is "run by and for autistic individuals" and explicitly operates within the neurodiversity movement. Their core principles state: "Autism is a developmental disability. Disability is a natural part of human diversity" [16].

**Position on Diagnostic Criteria:**

ASAN was **extremely active in lobbying the DSM-5 revision process from 2009 to 2012**, though they have not published a formal comparison of DSM-5 versus ICD-11. Key positions from their Joint Statement with the Autism Society of America include:

- "First, do no harm: it is essential that DSM-5 criteria ensure those who qualified under DSM-IV maintain access to an ASD diagnosis" [5]
- "Evidence suggests racial and ethnic minorities, women and girls, adults, and individuals from rural and low-income communities face challenges accessing diagnosis" [5]
- "Enhancing access to diagnosis can result in substantial improvements in quality of life and more competent service provision" [5]
- "Community engagement and representation within the DSM-5 process itself is a critical component of ensuring accurate, scientific and research-validated diagnostic criteria" [5]

**Specific Critiques of DSM-5:**
- ASAN expressed concerns about "decreased diagnostic sensitivity, especially in underrepresented groups like adults, women and girls, and racial and ethnic minorities" [4]
- ASAN criticized "the stricter social communication domain criteria (requiring all three traits instead of two)" [4]
- ASAN opposed "the introduction of a Social Communication Disorder (SCD) diagnosis outside the autism spectrum" [4]
- ASAN strongly criticized "the implementation of a severity scale which may impact service access" [4]
- ASAN highlighted "how compensation and masking behaviors in adolescents and adults, particularly females, can lead to underdiagnosis" [4]

**ASAN's Recommendations for DSM-5:**
- Shift social communication criteria from three of three to two of three traits
- Incorporate SCD within the autism spectrum as a subtype
- Add motor and movement impairments to diagnostic criteria
- Eliminate or revise the severity scale to prevent misuse [4]

**Documented Impact:**
The Springer Nature Link chapter "Lobbying Autism's Diagnostic Revision in the DSM-5" documents that ASAN achieved "partial successes, including revisions in the diagnostic text acknowledging masking and learned coping strategies, greater attention to diverse populations, and mitigating some of the severity scale's negative implications" [2]. This "marked the first successful instance of a disability community influencing their own diagnostic criteria" [2].

**Explicit Position on ICD-11:**
**No explicit position exists.** ASAN has not published any policy brief, statement, or public comment that explicitly compares DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria [1-5].

**Inferred Position:**
Based on ASAN's documented critiques and core principles:
- **Neither manual** is likely considered fully neurodiversity-affirming, as both frame autism as a "disorder" under the medical model
- ASAN would likely find aspects of **ICD-11 potentially less problematic** on certain dimensions (no severity level system, greater clinical flexibility, inclusion of regression)
- However, ASAN might also critique ICD-11's broader criteria for potentially diluting services
- ASAN's guiding principle of "Nothing About Us Without Us" underscores that neither system was developed with sufficient autistic representation

### D.2 National Autistic Society (UK)

**Organizational Background:** The National Autistic Society is the UK's leading autism charity, providing information, support, and advocacy.

**Official Guidance on Diagnostic Criteria:**
- "An autism diagnosis should be based on criteria listed in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition (DSM-5) or the International Classification of Diseases 11th Revision (ICD-11), according to the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence guidelines" [6]
- "Both DSM-5 and ICD-11 are the standard classification systems used by health care professionals to assess and diagnose autism" [6]
- "The core characteristics of autism must be present throughout a person's life for diagnosis" [6]

**Position on Neurodiversity:**
The NAS has a dedicated page on "Autism and neurodiversity" stating: "The neurodiversity paradigm calls for a shift from viewing neurodivergence as a disorder to seeing it as a valued difference" [7]. The NAS embraces the neurodiversity paradigm conceptually but does not apply it as a critical lens to the diagnostic manuals themselves.

**Explicit Comparative Position:**
**No explicit comparative position exists.** The NAS presents both manuals neutrally as clinical tools without endorsing or critiquing either from a neurodiversity perspective [6, 20].

**Inferred Position:**
- The NAS takes a pragmatic approach: diagnosis is essential for accessing services and support, and both manuals serve that purpose
- Neither manual would be explicitly preferred on neurodiversity grounds
- The focus is on ensuring that diagnosis, regardless of which manual is used, is conducted in a neurodiversity-affirming manner

### D.3 Autism Speaks

**Organizational Background:** Autism Speaks is one of the world's largest autism advocacy and research organizations, primarily US-based.

**Position on Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Provides a page explaining **DSM-5 criteria** for a general audience, stating: "To meet diagnostic criteria for ASD according to DSM-5, a child must have persistent deficits in each of three areas of social communication and interaction plus at least two of four types of restricted, repetitive behaviors" [9]
- Published an Expert Q&A stating: "The DSM-5 removed previous subtypes and formally created the diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder to acknowledge the spectrum nature of autism" [12]

**Position on ICD-11:**
**No specific ICD-11 position statement, policy paper, or blog post was found.** Autism Speaks does not appear to have published a comparative analysis of DSM-5 versus ICD-11.

**Evolution on Neurodiversity:**
- Historically (2005-2016): Criticized by autistic-led organizations for cure-focused messaging and "perpetuating stigma against autistic people" [15]
- 2016: The word "cure" was dropped from its mission statement [15]
- 2026: Published "Broadening our approach to neurodiversity" stating: "As a community, we have learned to embrace neurodiversity and recognize that autistic people are not defined by deficits, but by the richness they contribute to the human experience" [13]

**Inferred Position:**
- Autism Speaks would likely consider **DSM-5 adequate** for clinical purposes
- Has not applied neurodiversity critique to diagnostic manuals themselves
- Focus is on service access rather than diagnostic framework critique

### D.4 Scholarly Neurodiversity Critiques of Both Manuals

The chapter "Rethinking Autism Assessment, Diagnosis, and Intervention" (Rutherford and Johnston, NAIT Scotland) explicitly states: "Current diagnostic criteria for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), as per DSM-5 and ICD-11, remain deficit-focused and challenge the social and neurodiversity models that view autism as a difference rather than a disorder" [21].

### D.5 Summary Comparison of Advocacy Positions

| **Organization** | **Explicit Preference?** | **Which System More Neurodiversity-Affirming?** | **Basis** |
|------------------|--------------------------|------------------------------------------------|-----------|
| **ASAN** | No explicit comparison | **ICD-11 (likely)** — based on inferred principles of flexibility, masking recognition, less problematic severity system | ASAN's DSM-5 advocacy history; implicit from core principles |
| **National Autistic Society** | No — both equally valid | **Equal** — follows NICE dual endorsement | Explicit guidance page |
| **Autism Speaks** | No public position | **No position taken** | Absence of ICD-11 commentary |

**Overarching Finding:** None of the three organizations has published a formal comparative analysis declaring one manual more neurodiversity-affirming than the other. ASAN's history and principles suggest they would likely view ICD-11's flexibility favorably, but this remains inference rather than explicit position. The most explicit neurodiversity critique comes from scholarly sources stating that both manuals remain deficit-focused and challenge neurodiversity models.

## Section E: Diagnostic Safety Risks

### E.1 Overview of Opposing Diagnostic Safety Profiles

A critical finding of this analysis is that DSM-5 and ICD-11 create **opposing diagnostic safety risks**. The DSM-5's more restrictive counting approach primarily risks **underdiagnosis** (false negatives), while the ICD-11's broader, more permissive approach primarily risks **overdiagnosis** (false positives).

### E.2 Under-Diagnosis Risks

**Under DSM-5:**
- **DSM-5 criteria reduce ASD diagnoses by 20-40%** compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R across multiple studies. The Kulage meta-analysis found approximately 20% of previously diagnosed individuals no longer met DSM-5 criteria [4].
- **McPartland et al. (2012):** Only 60.6% of clinically diagnosed cases met proposed DSM-5 criteria; only 25% of Asperger's and 28% of PDD-NOS cases were captured [14]
- **Wilson et al. (2013):** Only 56% of adults with clinical ASD per ICD-10R met DSM-5 criteria
- **Higher-functioning individuals, females, and those previously diagnosed with PDD-NOS or Asperger's** are most likely to be under-identified
- **McPartland et al. (2012):** Only 46% of individuals with IQ ≥ 70 met DSM-5 criteria versus 70% with IQ < 70 [14]
- **Female underdiagnosis:** "Recent research shows that nearly **80% of autistic females are undiagnosed as of age 18**" [9]
- **DSM-5-TR change (2022):** Adding the word "all" to social communication criteria may further increase underdiagnosis

**Under ICD-11:**
- Paradoxically, ICD-11 may **reduce underdiagnosis** in some populations (females, adults, masking individuals) due to its broader criteria and flexibility
- However, the Kamp-Becker (2024) critique suggests that ICD-11's reliance on "inner experience" rather than observable behaviors may create new avenues for underdiagnosis when clinicians are uncertain about how to operationalize subjective criteria

### E.3 Over-Diagnosis Risks

**Under ICD-11 (Primary Concerns):**
- **Kamp-Becker (2024) in *Molecular Psychiatry*** provides the most rigorous academic critique: "This bears a **large danger of false positive diagnoses**, of further increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments" [10]
- **Mechanism for overdiagnosis:** With 304 possible symptom combinations and no mandatory minimum threshold, "a diagnosis can be made if 1 feature of social-communication and 1 feature of repetitive behaviors are documented, with an onset of any time in life" [6]
- ICD-11 "moves away from an observable, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorder to a disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively, containing many vague and subjective concepts leading to **non-falsifiable diagnoses**" [10]
- The broad criteria is "causing a lot of confusion in the community because the presentations are not specific to autism" [6]

**Historical Overdiagnosis Concerns:**
- Pioneering researcher Dame Uta Frith argues that "The autism spectrum has become so broad that it is 'close to collapse' and has 'lost all meaning' as a medical category" [7]
- About 13% of children who had once received an autism diagnosis were later reported by parents to have 'lost' it (CDC survey analysis) [7]
- About 23% of children previously diagnosed with autism were reclassified as 'non-spectrum' after comprehensive reassessment (Hodges et al., 2018) [7]
- Allen Frances, former DSM-IV task force leader, stated: "Our broadening the autism definition led to such massive, careless over-diagnosis" [7]

### E.4 Sensory-Focused Presentations: Specific Safety Risks

The **different weighting of sensory criteria** between the two systems creates unique diagnostic safety risks for individuals with prominent sensory presentations:

| **Risk Type** | **Under DSM-5** | **Under ICD-11** |
|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Sensory-dominant presentation misses diagnosis** | **HIGHER RISK** — an individual with primarily sensory symptoms may not meet threshold of at least 2 of 4 B criteria unless they also have other RRBs | **LOWER RISK** — sensory sensitivities are integrated as part of RRB domain requirement |
| **Non-autistic sensory sensitivity diagnosed as autism** | **LOWER RISK** — sensory features alone insufficient for B criterion | **HIGHER RISK** — broader inclusion may capture non-autistic individuals with sensory processing disorder or other conditions |
| **Diagnostic specificity of sensory features** | Grapel et al. (2015): Only "failure to respond to noises" had sensitivity >0.75 but specificity was poor (0.34) | Same concern applies — sensory features "are also frequent in other disorders" |

**Critical Evidence from Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015):**
This study of 776 school-age and adult individuals found:
- While sensory issues are common in ASD (**69-95%** of individuals), they are **also frequent in other disorders**, lacking sufficient specificity for diagnosis
- These results "question the rationale for including sensory items as a diagnostic criterion for autism"
- Sensory issues "may be important treatment targets but have limited utility as diagnostic features for autism in school-age and beyond" [14]

**Implication:** The inclusion of sensory features in both diagnostic systems, while clinically important, carries an inherent risk of false positives. The ICD-11's stronger integration of sensory features as a diagnostic requirement may increase this risk, particularly for children with primary Sensory Processing Disorder who may not have autism.

### E.5 Populations Differentially Affected

| **Population** | **Primary Risk** | **Evidence** |
|----------------|------------------|--------------|
| **Females** | Underdiagnosis under both systems, but especially under DSM-5 | 80% undiagnosed by age 18; more likely to be misdiagnosed at first evaluation than men [9, 16] |
| **Adults** | Underdiagnosis; late recognition | Average 8-year delay between first evaluation and ASD diagnosis [16] |
| **Higher-functioning individuals** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | 46% met DSM-5 criteria with IQ ≥70 vs 70% with IQ <70 [14] |
| **Culturally diverse groups** | Underdiagnosis; cultural factors affect expression and recognition | ICD-11 designed for broader global applicability [8] |
| **Minimally verbal / intellectual disability** | Better captured under ICD-11's detailed specifiers | ICD-11 provides detailed guidelines distinguishing autism with/without ID [8] |
| **Sensory-dominant presentations** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | Grapel et al. (2015) questions diagnostic utility of sensory features [14] |

### E.6 Inappropriate Interventions Resulting from Misdiagnosis

- **Mental health consequences of missed diagnosis:** "Misunderstanding and misinformation around variant and differing presentations means that misdiagnosed individuals and those who do not receive a diagnosis at all are often failed by medical, education, social care and criminal justice systems" [2]
- **Mislabeling with stigmatized conditions:** "Misdiagnosis and lack of diagnosis can lead to severe mental health consequences, including mislabeling with stigmatized conditions such as Borderline Personality Disorder and adverse outcomes like inpatient admissions" [2]
- **Dilution of services from overdiagnosis:** "Services become diluted and the diagnosis becomes trivial, while specific problems may be overlooked in efforts to address autism" [7]
- **Non-specific treatments:** Overdiagnosis leads to "limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments" [10]
- **Sensory processing confusion:** Women with a history of misdiagnosis showed higher sensory reactivity scores, suggesting a mediating role between sensory overload and misdiagnosis as personality disorders [16]

## Section F: Reimbursement and Coding Summary

### F.1 United States: Current Status

**ICD-10-CM F84.0 — "Autistic Disorder"**

The United States currently uses **ICD-10-CM** for all healthcare billing and reimbursement. The primary autism code, **F84.0 ("Autistic disorder")**, has remained unchanged since its introduction in the 2016 edition [1]:

- **2026 Edition:** Effective October 1, 2025 [1]
- **Definition:** Includes "autism spectrum disorder, Kanner's syndrome, infantile autism, and infantile psychosis" [1]
- **DRG Grouping:** MS-DRG 884, "Organic disturbances and intellectual disability" [1]
- **Related Codes:** F84.1 (atypical autism), F84.5 (Asperger's syndrome), F84.2 (Rett's syndrome), F84.9 (PDD unspecified)
- **Screening Code:** Z13.41 for autism screening, effective October 1, 2024 [8]
- **Exclusions:** F84.0 and F84.5 are mutually exclusive; Type 1 exclusion prevents concurrent use [1, 3]

**The United States Has NOT Adopted ICD-11 for Billing**

- The US adopted ICD-10-CM on October 1, 2015, taking over two decades after its WHO release
- ICD-11 came into effect globally on January 1, 2022, but has **"hardly been discussed in the United States"** for billing purposes [10]
- The National Committee on Vital and Health Statistics (NCVHS) established an ICD-11 Workgroup in 2023 to gather information and develop recommendations [10]
- **Estimated timeline:** Transition timelines are projected around **2027-2029 for morbidity/billing**, with full implementation potentially taking **10-15 years** [10, 23]
- **Transition costs:** Estimated at **$1-3 billion nationally** due to complex mapping (only **23.5% direct code mapping** between ICD-10 and ICD-11) [10, 23]
- 60 hours of training per coder estimated [23]

**Current US Payer Requirements:**
- **Private insurers uniformly require a diagnosis based on DSM-5 (or DSM-5-TR) criteria** for coverage of autism-related services [8]
- The ICD-10-CM code F84.0 is the billing code, but **the clinical diagnosis itself must be per DSM-5**
- **All 50 US states have adopted insurance mandates** for autism spectrum disorder requiring coverage for diagnosis and treatment [33]
- Coverage includes ABA, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and other medically necessary services [33]
- State-specific limits exist: Florida caps yearly benefits at $36,000 and lifetime at $200,000 [33]

### F.2 Sensory Processing Disorder Coding Issues in the US

**Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) is NOT recognized as a distinct diagnosis in DSM-5 or ICD-10 and therefore has no specific ICD-10 code** [30-32].

**Alternative codes used for SPD:**
- **F84.9** (Pervasive developmental disorder, unspecified) — used when sensory issues are part of a broader neurodevelopmental disorder [30, 31, 35]
- **F88** (Other disorders of psychological development) — used when SPD occurs as an isolated condition; includes "Sensory integration disorder" as a subcategory [30, 31, 32]
- **R20.3** (Hyperesthesia) — for specific sensory symptoms [31]

**Insurance Denial Issues:**
- **Sensory integration therapy is explicitly designated as "investigative and unproven" by some payers** (e.g., Medica coverage policy effective October 1, 2025) [34]
- The absence of a dedicated code "can hamper reimbursement processes and limit access to specialized treatments" [30]
- For children with ASD diagnosis (F84.0), when therapy is specifically for sensory integration (CPT 97533) rather than ABA, denials can occur even with an established ASD diagnosis [33, 34]

**How ICD-11 Addresses This:**
- ICD-11 represents a significant improvement because "atypical responses to sensory stimuli are included as part of the diagnostic requirements in ICD-11, in contrast to ICD-10" [25]
- This formal inclusion should make it easier to justify sensory-based interventions when billed under an ASD diagnosis

### F.3 European Union: Current Status

**United Kingdom (NHS):**
- The UK **still primarily uses ICD-10** within the NHS, with ICD-11 implementation underway but incomplete [27]
- NHS England announced "preliminary work to explore future implementation of ICD-11 MMS" as of December 2025 [29]
- NHS England has been piloting ICD-11 since 2024 with a planned full rollout in **2027 for mental health** [27]
- Over **125,000 people** wait for autism assessments in England [29]

**Germany:**
- Germany still uses **ICD-10-GM (German Modification)** for statutory health insurance (GKV) reimbursement via the **G-DRG system**
- No official ICD-11 adoption date has been set
- Clinical practice is increasingly aligned with ICD-11 and DSM-5 diagnostic concepts

**France:**
- France uses **ICD-10 (CIM-10)** for administrative and statistical coding
- The **Haute Autorité de Santé (HAS)** has updated its clinical recommendations based on **both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria**

**Early Adopters:**
- **Netherlands, Norway, and Finland** are identified as leading adopters, using ICD-11 in both public health reporting and clinical reimbursement contexts [21, 22, 27]
- These countries report "improvements in coding accuracy, efficiency, and interoperability" [21, 22]

### F.4 WHO ICD-11 Implementation Status

- WHO officially stopped maintaining ICD-10 in 2018; future enhancements will only appear in ICD-11 [28]
- Over **60 countries** have already adopted ICD-11 [28]
- As of the ICD-11 2026 release (February 16, 2026): supports "over **6.3 million searchable code combinations**" and is "available in **21 languages**" [17]
- No penalties for delayed adoption, but prompt transition is encouraged [28]
- The European medical coding market was valued at USD 7.30 billion in 2025, projected to reach USD 18.92 billion by 2034 [20]

### F.5 Summary: Practical Impact on Reimbursement

| **System** | **Current Billing Code** | **ICD-11 Adopted for Billing?** | **Impact of ICD-11 on Coverage** |
|------------|-------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **US Private Insurance** | ICD-10-CM F84.0 | No — DSM-5 criteria required clinically | None currently; future adoption may equalize access |
| **US Medicare/Medicaid** | ICD-10-CM F84.0 | No | None currently; DRG restructuring expected |
| **UK NHS** | ICD-10 (pilot ICD-11) | Partially — piloting since 2024 | Planned full rollout 2027 for mental health |
| **Germany GKV** | ICD-10-GM F84.x | No — clinical alignment only | None currently; DRG recalibration needed |
| **France Sécurité Sociale** | CIM-10 (ICD-10) | No — clinical alignment with both | None currently; PMSI system updates needed |
| **Netherlands/Norway/Finland** | ICD-11 | **Yes** — adopted for clinical use and reimbursement | Positive — improvements in coding accuracy reported |

**Key Takeaway:** Adopting ICD-11 criteria **does not currently affect coverage or reimbursement** in the US or most EU systems because billing codes have not transitioned. The significant structural change — replacing categorical subtypes (F84.0, F84.5) with the unified code 6A02 and its post-coordination severity specifiers — has the potential to equalize access across the spectrum but also requires substantial administrative and technological investment in transition. For sensory-focused presentations, ICD-11's explicit inclusion of sensory sensitivities as a diagnostic criterion represents an important improvement that may facilitate better reimbursement justification for sensory-based interventions.

## Conclusion

The DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR) and ICD-11 (2019/2022) represent a significant convergence in autism diagnostic criteria, both adopting a unified autism spectrum diagnosis that consolidates previously separate subcategories. Both systems now include sensory processing differences as diagnostically relevant features — a major advance from ICD-10, which did not recognize sensory issues as a core diagnostic criterion.

However, important differences remain. The DSM-5 employs a more restrictive, counting-based approach requiring deficits in all three social-communication domains and at least two of four restricted/repetitive behavior domains. The ICD-11 takes a more flexible approach, allowing diagnosis with fewer documented features and providing clinicians greater judgment latitude. These structural differences create opposing diagnostic safety profiles: the DSM-5 primarily risks underdiagnosis (particularly for females, higher-functioning individuals, and those who mask), while the ICD-11 risks overdiagnosis (false positives due to broad criteria and lack of specificity).

The evidence base for comparing the two systems remains limited. No direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies have been published as of May 2026, representing an urgent research priority. The available evidence — primarily studies comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria — consistently shows DSM-5 reduces ASD diagnoses by 20-40% compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R, with higher-functioning individuals and those with Asperger's or PDD-NOS most affected.

Clinical guideline organizations have taken different positions reflecting their national contexts. NICE (UK) endorses both frameworks equally. The AAP (US) uses DSM-5 as its reference framework. The WHO promotes ICD-11 globally, with 60+ countries at various implementation stages. The APA maintains DSM-5 as the US clinical standard while acknowledging ICD codes for billing.

Advocacy organizations have not issued definitive comparative judgments. ASAN's history of advocacy during the DSM-5 revision suggests they would likely view ICD-11's flexibility favorably, but no formal position exists. The National Autistic Society treats both systems as equally valid under NICE guidance. Autism Speaks has not taken a public position on ICD-11.

The reimbursement landscape is currently dominated by the fact that the US has not adopted ICD-11 for billing, and most EU countries (except early adopters like the Netherlands, Norway, and Finland) still use ICD-10 for administrative purposes. When full ICD-11 adoption occurs, the unified code 6A02 and its post-coordination severity specifiers will likely reshape service eligibility and reimbursement structures. For sensory-focused presentations, ICD-11's formal inclusion of sensory sensitivities as a diagnostic feature represents a significant improvement that may enhance clinical recognition and reimbursement justification.

For clinicians, the key practical consideration is that both systems can yield valid autism diagnoses, but awareness of their different risk profiles is essential. The DSM-5 requires careful attention to ensure individuals who mask or have atypical presentations are not excluded. The ICD-11 requires careful differential diagnosis to ensure individuals with autism-like traits from other conditions are not inappropriately diagnosed. In either system, clinical judgment, comprehensive multi-source information gathering, and sensitivity to diverse presentations remain paramount.
