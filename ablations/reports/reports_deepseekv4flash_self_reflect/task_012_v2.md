# Revised Comparative Analysis: Maternal Health Among Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp

## Introduction

This revised report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of maternal health outcomes and healthcare delivery models for Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand. The original report identified several critical gaps, including methodological weaknesses in data interpretation, insufficient exploration of data discrepancies, a thin analysis of funding impacts on Mae La, and a lack of systematic application of analytical frameworks. This revision addresses these gaps by incorporating new data, deeper contextual analysis, and a more rigorous comparative framework. The analysis covers the period from 2017 to 2026, examining key health indicators—antenatal care completion, skilled birth attendance, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), and neonatal mortality—while exploring how fundamentally different healthcare delivery models, conflict contexts, and demographic profiles correlate with health outcomes. The report systematically applies the **Three Delays Model** and the **WHO Health System Building Blocks** to structure the comparison and provide actionable policy recommendations.

## Section 1: Resolving the Data Discrepancy Between UNHCR and WHO Indicators

A critical weakness identified in the original report was the apparent discrepancy between UNHCR’s reported 93% skilled birth attendance (SBA) and a WHO suggestion of 43% facility births in Cox's Bazar. This revision clarifies this discrepancy through a systematic investigation of indicator definitions and data sources.

### 1.1 Indicator Definitions and Scope

**Skilled Birth Attendance (SBA) vs. Facility Delivery:**
- **SBA (SDG indicator 3.1.2):** Measures the percentage of live births attended by accredited health professionals (doctors, nurses, or midwives), regardless of location. This can include home births attended by a skilled provider [1](https://www.who.int/data/gho/indicator-metadata-registry/imr-details/25).
- **Facility Delivery (Institutional Births):** Measures the percentage of live births that occur in a health facility, regardless of who attends the birth [2](https://www.who.int/data/gho/indicator-metadata-registry/imr-details/institutional-birth).

Globally, these two indicators are strongly correlated, but they are conceptually distinct. A study of 80 low- and middle-income countries found that while 73.8% of births were attended by an SBA, 70.5% occurred in health facilities, with some countries showing notable home SBA rates [3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4912761).

### 1.2 Resolving the Specific Discrepancy

The original report’s confusion stemmed from conflating a global refugee figure with a context-specific one.

1.  **UNHCR’s 93% SBA is a global refugee average**, not specific to Rohingya refugees. The UNHCR Annual Public Health Global Review 2024 states that "skilled birth attendance among refugees remained stable at 93%," referring to a global aggregate of all refugees across UNHCR operations [4](https://www.unhcr.org/us/sites/en-us/files/2025-04/annual-public-health-global-review-2024_3.pdf). This figure reflects births attended by partners within the UNHCR health information system.
2.  **Rohingya-specific SBA and facility delivery rates are significantly lower** than this global average, though they have improved dramatically. The most reliable population-based survey data for Rohingya women in Cox's Bazar, a 2025 study of 415 women in Camp-4, found **59.5% delivered in adequately equipped facilities** and **69.4% had skilled birth attendants present** [5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12613593). Health Sector service statistics have reported figures exceeding 80%, but this discrepancy highlights methodological differences [6].

### 1.3 Methodological Explanation for the Discrepancy

The variation in reported figures is explained by four key factors:

1.  **Different Indicators:** SBA counts skilled attendants at births, which can include some home births. Facility delivery counts where the birth occurs. In Rohingya camps, many women use TBAs for home births, which are counted in neither SBA nor facility deliveries.
2.  **Different Populations:** UNHCR’s 93% is a global average, while the Rohingya-specific figures are much lower.
3.  **Temporal Trend:** Facility births have increased dramatically, from **22% in early 2018** to **53% by 2020**, and then to approximately **60-80% by 2024**, depending on the catchment area and survey methodology [7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888086). One targeted project saw an increase from 33% to 95% in its catchment area between 2020 and 2022 [8](https://cpintl.org/impact-area/health/training-rohingya-traditional-birth-attendants-to-support-safe-births).
4.  **Data Source Methodology:** UNHCR HIS data (service statistics from health partners) tends to produce higher coverage estimates than population-based surveys (which capture all births, including unassisted home births). Service statistics only count births that come into contact with the formal health system, potentially overestimating population-level coverage.

**Conclusion:** The previous report should have clearly distinguished that the 93% SBA is a global refugee metric, not a valid benchmark for the Rohingya response. The actual population-level SBA for Rohingya women is likely between **69-80%** , a substantial achievement from a 12% baseline in 2018 but still far lower than the stable **95% SBA** seen in Mae La camp.

## Section 2: Healthcare Delivery Models and Coordination Structures

### 2.1 Cox's Bazar: Large-Scale, Multi-Partner Emergency Response

The health response in Cox's Bazar is a massive, complex operation coordinated by the WHO-led Health Sector with over 49 partner organizations [6]. This model is characterized by a three-tier network of health posts, primary health centers, and field hospitals, all delivering a Minimum Package of Essential Health Services. While this model achieved rapid scale-up, it faces challenges with coordination, data quality, and high staff turnover. The **Sexual and Reproductive Health (SRH) Quality Improvement and Accountability System (SRH-QIAS)** was established in 2023 to address systemic quality gaps. Baseline data from 2024 showed that only **2% of Health Posts used standardized Maternal and Child Health (MCH) cards**, a figure that surged to **87% by 2025** after targeted interventions, demonstrating the effectiveness of a system-wide quality improvement approach [9](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/2025-08/MPMSR%20Annual%20Report%202024%20Final.docx.pdf).

### 2.2 Mae La Camp: Protracted System with Refugee Self-Governance

In contrast, the healthcare model in Mae La is a "state-oversight, NGO-implemented parallel system" with integration into the Thai national health system at the secondary level. The Thai Ministry of Public Health (MOPH) provides oversight, while the **Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU)** has been the primary provider of maternal care for decades. A unique feature of this system is the **refugee-led self-governance structure**. The camp is managed by a **Camp Committee** under the Karen Refugee Committee (KRC), which plays a direct role in coordination and service delivery [10](https://www.cas.go.jp/jp/seisaku/nanmin/yusikishakaigi/dai6/siryou4.pdf). This structure has fostered a more integrated and sustainable system, though it remains vulnerable to external funding shocks.

## Section 3: Systematic Comparison of Health Indicators

This section provides a revised and deepened comparison of key health indicators, now including a systematic application of the Three Delays Model.

### 3.1 Antenatal Care (ANC4+)

- **Cox's Bazar:** The 2024 SRH survey found that **59% of women attended four or more ANC visits** [11](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/2024-10/SRH%20survey%20Broucher%20%20%284%29.pdf). An average of **6.9 visits** was reported in a separate 2023-24 study [12](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12781834).
- **Mae La:** **More than 90%** of pregnant women attended four or more ANC visits, a rate sustained for over two decades [13](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/primary-health-care-research-and-development/article/mother-child-and-adolescent-health-outcomes-in-two-longterm-refugee-camp-settings-at-the-thaimyanmar-border-20002018-a-retrospective-analysis/5A2A03E5B33226D61F84A028BB229992). SMRU records show >90% compliance with essential ANC elements (supplement provision, risk factor recording) [14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-015-0444-0).

### 3.2 Skilled Birth Attendance (SBA)

- **Cox's Bazar:** Currently estimated between **69% and 80%** , a dramatic increase from a baseline of 12% in 2018.
- **Mae La:** Consistently high at **95%** , with SMRU providing delivery by skilled birth attendants for approximately 2,500 women annually in Mae La alone [15](https://www.ogmagazine.org.au/15/2/thai-myanmar-border).

### 3.3 Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) and Maternal Mortality

This section provides new, detailed data on PPH incidence and maternal mortality, using the **Maternal and Perinatal Mortality Surveillance and Response (MPMSR)** data for Cox's Bazar and the SMRU cohort data for the Thailand-Myanmar border.

**Cox's Bazar (MPMSR Data):**
The MPMSR system provides a year-by-year breakdown of maternal deaths and their causes.
- **2020:** 45 maternal deaths; **Obstetric hemorrhage was the leading cause at 26.7%** [16](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/annual_report_of_mpmsr_2020_03_august_2021.pdf).
- **2021:** 96 maternal deaths; **obstetric hemorrhage at 29.2%** [17](https://reliefweb.int/report/bangladesh/maternal-and-perinatal-mortality-surveillance-and-response-mpmsr-rohingya-refugees-response-coxs-bazar-bangladesh-annual-report-2021).
- **2022:** 83 maternal deaths; **obstetric hemorrhage at 44%** [18](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/mpmsr_annual_report_2022.pdf.pdf).
- **2024:** 46 maternal deaths (a 19% reduction from 2023); **obstetric hemorrhage remained the primary cause at 48%** [9](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/2025-08/MPMSR%20Annual%20Report%202024%20Final.docx.pdf).

A critical finding from the 2024 MPMSR report is that **74% of maternal deaths involved a home trial by a Traditional Birth Attendant (TBA)**, with an **average referral delay of 19 hours** before reaching a health facility. This directly points to a failure in Delay 1 (seeking care) [9](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/2025-08/MPMSR%20Annual%20Report%202024%20Final.docx.pdf). The maternal mortality ratio (MMR) in the camps has declined, from 229.17 per 100,000 live births in 2022 to **168 per 100,000 in 2024** [19](https://www.who.int/bangladesh/news/feature-stories/item/building-a-safer-future-for-mothers-and-newborns-in-cox-s-bazar). However, this is still nearly 2.5 times the global SDG target.

**Mae La / Thailand-Myanmar Border (SMRU Data):**
- The overall SMRU Refugee and Migrant Pregnancy Cohort (1986-2024) recorded an MMR of **176 per 100,000 live births** across 68,024 births [20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12921400).
- For **refugee-camps only**, the MMR has shown a six-fold decline from **499 per 100,000 (1986-1990) to 79 per 100,000 (2006-2010)** [21](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0040244).
- **PPH-related MMR** in the SMRU population declined from **57.0 per 100,000 live births (2000-2007) to 25.4 per 100,000 (2009-2016)** [22](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8204190).
- The leading causes of maternal death in the SMRU cohort were **hemorrhage, sepsis, and *P. falciparum* malaria**, collectively accounting for 67.6% of all deaths [21](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0040244).

**Comparative Assessment:** While the overall MMR figures appear similar (179 vs 176), context is critical. Mae La’s rate has been sustained over decades for a stable population, while Cox’s Bazar’s rate has declined rapidly from a much higher peak. PPH remains a leading cause of death in both settings, but in Mae La/SMRU, a functioning quality improvement system (ALSO/BLSO training) has been associated with a measurable reduction in PPH-specific mortality [22](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8204190). In Cox's Bazar, the persistent delay in seeking care (19-hour home trial) is a dominant driver of mortality.

### 3.4 Neonatal Mortality

- **Cox's Bazar:** The neonatal mortality rate (NMR) was **27 per 1,000 live births** in 2018. The 2023 Health Sector Bulletin reported **25.2 per 1,000** [23].
- **Mae La:** The NMR in Mae La declined by 51% from **21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births** after the introduction of a Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) [24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3749980). A study of the Malteser camps documented a 62% decline, reaching **7.7 per 1,000 live births** [13](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/primary-health-care-research-and-development/article/mother-child-and-adolescent-health-outcomes-in-two-longterm-refugee-camp-settings-at-the-thaimyanmar-border-20002018-a-retrospective-analysis/5A2A03E5B33226D61F84A028BB229992).

## Section 4: Systematic Application of the Three Delays Model

This section applies the Three Delays Model systematically to both contexts, providing a structured comparison of barriers to accessing and receiving adequate care.

### 4.1 Delay 1: Deciding to Seek Care

| Factor | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **TBA Preference** | 74% of maternal deaths in 2024 involved a home trial, with a 19-hour average delay in referral. Trust in TBAs is higher than in formally trained midwives [9]. | Preference for home births with TBAs is present, but >90% attend ANC and 80% deliver with an SBA [13]. TBAs are integrated into the health system as referral agents. |
| **Trust in System** | Significant mistrust of health facilities is rooted in past trauma from Myanmar. Rumors and fear of medical interventions like C-sections are common [25](https://scienceportal.msf.org/assets/abstract-why-mothers-give-birth-home-exploration-rohingya-refugees-perceptions-experiences-expectations-regarding-maternity-services-coxs-bazar). | Higher trust in the long-established camp health system. SMRU has built trust over decades by employing local refugee staff and culturally adapting its services [15]. |
| **Decision-Making** | Heavily collective; decisions are made by husbands and in-laws. Gender norms and patriarchy restrict women's autonomy in seeking care [25]. | More individual autonomy, with established family planning programs and community education. |
| **Knowledge of Danger Signs** | 2021 MPMSR data shows 31.6% of maternal deaths were due to a preference for informal care and 21.5% due to poor knowledge of danger signs [17]. | Higher health literacy, attributed to long-term community health worker programs and education. |

**Key Insight:** Delay 1 is the *dominant* driver of preventable maternal death in Cox's Bazar, characterized by a preference for home trials and profound mistrust of a relatively new health system. In Mae La, this delay is less significant due to a mature, trusted system that has successfully integrated TBAs.

### 4.2 Delay 2: Reaching Care

| Factor | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Topography & Infrastructure** | Camps are overcrowded with poor road infrastructure. Ambulances cannot access many areas, and women are often carried out on makeshift stretchers. Monsoon rains worsen conditions [7]. | Mae La is located in a remote area. Emergency referrals to the nearest hospital (Tha Song Yang, 57km away) can take 3.5–7 hours during the rainy season due to landslides [13]. |
| **Movement Restrictions** | Government-imposed restrictions, police checkpoints, and a surrounding wire fence severely limit freedom of movement, especially at night, creating a major barrier to accessing emergency care [26](https://www.fortifyrights.org/bgd-inv-2026-02-11). | Refugees face severe restrictions on movement from the camp, making it difficult to travel for work or to seek care outside the camp without authorization [27](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/08/11/thailand-aid-cuts-put-myanmar-refugees-at-grave-risk). |
| **Referral System** | Weak referral mechanisms with a "six-hour referral journey" documented. In-camp CEmONC was extremely limited until 2025-2026 [28](https://www.devex.com/news/q-a-nothing-could-have-prepared-me-for-cox-s-bazar-reproductive-health-expert-says-91949). | A Memorandum of Agreement exists between NGOs and Thai hospitals for secondary care. C-sections are not performed in-camp, requiring referral [15]. |
| **Geographic Distance** | Variable, with some camps being very close to facilities, while others are far. Disparities exist between camps near the main hospital and those on the periphery. | Predictable distance (57km to Tha Song Yang), but the time varies greatly with season and road conditions. |

**Key Insight:** In Cox's Bazar, Delay 2 is severe and unpredictable due to a combination of poor terrain and restrictive government controls. In Mae La, the distance is predictable but dangerously long, particularly for emergency C-sections which cannot be performed in the camp.

### 4.3 Delay 3: Receiving Adequate Care

| Factor | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **In-Camp CEmONC** | Critically limited. Until 2025-2026, only one facility (Hope Field Hospital) provided CEmONC. The opening of IOM CEmONC (Aug 2025) and Save the Children Hospital (Jan 2026) is a major step forward [29](https://www.medangel.org/maternal-hospital-rohingya-refugee-camps). | **No in-camp CEmONC capability.** All C-sections and complex emergencies must be referred to Thai hospitals [15]. |
| **Blood Transfusion** | Historically a major gap. Only 7% of HPs met a minimum midwife staffing threshold and none had an effective blood transfusion mechanism [9]. WHO is working to establish blood banks [30](https://www.who.int/bangladesh/news/detail/29-06-2021-laying-the-foundations-for-decentralized-blood-transfusion-services-in). | Blood transfusion services are available at the referral Thai hospitals, but not in the camp itself. |
| **Staffing & Quality** | High staff turnover (doctors leaving for government jobs) and a shortage of midwives (only 51% of required SBAs available). The 2024 MPMSR noted 45.1% of deaths involved poor quality care at the facility (Delay 3) [17]. | More stable staff. Long-term training (e.g., ALSO/BLSO) has demonstrably improved outcomes, with PPH-related mortality halving after its introduction [22]. |
| **C-Section Rate** | Reported as **5.7% in 2023** and **8.2% in 2024** [11]. | Extremely low at **3.7%** on average in camps, as it requires external referral [13]. |

**Key Insight:** In Cox's Bazar, Delay 3 was historically the most critical due to a lack of CEmONC. This is now improving, but the quality of care and staff retention remain challenges. In Mae La, the complete absence of in-camp C-sections represents the single greatest structural weakness and a critical failure for reducing Delay 3 for emergency cases.

## Section 5: Quality of Care (QoC) Indicators

The original report focused on coverage (quantity) but lacked a systematic analysis of *quality*. This section addresses that gap.

### 5.1 Respectful Maternity Care (RMC)

- **Cox's Bazar:** An MSF qualitative study in Camp 22 documented specific barriers to RMC: concerns about male staff, fear of being undressed, fear of unnecessary medical procedures, and a lack of cultural understanding by facility-based staff [25](https://scienceportal.msf.org/assets/abstract-why-mothers-give-birth-home-exploration-rohingya-refugees-perceptions-experiences-expectations-regarding-maternity-services-coxs-bazar). Despite these issues, a 2023-2024 patient satisfaction survey of outpatient services found a **high overall satisfaction rate of 90.37%** [31].
- **Mae La:** An evaluation of intrapartum care using the WHO Safe Motherhood Needs Assessment found that while staff followed protocols for most complications, they were **"slow to recognize severity and take action"** in cases of PPH [14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-015-0444-0). The assessment also flagged a **49% episiotomy rate in nulliparous women** and low newborn monitoring rates (30%) [14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-015-0444-0).

### 5.2 Content of ANC

- **Cox's Bazar:** The SRH-QIAS initiative revealed poor adherence to clinical standards initially. Only **2% of HPs used standardized MCH cards** in 2024, although this improved to 87% by 2025 [9](https://bangladesh.unfpa.org/sites/default/files/pub-pdf/2025-08/MPMSR%20Annual%20Report%202024%20Final.docx.pdf).
- **Mae La:** Scores for essential elements of ANC from maternal records were **>90%** (e.g., providing supplements, recording risk factors, correct partograph use) [14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-015-0444-0).

## Section 6: The Impact of the 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction and 2025 US Aid Cuts

This section corrects and deepens the previous report's analysis of funding impacts on Mae La. The original report incorrectly attributed the crisis to the 2021 UNFPA cuts; the primary driver was the 2025 US aid suspension.

### 6.1 The 2021 UNFPA Cuts vs. the 2025 US Aid Crisis

- **On Mae La:** The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction had a *limited direct impact* on maternal health services in Mae La. UNFPA's partnership with PPAT continued throughout 2021-2023. The devastating blow came from the **2025 U.S. government aid freeze and subsequent 83% cut to programs**, which directly affected the **International Rescue Committee (IRC)** —the primary healthcare provider in the camps—and **The Border Consortium (TBC)** —which managed food aid [27](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/08/11/thailand-aid-cuts-put-myanmar-refugees-at-grave-risk).
- **On Cox's Bazar:** The 2021 UNFPA cuts had a more direct and immediate impact, threatening the deployment of midwives and essential SRH supplies. This was compounded by the 2025 US aid cuts, which **forced the closure of at least 11 health centers** and schools, and reduced specialist care [32](https://reliefweb.int/report/bangladesh/rohingya-refugees-suffer-global-aid-cuts-slash-essential-services).

### 6.2 Cascading Effects of the 2025 Crisis in Mae La

In January 2025, the IRC suspended all health services in Mae La, Umpiem, and Nu Po camps, announcing they could no longer treat pregnant women or critically ill patients [33](https://www.thestar.com.my/aseanplus/aseanplus-news/2025/01/29/hospitals-in-thai-refugee-camps-shut-as-us-suspends-aid). This led to:
- **Closure of IRC-run hospitals**, forcing patients to seek care in Thai hospitals, which were already overburdened.
- **Severe food aid reductions**, with monthly allowances cut to roughly US$2.30 per adult [27](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/08/11/thailand-aid-cuts-put-myanmar-refugees-at-grave-risk).
- **Increased risks for pregnant women**, as the absence of nearby emergency obstetric care forced women to travel longer distances or forego care, leading to reports of preventable maternal deaths [34].
- **Government Response:** In August 2025, the Tak Province Public Health Department stepped in, announcing monthly financial support of **2,500 THB each to 80 health workers** in Mae La and surrounding camps to try and revive basic services [35](https://karennews.org/2025/08/thai-authorities-to-provide-financial-support-for-health-workers-in-border-refugee-camps).

## Section 7: Demographic Confounders and Conflict Context

This section addresses the critical gap of not accounting for demographic differences and the distinct conflict contexts that shape health-seeking behavior.

### 7.1 Demographic Profiles and Confounders

| Indicator | Rohingya (Cox's Bazar) | Karen (Mae La/SMRU) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Teen Pregnancy** | Declined from 18.4% (2018) to **7% (2024)** [11]. | **16.3% of pregnancies** are in 15-19-year-olds in the broader SMRU cohort [36](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6039938). |
| **High Parity** | **25% of women have 6+ children**, a major risk factor for PPH and other complications [37]. | Lower parity, influenced by longer exposure to family planning programs [38](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5322876). |
| **Female Literacy** | Extremely low at **~25%** . A key predictor of health-seeking behavior [37]. | Literacy is a strong protective factor; illiterate women have **2.35x higher odds of repeat pregnancy** [36]. |
| **Nutritional Status** | Double burden: **7.6% underweight** and **~30% overweight/obese** [39]. Mean height not stated. | **1 in 7 refugees are underweight** (low BMI). Mean height is **151.4 cm**, a risk factor for obstructed labor [20]. |

**Key Insight for Comparative Analysis:** The higher parity in the Rohingya population likely contributes to their higher PPH-related mortality, as does the lower female literacy and lower health literacy. Any direct comparison of MMR between the two populations must account for these significant demographic differences.

### 7.2 How Conflict Context Shapes Health-Seeking Behavior

- **Rohingya (Genocide and Systematic Sexual Violence):** The genocide in Myanmar, which included the **use of rape as a "systematic campaign of sexual violence" by the military** [40](https://news.un.org/en/story/2018/05/1008922), has created a profound and deep-seated mistrust of any uniformed authority or institution. This trauma is a primary driver of Delay 1, as women fear that entering a health facility (often associated with the Myanmar military or government) could lead to harm. This is compounded by their statelessness in Bangladesh, with restricted movement, no right to work, and living under a government of Bangladesh military oversight, which reinforces a sense of threat and lack of autonomy.
- **Karen (Protracted Displacement):** While the Karen have also faced severe ethnopolitical conflict and military oppression, their displacement is protracted, lasting over 40 years. They have been able to maintain a high degree of **cultural and administrative autonomy** within the camps, with their own governance structures (KRC, KWO) and health system (SMRU). This continuity has allowed for the development of a health system that is culturally adapted and trusted. Health-seeking behavior is shaped more by a sense of "dependency" and restriction from the Thai state than by an existential fear of authorities within the camps [41](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10308072).

## Section 8: Policy Recommendations

Based on the revised analysis, here are actionable, evidence-based policy recommendations for each setting.

### 8.1 For Cox's Bazar (Rohingya Response)

1.  **Systematically Train and Integrate TBAs:** The 2024 MPMSR data showing 74% of deaths involving a TBA home trial is a clear mandate. Funding should be secured for a large-scale, standardized program to train all 1,200+ TBAs as non-clinical referral agents and health educators, modeled on the successful CPI project which increased facility births from 33% to 95% in its catchment area [8](https://cpintl.org/impact-area/health/training-rohingya-traditional-birth-attendants-to-support-safe-births).
2.  **Mandate the Use of MCH Cards and Partographs:** The SRH-QIAS showed that simple interventions can drastically improve quality. The program must expand to all 33 camps, mandating the use of standardized MCH cards and partographs, with monthly audits to track progress.
3.  **Implement a "No Refusal" Referral Protocol for CEmONC:** The new IOM and Save the Children hospitals provide a platform for this. A clear, enforced protocol must ensure that women experiencing complications are referred directly and immediately to these facilities, bypassing lower-level HPs, to reduce the documented "six-hour referral journeys."
4.  **Scale-Up Community-Based Blood Transfusion:** Given PPH is the leading cause of death, the WHO's work on establishing blood banks must be accelerated. Mobile blood donation drives and point-of-care testing for hemoglobin in HPs should be prioritized.
5.  **Address Movement Restrictions:** A collaboration between humanitarian actors and the Government of Bangladesh is needed to exempt pregnant women in labor or those with obstetric emergencies from movement restrictions, police checkpoints, and nighttime curfews.

### 8.2 For Mae La Camp (Karen Response)

1.  **Establish In-Camp Capacity for Emergency C-Sections:** The complete absence of CEmONC is a critical, decades-old gap. A dedicated operating theater within the camp, run by a trained government-designated team, is the single most impactful intervention to reduce maternal mortality. This would eliminate the dangerous 57km referral for time-sensitive emergencies.
2.  **Secure a Sustainable Funding Mechanism:** The crisis of 2025 demonstrates a catastrophic over-reliance on a single donor (the US). The Thai government, in partnership with international donors like the EU and Japan, should establish a multi-year, pooled funding mechanism to ensure the stability of the health system.
3.  **Strengthen and Systematize TBA Integration:** While TBAs are already integrated, a formal, standardized curriculum for their training in danger sign recognition and linkage to care, along with a small stipend, would formalize their role and increase its effectiveness.
4.  **Address the High Episiotomy Rate:** The 49% rate identified in the quality of care study suggests a need for refresher training on evidence-based perineal management to reduce unnecessary interventions and improve the quality of respectful care [14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-015-0444-0).

## Section 9: Conclusion

This revised comparative analysis demonstrates that while Cox's Bazar and Mae La face similar challenges in providing maternal health to refugee populations, the nature, severity, and root causes of these challenges differ significantly based on the conflict context, health system maturity, and demographic profiles. Cox's Bazar is an emergency response system pioneering rapid scale-up, where the primary challenge is **building trust to reduce Delay 1** (deciding to seek care) and improving quality of care (Delay 3). Mae La is a mature, protracted system where the primary challenge is **addressing a single, critical health system gap** (Delay 3 - no in-camp C-sections) and **ensuring long-term funding stability**.

The revision highlights that the 93% SBA figure, previously cited, was a misleading global metric. The true SBA rate in Cox's Bazar is approximately 69-80%, while in Mae La it is 95%. Furthermore, the 2021 UNFPA cuts had a limited direct effect on Mae La, whereas the 2025 US aid suspension was catastrophic for both settings but especially for the border camps where it caused a near-total collapse of the health system.

Ultimately, the lessons from Mae La—the value of long-term investment, community integration, and refugee-led governance—provide a blueprint for Cox's Bazar. Conversely, the recent improvements in Cox's Bazar’s CEmONC coverage provide a model for Mae La to finally address its most critical gap. The future of maternal health in both settings depends not just on more data, but on translating that data into targeted, system-level changes that address the specific "delays" that cause mothers to die.
