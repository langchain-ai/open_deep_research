# Independent Directors Under NASDAQ Listing Standards: A Comprehensive Revised Report

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the independent director standards under the NASDAQ listing rules, reflecting the latest amendments and regulatory developments through May 2026. The analysis is based on primary sources, including NASDAQ Listing Rules in the 5600 Series (particularly Rule 5605 and related rules), SEC guidance and rulemakings, and reputable legal analyses. The report is structured to provide a practical, detailed reference for understanding and applying NASDAQ's independent director requirements.

---

## Section 1: Definition of an Independent Director Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 1.1 Core Definition

Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2), an "independent director" is defined through a two-part test that combines objective and subjective elements. The rule states that an independent director is a person who:

1. **Is not an executive officer or employee of the company**, and
2. **Does not have a relationship** which, in the opinion of the board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director [1][2][8].

This definition establishes the foundational framework for all independence determinations under NASDAQ rules. The rule text frames independence around the core concept of whether any relationship—whether financial, familial, professional, or otherwise—would interfere with the director's ability to exercise independent judgment [2][8].

### 1.2 The Two-Part Test: Objective Bright-Line Criteria and Subjective Board Determination

The independence determination involves two distinct and sequential components that must both be satisfied:

**Part A — Objective (Bright-Line) Criteria:** The board must first determine whether the director falls within any of the specific disqualifying relationships or thresholds enumerated in NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2). These include employment relationships, compensation thresholds, business relationships, auditor affiliations, and interlocking directorships. If any such disqualifying relationship exists, the director is automatically precluded from being considered independent. These are per se bars to independence that do not permit board override [1][4][9].

**Part B — Subjective Affirmative Determination:** Even if no bright-line disqualifiers apply, the board must affirmatively determine that the director has no material relationship with the listed company that would interfere with the director's exercise of independent judgment. This affirmative finding must be documented in board resolutions and disclosed in proxy statements or SEC filings [2][4][8].

The board's judgment is the ultimate authority—a director is independent only if the board concludes, in its business judgment, that no relationship exists that would interfere with independent judgment. Meeting the objective rule criteria does not automatically make a director independent; the board must still make the affirmative determination. Ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board finding of independence, but the board must consider all relevant facts and circumstances [1][6].

### 1.3 The Materiality Standard in the Subjective Determination

In assessing whether a "material relationship" exists that would interfere with independent judgment, NASDAQ guidance indicates that the board should consider all relevant facts and circumstances, including:

- **Stock ownership:** Large ownership positions, especially when combined with other relationships, require careful assessment of the totality of circumstances [1][2][4]
- **Family relationships:** Any familial relationship that could create a sense of obligation or loyalty must be evaluated, even if not covered by the bright-line definition of "family member" [4][5][9]
- **Business relationships:** Direct or indirect business relationships between the director (or an affiliate of the director) and the company or its affiliates [9]
- **Professional relationships:** Service as legal counsel, consultant, or other professional service provider to the company [2][4]
- **Charitable relationships:** Significant charitable contributions by the company to an organization with which the director is affiliated may be considered [9]
- **Past relationships:** The board considers both current and past relationships, with specific lookback periods codified in the bright-line rules [4][9]

### 1.4 The 2020 Amendment to the Definition of "Family Member"

On February 13, 2020, the SEC granted accelerated approval to NASDAQ's amended proposal to modify the definition of "family member" in Rule 5605(a)(2) [10][11][12]. Prior to this amendment, the definition included "children...by marriage," which inadvertently encompassed stepchildren. NASDAQ believed this was over-inclusive because it could capture attenuated relationships, such as where a director marries a person who has an adult child with whom the director has never acted in any capacity as a parent [11][14].

**The post-2020 definition of "Family Member" means a person's:**

- Spouse
- Parents
- Children (excluding stepchildren unless they share the director's home)
- Siblings
- Mothers-in-law and fathers-in-law
- Sons-in-law and daughters-in-law
- Brothers-in-law and sisters-in-law
- Anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home [10][11][12]

**Key Changes from the 2020 Amendment:**

- **Exclusion of stepchildren who do not share the director's home:** After the amendment, a stepchild living in the director's home is considered a family member through the "anyone who shares such person's home" catch-all provision, but an adult stepchild not living with the director does not automatically disqualify independence [11]
- **Exclusion of domestic employees:** Domestic employees who share a director's home are excluded because those relationships are typically commercial, not familial [9][11][12][14]
- **Alignment with NYSE:** Following the amendment, NASDAQ's definition became identical to the NYSE's definition of "immediate family member," which the SEC had previously approved [11][12][14]

**Important Note on Board Discretion:** Despite the amendment, the board must still review and consider the relationship between a director and any family member, including stepchildren, in determining whether the relationship would interfere with the director's ability to exercise independent judgment. Boards should continue to ask questions about stepchild relationships in their D&O questionnaires and make independence judgments based on the facts and circumstances of each unique relationship [10][12].

The 2020 amendment does not affect the additional, stricter independence criteria for audit committee members under Exchange Act Rule 10A-3 and NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2) [9][10][12].

## Section 2: Eligibility Criteria — Who Qualifies as an Independent Director

### 2.1 The Board's Affirmative Determination Process

NASDAQ expects a robust board process for making independence determinations. This typically includes:

- **Annual director and officer questionnaires** to gather information about relationships and affiliations
- **A relationship inventory** cataloging all direct and indirect relationships between directors and the company
- **A committee overlay review** assessing additional criteria for audit, compensation, and nominating committee members
- **A board resolution** reflecting the affirmative determinations for each director

These processes must comply with Regulation S-K Item 407(a) for proxy disclosure purposes [1][2][4].

### 2.2 Board Process Requirements

The board must make an affirmative finding that the director has no relationship which, in the board's opinion, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment. This is not a mere checklist compliance exercise—the board must affirmatively determine independence and document the consideration of all relevant relationships [1][6].

Companies must disclose which directors are independent and the basis for any board determination that a director with a relationship exceeding the bright-line thresholds is nonetheless independent. The board's independence determinations must be documented and disclosed in the company's proxy statement or annual report on Form 10-K [1][2].

### 2.3 Factors Considered in Materiality Assessments

In the subjective component of the independence determination, the board should consider all relevant facts and circumstances, including:

**Current and Past Relationships:** The board considers both current and past relationships between the director (or an affiliate of the director) and the company, its management, or its auditors. This includes any compensation or benefits received beyond standard director fees [1][2][8].

**Business and Professional Relationships:** Any material business, professional, personal, or family relationships must be evaluated. This includes service as legal counsel, consultant, or other professional service provider to the company [2][4].

**Affiliate Relationships:** The board must consider any affiliations with organizations that have business relationships with the company, including charitable organizations to which the company makes significant contributions [9].

**Stock Ownership:** Ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board finding of independence. However, when large ownership positions are combined with other relationships, the board must consider the totality of the circumstances [1][2][4].

**Soft Factors:** In an October 2024 enforcement action (SEC v. Craigie), the SEC targeted a former CEO and director who did not disclose a close personal friendship with a company executive that resulted in misleading proxy disclosures concerning his independence. Delaware courts have similarly looked past traditional financial thresholds and into softer factors such as close personal friendships or other relationships that are of a "bias-producing nature" [3][10].

### 2.4 Independence Is Not a Fixed Status

Independence is neither a fixed condition nor a universal status for all purposes. Events and relationships can disqualify an otherwise independent director from participating in decisions. A director who qualifies as independent to sit on an audit or compensation committee may not be deemed so when it comes to approving a transaction with an insider or assessing a shareholder demand to bring litigation claims against management [3].

A formal review of every director's independence should be conducted annually in connection with the preparation of the proxy statement [3][10].

## Section 3: Disqualification Criteria — Specific Relationships That Preclude Independence

Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2), the following specific relationships disqualify a director from being considered independent. Unless otherwise noted, the lookback period is three years preceding the determination of independence [3][4][9].

### 3.1 Employment Relationships — Rule 5605(a)(2)(A)

A director cannot be considered independent if:

- The director is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the company (including by any parent or subsidiary of the company) [3][4][9]
- The director is a family member of an individual who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the company as an executive officer [3][4][9]

The specific rule text states: "a director is not considered to be independent if he or she also is an executive officer or employee of the corporation" [13]. Limited exceptions exist for certain short-term interim roles [8].

### 3.2 Compensation Threshold — Rule 5605(a)(2)(B)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director (or a family member of the director) accepted any compensation from the company in excess of $120,000 during any period of 12 consecutive months within the three years preceding the independence determination [3][4][9][13].

The rule text provides: "a director is not consider[ed] independent if he or she accepted any compensation from our Company in excess of $120,000 during any period of twelve consecutive months within the three years preceding the determination of independence" [13].

**Exceptions to the $120,000 Threshold Include:**

- Director and committee fees (including reasonable director compensation and reimbursement for board-related expenses) are expressly excluded from this calculation [3][9]
- Compensation paid to a family member who is a non-executive officer employee of the company is excluded [9]
- Certain pension or deferred compensation payments that are not contingent on continued service may be excluded [8][9]
- Rule 10A-3 imposes even stricter compensation restrictions for audit committee members, prohibiting any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company or its subsidiaries (not just amounts over $120,000) [1][2][4][7]

### 3.3 Business Relationships — Rule 5605(a)(2)(D)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director is, or has a family member who is, a partner in, controlling shareholder of, or executive officer of, any organization to which the listed company made, or from which the listed company received, payments in excess of the greater of 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues or $200,000 in the company's most recently completed fiscal year [3][9][13].

The exact text from the rule states: "a director is not considered to be independent if he or she is a partner in, or a controlling shareholder or an executive officer of, any organization to which our Company made, or from which our Company received, payments for property or services in the current or any of the past three fiscal years that exceed 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues for that year, or $200,000" [13].

This disqualification covers payments to or from any organization where the director (or family member) serves as a partner, controlling shareholder, or executive officer [3][4][9].

### 3.4 Auditor Affiliation — Rule 5605(c)(2)(iv)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director (or a family member of the director) is:

- A current partner or employee of the company's outside auditor, or
- Was a partner or employee of the outside auditor who worked on the company's audit at any time during the past three years [3][4][9]

### 3.5 Interlocking Directorships / Compensation Committee Relationships — Rule 5605(c)(2)(v)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director is, or has a family member who is, an executive officer of another entity where any of the listed company's executive officers serve on that entity's compensation committee. This is the so-called "interlocking directorate" disqualifier [1][8].

The specific rule provides: A director is not independent if he or she is, or has a family member who is, an executive officer of another entity where any of the company's executive officers serve on that entity's compensation committee [1][8].

### 3.6 Audit Committee-Specific Disqualifications — Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) and SEC Rule 10A-3

For audit committee membership, NASDAQ imposes additional, more stringent independence requirements beyond the general director independence criteria. Under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3 [1][2][4][7]:

- Audit committee members must meet both the NASDAQ independence standards under Rule 5605(a)(2) and the SEC's audit committee independence criteria under Rule 10A-3 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 [2][4][7]
- Under SEC Rule 10A-3, audit committee members cannot accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company or its subsidiaries (other than director or committee fees) [1][2][4][7]
- Audit committee members cannot be an affiliated person of the company or any of its subsidiaries (subject to certain exceptions) [1][4][7]
- NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) specifically requires the audit committee to have at least three members, each of whom must be an independent director meeting both the NASDAQ and SEC independence standards [1][9][11]

### 3.7 Summary of Rules Applicable to Family Members

The following table summarizes how the family member definition applies across all disqualification categories:

| Disqualification Category | Family Member Application | Rule Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Employment | Family member of executive officer employed in past 3 years | Rule 5605(a)(2)(A) |
| Compensation | Family member accepted >$120,000 in any 12 months in past 3 years | Rule 5605(a)(2)(B) |
| Business Relationships | Family member is partner, controlling shareholder, or executive officer of organization with payments >5% gross revenues or $200,000 | Rule 5605(a)(2)(D) |
| Auditor Affiliation | Family member is partner/employee of auditor or worked on audit in past 3 years | Rule 5605(c)(2)(iv) |
| Interlocking Directorships | Family member is executive officer of entity where company executives serve on compensation committee | Rule 5605(c)(2)(v) |

## Section 4: Types of Companies Required to Have Independent Directors and Composition Requirements

### 4.1 Majority Independent Board Requirement — Rule 5605(b)(1)

Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(b)(1), a majority of the board of directors must be comprised of Independent Directors as defined in Rule 5605(a)(2) [9][11][13]. This requirement applies generally to all NASDAQ-listed companies, with specific exemptions and phase-in provisions described below [9][10][12].

The board must affirmatively determine independence for each director, and meeting the bright-line rule criteria does not automatically make a director independent—the board must document the consideration of all relevant relationships [11].

### 4.2 Controlled Companies — Rule 5615(c)

**Definition:** A "Controlled Company" is a company of which more than 50% of the voting power for the election of directors is held by an individual, a group, or another company [10][12][13].

**Exemption:** Under Rule 5615(c), controlled companies are exempt from the requirements of:
- Rule 5605(b) (majority independent board)
- Rule 5605(d) (compensation committee independence)
- Rule 5605(e) (nominating committee independence) [8][9][10][12]

**Limitations of the Exemption:**
- Controlled companies are not exempt from the audit committee requirements under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3. The audit committee must still be fully independent [8][9][10]
- Controlled companies must still hold executive sessions of independent directors (meetings at which only independent directors are present) [9][10]
- Controlled companies must disclose their controlled company status in their SEC filings [9][10]
- When a company ceases to be a controlled company, it must comply with audit committee requirements immediately and has a phase-in period for board and committee independence beginning on the date it ceases controlled status [9][10]

### 4.3 Foreign Private Issuers — Rule 5615(a)(3)

Under NASDAQ Rule 5615(a)(3), a foreign private issuer is permitted to follow its home country practice in lieu of most NASDAQ corporate governance requirements, including the independent director requirements under Rule 5605 [9][10][12].

**Requirements for Use of Home Country Exemption:**
- The foreign private issuer must disclose in its SEC filings (typically on Form 20-F) each requirement of NASDAQ that it does not follow and the home country practice it follows instead [9][10]
- The exemption applies to board independence requirements, committee composition requirements, and most other governance provisions in the 5600 Series [9][10]
- Audit committee requirements under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3 cannot be avoided through the home country practice exemption. Foreign private issuers must still have an audit committee that meets Rule 10A-3 independence standards, though the SEC allows certain accommodations for foreign private issuers (e.g., employee representatives on audit committees if required by home country law) [8][9][10]
- If a foreign private issuer ceases to qualify as a foreign private issuer, it has a six-month phase-in period to comply with domestic company requirements (except that it must immediately comply with audit committee independence requirements upon losing such status) [9][10][12]

### 4.4 Smaller Reporting Companies and Emerging Growth Companies

**Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs):**
- Under NASDAQ rules, smaller reporting companies are not granted any special exemption from the independent director or committee composition requirements under Rule 5605 [9]
- SRCs may be eligible for certain phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) for transitioning from other listing standards [9]
- SRCs benefit from certain SEC accommodations (e.g., scaled disclosure requirements under Regulation S-K and Regulation S-X), but these do not affect the NASDAQ independence rules [9]
- SRCs receive some relief on compensation committee requirements [6]

**Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs):**
- Under the JOBS Act, EGCs are permitted to delay compliance with certain new or revised accounting standards, but this does not provide an exemption from the independent director requirements under NASDAQ Rule 5605 [9]
- EGCs do not receive any special exemption from the majority independent board requirement or the independent committee requirements under NASDAQ rules [9]
- EGCs are subject to the same phase-in periods as other IPO companies under Rule 5615(b) [9][12]

### 4.5 IPO and Initial Listing Phase-In Periods — Rule 5615(b)

Companies listing in connection with an initial public offering (IPO) or certain other initial listing events are granted the following phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) [9][10][12]:

**Majority Independent Board:**
- 12 months from the listing date to comply with the majority independent board requirement under Rule 5605(b)(1) [10][12][13]

**Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c)):**
- By the listing date: at least one member on the audit committee meeting the independence and financial sophistication requirements [10][12]
- Within 90 days of the listing date: at least two members meeting the requirements [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: the full three-member audit committee meeting all independence and financial expertise requirements [10][12]
- No phase-in or exemption applies to certain audit committee independence requirements under SEC Rule 10A-3 — those must be met by the listing date [10][12]

**Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d)):**
- By the listing date (or within five business days of the listing date): at least one independent member [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: full compliance with the independent compensation committee requirement (at least two independent members) [10][12]

**Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e)):**
- By the listing date (or within five business days of the listing date): at least one independent member [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: full compliance with the independent nominating committee requirement [10][12]

**Companies Listing Through Carve-Out or Spin-Off Transactions:**
- These companies receive the same phase-in schedule as IPO companies under Rule 5615(b)(4) [10][12]

**Companies Emerging from Bankruptcy:**
- Must comply with audit committee requirements by the listing date unless exempt under Rule 10A-3 [10][12]
- Receive phase-in schedules for other governance requirements similar to IPO companies [10][12]

**Companies Transferring from Another Exchange:**
- Subject to the applicable phase-in provisions under Rule 5615(b) depending on their circumstances [10][12]

**Companies Listing Securities Registered Under Section 12(g):**
- Have adjusted phase-in periods similar to IPOs, except for audit committee size [5][10]

### 4.6 Cure Periods

For companies that become noncompliant with the independent director or committee requirements due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control (e.g., death, resignation, or disqualification of a director), NASDAQ provides cure periods [9][10][12]:

- Generally, the company has until the earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year from the event causing noncompliance to regain compliance [9][10]
- If the next annual meeting is within 180 days, the company has at least 180 days to comply [10]
- **Important:** A company relying on a phase-in period under Rule 5615(b) is not eligible for a cure period immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period — unless the company demonstrated compliance during the phase-in period but fell out of compliance before its expiration [10][12]

**Notification Requirements:** Companies must notify NASDAQ promptly if noncompliance with Listing Rule 5600 Series occurs, especially when invoking cure periods to regain compliance [10].

### 4.7 Committee Composition Requirements

#### 4.7.1 Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)(3)

- Must consist of at least three members [1][9][11]
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2) [9][11]
- Each member must also meet the heightened independence requirements of SEC Rule 10A-3 (no consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company, and no affiliation with the company or subsidiaries) [1][2][4][7]
- Each member must be financially sophisticated (able to read and understand basic financial statements) [9][11]
- At least one member must be an "audit committee financial expert" as defined by SEC rules [9][11]
- The committee must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities [9][11]
- The audit committee is directly responsible for the appointment, compensation, and oversight of the independent auditor [7][9][11]
- The committee must establish procedures for receiving and handling complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, and auditing matters [7][9]

#### 4.7.2 Compensation Committee — Rule 5605(d)(2)

- Must consist of at least two members [8][9][11]
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2) [9][11]
- Members may not accept consulting or advisory fees from the company (or receive any other compensatory fees beyond director compensation) [8][9][11]
- The committee must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities, including the authority to engage compensation consultants, independent legal counsel, and other advisers [9][11]
- Before selecting or receiving advice from a compensation consultant, the committee must consider the independence of that consultant under applicable NASDAQ and SEC standards, including six specific factors: (i) services provided to the company, (ii) fee proportions, (iii) conflict-prevention policies, (iv) relationships with committee members, (v) relationships with executives, and (vi) stock ownership [9][11][14]

**Limited Exception:** Under Rule 5605(d)(2)(B), one non-independent director may serve on the compensation committee for up to two years under exceptional and limited circumstances (e.g., death or disability of an existing member), provided the board determines it is in the best interests of the company and shareholders [8][10].

#### 4.7.3 Nominating Committee — Rule 5605(e)(2)

- Must consist entirely of Independent Directors (or alternatively, independent directors must constitute a majority of the board and must select nominees through a board process, even if a formal nominating committee does not exist) [8][9][11]
- The committee (or the independent directors acting as a group) must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities [9][11]
- The committee is responsible for identifying and recommending candidates for director nomination [9][11]
- The committee reviews the composition of and evaluates the performance of the Board, recommends persons for election to the Board, and evaluates director compensation [18]

**Limited Exception:** Under Rule 5605(e)(3), one non-independent director may serve on the nominating committee for up to two years under exceptional and limited circumstances, provided the board determines it is in the best interests of the company and shareholders [8][10].

### 4.8 Summary Table of Company Types and Applicability

| Company Type | Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)(1)) | Audit Committee Independence (Rule 5605(c)/10A-3) | Compensation Committee Independence (Rule 5605(d)) | Nominating Committee Independence (Rule 5605(e)) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| All listed companies generally | Required | Required (3 members, heightened independence) | Required (2 members) | Required (or process by independent directors) |
| Controlled companies (Rule 5615(c)) | Exempt | Required (no exemption) | Exempt | Exempt |

| Smaller reporting companies | Required | Required | Required | Required |

| IPO companies (Rule 5615(b)) | Phase-in: 12 months | Phase-in: up to 1 year | Phase-in: up to 1 year | Phase-in: up to 1 year |

## Section 5: Key Regulatory Developments

### 5.1 2024 Amendments to Phase-In and Cure Periods (Rules 5605, 5615, 5810)

On August 26, 2024, the SEC issued an order approving amendments to NASDAQ rules related to phase-in periods and cure periods [1][2][3][4]. The SEC order was published in the Federal Register on August 30, 2024 [6]. These amendments primarily clarify and codify existing policies rather than introduce new exemptions, and they modify NASDAQ Rules 5605, 5615, and 5810 to adjust phase-in schedules for independent director and committee requirements [1][2][4].

**Key Changes from the 2024 Amendments:**

- **Codification of Phase-In Schedules:** The amendments clarified and modified phase-in schedules for independent director and committee requirements, making them substantially similar to NYSE phase-in schedules [1][2][4]

- **Clarification on Cure Periods After Phase-In:** The amendments codify NASDAQ's existing policy that a company relying on any phase-in period is not eligible for a cure period immediately following the end of the phase-in period, unless the company was in compliance with the relevant requirements but fell out of compliance before the end of the phase-in period [1][2][4]

- **Extended Phase-In to Additional Company Types:** The amendments extended phase-in schedules to companies listing through carve-out or spin-off transactions and companies listing securities previously registered under Section 12(g) [4][6][7]

- **No Change to Audit Committee Requirements:** The amendments do not grant exemptions regarding NASDAQ Rule 5605(c) or Exchange Act Rule 10A-3, requiring immediate compliance for at least one audit committee member [1][3]

- **Compensation Committee Cure Periods:** Rule 5810 was amended to describe cure period procedures if a company fails to meet the compensation committee composition requirement due to one vacancy or one member ceasing to be independent. In such cases, cure deadlines are based on the company's annual meeting schedule or one year from the event, with a minimum of 180 days [5]

**SEC's Rationale:** The SEC found the changes consistent with the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, emphasizing the importance of meaningful corporate governance standards to protect investors and promote fair, orderly markets. The Commission stated: "It is reasonable not to provide a cure period under such circumstances because the company has already had a phase-in period and failed to comply throughout that period" [4][8].

### 5.2 The 2020 Family Member Definition Amendment

As detailed in Section 1.4 above, on February 13, 2020, the SEC granted accelerated approval to NASDAQ's amended proposal to modify the definition of "family member" in Rule 5605(a)(2) [10][11][12]. The amendment:
- Excluded stepchildren from the automatic definition unless they share the director's home
- Excluded domestic employees from the definition
- Aligned NASDAQ's definition with the NYSE's definition of "immediate family member"

### 5.3 SEC Rule 10A-3 Requirements

SEC Rule 10A-3, adopted pursuant to Section 301 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (effective April 25, 2003), establishes the baseline independence requirements for audit committee members that NASDAQ must enforce through its listing standards [16][17][18].

**Key Requirements of Rule 10A-3:**

- **Audit Committee Member Independence:** Each member of the audit committee must be a member of the board of directors and must be independent according to specified criteria, including: (i) no acceptance of any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee from the issuer (other than in their capacity as a board/committee member); and (ii) no affiliation with the issuer or any subsidiary of the issuer [17][18]

- **Direct Responsibility for Independent Auditor:** The audit committee must be directly responsible for the appointment, compensation, retention, and oversight of the work of any registered public accounting firm engaged [16][17][18]

- **Complaint Procedures:** Each audit committee must establish procedures for the receipt, retention, and treatment of complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, or auditing matters, including procedures for confidential, anonymous submission by employees [16][17][18]

- **Authority to Engage Advisors:** Each audit committee must have the authority to engage independent counsel and other advisors as it determines necessary to carry out its duties [16][17][18]

- **Funding:** Each listed issuer must provide appropriate funding, as determined by the audit committee, for payment of compensation to the independent auditor and any advisors engaged by the audit committee [16][17][18]

**How NASDAQ Incorporates Rule 10A-3:**

- NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2) incorporates the independence requirements of SEC Rule 10A-3 as additional criteria for audit committee members beyond the general independence standards under Rule 5605(a)(2) [12][15]
- No phase-in is available for the core audit committee independence requirement under Rule 10A-3 [1][3][8]
- The 2020 family member definition amendments explicitly did not affect these additional independence criteria for audit committee members [9][11][12]
