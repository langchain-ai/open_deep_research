# Revised UCC Article 2 Legal Analysis: Custom Widgets Color Deviation Dispute

## Executive Summary

This revised analysis reexamines a commercial color deviation dispute under UCC Article 2 involving 1,000 custom widgets delivered in navy instead of the contractually specified charcoal gray. The widgets meet all functional specifications (load capacity, durability). The buyer installed and operated all widgets for eight weeks, painted 600 of them black, sent emails praising performance without mentioning color, and only then notified the seller of the color issue on May 10. The buyer's architect confirmed that the navy color created genuine showroom contrast issues. The contract contains a 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause. Industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. The seller offered immediate replacement within two weeks; the buyer demanded a 40% refund while retaining the widgets.

This revised analysis critically reflects on each prior legal conclusion, acknowledging counterarguments and areas of legal uncertainty, and provides a balanced assessment of both parties' positions.

---

## I. Acceptance by Conduct Under § 2-606 vs. Rejection Under the Perfect Tender Rule (§ 2-601)

### A. The Perfect Tender Rule and Its Interaction with Usage of Trade

UCC § 2-601 provides that "if the goods or the tender of delivery fail in any respect to conform to the contract, the buyer may (a) reject the whole; or (b) accept the whole; or (c) accept any commercial unit or units and reject the rest." This "perfect tender rule" gives buyers a presumptive right to reject goods for any nonconformity, no matter how trivial [1][3].

However, this right is subject to important qualifications. As the Schmitt & Frisch article explains, "there are several limitations and exceptions" that restrict the perfect tender concept, including the seller's cure rights under § 2-508 and contractual limitations under §§ 2-718 and 2-719 [5]. Critically, the right to reject is lost once the buyer accepts the goods under § 2-606.

**The relationship between § 2-601 and usage of trade under § 1-303 (formerly § 1-205) requires careful analysis.** The prior report may have oversimplified this interaction. Under UCC § 1-303, "usage of trade" means "any practice or method of dealing having such regularity of observance in a place, vocation or trade as to justify an expectation that it will be observed with respect to the transaction in question" [11][15]. Usage of trade can "give particular meaning to and supplement or qualify terms of an agreement" [11].

**The critical analytical question is whether industry practice regarding minor color variations shapes what constitutes a "conforming" tender under the contract, or whether it merely provides context for the seller's cure rights.** 

The prior report took the position that trade usage "shifts" the conformity analysis. However, the hierarchy under § 1-303(e) provides: "Express terms of an agreement prevail over course of performance, course of dealing, and usage of trade; course of performance prevails over course of dealing and usage of trade; course of dealing prevails over usage of trade" [11]. Where a contract expressly specifies a color reference number for "charcoal gray," that express term would presumptively prevail. Trade usage interpreting what "charcoal gray" means in the industry—or whether minor shade variations are acceptable—could supplement or qualify the term, but only where such construction is reasonable [11].

**Counterargument for the buyer:** The buyer can argue that the express color specification (charcoal gray) is unambiguous, and trade usage cannot override a clear express term. The buyer's architect confirmed that navy (a distinctly different color) created genuine contrast issues in the showroom, suggesting this was not a "minor variation" but a fundamentally different color that undermined the showroom's aesthetic purpose.

**Counterargument for the seller:** The seller can argue that the contract emphasized performance criteria (load capacity, durability) over aesthetic specifications. Under UCC § 1-303, the express terms and trade usage "shall be construed wherever reasonable as consistent with each other" [11]. If industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components, this practice informs what the parties intended by "charcoal gray"—perhaps permitting shades within a reasonable tolerance range.

**The academic literature supports a nuanced view.** David McGowan's article on usage of trade notes that "the very fact of commercial acceptance makes out a prima facie case that the usage is reasonable, and the burden is no longer on the usage to establish itself as being reasonable" [23]. However, McGowan also distinguishes "between reciprocal usages, developed among parties participating on both sides of transactions likely maximizing welfare, and one-sided usages, such as those favoring sellers over consumers, which deserve greater scrutiny" [23].

### B. Acceptance by Conduct Under § 2-606: Three Independent Grounds

UCC § 2-606 defines acceptance as occurring when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect: (a) signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming; (b) fails to make an effective rejection; or (c) does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership [11][12][14].

**Prong (a): Signifying Conformity.** The buyer sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color. Under § 2-606(1)(a), acceptance occurs when the buyer "after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming." This is a strong argument for acceptance.

**Counterargument for the buyer:** The buyer may argue that the emails praised *performance* (load capacity, durability) but said nothing about color. The buyer could claim they were not signifying that the goods fully conformed—only that certain functional aspects met expectations. Official Comment 3 to § 2-606 notes that "payment made after tender is always one circumstance tending to signify acceptance of the goods but in itself it can never be more than one circumstance." Similarly, praise of specific attributes may not constitute a blanket acceptance of all contract terms.

**Prong (b): Failure to Make Effective Rejection.** Under § 2-606(1)(b), acceptance occurs when the buyer "fails to make an effective rejection." Section 2-602(1) requires rejection "within a reasonable time after their delivery or tender" with "seasonable notification" to the seller [7][9].

The 8-week delay is the seller's strongest argument. The color deviation was visually apparent immediately upon delivery. The contract contained a 10-day inspection clause. The buyer waited nearly six times the contractual period.

**Counterargument for the buyer:** The buyer can argue that under UCC § 1-204(1), contractually fixed times are enforceable only if they are "not manifestly unreasonable" [16][19]. Could a 10-day inspection clause be "manifestly unreasonable" for industrial widgets where the buyer might need to install and test the widgets in the showroom environment to evaluate the aesthetic impact? The buyer's architect confirmed the color issue only became apparent when evaluated in the showroom design context—not upon mere visual inspection of the widgets in isolation.

Section 1-204 "is clearly more applicable to the problem of time clauses than section 2-302" [16]. A limitation that "renders the warranty ineffective as regards latent defects . . . is manifestly unreasonable and therefore invalid under § 1-204 of the code" [16]. While the color itself was not latent, the *aesthetic impact* on showroom design might not have been immediately apparent without installation and evaluation in the showroom context.

**Prong (c): Act Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership.** The buyer painted 600 of the 1,000 widgets black during the 8-week period. Under § 2-606(1)(c), acceptance occurs when the buyer "does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership" [11][12][14].

Painting goods materially alters them and is a clear exercise of ownership rights. The Nebraska Supreme Court held in *Johnson v. Holdrege Coop. Equity Exchange* that "commingling by a buyer of one farmer's grain with that of other farmers is an act inconsistent with the buyer's ownership and may constitute acceptance under this section" [14].

**Counterargument for the buyer:** The buyer can argue that painting the widgets was an act of *mitigation*, not ownership. The buyer could claim they discovered the color mismatch, sought to resolve it practically by painting the widgets black to match the showroom, and only later realized the scope of the problem and contacted the seller. The buyer could argue that they were preserving value for both parties by making the goods usable, rather than exercising dominion over them.

Under § 2-604, "If the seller gives no instructions within a reasonable time after notification of rejection, the buyer may store the rejected goods for the seller's account or reship them to him or resell them for the seller's account. Such action is not acceptance or conversion" [11][12]. However, this section presumes a prior rejection, which did not occur here.

### C. The 10-Day Inspection Clause: Dispositive or Qualified?

The prior report treated the 10-day inspection clause as strongly supporting acceptance. This conclusion requires refinement.

**Arguments that the clause is enforceable:** UCC § 1-204(1) provides that "any time which is not manifestly unreasonable may be fixed by agreement" [16][19]. A 10-day period for inspecting industrial widgets is presumptively reasonable for a visually apparent defect like color. The purchaser order set specific inspection and rejection periods, which "supersede default UCC timings" [25].

**Arguments that the clause may not be dispositive:**

*Course of Performance.* Under UCC § 1-303(f), "a course of performance is relevant to show a waiver or modification of any term inconsistent with the course of performance" [11][12][15]. The seller delivered widgets on March 15. The buyer installed and used them for 8 weeks. If the seller knew of (or should have known of) the buyer's use without objection, this course of performance could be relevant evidence that the parties modified or waived the strict 10-day inspection requirement.

The Mayer Brown legal update emphasizes: "When parties routinely overlook or fail to enforce their express contract terms, their conduct may inadvertently signal acceptance of such deviations, thereby modifying the terms of the contract" [12]. However, this argument is stronger for ongoing obligations (e.g., recurring payments) than for a one-time inspection period. The seller had no obligation to "enforce" the inspection clause—the buyer bore the duty to inspect and reject within 10 days.

*Manifest Unreasonableness.* The buyer could argue that 10 days was "manifestly unreasonable" for widgets that needed to be installed in a showroom environment to evaluate their aesthetic impact. The "trial use" cases (e.g., *Don's Marine, Inc. v. Haldeman*, where multiple test runs in varying weather were required for a boat) suggest that reasonable inspection time depends on the nature of the goods and the defects alleged [2]. However, this argument is weakened because the color was visually apparent upon delivery—the buyer did not need 8 weeks to notice the widgets were navy, not charcoal gray.

## II. Revocation of Acceptance Under § 2-608

### A. The Substantial Impairment Standard

UCC § 2-608 allows revocation of acceptance only where the nonconformity "substantially impairs its value to him" [7][11][13]. This is a significantly higher standard than the perfect tender rule's "any respect" test. The *Durfee v. Rod Baxter Imports, Inc.* case established seven requirements, including substantial impairment [9][15].

The prior report concluded that a color deviation (navy vs. charcoal gray) is unlikely to constitute substantial impairment. This conclusion requires more careful analysis.

**The Jorgensen v. Pressnall test:** (1) a subjective inquiry into the needs and circumstances of the particular buyer; (2) an objective inquiry requiring evidence that the nonconformity in fact substantially impaired value [28].

**Subjective prong—buyer's argument:** The buyer's architect confirmed that navy created genuine "contrast issues" in the showroom design. If the buyer's showroom was designed around a charcoal gray aesthetic, the navy widgets could undermine the entire visual presentation. This is not merely a subjective preference—it is a documented design problem. The buyer's particular circumstances (running a showroom where aesthetics matter) make the color deviation more significant than it would be for a functional-only use.

**Objective prong—seller's argument:** Several factors cut against finding substantial impairment: (1) the widgets meet all functional specifications; (2) industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components; (3) the buyer painted 600 widgets black (demonstrating a practical remedy that cost less than 40% of the price); (4) the buyer used the widgets for 8 weeks before complaining.

Critically, the buyer's own conduct—painting 600 widgets rather than demanding replacement or rejecting them—suggests the color deviation did not substantially impair value. If the widgets were truly unsuitable, the buyer would have rejected them promptly, not painted and used them for 8 weeks.

**Shaken Faith Doctrine.** The *Zabriskie Chevrolet v. Smith* doctrine allows revocation where a defect destroys the buyer's confidence [31]. For color deviations, this doctrine is unlikely to apply unless the buyer shows that the color error undermines confidence in the seller's ability to deliver conforming goods—which seems strained for a one-time color error on functional widgets.

### B. Procedural Bars to Revocation

Even if substantial impairment could be shown, two procedural bars apply:

**Timeliness.** Section 2-608(2) requires revocation "within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it" [7][11][13]. The color deviation was immediately discoverable. The 8-week delay exceeds any reasonable time for revocation. The *Durfee* case held that revocation must occur "within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it" [9][15].

**Substantial change in condition.** The buyer painted 600 of the 1,000 widgets black. Section 2-608(2) requires revocation "before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects" [7][11][13]. Painting 600 widgets constitutes a deliberate, substantial alteration not caused by any defect. This alone likely bars revocation.

**Counterargument for the buyer:** The buyer could argue that the "change in condition" was not a change *in the goods* but rather an improvement (painting them to conform). However, the statute focuses on whether the goods' condition has changed, not whether the change was beneficial or detrimental. The widgets are indisputably in a different condition.

### C. Neither Subsection (a) nor (b) of § 2-608(1) Is Satisfied

For revocation, the buyer must have accepted either (a) "on the reasonable assumption that its non-conformity would be cured" or (b) "without discovery of such non-conformity if his acceptance was reasonably induced either by the difficulty of discovery before acceptance or by the seller's assurances" [7][11][13].

The color was immediately apparent—no difficulty of discovery. There were no seller assurances inducing acceptance. The buyer did not accept with any expectation of cure. Neither subsection is satisfied.

**Conclusion on revocation:** While the buyer's architect's testimony is a factor supporting the buyer's position, the procedural bars (8-week delay, substantial change in condition, failure to satisfy either subsection ground) make revocation highly unlikely to succeed.

## III. Good Faith Analysis Under § 1-304

### A. The Good Faith Obligation

UCC § 1-304 provides: "Every contract or duty within the Uniform Commercial Code imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement" [1][2][3]. Under the revised Article 1, good faith means "honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing" [6]. For merchants, the definition includes "observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing" [8][9].

### B. Buyer's Good Faith: Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments that buyer acted in good faith:**

1. **Architect confirmation.** The buyer's architect documented genuine design problems created by the navy color. This is not a trivial or manufactured complaint—it is a verified issue affecting the buyer's business operations.

2. **Mitigation, not bad faith.** The buyer painted 600 widgets black to address the problem and continue using the widgets. Rather than leaving the goods idle (which could be wasteful), the buyer took practical steps to mitigate the problem. The buyer could argue this demonstrates good faith—they attempted to solve the problem themselves before contacting the seller.

3. **The May 10 notification was not entirely unreasonable.** The buyer might argue that they needed time to evaluate the full scope of the problem, consult with their architect, consider options (repair vs. replacement), and determine their preferred remedy. Eight weeks may be excessive, but the buyer was not entirely silent—they were using the goods and attempting to resolve the color issue.

4. **No evidence of falling market.** The "bad faith rejection" cases typically involve buyers seeking to escape a bad bargain due to a falling market. Here, there is no allegation that the buyer's demand for a refund was motivated by market conditions rather than genuine dissatisfaction with the color.

**Arguments that buyer acted in bad faith:**

1. **Contradictory conduct.** The pattern is difficult to reconcile: using widgets for 8 weeks, praising performance, painting 600 of them, and then demanding a 40% refund. This sequence suggests the buyer was satisfied until they decided to seek a price reduction.

2. **Opportunistic demand.** Demanding a 40% refund while keeping all 1,000 widgets (including the 600 already painted to the buyer's satisfaction) suggests the buyer is seeking a windfall rather than compensation for actual injury.

3. **Delay to seller's prejudice.** By waiting 8 weeks, the buyer prevented the seller from curing promptly. The seller could have replaced the widgets early on, but the buyer's delay made cure more difficult (the buyer had already painted 600 widgets).

4. **Professor Lawrence's analysis.** "A buyer's insistence upon rejection for a minor contract deviation in order to avoid an unfavorable bargain is an unfair use of buyers' rejection rights." While the facts here don't suggest a falling market, the delay and contradictory conduct suggest opportunistic behavior.

### C. Seller's Good Faith: Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments that seller acted in good faith:**

1. **Immediate cure offer.** The seller offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time—a commercially reasonable response.

2. **Industry practice.** The seller could reasonably believe, based on industry practice, that minor color variations are acceptable for functional components.

3. **No prior objections.** The buyer never raised color issues until May 10, and the buyer's emails praised performance.

**Arguments that seller may not have acted entirely in good faith:**

1. **Delivery of clearly wrong color.** Navy vs. charcoal gray is not a "minor shade variation"—it is a distinctly different color. If the seller knew or should have known they were delivering the wrong color, this could implicate good faith.

2. **Silence despite knowledge.** If the seller knew the widgets were navy (not charcoal gray) and delivered them anyway, hoping the buyer would not notice or would accept them, this would be bad faith.

**Balanced assessment:** The good faith analysis cuts both ways. The buyer's delay and contradictory conduct raise concerns, but the architect's documentation of genuine design problems provides some justification. The seller's immediate cure offer supports their good faith, but delivering a clearly different color without pre-notification or apology may suggest negligence or indifference.

## IV. Cure Rights Under § 2-508

### A. Section 2-508(1): Cure Within Contract Time

If the contract delivery date had not yet expired when the buyer rejected (which is debatable), the seller has an absolute right to cure under § 2-508(1) by seasonably notifying the buyer and making a conforming delivery within the contract time [4][6][13].

### B. Section 2-508(2): Cure After Contract Time—The "Reasonable Grounds" Test

The more likely scenario is that the contract time had expired. Under § 2-508(2), the seller may cure if the seller had "reasonable grounds to believe the non-conforming tender would be acceptable" [4][6][13].

**The T.W. Oil standard:** The leading case, *T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison Co.*, held that the test for reasonable grounds is objective. A seller should have recourse to cure "as long as it can establish that it had reasonable grounds, tested objectively, for its belief that the goods would be accepted" [8][9][10][11].

**Factors supporting the seller's reasonable belief:**

1. **Industry practice.** Usage of trade treating minor color variations as acceptable for functional components is a factor under § 1-303 [11].

2. **Functional specifications emphasized.** The contract emphasized load capacity and durability. The seller delivered goods meeting all performance criteria.

3. **Buyer's conduct.** The buyer used the widgets for 8 weeks, painted 600 of them, and sent praise emails. This conduct objectively suggests acceptance.

4. **Good faith and lack of knowledge.** The T.W. Oil court emphasized that a seller acting in good faith who did not know of the defect can avail itself of § 2-508(2) [10][11].

**Factors undermining the seller's reasonable belief:**

1. **Clearly different color.** Navy vs. charcoal gray is not a subtle shade variation—it is a distinctly different color. A reasonable seller should know that delivering navy instead of charcoal gray would be problematic.

2. **Buyer's specific needs.** If the seller knew the widgets were for a showroom where aesthetics mattered, the seller should have been more careful.

3. **Architect confirmation.** The buyer's architect confirmed genuine design problems. If the seller knew of the showroom use, the seller cannot claim reasonable belief that any color would be acceptable.

4. **The Bartus v. Riccardi distinction.** In *Bartus*, the seller delivered a "modified and improved" model (Model A-665 instead of A-660)—a superseding product that the seller reasonably believed would be acceptable [6][8][9]. Here, navy is not an "improved" version of charcoal gray; it is simply the wrong color.

### C. Is Two-Week Replacement "Reasonable Cure"?

The prior report assumed the seller's offer of immediate replacement within two weeks was reasonable. This assumption deserves scrutiny.

**Arguments that two weeks is reasonable:** For custom-manufactured goods, a two-week lead time for replacement is commercially reasonable. The seller acted promptly upon learning of the defect. In *Wilson v. Scampoli*, the court held that "removal of a television chassis for a short period of time" was no great inconvenience, and the buyer must grant the seller a reasonable opportunity to cure [13][14].

**Arguments that two weeks may be inadequate:**

1. **Buyer's 8-week usage.** The buyer had already invested 8 weeks of use. Expecting the buyer to return all widgets and wait two more weeks for replacement imposes additional downtime and disruption.

2. **Buyer painted 600 widgets.** The buyer has already altered 60% of the goods. Returning painted widgets to the seller is problematic—who bears the cost of the buyer's painting? How does the seller handle widgets that have been modified by the buyer?

3. **Lost use damages.** If the buyer needs widgets for continuous showroom operation, two weeks without functional widgets could cause business disruption.

4. **The "shaken faith" doctrine.** The *Zabriskie* "shaken faith" doctrine limits cure when the defect destroys buyer confidence. For a simple color error, this doctrine is unlikely to apply, but it is worth noting as a potential limitation [12][31].

**Interaction with buyer's partial painting:** The seller's cure offer—immediate replacement—does not address what happens to the 600 painted widgets. Does the buyer return them? Does the seller compensate the buyer for the painting costs? This gap in the cure offer weakens the seller's position. A reasonable cure offer might include: (a) replacement of all 1,000 widgets; (b) compensation for the buyer's painting of 600 widgets; (c) pickup of the non-conforming widgets at seller's expense. The seller's bare offer of "replacement" without addressing these details could be argued as inadequate.

## V. Damages Analysis

### A. The Proper Measure: § 2-714 for Accepted Goods

Since the buyer has likely accepted the goods by conduct, the remedy is damages under § 2-714. Subsection (2) provides: "The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted, unless special circumstances show proximate damages of a different amount" [1][2][3][4][5].

**Impact of buyer painting 600 widgets:** This is a critical analytical point. By painting 600 widgets black, the buyer has increased the "value of the goods accepted." Black widgets that match the showroom are worth more than unpainted navy widgets. The buyer's own mitigation efforts may have **increased** the value of the accepted goods, thereby **reducing** the seller's direct damage liability under § 2-714(2).

The cost to remedy the remaining 400 widgets (repainting them black) could be a reasonable measure of the value differential. This cost may be relatively small—likely far less than 40% of the contract price.

**Special circumstances clause:** The buyer could argue that "special circumstances" show proximate damages of a different amount—for example, the lost aesthetic value to the showroom, the cost of the architect's consultation, or the disruption caused by the color issue. However, these damages would need to be proved with specificity.

### B. Incidental and Consequential Damages Under § 2-715

Under § 2-715, incidental damages include "expenses reasonably incurred in inspection, receipt, transportation and care and custody of goods rightfully rejected" and "any other reasonable expense incident to the delay or other breach" [7][8][9][10].

The buyer's cost of painting 600 widgets could be recoverable as incidental damages if those costs were "reasonably incurred" incident to the breach. The buyer could argue: "We painted the widgets to make them usable while we worked out the dispute with the seller. These painting costs are expenses incident to the seller's breach."

Consequential damages under § 2-715(2)(a) include "any loss resulting from general or particular requirements and needs of which the seller at the time of contracting had reason to know" [7][8][9][10]. If the seller knew the widgets were for a showroom where aesthetics matter, the buyer could claim lost profits or business disruption from the color mismatch. However, "the buyer bears the burden of proving the loss with reasonable certainty; mere speculation is not enough" [6].

### C. § 2-717 Deduction Rights

UCC § 2-717 provides: "The buyer on notifying the seller of his intention to do so may deduct all or any part of the damages resulting from any breach of the contract from any part of the price still due under the same contract" [11][12][13][14].

The prior report correctly noted that the deduction is limited to **actual damages**, not a subjective percentage. The buyer cannot unilaterally deduct 40% unless that represents actual proven damages. The buyer must: (1) first satisfy the notice requirement under § 2-607(3); (2) give separate notice of intention to deduct under § 2-717; and (3) ensure the deduction reflects actual damages [12].

**Practical problem:** If the buyer deducts 40% and the seller sues for the unpaid balance, the buyer bears the burden of proving that 40% represents actual damages. Given the cost of repainting remaining widgets is likely far less than 40%, the buyer may face significant liability if they unilaterally deduct without justification.

### D. Whether the Seller's Cure Offer Affects Damages

If the buyer rejects the seller's reasonable cure offer, the buyer may be barred from recovering damages that could have been avoided by accepting cure. Under general mitigation principles and UCC § 1-305(a) ("The remedies provided by this code shall be liberally administered to the end that the aggrieved party may be put in as good a position as if the other party had fully performed"), the buyer who rejects a reasonable cure may be limited to damages that would not have been incurred had the cure been accepted.

## VI. Missing Legal Arguments

### A. Implied Warranty of Merchantability (§ 2-314)

The prior report did not adequately address § 2-314. Goods are merchantable if they "pass without objection in the trade under the contract description" and are "fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used" [34].

**Analysis:** The widgets meet all functional specifications (load capacity, durability) and industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. For ordinary functional purposes, navy widgets are as merchantable as charcoal gray widgets. However, if the contract description included "charcoal gray" as a specific color, and the goods "pass without objection in the trade" only for charcoal gray widgets (not navy), there could be a merchantability issue.

**Counterargument:** Color deviation alone does not typically breach the implied warranty of merchantability for functional components. The *Freeman Oldsmobile Mazda Co. v. Pinson* case held that minor aesthetic defects (small dents, imperfect paint on a new car) did not constitute a breach of merchantability [33].

### B. Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose (§ 2-315)

UCC § 2-315 provides: "Where the seller at the time of contracting has reason to know any particular purpose for which the goods are required and that the buyer is relying on the seller's skill or judgment to select or furnish suitable goods, there is unless excluded or modified under the next section an implied warranty that the goods shall be fit for such purpose" [34].

**This is potentially a significant argument for the buyer.** If the buyer communicated that the widgets were for a showroom where aesthetics and color coordination were critical, and the buyer relied on the seller's judgment to select appropriate goods, the seller's delivery of navy instead of charcoal gray could breach the fitness warranty. The buyer's architect's confirmation that navy created contrast issues could support this claim.

**Factors weighing against the buyer:** (1) the buyer apparently selected the specific color (charcoal gray)—they were not relying on the seller's judgment to select a suitable color; (2) the contract specified a color reference number, suggesting the buyer knew what they wanted; (3) the widgets are "functional components" where color may not be essential to fitness.

### C. Mutual Mistake

The prior report did not discuss mutual mistake. Both parties believed (or should have believed) the widgets were charcoal gray. If both parties were mistaken about a material fact—the color of goods central to the contract—this could constitute a mutual mistake under the Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 152 [24][25].

**Requirements for mutual mistake:** (1) both parties were mistaken; (2) the mistake concerns a basic assumption of the contract; (3) the mistake has a material effect on the agreed exchange; (4) the party seeking relief did not bear the risk of the mistake.

**Analysis:** Both parties believed the widgets were charcoal gray; they were navy. Color is a basic assumption of a contract specifying color. If the buyer cannot use navy widgets in their showroom, the mistake has a material effect. However, the buyer bears some risk: they had a 10-day inspection period and could have discovered the error promptly.

**Practical effect:** Mutual mistake could make the contract voidable, allowing either party to rescind. This would be a more dramatic remedy than damages, and courts are reluctant to grant rescission where damages would suffice.

### D. No Rejection Clause for Shade Variance

The original query mentioned "no rejection clause for shade variance." The prior report did not address this. If the contract contained such a clause, it would be analyzed under §§ 2-719 and 2-302.

Under § 2-719(1), parties may limit remedies, including limiting the buyer's right to reject [51][52]. However, under § 2-719(2), "where circumstances cause an exclusive or limited remedy to fail of its essential purpose, remedy may be had as provided in this Act" [51][52].

A "no rejection for shade variance" clause would be enforceable unless: (1) it is unconscionable under § 2-302; (2) it fails of its essential purpose under § 2-719(2); or (3) the shade variance is so extreme that it falls outside the scope of the clause (navy is not a "shade" of charcoal gray—it is a different color entirely).

The buyer could argue that navy vs. charcoal gray is not a "shade variance" but a fundamentally different color. A clause limiting rejection for "shade variance" would not apply to a completely different color. This is a contractual interpretation question.

## VII. Revised Comprehensive Analysis and Balanced Conclusion

### A. Strengths of the Seller's Position

1. **Acceptance by conduct.** The buyer's 8-week use, painting of 600 widgets, and praise emails constitute strong evidence of acceptance under § 2-606(1)(a), (b), and (c).

2. **10-day inspection clause.** While subject to the "manifestly unreasonable" qualifier of § 1-204, the clause is presumptively enforceable for a visually apparent defect.

3. **Industry practice.** Usage of trade supports the seller's position that minor color variations are acceptable for functional components.

4. **Recovery precluded by actions.** The buyer's painting of 600 widgets constitutes a "substantial change in condition" that bars revocation under § 2-608(2).

5. **Good faith concerns.** The buyer's contradictory conduct raises serious questions about whether the color objection is genuine or opportunistic.

6. **Cure offer.** The seller's immediate replacement offer is commercially reasonable and demonstrates good faith.

7. **Damages limited.** The buyer's damages are likely limited to the cost of repainting the remaining 400 widgets—far less than 40% of the contract price.

### B. Strengths of the Buyer's Position

1. **Perfect tender rule.** Under § 2-601, the buyer has a presumptive right to reject for nonconformity "in any respect." Navy is not charcoal gray.

2. **Architect confirmation.** The buyer's architect documented genuine design problems. This is not a trivial or manufactured complaint.

3. **Manifestly unreasonable clause.** The 10-day inspection clause may be "manifestly unreasonable" under § 1-204 if it required the buyer to evaluate aesthetic impact without installation.

4. **Course of performance.** The parties' conduct for 8 weeks may have modified or waived the strict inspection clause under § 1-303(f).

5. **Mitigation argument.** The buyer's painting of 600 widgets can be framed as good faith mitigation, not acceptance.

6. **Implied warranty of fitness.** If the buyer communicated showroom requirements, § 2-315 could provide a basis for recovery.

7. **Trade usage limits.** The express color term (charcoal gray) may prevail over trade usage under the § 1-303(e) hierarchy.

8. **Mutual mistake.** Both parties were mistaken about a basic assumption of the contract.

### C. Areas of Legal Uncertainty

1. **The classification of the color deviation.** Is navy vs. charcoal gray a "minor shade variation" or a fundamentally different color? This factual determination significantly affects the legal analysis.

2. **Interaction of § 2-601 and § 1-303.** Whether trade usage can "quality" what constitutes conformity under the perfect tender rule, or whether express terms always prevail, is not definitively settled.

3. **Application of § 2-508(2) to these facts.** Whether the seller had "reasonable grounds to believe" the navy widgets would be acceptable depends on multiple factual determinations (industry practice, buyer's conduct, seller's knowledge of showroom use).

4. **Cure offer adequacy.** Whether two-week replacement is "reasonable cure" given the buyer's 8-week usage and painting of 600 widgets is fact-intensive.

5. **Damages calculation.** The proper measure of damages when the buyer has already remedied 60% of the goods is uncertain and may depend on what is "reasonable" under § 2-714.

6. **Effect of "no rejection for shade variance" clause.** If such a clause exists, its scope (shade variance vs. different color) would be a contractual interpretation issue.

### D. Strategic Considerations for Resolution

**For the seller:** The seller's strongest position is that the buyer accepted the goods and cannot reject or revoke. The seller should argue that the proper remedy is limited to damages under § 2-714 (cost of repainting remaining 400 widgets) and should emphasize the buyer's contradictory conduct. The seller's cure offer is a significant advantage—the buyer's rejection of a reasonable cure weakens the buyer's claim.

**For the buyer:** The buyer's strongest arguments are (1) the perfect tender rule gives a presumptive right to reject; (2) the architect's confirmation of genuine design problems; and (3) the potential "manifest unreasonableness" of the 10-day clause. The buyer should also explore § 2-315 (fitness for particular purpose) as a potential basis for additional damages. However, the buyer faces significant hurdles on acceptance, revocation, and timeliness.

**Practical resolution:** The litigation risks for both parties suggest a negotiated resolution is preferable. A reasonable outcome might be: the seller provides replacement widgets for the 400 unpainted units (or a price reduction reflecting the cost to repaint them), the buyer retains the 600 already-painted widgets at the contract price, and both parties bear their own costs. The buyer's demand for 40% of the entire contract price appears disproportionate to the actual harm.

### E. Final Conclusion

The buyer did **not** effectively reject the goods under the perfect tender rule (§ 2-601). The buyer **accepted** the goods by conduct under § 2-606 through: (a) signifying conformity by praising performance via email; (b) failing to make an effective rejection within a reasonable time (8-week delay, especially with a 10-day inspection clause); and (c) painting 600 widgets black, an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership.

The buyer has **no right to revoke acceptance** under § 2-608. While the buyer's architect documented genuine design concerns, the color deviation likely does not meet the "substantial impairment" standard, and procedural bars (8-week delay, substantial change in condition, failure to satisfy either subsection ground) independently preclude revocation.

The buyer's **notice was untimely and ineffective**. While the 10-day clause may be subject to the "manifestly unreasonable" qualifier of § 1-204, and course of performance could potentially modify it, the 8-week delay for a visually apparent defect is difficult to justify.

**Good faith concerns exist on both sides.** The buyer's delay and contradictory conduct raise questions, but the architect's documentation of design problems provides some justification. The seller's immediate cure offer demonstrates good faith, but delivering a clearly wrong color without pre-notification may suggest at least negligence.

If the buyer's rejection were effective (which it is not), the seller would likely have cure rights under § 2-508(2). The seller had reasonable grounds—supported by industry practice, the buyer's conduct, and the emphasis on functional specifications—to believe the initial tender would be acceptable. The seller's offer of immediate replacement within two weeks is reasonable, though the failure to address the 600 painted widgets weakens the offer.

The buyer's demand for a 40% refund is **unsupported** by UCC damage principles. The proper remedy is the difference in value between navy and charcoal gray widgets—likely the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets, plus any reasonable incidental damages incurred. The buyer may deduct actual damages from remaining amounts due under § 2-717, but only to the extent of proven damages, not an arbitrary 40%.

**Recommended course:** Both parties should pursue negotiated resolution. The buyer should accept the seller's cure offer (with clarification regarding the painted widgets) or agree to retention of all widgets with a price adjustment reflecting the cost to repaint the remaining 400 units. The buyer is not entitled to reject the goods, revoke acceptance, or unilaterally demand a 40% refund.
