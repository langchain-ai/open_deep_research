# Comprehensive Analysis of Vertical Integration Strategies in the Global Cocoa Sector: Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (2018–2026)

## Executive Summary

This revised and updated report analyzes the vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (via Olam Food Ingredients/ofi) from 2018 to 2026, with a focus on upstream investments in West African cocoa farming cooperatives, sustainability certification programs, EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) compliance, and competitive positioning in premium chocolate manufacturing contracts. The three companies collectively process approximately 60% of the world's cocoa.

The analysis reveals a sector undergoing profound transformation driven by three forces: the EUDR's traceability requirements (now effective December 30, 2026 for large operators after two delays), historic cocoa price volatility (prices surged above $12,000/ton in 2024 before declining sharply), and growing demand from premium chocolate manufacturers for verifiably sustainable cocoa. Key findings include: Olam's absence from Ferrero's tier 1 supplier list, the abandonment of Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate split following a CEO departure, and the continued absence of independent peer-reviewed farmer income impact evaluations for any of the three companies' sustainability programs.

## 1. Direct-Sourced vs. Broker-Purchased Cocoa Percentages

### 1.1 Data Transparency Assessment

**Critical finding:** None of the three companies has updated its direct-sourced vs. broker-purchased cocoa percentage in publicly available reports published between 2024 and May 2026. **Most recently available figure: Approximately 40% direct-sourced** (from a 2021 IDH/C-Lever.org/GISCO case study).

The 2021 case study states: "Barry Callebaut's direct supply chain, which accounts for 40% of its cocoa sourcing primarily from Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire, boasts 100% traceability from farm to first purchase point through their proprietary SAP-based Katchilè application." [1]

## 2. Capital Expenditures in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana (2018–2026)

### 2.1 Barry Callebaut

**Côte d'Ivoire — SACO Abidjan Expansion (2019, CHF 55 million / ~$55.2 million)**

On March 29, 2019, Barry Callebaut inaugurated a new state-of-the-art cocoa processing unit at its Société Africaine de Cacao (SACO) plant in Abidjan, with operations beginning April 1, 2019. [10][12] The CHF 55 million investment (approximately $55.2 million, equivalent to FCFA 30 billion) increased SACO's cocoa bean processing capacity by over 40%, reaching 40,000 tonnes for the new unit. Combined with the San Pedro facility's 130,000 tonnes capacity, the company's total Ivorian capacity reached approximately 230,000 tonnes. The expansion created 45 new direct jobs and 120 indirect jobs. [10][12]

Antoine de Saint-Affrique, then CEO of Barry Callebaut, stated: "This significant investment in our cocoa processing capacities in Côte d'Ivoire is one more sign of our long-term commitment to the country and to the African continent." [12]

**Ghana — Tema Solar Energy Investment (2023–2024)**

Barry Callebaut partnered with Empower New Energy, a Norwegian solar investment firm, to install a 640 kWp solar photovoltaic (PV) system at its Tema manufacturing site. [3] Fully financed by Empower New Energy, the project is expected to generate 0.98 GWh annually, reduce CO₂ emissions by 497 tonnes annually, and secure approximately 100 local jobs. The solar expansion raises solar electricity contribution on-site from 6% to 14%. [3]

**Sustainability Program Investments (Ongoing)**

The Forever Chocolate strategy, launched in 2016, has lifted 557,739 cocoa farmers above the poverty line and mapped over 1.5 million cocoa farms. [5] The BC Next Level program, launched in late 2023, involves a CHF 500 million investment to shift from volume to value, streamline operations, and strengthen customer proximity. [5]

**No new specific processing facility CapEx announcements for Côte d'Ivoire or Ghana** were found for the 2024–2026 period beyond ongoing sustainability and traceability programs.

### 2.2 Cargill

**Côte d'Ivoire — Yopougon Cocoa Processing Plant ($100 million, completed November 2021)**

On November 2, 2021, Cargill completed a $100 million expansion of its cocoa processing plant in Yopougon, Côte d'Ivoire, making it the largest cocoa-grinding plant in Africa. [6][10][16] The upgrade increased production capacity by 50%, creating nearly 100 full-time local jobs. The expanded facility now has a capacity of 160,000 metric tons. [13]

Harold Poelma, President of Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate, stated: "This investment will serve as a catalyst for the establishment of a broader, local agri-food industry, as we shift a greater share of our global grinding activities to the countries of origin." [6]

**Ghana — Tema Cocoa Processing Facility ($13 million expansion, completed November 2021)**

Cargill completed a $13 million expansion of its Tema cocoa processing plant, increasing annual grinding capacity by 20% to 90,000 tonnes. [11] The plant is now the largest cocoa processing facility in Ghana, employing 530 people (up from 400). Ghana's President Nana Addo Dankwa Akufo-Addo attended the commissioning ceremony: "The commissioning of this expansion today underscores the importance of the private sector to government's industrial transformation program." [11]

**Sustainability Program Investments**

- $7.7 million in sustainability programming in Côte d'Ivoire (2019) with partners CARE, Save the Children, and the International Cocoa Initiative
- $3.4 million in sustainability programming in Ghana (2019)
- An additional $13 million commitment in 2021: nearly $6 million for farmer coaching and $3.5 million for traceability expansion [6][10]

**Supply Chain Sustainability Transformation (July 2025)**

On July 1, 2025, Cargill announced a comprehensive transformation of its global cocoa supply chain, including reutilizing cocoa shells as biomass boiler fuel in Côte d'Ivoire, solar energy in Ghana, and fully electric barges in Europe reducing CO₂ emissions by 190,000 kg annually. [18]

**Note:** In September 2025, Cargill halted cocoa grinding operations in Côte d'Ivoire for the first time in decades due to deteriorating bean quality — rejecting 5-6% of beans compared to the usual 1% threshold. [Sources: Cocoaradar; The High Street Journal]

### 2.3 Olam / ofi / Olam Agri

**Côte d'Ivoire — San Pedro Cocoa Processing Facility ($75 million, commissioned July 2014, inaugurated March 2015)**

The $75 million facility is among Africa's most advanced cocoa plants, processing 75,000 metric tonnes annually. [6][7] The facility created 450 local jobs and supports 50,000 cocoa farmers. [6][7]

**Côte d'Ivoire — Abidjan Cocoa Processing Facility ($43.5 million, commissioned Q1 2012)**

A greenfield plant processing approximately 60,000 metric tonnes of cocoa beans annually. [1]

**Côte d'Ivoire — Olam Agri $200 Million Cocoa Processing Capacity Expansion (announced 2025)**

Olam Agri has committed $200 million to expand cocoa processing capacity in Côte d'Ivoire, focusing on increasing grinding capacity to boost production of cocoa liquor, butter, and powder for export. [12][1][2][6] This expansion aligns with government-led industrialization efforts to retain more value domestically.

**Ghana Operations**

Olam Cocoa Processing (OCP) Ghana Limited operates in the Ashanti Region with certifications including SMETA, FSSC 22000, and UTZ. [16] In April 2025, OCP Ghana hosted the CEO of the Ghana Free Zones Authority, who noted "This is the time and season that companies like Olam will expand and thrive." [16]

**Olam Group Re-organisation (2020–2026)**

Olam Group completed a strategic re-organisation creating three distinct operating groups: ofi (value-added ingredients including cocoa), Olam Agri, and Remaining Olam Group. [5] In April 2026, Olam Group completed the sale of a 44.58% stake in Olam Agri to Saudi Agricultural and Livestock Investment Company (SALIC) for approximately $1.88 billion. [7][8][9][10] The updated plan aims to invest US$500 million into ofi. [5]

### 2.4 Industry Context: Processing Capacity in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana

**Côte d'Ivoire:** The country aims to process 80% of its cocoa beans locally by 2030. [9] During the 2024/25 season, 15 companies operate processing plants with total capacity near 1 million tonnes, backed by about EUR 1 billion in capital expenditure. [9] Côte d'Ivoire has become the world's largest cocoa grinder, with investments in processing surging 58% between 2020 and 2025. [USDA FAS Cocoa Sector Overview 2025]

**Ghana:** Domestic processing capacity stands at approximately 505,000 MT, but processors operate at 50% capacity due to inconsistent bean supply. [13] Ghana announced a reform package in February 2026 with a binding target for 50% domestic cocoa processing by 2026/27.

## 4. EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) Compliance (Updated to May 2026)

### 4.1 Regulatory Background and Timeline

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR, Regulation (EU) 2023/1115) mandates that seven key commodities — including cocoa — placed on or exported from the EU market must be deforestation-free and legally produced, with a cut-off date of December 31, 2020. [1][2]

**Current deadlines (postponed twice):**
- **Large and medium operators**: December 30, 2026 [3][4]
- **Micro and small enterprises**: June 30, 2027 [3][4]
- Original deadline was December 30, 2024; first delay extended to December 30, 2025; second delay extended to December 30, 2026 [1]

**Reasons for delays:** The EU's centralized IT system for Due Diligence Statement (DDS) submissions was not fully operational, and there were concerns over supply chain disruptions in major producer countries. [2][5]

**May 2026 Simplification Package:** The European Commission released a package reducing annual compliance costs by approximately 75% compared to the original regulation. Key changes include simplified DDS for micro and small operators, voluntary grouping for businesses, and upgrades to the EUDR Information System. [6]

**Country Risk Classification (Implementing Regulation (EU) 2025/1093):** Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana are classified as **standard-risk**, requiring full due diligence with plot-level GPS documentation and third-party verification. [2][7]

**Penalties for non-compliance:** Fines up to 4% of total EU annual turnover, confiscation of products and revenues, prohibition from placing products on the EU market, and temporary exclusion (up to 12 months) from EU public funding and procurement. [4][9]

### 4.2 Barry Callebaut

**Public position:** Strong advocate for EUDR implementation without further delay. On March 18, 2026, Barry Callebaut joined Ferrero, Nestlé, Valrhona, and Tony's Chocolonely in issuing an open letter urging the EU to maintain current deforestation regulations without weakening them. [13]

**Traceability systems:**
- **Katchilè System:** Proprietary SAP-based tool launched in 2015 for mobile phone-based data collection and farm mapping
- **Starling Satellite Monitoring (Airbus Defence and Space):** Announced December 15, 2025. Uses high-resolution satellite imagery (from 10m to 30cm resolution) and AI algorithms for continuous forest cover monitoring. Co-developed with the Earthworm Foundation since 2016. [17][18]

**Mapping completion rates:**
- 100% traceability of direct cocoa supply chain in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana achieved by 2019 [15]
- 91% of cocoa farmers' plots mapped by 2024 [16]
- 1.5 million+ cocoa farms mapped as of 2025 [4]
- 260,511 farms mapped under CFI program in 2024 [Source: Barry Callebaut Annual CFI Progress Report 2024]

**Forest and Natural Ecosystems Policy (updated January 2026):** Commits to "achieving zero deforestation and zero conversion in at-risk supply chains by December 31, 2025, and deforestation-free soy by December 31, 2030." [14]

**Non-compliant volume disclosures:** **Not publicly disclosed.**

**Penalties, warning letters, investigations:** **None found.**

### 4.3 Cargill

- 295,060 hectares assessed for deforestation risk [8]

**Due Diligence Statement process:** Starting December 30, 2025, customers will receive DDS reference and verification numbers via email alongside product delivery. DDS will be submitted via the EU's TRACES platform. [21]

**Non-compliant volume disclosures:** **Not publicly disclosed.** However, AidEnvironment's Compliance Checker (December 2024) identified two potential cocoa-related noncompliance instances involving Cargill (one in Côte d'Ivoire, one in Brazil). [50]

**Independent audit:** KPMG provided limited assurance on selected sustainability KPIs for crop year 2024–2025.

### 4.6 Cross-Cutting Industry Developments

**Côte d'Ivoire national traceability:** The country traced only 40% of cocoa beans for the 2024/2025 season. About 55-60% of cocoa remains untraceable. [32]

## 5. Competitive Positioning in Premium Chocolate Contracts

### 5.1 Global Market Structure

The three companies collectively process approximately 60% of global cocoa. [57] Barry Callebaut holds approximately 21.6–25% of the global industrial chocolate market, Cargill approximately 15.3%, and Olam/ofi completes the top-three. [58][59][60]

The EU industrial chocolate market, valued at USD 18.50 billion in 2025, is projected to grow at 4.3% CAGR to reach USD 29.40 billion by 2036. The market is "moderately consolidated, with very few global players that carry heavy market shares due to integrated supply chains, robust sourcing networks, and expansive distribution capacity." [70]

## 6. New Competitive Dynamics (2024–2026)

### 6.1 Barry Callebaut's Potential Cocoa/Chocolate Division Split

**Reuters Report (December 16, 2025):** Barry Callebaut was in early discussions about potentially separating its global cocoa division from the rest of the company. [8] The aim was to reduce exposure to volatile cocoa prices and improve the company's financial profile. Options included carving out the division, selling a minority stake, forming a joint venture or merger, or complete divestiture. [1][3][7]

**CEO Departure Over Disagreement (January 2026):** Barry Callebaut parted ways with CEO Peter Feld due to disagreements over the strategic proposal. Feld supported separating the lower-margin cocoa unit (31% of total sales revenue, 15.5% of operating profit in FY2024/25), while the board, led by Chairman Patrick De Maeseneire, opposed the plan, considering it "a non-starter." [11]

**Hein Schumacher Appointed CEO (January 21, 2026):** Former Unilever CEO (2023-2025) appointed effective January 26, 2026. [24] The Board stated Schumacher was "the right leader at this stage to chart Barry Callebaut's next phase... based on our fully integrated cocoa and chocolate business model." [11]

**Status as of May 2026: The potential split has been effectively abandoned.** Schumacher's five-point strategy centers on: (1) risk-sharing across the value chain, (2) advancing BC Next Level transformation, (3) prioritizing quality growth over volume, (4) redefining customer engagement as strategic partner, and (5) rebuilding investor confidence through financial discipline. [22][25]

**Profit Forecast Revision (May 2026):** Barry Callebaut slashed its operating profit forecast from mid-to-single-digit growth to a mid-teens decline. CEO Hein Schumacher attributed the profit slump to supply chain disruptions linked to the conflict in Iran, overcapacity in the global cocoa bean market, and faster-than-expected declines in cocoa prices. ## 7. Key Data Gaps and Transparency Limitations

### 7.1 Cargill's Total Cocoa Volume from West Africa

**Not publicly disclosed.** Cargill has never publicly disclosed its total cocoa volume sourced from West Africa (Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana) or globally. The company states it is "the second-largest producer of cocoa and chocolate globally" and has 27 processing facilities worldwide (per 2025 Impact Report), but absolute volume figures are not provided.

**What is known about Cargill's processing capacity:**
- Yopougon (Côte d'Ivoire): Capacity doubled in 2021 to become Africa's largest cocoa grinding plant; absolute capacity not disclosed but Côte d'Ivoire's total domestic grinding is an estimated 685,000-800,000 MT for MY 2024/2025 [USDA FAS]
- Tema (Ghana): Capacity expanded to 90,000 tonnes per year [9][11][12][13]

### 7.2 Company-Specific Disclosures of Non-Compliant Volumes

**None of the three companies has disclosed specific volumes of cocoa excluded from supply chains due to deforestation risk, non-compliance with EUDR, or sustainability criteria.** All three companies report extensive traceability and mapping metrics, but the actual volume of cocoa that fails to meet deforestation-free requirements and is consequently excluded from EU-bound supply chains remains undisclosed.

### 7.3 Lindt's Processor-Level Supplier List

**Still not publicly disclosed.** Lindt & Sprüngli has not disclosed its cocoa processor-level supplier partners in any public report as of May 2026. While the company provides extensive information about its Farming Program cooperatives and the countries/regions where it sources, it does not provide a specific list of named cocoa processor partners.

## 8. Conclusions

The vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have continued to converge over the 2018–2026 period, driven by three primary forces: the EUDR's traceability requirements (now effective December 2026), premium chocolate manufacturers' demands for verifiably sustainable cocoa, and the need to manage unprecedented price volatility.

**On direct sourcing:** Olam leads with the only explicit disclosure of a 67% direct sourcing ratio (though now six years old). Barry Callebaut's 40% direct sourcing figure (from 2020) suggests significant room for growth. Cargill's complete lack of disclosure on this metric represents the most significant transparency gap among the three.

**On capital investments:** Cargill invested the most in West African processing capacity during the 2018–2024 period ($100 million in Côte d'Ivoire, $13 million in Ghana, plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan plus solar in Tema). Olam's major investments predate the analysis window, though the new $200 million Olam Agri expansion in Côte d'Ivoire represents a significant new commitment.

**On farmer income:** All three companies report positive income impacts, but the evidence base relies entirely on company-commissioned or co-authored research. The gap between "poverty line" metrics (~$2.15/day) and "living income" benchmarks (~$4,315–$6,904/year) remains vast. The DISCO 2024 report found only 10.8% of households earn above the living income benchmark, showing stagnation in actual income improvements. The SWISSCO Ghana study found 91% of farmers earn below the Living Income Benchmark.

**On EUDR compliance:** All three companies have invested heavily in traceability systems, satellite monitoring, and polygon mapping. Barry Callebaut appears most advanced in farm mapping (1.5 million+ farms) and has been the most vocal advocate for no further delays. Cargill has achieved 100% polygon mapping in its Promise supply chain. Olam benefits from its earlier 2020 traceability milestone in direct supply. None have disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions. The Côte d'Ivoire national traceability rate of just 40% for the 2024/2025 season underscores the systemic challenges that persist.

**On competitive positioning:** Ferrero's latest supplier list (2023-2024) confirms Barry Callebaut and Cargill as tier 1 suppliers while Olam is absent for the second consecutive year — a significant competitive blow to Olam. Mondelēz works with all three processors. Lindt's continued confidentiality on processor relationships prevents direct comparison. The new Mars/ofi partnership represents a significant counterweight to Olam's Ferrero exclusion, demonstrating the company can still win major premium clients.

**On new competitive dynamics:** The abandonment of Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate split with the appointment of Hein Schumacher as CEO represents a decisive strategic choice to maintain the fully integrated business model. Cargill's flexibility strategy positions the company as an adaptive partner in volatile markets. Olam/ofi's AtSource platform adoption by both Nestlé and Mars validates the technology-enabled sustainability partner approach.

The strategic differentiation among these three processors is narrowing as sustainability and traceability become baseline requirements rather than competitive advantages. The next phase of competition will likely center on innovation capabilities (Barry Callebaut's ChoViva and Chocolate Academies), platform-based services (Olam's AtSource), flexibility in formulation and supply chain management (Cargill's NextCoa and portfolio approach), and the ability to demonstrate genuine, independently verified farmer income impact — a gap that none of the three companies has yet closed.
