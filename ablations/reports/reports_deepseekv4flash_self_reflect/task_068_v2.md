# The Cambrian Explosion Revisited: An Updated Synthesis of Evidence, Hypotheses, and Emerging Consensus

## Thesis Statement

The Cambrian Explosion—the apparently rapid appearance of nearly all major animal phyla approximately 540–515 million years ago—is best understood not as a singular event triggered by a single cause, but as an autocatalytic cascade driven by the convergence of three necessary factors: permissive environmental conditions (particularly localized shallow-water oxygenation), a pre-existing genetic toolkit (Hox genes and gene regulatory networks), and escalating ecological feedbacks (predator-prey arms races and niche construction). The most recent evidence from 2024–2026 has substantially weakened the oxygen-trigger hypothesis as a standalone explanation while simultaneously strengthening the ecological feedback model through direct empirical evidence of Cambrian arms races. However, no single hypothesis has been falsified or proven predominant; rather, the field is converging on an integrated multi-causal framework in which oxygen was a necessary but insufficient precondition, the genetic toolkit was assembled gradually during the Ediacaran, and ecological interactions provided the proximate driver of rapid morphological diversification.

## Section 1: Evaluating the Latest Evidence (2024–2026)

### 1.1 The 2025 PNAS Halite Study: High Oxygen 1.4 Billion Years Ago

The most provocative recent challenge to the environmental trigger hypothesis comes from **Park, Schaller, et al. (2025)**, published in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*. This study analyzed microscopic fluid inclusions—tiny droplets of ancient water and gas bubbles—trapped within 1.4-billion-year-old halite (rock salt) crystals from a now-vanished subtropical lake in northern Ontario, Canada. The team developed custom laboratory equipment to differentiate gases from ancient air bubbles versus gases dissolved in coexisting brine, enabling the first direct measurements of Mesoproterozoic atmospheric composition. [1][2][3]

**Key findings:**
- Oxygen was ~3.7% of present atmospheric levels (PAL)—sufficient to meet the metabolic requirements of early complex multicellular animal life.
- Carbon dioxide was ~10× pre-industrial levels (roughly 2,800 ppm), helping resolve the "faint young Sun paradox" by keeping Earth warm despite the Sun emitting only ~70% of its current output.
- The data may represent a **transient oxygenation event** rather than sustained high oxygen throughout the "Boring Billion."

As lead graduate student Justin Park stated: *"It may reflect a brief, transient oxygenation event in this long era that geologists jokingly call the 'Boring Billion.'"* [2] Morgan Schaller noted: *"It's possible that what we captured is actually a very exciting moment smack in the middle of the Boring Billion."* [2]

**Implications for the Cambrian Explosion debate:**
The study directly challenges the classic "rising oxygen trigger" model. If oxygen levels sufficient for animal respiration existed ~800 million years before the Cambrian Explosion, then rising oxygen alone cannot explain why animals did not appear until ~600 million years later. The researchers themselves acknowledge this tension: *"Our results confirm atmospheric pO2, at least transiently, exceeded the thresholds necessary for early animal respiration. A short pulse of oxygenation may help explain the lag between the origin and ecological rise of complex eukaryotes. However, if these O2 levels were sustained for hundreds of My, it would challenge the view that rising atmospheric oxygen concentrations drove animal evolution."* [1]

Critics cited in accompanying commentary noted that *"oxygen doesn't generate new genetic information. But such information had to be the proximal cause of the Cambrian explosion"* and that *"the real trigger behind the Cambrian explosion is something a lot more powerful than merely a higher pO2 level in the atmosphere."* [1] The study **weakens the environmental trigger hypothesis** (at least the simple oxygen version), strengthens the view that oxygen was a **permissive factor** rather than a **proximate cause**, and redirects attention toward genetic and ecological triggers.

### 1.2 The 2024 *Nature Geoscience* Study: Sustained but Modest Oxygen Increases

**Stockey, Sperling, Cole, Farrell, et al. (2024)** published a landmark study in *Nature Geoscience* that used statistical learning (machine learning) analyses of an extensive geochemical database spanning ~1,000 to 300 million years ago, combined with cGENIE Earth System Model simulations, to link atmospheric oxygen changes to marine dissolved oxygen. [4][5][6]

**Key findings:**
- **No wholesale ocean oxygenation** occurred during the late Neoproterozoic. Contrary to the classic "Neoproterozoic Oxygenation Event" narrative, deep oceans remained largely anoxic.
- Instead, there was a **moderate, sustained, long-term increase** in atmospheric oxygen and marine productivity throughout the Neoproterozoic and into the Palaeozoic.
- Critically: *"Dissolved oxygen in shallow shelf environments scales essentially linearly with atmospheric oxygenation, while deep ocean oxygenation remains limited until the Silurian–Devonian."* [4]
- *"Shallow marine habitats were probably suboxic or severely hypoxic in the Neoproterozoic and did not become generally oxic until the Devonian."* [4]
- Full ocean oxygenation to near-modern levels did not occur until the mid-Palaeozoic (~400 million years ago), coinciding with the rise of large land forests.

Erik Sperling commented: *"Cambrian animals likely did not require as much oxygen as scientists used to believe. We found minor increases in oxygenation that are at the correct magnitude to drive big changes in ecology."* [5] Richard Stockey explained: *"From a global perspective, we didn't see the full oxygenation of the oceans to near modern levels until about 400 million years ago, around the time that we see the appearance of large forests on land."* [5]

**Significance for the three hypotheses:**
This study supports a **refined environmental trigger hypothesis**—not a single dramatic oxygenation event, but a moderate, spatially heterogeneous rise in oxygen and food availability in shallow waters. It reconciles conflicting proxy records by showing that trace metal proxies (Mo, U) primarily track global/deep-ocean redox, not the shallow habitats where animals actually lived. The study provides *"some of the most direct evidence for potential physiological drivers of the Cambrian radiation"* while recognizing that global deep-ocean oxygenation took another 150+ million years. [4][5]

### 1.3 The 2025 Bicknell et al. Study: Oldest Direct Evidence of a Predator-Prey Arms Race

**Bicknell, Campione, and Paterson (2025)** published in *Current Biology* the **first direct empirical evidence** of a microevolutionary predator-prey arms race in the Cambrian fossil record. Analyzing over 200 organophosphatic sclerites of the tommotiid species *Lapworthella fasciculata* from a lower Cambrian carbonate succession in South Australia (~517 million years old), scanning electron microscopy revealed over 200 shells with predator-made holes, likely caused by a soft-bodied mollusk or worm. [7][8][9]

**Key findings:**
- The frequency of perforated sclerites increased over time.
- This correlated positively with an increase in sclerite thickness.
- This demonstrates a **microevolutionary cycle** where the prey evolved thicker shells to resist predation, while the predator concurrently enhanced their ability to penetrate this armor.

As Bicknell stated: *"Predator-prey interactions are often touted as a major driver of the Cambrian explosion, especially with regard to the rapid increase in diversity and abundance of biomineralizing organisms at this time. Yet, there has been a paucity of empirical evidence showing that prey directly responded to predation, and vice versa."* [7][8]

The study concluded: *"Propagation of such interactions across early Cambrian ecosystems likely resulted in the proliferation of biomineralizing taxa with enhanced defenses, illustrating the importance of predation as a major ecological driver of early animal evolution."* [7][8]

**Significance for the ecological feedback model:**
This is powerful support for the ecological feedback hypothesis. Predation pressure drove the rapid evolution of biomineralized defenses, which in turn opened new ecological opportunities, creating a positive feedback loop that accelerated diversification. This study fills a critical gap: while predation has long been invoked as a driver, *empirical fossil evidence of reciprocal adaptation* had been lacking. The study demonstrates that ecological interactions were actively driving morphological change during the Cambrian Explosion. [7][8]

### 1.4 The 2025/2026 Zhang Cellular Predation Hypothesis

**Zhang, L. (2025/2026)** published in *Frontiers in Ecology and Evolution* a novel **"Cellular Predation–Motility Hypothesis"** that proposes a fundamentally different mechanism operating at a different level of biological organization. [10]

**Core proposal:**
Early unicellular ancestors of animals engaged in repeated predator–prey interactions via phagocytosis. Crucially, it is **failed predation events**—cases where prey escaped or predators suffered cellular damage—that drive the mechanism. These failed attempts exposed cells to oxidative, enzymatic, and mechanical stress, triggering error-prone DNA repair mechanisms and transposable-element activation. This produced **chromosomal rearrangements** including Hox cluster reorganization, regulatory network rewiring, and gene duplications—changes with direct consequences for segmentation, symmetry, and appendage architecture.

**How it differs from the standard ecological feedback model:**
- **Standard model**: Predator-prey arms races operate at the **organismal level** among already-complex animals—explaining the *elaboration* of existing body plans.
- **Zhang's model**: Predation operates at the **cellular level** among *unicellular* eukaryotes, with genomic stress from failed phagocytosis generating the raw genetic variation that *enables* the evolution of multicellular body plans in the first place.

Motility functions as an evolutionary filter: highly motile cells evaded engulfment and persisted as unicellular forms (like choanoflagellates), while low-motility cells repeatedly experienced predation-induced genomic stress, accumulating heritable variants that promoted obligate multicellularity.

**Supporting evidence presented:**
1. **Mitochondrial endosymbiosis as precedent**: Failed phagocytosis of the mitochondrial ancestor resulted in massive gene transfer to the host nucleus.
2. **Comparative genomic signatures**: Clustered structural variants and gene family expansions near developmental genes in early animal lineages.
3. **Ancient cellular capabilities**: Phagocytosis and DNA repair mechanisms are ancient and present in holozoans.
4. **Stress-activated transposable elements**: Cellular stress can activate transposable elements, leading to genomic rearrangements.
5. **Testable predictions**: The hypothesis predicts that cells surviving failed predation should exhibit genomic signatures of stress-induced repair, and experimental co-cultures should detect increased heritable genomic changes.

**Implications:** If validated, this hypothesis would link ecological interactions (predation) directly to genomic innovation, providing a mechanism that bridges the genetic toolkit and ecological feedback models. It explicitly incorporates a Cambrian "Goldilocks" window—rising oxygen, elevated resources, and low ecological incumbency—to explain why this mechanism produced rapid diversification specifically during the Cambrian. [10]

### 1.5 The 2026 Chanchai et al. Study: Localized, Transient Oxygenation at the Ediacaran–Cambrian Boundary

**Chanchai, Lonsdale, et al. (2026)** published in *Geobiology* a multi-proxy redox analysis of carbonate successions from the Deep Spring Formation (Nevada, USA) and La Ciénega Formation (Sonora, Mexico), spanning the Basal Cambrian carbon isotope Excursion (BACE) (~536–532 Ma). Using carbonate-bound iodine ratios (I/(Ca+Mg)), cerium anomalies (Ce/Ce*), and carbonate-associated uranium isotopes (δ²³⁸Ucarb), the study provides high-resolution constraints on marine redox conditions during this critical interval. [11][12]

**Key findings:**
- **No global ocean oxygenation event** coincided with the BACE. The study states: *"Contrary to previous suggestions that a global ocean oxygenation event coincided with the BACE, we do not observe a shift in δ²³⁸Ucarb concurrent with the carbon isotope excursion in either section."* [11]
- Instead, widespread **low-oxygen (anoxic) conditions** persisted in shallow marine environments.
- At **one site** (La Ciénega Formation), a **brief, localized oxygenation event** was detected at the BACE nadir, via negative Ce anomalies.
- Consistently low iodine ratios *"below values observed in today's oxygenated oceans, suggest a broadly redox-stratified surface ocean."* [11]
- Uranium isotopes indicate oceans were **generally more anoxic than present.**

The study concludes: *"We do not find evidence for major, widespread oxygenation coincident with the BACE, but a continuation of low-oxygen conditions punctuated by a short-lived oxygenation event in the shallow oceans."* [11] These brief fluctuations in oxygen levels *"may have played a role in the onset of behavioral complexity among bilaterian invertebrates during this critical transition."* [11]

**Significance:** This challenges the long-held assumption of a global oxygenation event at the dawn of the Cambrian. Instead, oxygen availability was **spatially patchy and temporally transient**. This further undermines the simple oxygen-trigger model while supporting more nuanced views of heterogeneous marine redox landscapes. [11][12]

### 1.6 The 2026 Huayuan Biota (Zeng et al.): Post-Sinsk Extinction Cambrian Life

**Zeng, Liu, Zhu, et al. (2026)** published in *Nature* the discovery of the **Huayuan biota** from Hunan Province, southern China—a Burgess Shale-type (BST) Lagerstätte dating to ~512 million years ago (Cambrian Stage 4), shortly after the **Sinsk Event** extinction (~513.5 Ma). [13][14]

**Key details:**
- >50,000 fossils from a single quarry, representing **153 species across 16 phyla**, with ~59% (90 species) new to science.
- Exceptional soft-tissue preservation (guts, nerves, gills, eyes, neural tissues).
- 16 animal phyla including arthropods, sponges, tunicates, molluscs, brachiopods, cnidarians, chordates, and complete radiodonts (apex predators like *Guanshancaris kunmingensis*).
- Strong faunal links to the **Burgess Shale** (Canada), including shared genera like *Helmetia*—suggesting long-distance larval dispersal via ocean currents.

Han Zeng commented: *"The Huayuan biota fills the blank of top-tier soft-bodied fossil deposits of the critical period after the Cambrian explosion."* [13] The biota represents a deep-water environment that was less impacted by the Sinsk extinction, suggesting *"Deep-water environments such as that preserved by the Huayuan biota were less affected by the Sinsk event and perhaps served as refuges for life or sites of evolutionary innovation."* [13][14]

**Significance:** Fills a critical temporal gap between pre-extinction (Chengjiang-type Stage 3 biotas) and post-extinction communities, demonstrating the resilience and rapid recovery of Cambrian ecosystems after a mass extinction. Robert R. Gaines commented: *"The Huayuan Biota is a global-tier fossil treasure trove on par with the Chengjiang and Burgess Shale biotas."* [13]

### 1.7 The 2026 Jiangchuan Biota (Li, Dunn, Parry et al.): Ediacaran Complex Animals

**Li, Dunn, Parry, Anderson, Tang, et al. (2026)** published in *Science* the discovery of the **Jiangchuan biota** from Yunnan Province, southwest China—exceptionally preserved fossils dating to **554–539 million years ago** (late Ediacaran), predating the Cambrian Explosion by at least 4 million years. [15][16]

**Key details:**
- >700 fossil specimens collected, preserved as carbonaceous films (not the typical Ediacaran-style sandstone impressions).
- **Deuterostomes** (cambroernids including *Herpetogaster*- and *Phlogites*-like organisms related to starfish and acorn worms)—the **oldest known** representatives of the group that includes all vertebrates.
- **Ambulacrarians** (early starfish and acorn worm relatives)—implying chordates must have existed simultaneously.
- **Bilaterian worms** with evertible proboscises, complex feeding adaptations, and a discoidal holdfast.
- Possible early **ctenophores** (comb jellies).
- Ediacaran-type organisms such as a *Haootia*-like cnidarian.

Gaorong Li stated: *"Our discovery closes a major gap in the earliest phases of animal diversification. For the first time, we demonstrate that many complex animals, normally only found in the Cambrian, were present in the Ediacaran period, meaning that they evolved much earlier than previously demonstrated by fossil evidence."* [15][16]

Frankie Dunn added: *"The presence of these ambulacrarians in the Ediacaran period is really exciting... The discovery of ambulacrarian fossils in the Jiangchuan Biota also means that the chordates—animals with a backbone—must also have existed at this time."* [15][16]

Luke Parry noted: *"This discovery is extremely exciting because it reveals a transitional community: the weird world of the Ediacaran giving way to the Cambrian... something totally unique and unexpected."* [15]

Ross Anderson commented: *"Our results indicate that the apparent absence of these complex animal groups from other Ediacaran sites may reflect differences in preservation rather than true biological absence."* [15]

**Significance:** Demonstrates that many "Cambrian-type" complex animals actually evolved **in the Ediacaran**, closing a major gap between molecular clock estimates (which predicted earlier divergences) and the fossil record. The biota represents a **transitional community** bridging Ediacaran and Cambrian ecosystems, and strongly supports the view that preservation biases—not genuine absence—explain the scarcity of complex fossils in most Ediacaran deposits. [15][16]

### 1.8 Additional 2024–2026 Studies

**Orbital Forcing Hypothesis (2025, *Geophysical Research Letters*):** Mills et al. proposed that Earth's long-period orbital eccentricity cycles triggered nutrient pulses into oceans that stimulated photosynthesis, increasing oxygen levels. Using a newly developed Earth Evolution Model, the study found orbital-induced climate changes corresponded with geological records of oxygenation and biological radiation during the Cambrian. The researchers proposed that *"the long‐period 'orbital forcing' is responsible for the pulses of oxygenation and biological radiation observed during the Cambrian Explosion."* [17][18]

**Brain-First Hypothesis (Chipman, 2026, *BioEssays*):** Chipman proposed that the evolution of complex nervous systems and brains preceded and enabled the development of diverse body plans: *"Neural complexity is not a consequence of articulated bodies, on the contrary, it is the condition that made them possible."* Chipman argues: *"Rather than thinking about a single 'explosion,' we should think in terms of a series of linked stages."* [19][20]

**Deep Ocean Oxygen Instability (Ostrander, Sperling, Strauss, 2025, *Science Advances*):** Using thallium stable isotopes in ancient marine mudrocks from the Yukon, Canada, this study found that deep ocean oxygenation was highly unstable for at least 160 million years after the Cambrian. *"It wasn't like someone flipped a switch and the deep ocean became forever oxygenated... The more we actually look at this, the more complex it is. It's really unstable."* [21]

## Section 2: The "Explosion vs. Artifact" Debate—A Detailed Taphonomic Analysis

### 2.1 Butterfield's Foundational Taphonomic Studies

**Butterfield (1990)** — "Organic preservation of non-mineralizing organisms and the taphonomy of the Burgess Shale" (*Paleobiology*, 16: 272–286)—established the fundamental understanding that the "soft-bodied" fossils of the Burgess Shale are not preserved through mineral replacement but rather as **primary, kerogenized organic carbon**. The organic-walled Burgess Shale fossils were studied both in petrographic thin section and isolated from the rock matrix following careful acid maceration. Butterfield identified that preservation results from interactions between organic matter and **clay minerals** (phyllosilicates) during sediment transport and burial, noting that one of the most pervasive natural inhibitors of biodegradation results from substrate and enzyme adsorption onto and within clay minerals. [22][23]

**Key implications:**
- Burgess Shale preservation depends on specific clay-organic interactions.
- The process favors **decay-resistant extracellular tissues** (cuticles, chaetae, sclerites) over labile cellular tissues.
- This creates a systematic bias: BST fossils preferentially preserve organisms with recalcitrant integuments. [22][23]

**Butterfield (1995)** — "Secular distribution of Burgess-Shale-type preservation" (*Lethaia*, 28: 1–13)—made the critical observation that **Burgess Shale-type preservation is not uniformly distributed through time**. BST preservation is overwhelmingly concentrated in the **Cambrian and earliest Ordovician**, with only rare occurrences before and after. Butterfield defined BST preservation as a taphonomic pathway involving the exceptional organic preservation of non-mineralizing organisms in fully marine shales, and demonstrated that this pathway operated as a distinct **taphonomic window** that opened in the Cambrian and largely closed by the end of the Early Ordovician. [24]

**Why this window opened and closed:**
- **Opening:** Cambrian oceans had unique geochemical conditions—lower sulfate concentrations, low oxygen bottom waters, and abundant reactive clay minerals that facilitated clay-organic interactions.
- **Closing:** The **Cambrian Substrate Revolution**—the evolution of deep-burrowing organisms that mixed sediments, oxygenated pore waters, and disrupted the delicate geochemical conditions needed for organic preservation—destroyed the conditions necessary for BST preservation. Additionally, the evolution of silica biomineralizers (sponges, radiolarians) depleted silica availability, affecting other preservational pathways. [24]

**Crucial implication:** If BST preservation is restricted to a narrow temporal window, then the apparent concentration of soft-bodied animal fossils in the Cambrian may partly reflect the operation of this taphonomic window rather than solely a genuine evolutionary signal. [24]

**Butterfield (2003)** — "Exceptional fossil preservation and the Cambrian explosion" (*Integrative and Comparative Biology*, 43: 166–177)—is Butterfield's most explicit statement on the "explosion vs. artifact" question. He identifies **six distinct types of exceptional preservation** in the terminal Proterozoic–Cambrian interval:
1. Bitter Springs-type (silica permineralization in carbonates)—restricted to Proterozoic
2. **Burgess Shale-type** (carbonaceous compressions in shales)—**Cambrian–Early Ordovician**
3. Doushantuo-type (phosphate permineralization)—Ediacaran–Cambrian
4. Orsten-type (phosphatization of small cuticular organisms)—Cambrian
5. Ediacaran-type (casts and molds in sandstones)—Ediacaran
6. Beecher's Trilobite-type (pyritization in shales)—Ordovician–Devonian [25][26]

**The central question:** Butterfield explicitly frames the debate: *"Is this [the Cambrian Explosion] a real evolutionary signal, or merely the opening and closing of a taphonomic window?"* [25]

**Butterfield's answer:**
- **The Cambrian Explosion IS real**—it is most clearly recorded in the **conventional fossil record** of mineralized skeletons, phytoplankton cysts (acritarchs), and trace fossils, which are not subject to the same taphonomic biases as BST deposits.
- **BUT the magnitude and perceived suddenness may be exaggerated** by the unique taphonomic circumstances: *"Exceptionally preserved, non-biomineralizing fossils contribute importantly to resolving details of the Cambrian explosion, but little to its overall patterns."* [25][26]
- Different preservation types capture different biological information: BST preservation favors decay-resistant extracellular structures (cuticles, chaetae) as flattened carbon films; three-dimensional mineralization (Doushantuo-type, Orsten-type) captures labile cellular tissues. The combination of flattened cuticular structures and three-dimensional gut structures observed in some Burgess Shale arthropods reflects fundamental histological differences and their distinct taphonomic pathways. [25][26]

Butterfield's central conclusion: *"The fact of the Cambrian explosion is most clearly and consistently recorded in the conventional fossil record, simply because it is unexceptional; exceptionally preserved biotas add detail but cannot alone define large-scale patterns."* [25]

### 2.2 Gaines et al. (2025): Anactualistic Fossil Preservation

**Gaines, R.R. and Droser, M.L. (2025)** published in *Paleobiology*—"Fossil Lagerstätten and the enigma of anactualistic fossil preservation" (*Paleobiology*, 51(1): 29–43)—providing a comprehensive synthesis of the environmental and geochemical controls on exceptional fossil preservation. [27]

**What "anactualistic" means:** The term, coined by Adolf Seilacher, refers to modes of fossilization that are restricted in time and no longer occur in modern environments. It represents a direct challenge to uniformitarian assumptions. [27]

**Key arguments:**
- The concentration of Konservat-Lagerstätten in the Ediacaran and early Paleozoic is driven overwhelmingly by **only two modes of preservation**: **Ediacara-type** and **Burgess Shale–type preservation**. Both are *"extinct' modes of fossilization that are no longer present in marine environments."* [27]
- BST preservation is unique to the Cambrian and Early Ordovician—it does not occur in modern environments, making it fully anactualistic.
- **Anoxia alone is insufficient** for exceptional preservation—specific geochemical conditions (low sulfate, high alkalinity, clay-organic interactions) are required.
- The decline of anactualistic preservation reflects fundamental changes in Earth's surface environments—particularly ocean oxygenation and the establishment of thorough bioturbation after the Cambrian Substrate Revolution.
- The temporal distribution of exceptional preservation is **fundamentally non-uniformitarian**—we cannot use modern taphonomic observations to calibrate expectations for the early Paleozoic. [27]

**Relationship to Butterfield's thesis:** Gaines and Droser **strongly support and extend** Butterfield's core argument. The concept of "anactualistic" preservation provides a powerful framework for understanding Butterfield's "taphonomic window." While Butterfield (2003) was more cautious—arguing that the explosion is real but exaggerated—Gaines and Droser focus on the **environmental controls** and the radical difference between ancient and modern preservational regimes. They provide the geochemical and sedimentological mechanism that explains **why** Butterfield's taphonomic window opened and closed. [27]

### 2.3 Muscente et al. (2017): The Meta-Analysis

**Muscente, Schiffbauer, Broce, Laflamme, O'Donnell, Boag, Meyer, Hawkins, Huntley, McNamara, MacKenzie, Stanley, Hinman, Hofmann, and Xiao (2017)** published in *Gondwana Research*—"Exceptionally preserved fossil assemblages through geologic time and space" (*Gondwana Research*, 48: 164–188)—a comprehensive database of **694 exceptionally preserved fossil assemblages** (Konservat-Lagerstätten) spanning approximately 610 million to 3 million years ago. Using hierarchical clustering analysis and geospatial analyses, the study assessed temporal and spatial patterns. [28][29]

**Major conclusions:**
1. **Exceptional preservation shows a clear secular decline:** The clusters of exceptional fossil assemblages **decrease in number** across the Cambrian-Ordovician interval and **shift from open marine settings** (dominant in the Cambrian) to **transitional and non-marine environments** (more common later in the Paleozoic). Assemblages with similar ages and depositional settings commonly occur in clusters signifying ancient geographic regions. [28][29]
2. **The decline was driven by environmental transformation:** Conditions conducive to exceptional preservation declined worldwide during the early Paleozoic in response to transformations of near-surface environments that promoted degradation of tissues and curbed authigenic mineralization potential. The authors propose a holistic explanation relating these environmental transitions to **ocean oxygenation and bioturbation**, which affected virtually all taphonomic pathways. [28][29]
3. **The Cambrian-Ordovician interval represents a unique peak** in the preservation of non-biomineralized tissues in open marine settings.
4. **Exceptions prove the rule:** The few post-Ordovician occurrences of exceptional preservation in open marine settings are associated with **episodes of widespread oceanic anoxia** (e.g., oceanic anoxic events in the Mesozoic), which temporarily recreated conditions similar to the Cambrian taphonomic window. [28][29]

**Implications for the explosion vs. artifact debate:**
- **For the artifact argument:** The concentration of BST deposits in the Cambrian is statistically real—there genuinely was a unique taphonomic window that has no modern analogue.
- **For the genuine radiation argument:** The conventional fossil record (shelly fossils, trace fossils, acritarchs) also shows a Cambrian Explosion signal. Pre-Cambrian deposits with comparable preservational conditions preserve abundant algae but **no unambiguous animal fossils**, suggesting animals genuinely were absent or extremely rare before the Cambrian.
- **Synthesis:** The Cambrian Explosion reflects a **genuine evolutionary radiation that was amplified by a unique, anactualistic taphonomic window**. The radiation was real, but the BST deposits make it appear even more dramatic than it might have appeared under ordinary preservational conditions. [28][29]

### 2.4 Weighting the Evidence: Do Chengjiang and Burgess Shale Support a Genuine Explosion or a Taphonomic Artifact?

**Arguments that the Cambrian Explosion IS primarily a genuine radiation:**

1. **Stratigraphic consistency across multiple taphonomic modes:** The Chengjiang biota (~518 Ma) and Burgess Shale (~505 Ma) both preserve complex bilaterian faunas, but older Ediacaran deposits (~575–541 Ma) preserve predominantly simple, soft-bodied organisms. This pattern of increasing complexity over time is not easily explained by preservation bias alone, especially given that the Ediacara-type preservation (a completely different taphonomic mode) was operating during the Ediacaran and should have captured complex animals if they existed. [25][26]

2. **Conventional fossils show the same pattern:** As Butterfield (2003) emphasized, the Cambrian Explosion is *"most clearly and consistently recorded in the conventional fossil record"* of shelly fossils, trace fossils, and acritarchs—not just in BST deposits. Trace fossils show increasing complexity across the Ediacaran-Cambrian boundary, with a genuine diversification of behavioral patterns. [25]

3. **Evidence for absence, not absence of evidence:** The Anderson et al. (2023) *Trends in Ecology & Evolution* study found that some Neoproterozoic sites (800–789 million years ago) had mineralogical conditions (clay minerals like kaolinite) comparable to Cambrian BST sites. Despite this, no metazoan fossils were found in these deposits, indicating a true absence of animal ancestors at those times. This provides the first "evidence for absence" and supports the view that animals had not evolved by the early Neoproterozoic. [30]

4. **The Jiangchuan Biota complicates but doesn't refute the pattern:** The 2026 Jiangchuan discovery pushes complex animals back into the Ediacaran, but **still not deep into the Proterozoic**. The complex animals appear at ~554–539 Ma, which is immediately predating the Cambrian boundary. This is consistent with a phased model where complex animals evolved during the Ediacaran but underwent their major radiation in the Cambrian. [15][16]

**Arguments that the Explosion IS primarily a taphonomic artifact:**

1. **BST preservation is anactualistic:** Gaines & Droser (2025) demonstrate that BST preservation is an "extinct" mode of fossilization that no longer occurs in marine environments. The apparent concentration of soft-bodied fossils in the Cambrian is partly an artifact of this unique taphonomic window. [27]

2. **The 240-million-year sponge spicule gap:** Sperling et al. (2010) demonstrated that molecular data require siliceous sponges to have originated in the Cryogenian (~759–699 Ma), but their spicules do not appear in the fossil record until the Cambrian (~520 Ma)—a gap of approximately 240 million years, attributed to taphonomic bias (lower clay content in Precambrian sediments leading to enhanced dissolution of biogenic silica). [31]

3. **Molecular clock estimates of deep divergence:** Studies by Blair and Hedges (2005) and others support metazoan divergences dating to 900–1,500 million years ago, hundreds of millions of years before the Cambrian. If these estimates are correct, the fossil record dramatically underestimates the timing of animal origins, and the Cambrian represents the first time animals were fossilizable, not the first time they existed. [32]

4. **The "Cambrian Conundrum":** Erwin et al. (2011) argued that *"the major animal clades diverged many tens of millions of years before their first appearance in the fossil record,"* suggesting that the fossil record significantly underestimates the timing of animal origins. [33]

**The consensus view (synthesis):**

The most widely accepted position is a **hybrid model**:
1. **The Cambrian Explosion represents a genuine evolutionary radiation** of animal body plans, biomineralization, and ecological complexity.
2. **This radiation was amplified and made more visible by anactualistic taphonomic conditions** that operated only in the Cambrian-Ordovician interval.
3. **BST deposits do not create a false signal** of animal appearance—the animals were genuinely diversifying—but they do **exaggerate the apparent speed and magnitude** by preserving soft-bodied components that would otherwise be invisible.
4. **The taphonomic window and the evolutionary radiation may share common causes:** the same environmental conditions (changing seawater chemistry, oxygenation, substrate conditions) that enabled BST preservation may have also driven the evolutionary innovations of the Cambrian Explosion.
5. **The Jiangchuan Biota (2026) pushes back the origin of complex animals but confirms the phased model**—complex animals existed in the late Ediacaran but underwent their major diversification and ecological expansion in the Cambrian. [15][25][26][27][28]

The critical distinction remains: the **conventional fossil record** (trace fossils, shelly fossils, acritarchs) shows a genuine Cambrian radiation that is independent of BST preservation. The BST deposits provide high-resolution snapshots of what that explosion looked like, but the broad pattern is already recorded in the unexceptional fossil record.

## Section 3: Adjudicating the Three Competing Hypotheses

### 3.1 Environmental Trigger Hypothesis (Rising Oxygen)

**Where it succeeds based on the newest data:**

1. **The Stockey et al. (2024) *Nature Geoscience* study** provides the most refined oxygen model yet: minor, sustained increases in atmospheric oxygen and marine productivity in shallow shelf environments were sufficient to cross critical ecological thresholds. The study found that early animals did not require as much oxygen as previously thought, and that modest increases could have driven major ecological changes. [4][5]
2. **The orbital forcing hypothesis (Mills et al., 2025)** provides a plausible mechanism linking astronomical cycles to nutrient pulses, photosynthesis, and oxygenation pulses that correspond with Cambrian radiation events. [17][18]
3. **The oxygen hypothesis** successfully explains *why the explosion happened when it did* (the Phanerozoic oxygenation event, however modest, coincides temporally with the radiation), which the genetic toolkit hypothesis cannot do. [4]

**Where it fails based on the newest data:**

1. **The 2025 PNAS halite study (Park & Schaller)** is the most damaging single piece of evidence. It demonstrates that oxygen levels sufficient for animal respiration existed **1.4 billion years ago**—approximately 800 million years before the Cambrian Explosion. If oxygen was already available, the oxygen-trigger model cannot explain the timing delay. As the study's authors state: *"If these O₂ levels were sustained for hundreds of My, it would challenge the view that rising atmospheric oxygen concentrations drove animal evolution."* [1][2]
2. **The Chanchai et al. (2026) study** shows that the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary was characterized by **persistent low oxygen** with only brief, localized oxygenation events—the opposite of a global oxygenation event. [11][12]
3. **The Ostrander et al. (2025) *Science Advances* study** demonstrates that deep ocean oxygenation was highly unstable for at least 160 million years after the Cambrian, pushing the timeline for permanent deep ocean oxygenation forward by at least 160 million years. [21]

**Verdict: Significantly weakened as a standalone explanation, but remains an important component of integrated models.** The hypothesis has evolved from a simple "oxygen rise caused the Explosion" model to a more nuanced framework:
- Relatively modest oxygen increases crossed critical ecological thresholds.
- Localized, transient shallow-shelf oxygenation events were more important than global oxygenation.
- The deep ocean remained largely anoxic well after the Cambrian.
- Oxygen was a **necessary precondition** but not a sufficient trigger—it interacted with other factors.

**Can it be considered falsified?** No. The halite study is a single data point and may represent a transient event rather than sustained oxygen. The hypothesis has been refined but not falsified. However, **the simplest version of the hypothesis (that oxygen rise was the primary and sufficient trigger) is no longer tenable.**

---

### 3.2 Genetic Toolkit Hypothesis (Hox Genes and Gene Regulatory Networks)

**Where it succeeds based on the newest data:**

1. **The genetic toolkit hypothesis successfully explains *how* rapid morphological evolution was possible**—through the hierarchical architecture of GRNs and Hox gene duplications that allowed regulatory changes to produce diverse body plans. Developmental genes are highly conserved across widely divergent animal groups, indicating their presence in the last common ancestor. [34]
2. **The weakening of oxygen-only models indirectly strengthens the genetic toolkit hypothesis.** If oxygen was already available (as the halite study suggests), the delay in animal evolution must be explained by **something else**—likely genomic and developmental innovations. As one commentary noted: *"Oxygen can do whatever it wants throughout Earth's history and animals won't come into existence until the actual trigger is pulled."* [1]
3. **The Jiangchuan Biota (2026)** pushes back the origin of key animal groups (deuterostomes, ambulacrarians) into the Ediacaran, consistent with molecular clock estimates suggesting deep Precambrian origins of animal phyla. This supports the idea that the genetic toolkit for complex animal body plans was assembled gradually over deep time. [15][16]

**Where it fails based on the newest data:**

1. **Timing problem:** The genetic toolkit existed before the Cambrian. Why didn't the Explosion happen earlier if the genes were already in place? The hypothesis lacks an explicit **trigger mechanism** to explain the specific timing of the Cambrian.
2. **Davidson's GRN critique:** Mutations affecting developmental gene regulatory network (dGRN) kernels often result in lethal outcomes rather than evolutionary innovations. Davidson himself noted that *"interference with expression of any [genes in the dGRN kernel] by mutation or experimental manipulation has severe effects on the phase of development that they initiate. This accentuates the selective conservation of the whole subcircuit, on pain of developmental catastrophe."* [34]
3. **Early GRNs were likely fundamentally different:** Davidson himself noted that *"the earliest ones [dGRNs] were likely hierarchically shallow rather than deep, so that in the beginning adaptive selection could operate in a larger portion of their linkages. Furthermore, we can deduce that the outputs of their sub-circuits must have been polyfunctional rather than finely divided and functionally dedicated, as in modern crown group dGRNs."* [34]

**Verdict: Widely accepted as a necessary precondition but insufficient as a standalone explanation.** The hypothesis succeeds in explaining the *substrate* for morphological innovation but fails to explain the *timing* or *trigger* of the Cambrian Explosion. It has been largely **absorbed into integrated models** as one component of a multi-causal framework.

**Can it be considered falsified?** No. The genetic toolkit hypothesis is not falsifiable in the traditional sense—it makes no testable predictions about timing. It remains a valid description of the molecular substrate that enabled the Explosion, but it does not explain the Explosion itself.

---

### 3.3 Ecological Feedback Model (Predator-Prey Arms Races)

**Where it succeeds based on the newest data:**

1. **The Bicknell et al. (2025) study provides the strongest empirical support yet.** It is the **first direct empirical evidence** of a microevolutionary predator-prey arms race in the Cambrian fossil record—showing reciprocal adaptation of prey shell thickness and predator penetrating ability over time. As Bicknell stated: *"This critically important evolutionary record demonstrates, for the first time, that predation played a pivotal role in the proliferation of early animal ecosystems and shows the rapid speed at which such phenotypic modifications arose during the Cambrian Explosion event."* [7][8]
2. **The Huayuan Biota (Zeng et al., 2026)** reveals complex food webs (with apex radiodont predators, diverse arthropods, and filter-feeders) established shortly after a mass extinction, showing the resilience and power of ecological structuring. [13][14]
3. **The ecological feedback model** successfully explains *why the Explosion was self-accelerating*—predator-prey arms races, niche saturation, and ecosystem engineering driving positive feedback loops of diversification. This is the only hypothesis that explains the **"explosive" character** of the event.
4. **The Zhang (2025/2026) cellular predation hypothesis**, if validated, would provide a mechanistic link between ecological interactions and genomic innovation, potentially explaining how predation drove not just the elaboration of body plans but also their origin. [10]

**Where it fails based on the newest data:**

1. **The López-Villalta (2016) correlation problem:** A statistical test of the predation-diversification hypothesis found **no positive correlation** between origination rates and predator ratios across the Cambrian and Ordovician. The study found that *"the median predator ratio increased up to a peak in the middle–late Cambrian then decreased during the Ordovician, opposite to expectations under the hypothesis"* and that *"time-series analysis shows no positive correlation between origination rates and predator ratios across the Cambrian and Ordovician."* [35] This directly challenges the assumption that predation accelerated diversification.
2. **The pre-condition problem:** Ecology alone cannot explain the Explosion without environmental preconditions. The ecological model explains *how* diversification proceeded but not *why* it started when it did, after billions of years of unicellular life.
3. **The ecological feedback model** requires the prior existence of complex animals to have predator-prey interactions at the organismal level. It does not explain the origin of the first complex animals.

**Verdict: Strongly supported by the newest evidence, but incomplete alone.** The Bicknell study provides the first direct empirical evidence for predation-driven evolution in the Cambrian, which is a major advance. However, the López-Villalta correlation problem raises important questions about whether predation truly accelerated diversification or was merely a consequence of it. The model needs to be integrated with environmental and genetic factors to provide a complete explanation.

**Can it be considered falsified?** No. The model has been strengthened by the Bicknell study, not weakened. However, if further studies fail to find positive correlations between predation and diversification across the Cambrian, the model may need significant revision.

---

### 3.4 Summary of Hypothesis Status

| Hypothesis | Strengthened/Weakened | Current Status | Predominant? |
|------------|----------------------|----------------|--------------|
| Environmental (Oxygen) | **Weakened** by halite study (1.4 Ga oxygen), Chanchai et al. (localized oxygenation), Ostrander et al. (late deep ocean oxygenation) | Survives as refined model (permissive factor, not trigger); simple version falsified | No |
| Genetic Toolkit | **Indirectly strengthened** by weakening of oxygen hypothesis; unaffected by direct tests | Accepted as necessary precondition; fails to explain timing | No |
| Ecological Feedback | **Strongly strengthened** by Bicknell et al. (2025) direct empirical evidence | Best-supported by newest fossil evidence; faces correlation problem | No (single strongest, but incomplete) |
| **Integrated Multi-Causal** | **Strengthened** by all new studies supporting multi-factor interactions | **Current consensus** | **Yes** |

**Current scientific consensus: No single hypothesis is currently predominant.** The field is converging on an **integrated multi-causal framework** in which:
- Oxygen was a necessary but insufficient precondition.
- The genetic toolkit was assembled gradually during the Ediacaran.
- Ecological interactions (predation, competition, niche construction) provided the proximate driver of rapid morphological diversification.
- The "explosive" character of the event reflects positive feedback loops among all three factors.

## Section 4: Updated Molecular Clock Analysis

### 4.1 The Dos Reis et al. (2015) Uncertainty Analysis

**Dos Reis, Thawornwattana, Angelis, Telford, Donoghue, and Yang (2015)** published in *Current Biology*—"Uncertainty in the Timing of Origin of Animals and the Limits of Precision in Molecular Timescales"—applying Bayesian relaxed-clock methods (implemented in MCMCTree, part of the PAML package) to estimate divergence times using an extensive molecular dataset combined with four different fossil calibration strategies. [36][37]

**Key findings:**
- *"Although we can assert that crown Metazoa originated during the Cryogenian (with most crown-bilaterian phyla diversifying during the Ediacaran), it is not possible with current data to pinpoint the divergence events with sufficient accuracy to test for correlations between geological and biological events in the history of animals."* [36]
- *"The uncertain nature of ancient fossils and violations of the molecular clock impose a limit on the precision that can be achieved in estimates of ancient molecular timescales."* [36]
- *"Attempts to build evolutionary narratives of early animal evolution based on molecular clock timescales appear to be premature."* [36]

**Implications for the Cambrian Explosion debate:**
The study demonstrated that molecular clocks have **inherent limits of precision** when dating deep divergences. The confidence intervals for animal divergence times are so wide that they encompass both the "deep Precambrian" scenario (divergences 800–1,200 million years ago) AND the "near-Cambrian" scenario (divergences just before 541 million years ago). This means molecular data alone **cannot definitively resolve** whether the major animal phyla diverged deep in the Neoproterozoic or radiated shortly before the Cambrian. The uncertainty is constrained more severely by fossil calibrations than by the amount of molecular sequence data. [36][37]

---

### 4.2 How Bayesian Relaxed-Clock Methods Have Changed Estimates

The transition from **strict molecular clocks** (which assumed a constant rate of molecular evolution across all lineages) to **relaxed clocks** (which allow rates to vary among lineages) has significantly changed divergence estimates. [38][39]

**Early strict clocks:** Produced dates placing animal origins deep in the Precambrian, much earlier than fossil evidence suggested (e.g., 1,200–1,500 Ma for basal animal phyla).

**Relaxed clocks:** By allowing rates to vary among lineages and using fossil calibrations, relaxed-clock methods produce date estimates more compatible with fossil records, placing major metazoan divergences in or just before the early Cambrian. However, as a 2015 review notes: *"These methods rely on strong assumptions about rate changes and speciation/extinction patterns, often chosen for statistical convenience rather than biological realism. Different models and assumptions significantly influence date estimates, sometimes producing widely varying and even contradictory results. This complexity means molecular dates cannot be accepted uncritically."* [38][39]

**Types of relaxed clocks:**
1. **Strict Clock:** Uniform rate across all branches.
2. **Fixed Local Clock:** Specific clades evolve at distinct, constant rates.
3. **Uncorrelated Relaxed Clock:** Each branch independently draws rates from a statistical distribution (log-normal, exponential, gamma), permitting abrupt rate changes.
4. **Random Local Clock:** Multiple local clocks over subregions of the tree; the number and location of rate changes are sampled during MCMC. [39]

**Impact on divergence estimates:**
- Relaxed clocks have **both widened and shifted** divergence estimates—sometimes bringing them closer to the fossil record (when using more conservative calibrations) and sometimes pushing them deeper into the Precambrian (depending on the models and priors used).
- The uncertainty introduced by different models is substantial, and confidence intervals often overlap across competing hypotheses.
- As one review concluded: *"The most obvious conclusion to be drawn from this array of estimates is that molecular dates cannot be taken at face value: the data and the methods employed can dramatically change the date estimates gained."* [38]

The most recent developments (BEAST X, 2025) introduce *"advanced evolutionary models, including novel clock and substitution models that capture site- and branch-specific heterogeneity"* and leverage *"fast, gradient-informed computational techniques such as linear-time algorithmic evaluations and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) sampling to efficiently explore high-dimensional parameter spaces."* [40] These advances may improve precision but have not yet resolved the fundamental uncertainties identified by Dos Reis et al. (2015). [40]

---

### 4.3 The Blair & Hedges vs. Peterson et al. Disagreement

This long-running debate centers on whether animal phyla diverged **deep in the Precambrian** (the Hedges/Blair school) or **closer to the Cambrian boundary** (the Peterson school). [32][41][42]

**The Blair & Hedges School (deep Precambrian divergences):**
- **Key argument:** Molecular data analyses over the past three decades have consistently found deeper divergences among animals (approximately 800 to 1,200 MYA). The fossil record is highly biased and provides only minimum estimates; molecular clocks *"start counting mutations at the actual time of divergence, whereas fossil estimates are minimum estimates only."* [32][41]
- **Methodology:** Uses local (Bayesian) molecular clock methods. Argues that Peterson et al. (2004) *"underestimated ages by using fossil calibrations as maximum limits instead of as minimum limits."* [32]
- **Key findings:** *"Most major lineages of deuterostomes arose prior to the Cambrian Explosion of fossils and that several lineages had originated before periods of global glaciation in the Precambrian."* [32]

**The Peterson et al. School (more recent divergences):**
- **Key argument:** Their results *"are in accord with the known fossil record and support the view that the Cambrian explosion reflects, in part, the diversification of bilaterian phyla."* [41][42]
- **Methodology:** Uses both minimum evolution (ME) and maximum likelihood (ML) molecular clocks, explicitly testing ecological predictions from molecular dates against the fossil record. Peterson & Butterfield (2005) found that *"ME brackets the appearance of Eumetazoa between 634 and 604 Ma, whereas ML suggests it was between 867 and 748 Ma."* The ME estimate aligns with a major ecological regime shift in the Proterozoic acritarch record. [42]
- **Key finding:** *"The conspicuous stasis exhibited by pre-Ediacaran acritarch assemblages stands as bona fide evidence of the absence of pre-Ediacaran eumetazoans, while the early Ediacaran turnover provides compelling evidence for their evolutionary appearance."* [42]

**The core methodological dispute:**
- **Fossil calibration use:** Hedges/Blair argue that Peterson et al. used fossils as maximum limits rather than minimum limits, artificially pushing dates younger. Peterson et al. argue that their approach is validated by congruence with the fossil record and ecological tests.
- **Model selection:** Hedges/Blair argue that complex rate models used in some Bayesian studies produced flawed results. Peterson argues that their validation against ecological data confirms their approach.

**Current status of the debate:**
The debate remains **unresolved**. The Dos Reis et al. (2015) analysis provides the most sobering assessment: the uncertainty is simply too large to discriminate between these competing hypotheses. The Jiangchuan Biota (2026) discovery pushes complex animal fossils back to ~554–539 Ma, which is more consistent with the Peterson school's timeframe but does not definitively resolve the debate. [15][36]

## Section 5: Phylogenetic Implications of the Ctenophora-Sister vs. Porifera-Sister Debate

### 5.1 The Debate: Which Animal Lineage Branched First?

The debate concerns which extant animal lineage is the sister group to all other animals—the root of the animal tree of life. The two competing hypotheses are:
1. **Porifera-sister (Sponge-first):** Sponges (phylum Porifera) are the earliest branching animal lineage.
2. **Ctenophora-sister (Ctenophore-first):** Comb jellies (phylum Ctenophora) are the earliest branching animal lineage. [43][44][45]

### 5.2 Evidence for Ctenophora-Sister

**Ancient gene linkages (synteny) — Schultz et al. (2023), *Nature*:**
This study used a novel phylogenetic approach based on chromosome-scale gene linkage. Key findings: *"Conserved syntenic characters unite sponges with bilaterians, cnidarians, and placozoans in a monophyletic clade to the exclusion of ctenophores, placing ctenophores as the sister group to all other animals."* The study identified *"seven derived fusions shared by bilaterians, cnidarians and sponges to the exclusion of ctenophores, and four of these involve irreversible mixing of ancestral chromosomes."* Chromosome-scale gene linkage (synteny) evolves slowly, and rare irreversible fusion-with-mixing events provide robust phylogenetic characters. Bayesian phylogenetic analyses of the fusion character matrix *"strongly support the ctenophore-sister topology with high posterior probability."* [43]

**Recent phylogenomic analyses (Dunn et al., 2025 critique):**
The corrected re-analysis of the retracted Steenwyk and King (2025) *Science* paper found that after correcting methodological errors, *"the sponge-sister signal disappears and is replaced by strong support for ctenophore-sister."* [46]

### 5.3 Evidence for Porifera-Sister

**Traditional morphology:** *"The results of this study support the traditional morphologist's perspective on early animal evolution, placing sponges at the root of the animal tree."* [44]

**The (retracted) Steenwyk & King (2025) *Science* paper:**
Originally, this study found: *"All 490 statistically significant tests supported the sponge-sister hypothesis and none supported the ctenophore-sister hypothesis; the remaining 295 tests were inconclusive."* They *"constructed six large, highly informative data matrices from 100 genomes and transcriptomes, enriched with extensive nonbilaterian taxa, including sponges, ctenophores, and related outgroups."* [44][45]

**However, this paper was retracted in 2026** after a detailed critique by Casey Dunn and colleagues identified:
1. **Biased quartet scoring due to improper reference trees:** *"These choices create a systematic bias in favor of sponge-sister due to the substantially greater number of 29 sponges than 13 ctenophores in the dataset."* [46]
2. **Misuse of IQ-TREE software:** *"Instead of using the constraint trees to build maximum-likelihood trees... they calculated site log likelihoods on the constraint trees themselves."* [46]
3. **Incorrect likelihood calculations:** *"After correcting both likelihood and quartet scoring, the sponge-sister signal reported by SK disappears and is replaced by strong support for ctenophore-sister."* [46]

The retraction statement noted: *"We found that phylogenetic trees based on the 88 consistent genes identified by SK... support the ctenophore-sister hypothesis... Strikingly, the phylogenetic tree based solely on the 82 genes SK scored as consistent for sponge-sister also strongly supports the ctenophore-sister hypothesis."* [46]

### 5.4 The 2026 Preprints.org/ResearchGate Synthesis

**Yue, W. (2026)** — "Sponges or Ctenophores? A Synthesis of Evidence on the Root of the Animal Phylogeny" (Preprints.org, 202601.1072)—reviews the state of the debate. [47]

**Key findings from the synthesis:**
- "Porifera-sister" and "Ctenophora-sister" each have strong bodies of evidence, and the debate remains unresolved.
- Gene loss can affect tree topology as a key confounding factor.
- Long-branch attraction (LBA) and model fitting errors significantly misrepresent phylogenetic trees, shifting support between hypotheses.
- The synthesis documents that trait evolution differs fundamentally under each hypothesis. [47]

### 5.5 Implications for Interpreting the Cambrian Explosion

**Under Porifera-sister (sponge-first):**
- The ancestral animal was **simple**—resembling a sponge without neurons, muscles, or a complex gut.
- Nervous systems, muscles, and complex body plans evolved **once** in the eumetazoan ancestor after sponges branched off.
- This is consistent with a relatively straightforward narrative: complexity increased gradually, with the Cambrian Explosion representing the rapid diversification of complex body plans once the genetic toolkit was assembled.
- The Cambrian Explosion becomes about the **origin and elaboration of complexity** from simple beginnings.

**Under Ctenophora-sister (ctenophore-first):**
- The ancestral animal was surprisingly **complex**—with neurons, muscles, and a digestive system.
- This creates two possibilities, both with major implications for the Cambrian Explosion:
  - **Option A:** Nervous systems evolved **twice independently**—once in ctenophores and once in the lineage leading to cnidarians+bilaterians. This would mean that the evolution of nervous systems is easier than we think, and the Cambrian Explosion may partly represent the independent assembly of complex features.
  - **Option B:** The ancestral animal had a nervous system that **sponges subsequently lost**. This would mean that the ancestral animal was complex, and the Cambrian Explosion is not about the origin of complexity but about the **diversification of body plans** built upon pre-existing complex foundations.
- Either scenario complicates our understanding of the Cambrian Explosion. If a complex animal existed before the Cambrian, then the "explosion" may not be about the **origin** of animal complexity but about the **ecological success and morphological diversification** of already-complex animals.

**Current state of the debate:**
The debate has **NOT been resolved**. The 2025–2026 episode (the Steenwyk & King retraction) demonstrates the extreme sensitivity of these analyses to methodological choices, taxon sampling, and model selection. The Schultz et al. (2023) synteny study provides strong evidence for Ctenophora-sister, but the debate remains active. [43][46][47]

**Most recent status (May 2026):** Following the retraction, the balance of evidence currently favors **Ctenophora-sister**, though this remains contested. The Yue (2026) synthesis concludes that both hypotheses have strong support, and the debate is unresolved. The implications for the Cambrian Explosion are profound but speculative until the root of the animal tree is firmly established. [47]

## Section 6: Conclusions—The Most Up-to-Date Integrated Framework

### 6.1 The Thesis Revisited

The Cambrian Explosion is **not a single event with a single cause**. It is best understood as an **autocatalytic cascade** in which environmental, genetic, and ecological factors reinforced each other through positive feedback loops, producing the apparently explosive appearance of animal phyla in the fossil record. The newest evidence from 2024–2026 has refined—but not fundamentally overturned—this integrated view.

### 6.2 Which Synthesis Is Best Supported by Current Evidence?

The emerging synthesis supported by the newest evidence (2024–2026) can be characterized as follows:

**Phase 1: Necessary Preconditions (established well before the Cambrian, ~1,400–635 Ma)**
- **Oxygen availability:** The 2025 PNAS halite study demonstrates that oxygen levels sufficient for animal respiration existed at least transiently as early as 1.4 billion years ago. This does not mean oxygen was continuously high, but it means the *capacity* for oceanic oxygenation predates animals by hundreds of millions of years. [1][2]
- **Genetic toolkit:** The Hox gene cluster, GRN architecture, and developmental signaling pathways were assembled gradually during the Cryogenian–Ediacaran, as documented by Erwin et al. (2011) and Davidson & Erwin (2006). [33][34]
- **Cellular capabilities:** Phagocytosis, DNA repair mechanisms, and transposable-element responses were ancient features of unicellular eukaryotes, as the Zhang (2025/2026) cellular predation hypothesis emphasizes. [10]

**Phase 2: Trigger(s) (concentrated around 635–520 Ma)**
- **Moderate, sustained increase in shallow-water oxygenation:** The Stockey et al. (2024) *Nature Geoscience* study shows that a modest rise in atmospheric oxygen and marine productivity crossed critical ecological thresholds in shallow shelf environments—precisely where early animals lived. [4][5]
- **Localized, transient oxygenation events:** The Chanchai et al. (2026) study demonstrates that the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary was characterized by brief, localized oxygenation events rather than global anoxia-to-oxia transitions. [11][12]
- **The Jiangchuan Biota (2026)** demonstrates that complex bilaterian animals (deuterostomes, ambulacrarians) existed in the late Ediacaran, closing the gap between molecular clock predictions and the fossil record. [15][16]
- **Possible orbital forcing:** The Mills et al. (2025) hypothesis suggests that long-period orbital cycles triggered nutrient pulses and oxygenation pulses that coincided with Cambrian radiation events. [17][18]

**Phase 3: Amplifiers and Accelerators (520–505 Ma)**
- **Predator-prey arms races:** The Bicknell et al. (2025) study provides direct empirical evidence of reciprocal adaptation between predator and prey, demonstrating that ecological interactions were actively driving morphological evolution. [7][8]
- **Ecological feedback loops:** Complex food webs (documented by the Huayuan Biota, 2026) with apex predators, diverse arthropods, and filter-feeders created self-reinforcing selective pressures. [13][14]
- **Low ecological incumbency:** The "empty ecospace" of the early Cambrian allowed experimental body plans to persist and diversify without competitive exclusion.
- **The Zhang (2025/2026) cellular predation hypothesis** proposes that predation at the cellular level generated genomic raw material (chromosomal rearrangements, gene duplications) that fed into morphological innovation. [10]

### 6.3 The Role of Each Hypothesis in the Integrated Framework

| Factor | Role | Evidence Level |
|--------|------|----------------|
| **Oxygen** (Environmental) | **Permissive precondition**—necessary but not sufficient; modest shallow-shelf increases crossed critical thresholds | Strong (multiple geochemical proxies, but timing challenged by halite study) |
| **Genetic Toolkit** (Developmental) | **Substrate**—provided the developmental potential for morphological innovation | Very strong (molecular evidence), but does not explain timing |
| **Predator-Prey Arms Races** (Ecological) | **Proximate driver**—provided selective pressure for rapid morphological change | Strongest new evidence (Bicknell 2025), but correlation question remains |
| **Cellular Predation** (Zhang) | **Proposed mechanism linking ecology to genomics** | Novel/testable hypothesis; not yet validated |
| **Orbital Forcing** (Mills) | **Potential external pacemaker** for oxygenation pulses | Novel hypothesis; requires further testing |

### 6.4 What Remains Unknown

1. **The root of the animal tree:** The Ctenophora-sister vs. Porifera-sister debate remains unresolved, fundamentally affecting how we interpret the ancestral animal and the origins of nervous systems. [43][46][47]
2. **The precise timing of animal divergences:** Molecular clock estimates remain too uncertain (Dos Reis et al., 2015) to definitively correlate with geological events. [36]
3. **The relative importance of oxygen versus other factors:** The halite study (2025) demonstrates that oxygen existed earlier than expected, but whether it was sustained or transient remains unknown. [1][2]
4. **The viability of the cellular predation hypothesis:** Zhang's (2025/2026) proposal is novel but lacks direct experimental validation. [10]
5. **The extent of preservation bias:** The Jiangchuan Biota (2026) proves that some Ediacaran deposits can preserve complex animals—suggesting that the apparent absence of such animals from other Ediacaran sites is partly a taphonomic artifact. But the extent of this bias remains unknown. [15][16]

### 6.5 Final Thesis Statement

**The Cambrian Explosion was a genuine, rapid evolutionary radiation of animal body plans, driven by positive feedback loops among three necessary factors: (1) permissive environmental conditions, particularly moderate increases in shallow-water oxygenation and marine productivity; (2) a pre-existing genetic toolkit assembled during the Ediacaran, including Hox genes and hierarchical gene regulatory networks; and (3) escalating ecological interactions, particularly predator-prey arms races, which provided the selective pressure for rapid morphological diversification. The newest evidence (2024–2026) has weakened the oxygen-trigger hypothesis as a standalone explanation, strengthened the ecological feedback model through direct empirical evidence, and demonstrated that complex animals existed earlier than previously thought (Jiangchuan Biota). However, no single hypothesis has been falsified or proven predominant. The field is converging on an integrated multi-causal framework that recognizes the Cambrian Explosion as the product of convergent—and mutually reinforcing—environmental, genetic, and ecological factors operating within a unique window of Earth history.**
