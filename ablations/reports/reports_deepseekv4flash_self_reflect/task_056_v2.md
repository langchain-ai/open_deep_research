# New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market Analysis: Revised and Updated Report (May 2026)

## Executive Summary

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market, valued at approximately **NZD $1.8 billion** in 2025–2026, has experienced a complex trajectory from 2018 to 2024 marked by post-COVID contraction, rising spare capacity, and significant structural change. The industry comprises approximately **267 businesses** (2026) and employs around **4,011 workers**, processing roughly **100,000 tonnes of structural steel annually** against a total capacity that expanded from 120,000 to 150,000 tonnes between 2018 and 2023—resulting in spare capacity estimated at 41% as of September 2023.

The sector enters mid-2026 at a pivotal inflection point. Near-term headwinds from reduced construction activity, import competition, and global trade disruption are increasingly balanced by: (i) a **$267.7 billion national infrastructure pipeline** (Te Waihanga, December 2025); (ii) growing demand for green steel solutions driven by two major electric arc furnace (EAF) developments—New Zealand Steel's $300 million Glenbrook EAF now in commissioning and National Green Steel's $100 million Hampton Downs plant fast-track approved in March 2026; (iii) structural advantages in seismic-resistant construction where steel commands over 50% of multi-level construction nationally and 80% in Christchurch; and (iv) early signs of market recovery with construction activity forecast to reach $65.4 billion by 2030.

Key developments since the previous report include: the termination of anti-dumping duties on galvanised wire from China and Malaysia (effective November 2025); the initiation of new dumping and subsidy investigations into reinforcing steel bar and coil from China and Malaysia; Fletcher Building's sale of its Construction division to VINCI Construction (completed 29 May 2026) and binding agreement to sell Fletcher Reinforcing and Wire to United Industries for $15.7 million; Steel & Tube's FY25 net loss of $24.4 million followed by improved 1H26 results showing 8.1% revenue growth; and a sharp escalation in US Section 232 tariffs on steel to 50%.

**Reconciliation of Discrepant CAGR Figures:**

The two divergent CAGR figures—a **-6.0% decline from 2020–2025** and **+2.1% growth from 2021–2026**—reflect fundamentally different measurement bases:

- **The -6.0% CAGR (2020–2025)** is a **historical backward-looking measure** capturing the actual decline in market size over the five-year period ending in 2025. This captures the post-COVID construction downturn, weaker local construction trends, declining import penetration, rising interest rates, and the broader economic slowdown that saw total construction activity decline from $63.0 billion in 2023 to $55.7 billion in 2025.

- **The +2.1% CAGR (2021–2026)** is a **forecast/forward-looking measure** projecting modest recovery and growth over the 2021–2026 period. The different base year (2021 vs 2020) and forward-looking nature explain the discrepancy. IBISWorld forecasts continued growth over the next five years, though the industry remains sensitive to economic factors, regulation, and market volatility.

These are fundamentally different metrics covering different time periods with different bases. The +2.1% figure should be interpreted as IBISWorld's projected recovery trajectory, while the -6.0% figure captures the actual historical trough.

### (iii) Tonnage Fabricated

**Actual Throughput:**
The industry has consistently processed approximately **100,000 tonnes of structural steel annually** throughout the 2018–2024 period, as reported across multiple SCNZ quarterly updates from November 2020 through September 2023. More recent data from HERA's FY2025 Annual Report (July 2025) confirms "declining steel demand due to a slowdown in construction activity" and "steel volumes saw a significant downturn" [5][6][7][9][10].

**Industry Capacity:**
- **2018–2020**: Estimated fabrication capacity of **120,000 tonnes per annum**
- **September 2023**: Capacity had risen to **150,000 tonnes per annum**—a 25% increase over five years, driven by investment in new workshops and technology
- Historical growth trajectory: "The industry has progressively increased its capacity from 20,000 to 120,000 tonnes over 20 years" (November 2020), then further to 150,000 tonnes by September 2023 [7][9][15]

**Capacity Utilisation and Spare Capacity—Explanation of the Gap:**

The gap between capacity (~120,000–150,000 tonnes/year) and actual throughput (~100,000 tonnes historically, estimated ~88,500 tonnes at 41% spare capacity in September 2023) reflects the **cyclical nature of construction demand**:

| Period | Capacity | Spare Capacity | Implied Throughput |
|--------|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Year ending December 2021 | 120,000 t/yr | **35%** | ~78,000 t |
| Year ending March 2022 | 120,000 t/yr | **37%** | ~75,600 t |
| Year ending September 2023 | 150,000 t/yr | **41%** | ~88,500 t |
| Year ending March 2024 | 120,000 t/yr | **28%** | ~86,400 t |

**Key implications of the capacity-throughput gap:**

1. **Capacity has been actively increasing** through industry investment in advanced machinery, technology, and new facilities. SCNZ notes: "Over the past five years, New Zealand's structural steel industry has seen its total capacity rise from 120,000 to 150,000 tonnes, underscoring the sector's forward-thinking nature."

2. **Actual throughput fluctuates with construction demand**—the MBIE National Construction Pipeline Report confirms total construction activity declining from $63.0 billion in 2023 to $55.7 billion in 2025. The HERA FY2025 Annual Report confirms "steel volumes saw a significant downturn."

3. **Spare capacity is deliberately maintained** as a strategic buffer. SCNZ explicitly states that the spare capacity demonstrates "the industry has sufficient local capacity and skilled workforce, negating the need for offshore sourcing." The capacity buffer allows the industry to handle demand spikes, such as post-earthquake reconstruction or major infrastructure projects.

4. **The buffer is now shrinking** as the market recovers—from 41% in September 2023 to 28% in March 2024. As construction activity rebounds from 2026, spare capacity will absorb demand before requiring new capacity additions.

The IBISWorld industry ranking identifies the top **pure structural steel fabricators** by market share within ANZSIC code C2221 (Structural Steel Fabricating). **This report presents two parallel rankings:**
1. **Pure structural steel fabricators** (IBISWorld methodology): Companies primarily engaged in fabricating structural steel components
2. **Steel companies with fabrication capability**: Larger entities where structural steel fabrication is part of a broader product portfolio

Both are presented to give a complete picture, with clear labelling.

### (ii) Preferential Tariff Rates Under Free Trade Agreements

New Zealand has negotiated comprehensive FTAs providing **0% duty-free access** for steel imports from FTA partner countries:

**Active FTAs with 0% Steel Tariffs:**
- **Australia** (CER)—NZ's closest trading partner
- **China** (NZ-China FTA, 2008)—NZ's largest steel import source
- **Korea** (NZ-Korea FTA, 2015)
- **EU** (NZ-EU FTA, effective 1 May 2024)—91% of tariffs eliminated immediately, all EU steel products duty-free from entry into force
- **UK** (NZ-UK FTA)—immediate elimination of NZ tariffs on UK goods
- **CPTPP**—covers Japan, Canada, Australia, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, Brunei, Chile, Mexico, Peru (now 12 members with UK accession)
- **RCEP**—includes China, Japan, Korea, Australia, ASEAN countries
- **India** (India-NZ FTA signed 27 April 2026)—New Zealand will provide 100% duty-free access to Indian exports; India will liberalize ~70% of tariff lines covering ~95% of NZ exports

**Key Insight:** The **preferential rate of 0% applies to steel imports from virtually all significant steel-exporting nations** that trade with New Zealand. Under the Working Tariff Document, preferential tariff codes include: AU, CN, EU, HK, KR, LDC, LLDC, MY, PPP, RCEP, SG, UK, AAN, CPT.

The NZ-EU FTA (effective 1 May 2024) has made all EU steel products entering NZ duty-free from day one. European Union-New Zealand trade increased by 14% since the FTA came into force.

### (iii) Anti-Dumping Duties on Steel Products—Comprehensive Update (as of May 2026)

#### Termination of Anti-Dumping Duties on Galvanised Steel Wire (China & Malaysia)—CONFIRMED

**Status: Terminated effective 1 November 2025.**

On 28 October 2025, Hon Scott Simpson, Minister of Commerce and Consumer Affairs, announced the termination of anti-dumping duties on galvanised steel wire imported from Malaysia and China, effective 1 November 2025. The decision was made under section 17Y(1) of the Trade (Anti-dumping and Countervailing Duties) Act 1988 due to the **cessation of production of like goods in New Zealand**.

The official Gazette notice (2025-go6156) states: "I hereby terminate, with effect from 1 November 2025, anti-dumping duties on the goods specified in the schedule to this notice, due to the cessation of production of like goods in New Zealand."

The duties had applied to galvanised steel wire of high, medium, and low tensile strength between 2mm and 4.5mm in diameter, classified under tariff items ex 7217.20.10 and ex 7217.20.90.

**Historical Context:** In November 2020, following MBIE's Step 1 investigation, anti-dumping duties ranging from **24% to 36%** were imposed on Chinese suppliers found to be dumping (except specific suppliers like Bekaert (Qingdao) and Tianjin Bluekin/Ocean King). Imports from Indonesia were cleared. The Step 2 investigation confirmed the duties were in the public interest. The New Zealand market for galvanized wire was found to be highly price sensitive, with alternative suppliers available from Indonesia, Australia, Canada, Korea, Thailand, and Taiwan.

#### NEW Anti-Dumping Investigations (2025–2026): Reinforcing Steel Bar and Coil from China and Malaysia

MBIE has launched **two new investigations** into alleged dumping and subsidisation of **reinforcing steel bar and coil (rebar) from China and Malaysia**:

1. **Dumping investigation**: Reinforcing steel bar and coil from China and Malaysia
2. **Subsidisation investigation**: Reinforcing steel bar and coil from China

The complainant is **New Zealand Steel (BlueScope)**, which claims Chinese steel imports are subsidised by the Chinese government, causing material injury to New Zealand's steel industry. The company has publicly warned that a "tremendous surge" in Chinese steel production has resulted in more steel than there is world demand, causing job losses globally.

**Important distinction:** This is a **NEW investigation**—distinct from the 2017 investigation into rebar from China and Malaysia, which was terminated after MBIE concluded "on the basis of a weighted-average export price to weighted-average normal value comparison, there was no dumping of the subject goods occurring on imports from China or Malaysia during the period of investigation."

The MBIE Trade Remedies page confirms the new investigation is ongoing as of May 2026, with "No duty applied" as yet.

**Broader context:** China's galvanized coil capacity is projected to expand further in 2026. A massive wave of anti-dumping actions across Asia in 2025–2026 indicates significant steel diversion pressures from global overcapacity (552 million tonnes excess in 2023) and the closure of major markets:

### (iv) US Trade Policy Impact on New Zealand Steel

**Section 232 Tariffs—Escalation:**

On 3 June 2025, President Trump issued a proclamation increasing Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminium, and derivative products from **25% to 50%**, effective 4 June 2025. This global tariff increase applies to all countries **except the UK** (which maintained 25%).

**New Zealand-specific situation**: New Zealand has **not secured any exemptions** or quota arrangements. The effective tariff rate on NZ steel exports to the US is **53%** (3% MFN base rate + 50% Section 232 duty). The UK is the only country with a partial exemption.

**Further Escalations (2026):**

1. **24 February 2026**: President Trump signed Proclamation 11012 instituting a 10% tariff on most goods under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, lasting up to 150 days

2. **6 April 2026**: Revised Section 232 tariff structure—25% duty on the full value of imported products containing steel and aluminium (replacing previous 50% on embedded metal content). Products with ≤15% metal content are exempt and face a separate 10% baseline tariff

3. **US Supreme Court ruling (February 2026)** : The Court ruled (6-3) that President Trump exceeded his authority under IEEPA. Trump immediately announced new tariffs under Section 122, allowing temporary 15% tariffs. **Refunds of up to NZ$1 billion** may be available to NZ exporters (application process commenced 20 April 2026)

**Impact on NZ Steel Exports to US:**

NZ steel exports to the US are minimal—US imported **$5 billion** in goods from New Zealand in 2024, with steel & iron products forming a small segment. NZ steel exports to the US were valued at **US$61.04 million** during 2025. The 53% effective tariff rate renders NZ steel exports to the US uneconomic compared to competitors like Australia and the UK (15% effective rates).

**Global Diversion Impact:**

The US tariffs are causing significant global steel diversion. With Chinese steel exports reaching an estimated 100 million metric tons in 2024 (highest since 2016), and global steelmaking capacity exceeding production by 552 million metric tons, surplus steel is being diverted to markets like New Zealand. This increases import competition pressure on local fabricators despite NZ being a relatively small market ($360M iron & steel imports in 2024).

[42][43][44][45][46][47]

### (v) Effective Tariff Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

**Direct Tariff Cost Impact:**
- For fabricators sourcing steel from **FTA partner countries** (the vast majority of NZ's steel imports): **effective tariff impact is 0%** (duty-free entry)
- For fabricators sourcing from **non-FTA countries**: typical MFN rate of **5%** adds to landed cost, directly compressing profit margins
- In practice, the **0% preferential rate covers China, Australia, Korea, Japan, EU, UK, CPTPP, and India (from signature)** —covering the overwhelming majority of steel imports

**IBISWorld Assessment:**
The Structural Steel Fabricating industry analysis states: "Import tariffs moderately impact the industry, while export tariffs have minimal effect" and "Import penetration has plummeted in recent years, partly due to weaker local construction trends, the slump in global steel prices and the escalation in freight costs."

**Quality Compliance as a Non-Tariff Barrier:**
Imported fabricated steel faces additional quality compliance costs: NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing for mechanical properties and Charpy V-notch toughness at seismic-critical temperatures. This creates a significant non-tariff barrier that can negate import price advantages.

The collapse of Christchurch-based **Challenge Steel** (with debts of $10 million, unsecured creditors owed $6.1 million) illustrates that **quality assurance costs can negate import price advantages** from Chinese steel, particularly when extended project delays compound financial pressures.

## (e) Top 3 Strategic Risks and Opportunities—Updated (May 2026)

### Top 3 Strategic Risks

#### Risk 1: Competition from Imported Fabricated Steel and Global Steel Diversion

**Nature of the Risk (Escalated):**
NZ structural steel fabricators face sustained and potentially increasing competition from cheaper imported fabricated steel, particularly from China, compounded by global trade diversion from US tariffs.

**Evidence:**

- **Global Steel Overcapacity**: Global steelmaking capacity exceeded production by **552 million metric tons** in 2023. China's steel exports reached an estimated **100 million metric tons in 2024** (highest since 2016). With US tariffs closing the world's largest market, diverted steel is finding destinations in markets like New Zealand.

- **New Anti-Dumping Investigations**: MBIE's new investigations into reinforcing steel bar and coil from China and Malaysia (initiated 2025–2026) confirm that New Zealand Steel (BlueScope) alleges material injury from Chinese subsidised imports. This follows the termination of galvanised wire duties in November 2025 due to cessation of domestic production.

- **Asia-wide Anti-Dumping Wave**: Japan (on Korean/Chinese galvanized coil, August 2025), Malaysia (on Chinese/Korean/Vietnamese galvanized iron, February 2025), South Korea (on Chinese steel bars, May 2026), Thailand, Canada, US, Dominican Republic, and Ukraine have all initiated or extended steel anti-dumping actions—indicating global steel diversion pressures.

- **Import Data**: NZ imports from China of articles of iron or steel reached **US$367.92 million** (2025, UN COMTRADE). Total NZ iron & steel imports were **$360 million in 2024** (OEC World) and **$249.92 million in 2025** (narrower classification). While import penetration has "plummeted" in recent years due to weaker domestic construction, the risk of resurgence as global markets close is significant.

- **Quality Compliance as Partial Defence**: NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing for seismic-critical properties. The collapse of Challenge Steel (Christchurch, ~2018, $10M debts) illustrates that quality assurance costs can negate import price advantages.

**Mitigating Factors:**
- 5% MFN tariff provides modest buffer (though 0% FTA rates for Chinese-origin steel eliminate this)
- Quality compliance costs for imported steel reduce price differential
- SFC certification covering 92.5% of output provides quality assurance advantage
- Seismic performance requirements favour local expertise and certified materials

**Impact on Margins:** **High and increasing.** Import competition compresses pricing power and forces local fabricators to compete on quality, speed, and technical service rather than price alone. The global steel diversion dynamic is a new and escalating risk factor.

## Conclusion and Outlook (May 2026)

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market enters mid-2026 at a critical inflection point. The industry has weathered a period of severe contraction—market size down 6% in 2025, employment declining 8.9% annually since 2020, and major players like Steel & Tube recording $24.4 million losses. However, multiple indicators point to a cyclical recovery underway:

### Near-Term Outlook (2026–2027)

- **Recovery is beginning but uneven**: Steel & Tube 1H26 shows 8.1% revenue growth and 11.3% volume increase. MBIE forecasts total construction activity rising from $55.7B (2025) to $65.4B (2030). The Reserve Bank's OCR cuts to 2.25% support investment. However, the Middle East conflict has "delayed but not derailed" recovery, with GDP growth downgraded to 2.3%.

- **Infrastructure pipeline provides demand visibility**: $267.7 billion national pipeline (Te Waihanga), $21.4B projected spend in 2026, $11.8B entering procurement. Budget 2026 added $7B in capital investment across transport, health, schools, prisons, and defence. Major projects (CRL opening H2 2026, Dunedin Hospital steel from July 2026, Northland Expressway PPP commencing construction) provide near-term steel demand.

- **Green steel transition is now project-defined**: Glenbrook EAF commissioning underway ($300M); National Green Steel consent granted ($100M, 200,000 t/yr). Combined, these projects will retain up to 500,000 tonnes of scrap steel annually that would otherwise be exported, reducing import dependency by up to NZ$320M/year.

- **Labour market remains constrained**: Unemployment at 5.3% but skilled trades still hard to fill. Green List additions for welders (August 2025) reflect structural shortages. Automation and modular construction offer pathways to address capacity constraints.

### Medium-Term Outlook (2027–2030)

- **Construction activity forecast to reach $65.4B by 2030** (MBIE), with infrastructure at $19.6B
- **Steel market projected to grow at CAGR 6%** (Grand View Research, US$50.3M to US$68.5M)
- **Non-residential construction recovering to $13.5B by 2030**
- **Spare capacity being absorbed**—from 41% (September 2023) to likely tight market by 2028–2029
- **Green steel premium markets emerging**—government and institutional projects increasingly mandating low-carbon materials

### Principal Risks Requiring Active Management

1. **Import competition from diverted global steel**: US tariffs (50% Section 232), global overcapacity (552M tonnes), Chinese exports at 100M tonnes—surplus steel seeking destinations, putting pressure on NZ market. New anti-dumping investigations into rebar from China/Malaysia confirm industry concern.

2. **Labour and skills shortages**: Persistent structural gap despite rising unemployment. Requires continued investment in automation, training (20,000 construction apprentices now in system), and immigration pathways.

3. **Trade policy uncertainty**: US tariffs, Middle East conflict effects on energy/input costs, global trade fragmentation. OCR expected to rise (to ~3.28%), inflation above target (3.1%), posing headwinds to recovery.

4. **Capacity vs demand dynamics**: If infrastructure pipeline accelerates faster than expected, the industry's spare capacity (28–41%) could be rapidly absorbed, creating bottlenecks and cost inflation. Conversely, if recovery is further delayed, thin margins could force further consolidation.

### Strategic Opportunities

1. **Infrastructure super-cycle**: The $267.7B pipeline is the largest in NZ history, with funding certainty at $185B

2. **Green steel leadership**: NZ has two of the world's most advanced EAF projects for a country of its size, creating potential for export capability and premium-priced low-carbon products

3. **Technology and modular solutions**: Construction 4.0 ($10.3M research programme), global modular market (CAGR 5.4%), and proven steel advantages (10–15% shorter programmes, seismic performance, full recyclability) position steel fabricators for market share gains

4. **Seismic retrofit premium**: With structural steel at 50–80% of multi-level construction and NZS 3404 revisions in progress, steel fabricators with seismic expertise command premium positioning

Fabricators that successfully navigate these challenges—through investment in technology, quality certification (SFC, SSC), green steel capability, and workforce development—will be well-positioned to benefit from New Zealand's substantial infrastructure investment programme over the next decade. The industry's demonstrated capacity expansion (120,000 to 150,000 tonnes) and technology leadership (world-first robotic adoption) provide confidence in its ability to rise to the challenge.

### Data Limitations and Methodological Notes

1. **IBISWorld vs SCNZ data**: Employment figures differ (~4,100 vs ~5,000) due to scope differences. IBISWorld covers only ANZSIC C2221NZ (pure fabrication); SCNZ includes erectors, distributors, and related services. Both sources are cited with clear attribution.

2. **CAGR reconciliation**: The -6.0% (2020–2025) figure is historical; the +2.1% (2021–2026) figure is forecast. They cover different periods and should not be directly compared.

3. **Private company data**: Pure fabricators (Culham, D&H, Pacific Steel) are privately held. Revenue estimates are from third-party sources (IBISWorld, Growjo, ZoomInfo, Adapt.io) and should be treated as indicative.

4. **Anti-dumping timings**: The new rebar investigations from China and Malaysia are ongoing as of May 2026. No duties have been applied. The status can be confirmed at MBIE's Trade Remedies page.

5. **US tariff refunds**: The refund process (up to NZ$1B) commenced 20 April 2026 but requires exporters or US-based importers to apply. Outcomes are uncertain.

6. **Project timings**: Major infrastructure project completion dates are subject to change. CRL opening is stated as "H2 2026" with August–September 2026 expected. Dunedin Hospital outpatient building is "late 2026" and inpatient building "2029–2031."

7. **Green steel timelines**: National Green Steel's construction is conditional on meeting Fast-track consent requirements. Timeline to full production is stated as four years. Glenbrook EAF commissioning is "underway" with full production expected from "late 2026."
