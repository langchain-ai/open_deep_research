# Beyond the Cap: A Critical Re-Analysis of Carbon Pricing in California, the EU, and China (2020–2026)

## Executive Summary

This report provides a critical re-analysis of carbon pricing mechanisms across California's Cap-and-Invest Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China's national carbon market from 2020 to early 2026. It moves beyond descriptive comparison to examine the fundamental tensions, trade-offs, and political economy dynamics that shape each system's design and effectiveness. The core finding is that carbon pricing alone has not been the primary driver of emissions reductions in any of the three jurisdictions. Complementary policies—renewable portfolio standards, industrial policy, technology mandates, and energy market reforms—consistently account for the majority of observed emissions reductions, with carbon markets functioning primarily as backstop mechanisms and revenue-generating instruments.

Three overarching analytical themes emerge. First, **design tensions between cost containment and environmental ambition** are pervasive and inherent, not fixable through technical optimization. California's 2026 decision to allocate $4 billion in free permits to oil refineries, the EU's 2026 proposal to halt Market Stability Reserve invalidations, and China's decade-long reliance on intensity-based benchmarks all illustrate how political pressures consistently push systems toward cost containment at the expense of environmental stringency. Second, **cross-jurisdictional comparisons of carbon prices, compliance rates, or emissions outcomes are methodologically invalid without controlling for structural differences** in economic growth rates, industrial composition, energy mix, point of regulation, and stage of market development. Comparing China's ~$12/tonne price to the EU's ~€78/tonne price is meaningless when the former operates under an intensity-based system with no binding cap and 100% free allocation. Third, **the most transferable design features are institutional capacity-building mechanisms—robust MRV systems, legal frameworks, and price stability reserves—rather than specific policy instruments like absolute caps or auctioning percentages**, which must be adapted to each jurisdiction's political economy context.

Each system faces distinct existential challenges in 2026: California's program risks becoming a de facto carbon tax with an emissions ceiling rather than a declining cap; the EU faces an organized industrial revolt threatening to unravel the Green Deal's most ambitious reforms; and China's landmark pledge to transition from intensity-based to absolute caps by 2027 has yet to demonstrate credible enforcement capacity.

## 1. Introduction: Moving from Description to Critical Analysis

Carbon pricing has become the centerpiece of climate policy in developed and developing economies alike. By early 2026, carbon pricing instruments covered approximately 28% of global greenhouse gas emissions, up from 24% in 2024, a dramatic increase driven largely by China's expansion of its national ETS to cover steel, cement, and aluminum [1]. The three systems analyzed here—California's Cap-and-Invest Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China's national carbon market—represent the most significant experiments in market-based climate regulation globally, collectively covering over 9 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent annually.

However, the dominant policy discourse has focused overwhelmingly on descriptive comparison: which system has the highest carbon price, the broadest sectoral coverage, or the highest compliance rate. This approach obscures more than it reveals. It fails to address the fundamental question of whether carbon pricing mechanisms are actually driving emissions reductions or simply providing political cover for complementary policies doing the actual work. It ignores the deep structural differences that make naive cross-system comparisons methodologically indefensible. And it sidelines the political economy dynamics—interest group pressure, electoral cycles, economic shocks, and distributional conflicts—that determine whether carbon pricing systems are strengthened or hollowed out over time.

This report takes a different approach. It interrogates the evidence on emissions reductions attributable specifically to carbon pricing, examines the tensions between cost containment and environmental ambition that pervade all three systems, analyzes the political economy constraints that shape design decisions, and assesses the transferability of specific design features across jurisdictions. The conclusion offers actionable insights that move beyond the standard policy recommendations to address the political and institutional conditions that determine whether carbon pricing can deliver on its theoretical promise.

## 2. Design Tensions: Cost Containment vs. Environmental Ambition

### 2.1 The Universal Trade-Off

Every carbon pricing system faces a fundamental tension between cost containment (keeping carbon prices low enough to maintain political support and avoid economic disruption) and environmental ambition (setting a carbon price high enough to drive meaningful emissions reductions and technological transformation). This tension is not a design flaw that can be optimized away; it is an inherent feature of carbon markets operating within real political economies. The German Environment Agency's analysis of EU ETS Market Stability Reserve reform proposals crystallizes this trade-off: more generous or prolonged MSR releases lower prices but inflate supply and cumulative emissions, undermining cap integrity [2]. Conversely, tighter supply drives higher prices but triggers industrial backlash and political resistance.

The three systems have managed this tension in fundamentally different ways, reflecting their distinct political contexts. California has opted for explicit price containment mechanisms (floors, ceilings, and reserves) that treat price levels as a direct object of policy design. The EU has chosen volume-based adjustments through the MSR, focusing on managing allowance supply rather than setting prices directly. China has effectively avoided the tension entirely by maintaining a system where allowance supply is endogenous to production (intensity-based), carbon prices remain below levels that would meaningfully constrain economic activity, and allowances are 100% freely allocated.

### 2.2 California: The $4 Billion Price Ceiling Controversy

California's approach to price containment is the most explicit and codified of the three systems. The program features an auction reserve price (set at $27.94 per allowance in 2026), an Allowance Price Containment Reserve (APCR) with two tiered price thresholds (starting at approximately $65 in 2021 and rising annually), and a hard price ceiling (starting at $65 in 2021 and rising to $102.52 by 2026) [3]. When the price ceiling is triggered, CARB must create as many new allowances as needed to meet demand, regardless of the emissions cap. As critics have noted, this transforms the cap-and-trade program into "a backdoor carbon tax that provides no emissions certainty and guts the revenue-raising capacity of the program" [4].

The tension inherent in this design became starkly visible in May 2026, when CARB approved a comprehensive update creating a new Manufacturing Decarbonization Incentive pool of 118.3 million additional allowances above the cap—worth up to **$4 billion in free pollution permits**—with half reserved specifically for the fossil fuel sector [5]. The free permit pool was capped at the exact number of permits that must be retired to hit California's 2030 climate target, meaning the new subsidies could completely offset the planned emissions reductions. The Legislative Analyst's Office projected that quarterly auction revenue for state climate programs would drop from roughly $4 billion to $2 billion per year, jeopardizing funding for affordable housing, public transit, and drinking water in low-income communities [6].

The official justification was framed as a response to soaring gasoline prices (averaging above $6 per gallon) and global market pressures from the Iran-Israel conflict. "We want to make sure that there's reliable, affordable fuel for California consumers while the demand persists," said Rajinder Sahota of CARB [7]. Environmental advocates were scathing: "We are really concerned that this would significantly kneecap the program," said Chloe Ames of NextGen Policy [8]. Three of CARB's own board members voted against the measure.

This episode illustrates a broader pattern: when cost containment and environmental ambition collide, **cost containment consistently wins in California**. The Western States Petroleum Association (WSPA), which spent $20.1 million lobbying on cap-and-trade from 2015 through early 2017, has successfully shaped the program's trajectory to prioritize affordability and refinery viability over emissions reductions [9]. The 2017 AB 398 compromise—which made CARB the sole regulator of GHGs (preempting local air districts), capped auction prices, and inserted industry-friendly provisions—was reportedly written at WSPA's request [10].

### 2.3 EU ETS: MSR Success, Then Retreat

The EU's Market Stability Reserve has been widely celebrated as a successful mechanism for managing the tension between cost containment and ambition. By withdrawing surplus allowances when the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation (TNAC) exceeds 1,096 million tonnes and releasing allowances when surplus falls below 400 million, the MSR has reduced the structural surplus from over 2.1 billion allowances in 2013 to approximately 1.14 billion in 2024 [11]. Since 2023, allowances held in the MSR above threshold levels have been invalidated annually—permanently removing them from the market—with 3.2 billion allowances invalidated in total [12].

However, on April 1, 2026, the European Commission proposed **COM (2026)153**, a decision to cease the automatic invalidation of allowances held in the MSR above 400 million [13]. The Commission argued that the historical surplus has been almost entirely reabsorbed and that continued invalidation risks creating "structural scarcity in the second half of the next decade" that would drive prices to unsustainable levels. The proposal transforms the MSR from a "destructive" mechanism into a strategic buffer to "make the Green Deal economically sustainable for European industry" [14].

This retreat from invalidation demonstrates that even the most mature, institutionally robust carbon market faces political limits on environmental ambition. Helen Clarkson, CEO of Climate Group, criticized the proposal as "a knee-jerk reaction to the war, but it's only creating uncertainty for companies. It risks weakening Europe's carbon market by lowering the carbon price long-term and reducing pressure on industries to cut emissions" [15]. Yet the Commission's assessment—that the MSR has eliminated the surplus and now faces the opposite problem of potential scarcity—highlights a fundamental asymmetry: political systems find it far easier to tighten supply when there is a visible surplus than to maintain tight supply when scarcity becomes real and imposes costs on industry.

### 2.4 China: Avoiding the Tension Through Design

China's national carbon market has effectively managed the cost containment vs. ambition tension by designing a system where the tension barely exists. The system uses an **intensity-based tradable performance standard** rather than an absolute cap: allowances are allocated per unit of output based on sectoral benchmarks, meaning total allowable emissions rise with production [16]. This was a deliberate choice: as Zhu Keli, founding director of the China Institute of New Economy, noted, "While this approach avoids hurting economic growth from restricting output, it has also resulted in overgenerous quota allocations and weakened the effectiveness of the carbon market" [17].

The implications are profound. A Chinese power plant that increases output can have increasing absolute emissions while still complying (if its intensity improves). An EU plant with the same intensity improvement but constant or declining output would show absolute reductions. Comparing the two systems' "effectiveness" requires choosing a metric, and that choice embeds a normative judgment about what constitutes success.

China's carbon price has risen steadily from ~$7/tonne at launch in 2021 to approximately $12-14/tonne in 2024-2025—a 22% increase—but remains a fraction of the EU's ~€78/tonne price [18]. This price gap is not simply a matter of "less ambition"; it reflects a fundamentally different system structure where allowance supply adjusts with output, making scarcity-driven price increases structurally impossible. The IEA projects that transitioning to a cap-and-trade system with auctioning could double China's electricity-related emissions reductions by 2035 and generate significant revenues [19].

### 2.5 Offsets: A Shared Failure of Integrity

All three systems have grappled with offset quality issues, though the severity varies dramatically. California's offset program has been the subject of intense controversy. The Berkeley Carbon Trading Project's May 2025 policy brief found that California sends approximately **$140 million out of state each year for carbon offsets, most of which have little-to-no actual climate benefit** [20]. Three-quarters of offset credits issued are from improved forest management projects, but studies found "little evidence that these projects result in real emissions reductions" [21].

The EU ETS has already phased out international offsets entirely (no CDM/JI credits accepted since Phase IV began), learning from "past mistakes, above all the generous issuing of offsets resulting from dubious international climate protection projects" [22]. However, the 2040 climate target proposal allows up to 3% of emissions reductions to be met with international carbon credits, representing a significant policy shift [23].

China's domestic offset program (CCER), relaunched in January 2024 after six years of suspension, has strict protocols but limited operational history. The German Environment Agency's approval of 66 Chinese carbon projects without on-site inspections—later revealed to involve widespread fraud—illustrates the global challenge of offset integrity [24]. A joint investigation by DW and ZDF uncovered a billion-euro carbon credit scam involving Chinese projects where "preexisting installations were falsely presented as new" and major oil firms including Shell, Exxon, Total, and BP had invested in these credits [25].

## 3. Political Economy: The Real Determinant of Carbon Pricing Trajectories

### 3.1 California: Interest Group Capture and Electoral Dynamics

California's cap-and-invest program illustrates how interest group pressure, not technical optimization, determines carbon market design. The Western States Petroleum Association's $20.1 million lobbying expenditure from 2015 through early 2017 was instrumental in shaping AB 398, which included a provision barring local air districts from regulating GHGs—language reportedly inserted at WSPA's request [26]. Utilities including Pacific Gas & Electric, Southern California Edison, and Sempra Energy collectively spent over $9 million lobbying, supporting cap-and-trade for its flexibility while advocating against changes to free allowances for gas and electric utilities [27].

The 2025 reauthorization battle demonstrated the fusion of climate policy with broader political horse-trading. Governor Newsom struck a late-night deal in September 2025 to extend cap-and-trade through 2045, simultaneously approving measures to boost California's domestic crude oil production by waiving certain environmental reviews in Kern County and facilitating new drilling permits and pipeline rehabilitation [28]. The deal guaranteed $1 billion annually for high-speed rail and another $1 billion for discretionary use by lawmakers, alongside creating a Western regional energy market and $18 billion replenishment of the state's wildfire liability fund [29].

Public opinion provides a partial counterweight. A July 2025 PPIC statewide survey found approximately 60% of Californians support the cap-and-trade system, with 69% believing it is important for revenues to support low-income communities [30]. However, the structure of political influence—concentrated industry benefits versus diffuse consumer costs—creates an asymmetry that consistently favors industry interests when design decisions are made.

The state's **$12 billion budget deficit** in 2025 created additional pressure to prioritize revenue generation over environmental ambition. The cap-and-trade program has generated $34.5 billion in cumulative auction revenue since inception, making it a critical revenue source for a state facing fiscal constraints [31].

### 3.2 European Union: The Ten-Country Revolt and Green Deal Hollowing

The EU ETS faces the most organized industrial pushback in its history. In March 2026, **ten EU countries**—Austria, the Czech Republic, Croatia, Greece, Hungary, Italy, Poland, Romania, and Slovakia—jointly condemned the ETS as an "existential risk" to their strategic industries [32]. Their demands included extending free carbon allowances beyond 2034, slowing the phase-out from 2028, reducing carbon price volatility, and preventing excessive electricity price hikes. The letter warned that "energy prices have skyrocketed, inflation has made the investments required for the transition even more costly, and current decarbonising solutions are not yet sufficiently developed for economic sustainability for hard-to-abate industries" [33].

The chemical sector reported that over 100 facilities have shut down, wiping out 75,000 jobs, attributed partly to high carbon costs under the ETS [34]. Seven other EU nations—Denmark, Finland, Latvia, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Portugal, and Sweden—defended the ETS, arguing it "saved Europe about €34 billion per year" and supports cross-border electricity trade. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen hailed the ETS as "essential for lowering emissions and for channeling funds to clean technologies" [35].

This revolt is set against a broader pattern of "Green Deal hollowing out" identified by the Jacques Delors Centre. A succession of legislative revisions is diluting carbon pricing, investment support, and green standards. The 2040 climate target was weakened by allowing international carbon credits. ETS2 for heating and transport was delayed by one year. Vehicle emission standards were diluted. "Climate policies are being dismantled piece by piece, weakening Europe's competitiveness, resilience, and standing in the world" [36].

The June 2024 European Parliament elections, in which Green parties lost seats and climate-skeptic voices gained ground, created political headwinds for further ambition. However, analysts note that "the EU CO2 emissions trading scheme has been in existence for too long and has become a sophisticated market... it is hard to imagine regulatory changes that are too drastic" [37].

### 3.3 China: Authoritarian Coordination with SOE Constraints

China's political system enables rapid policy shifts that would be impossible in democratic contexts. The August 2025 "Opinions on Promoting Green and Low-Carbon Transformation and Strengthening the Development of the National Carbon Market"—issued jointly by the General Office of the Chinese Communist Party Central Committee and the State Council—represented a top-down directive to transition from intensity-based to absolute caps by 2027 and introduce auctioning by 2030 [38]. This is China's first central-level document dedicated to carbon market development, elevating oversight from the ministerial to the State Council level.

However, implementation faces structural constraints that no amount of central planning can easily overcome. **Chinese state-owned enterprises (SOEs) are responsible for 69% of all SOE emissions globally**. Environmental KPIs now constitute around 20% of total evaluation points for SOE leadership, but remain subordinate to political priorities, economic stability, and risk management [39]. SASAC (State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission) requires SOEs to focus investments on core activities, "limiting diversification into cleaner technologies and fostering risk-averse decision-making" [40].

The power sector's regulated structure fundamentally constrains ETS effectiveness. Because the government controls electricity prices and prevents carbon cost pass-through, the carbon price signal does not reach consumers or affect dispatch decisions [41]. The 2015 power market reforms "have progressed slowly, with capacity payments encouraging coal power expansion contrary to decarbonization goals" [42].

President Xi Jinping's direct involvement provides political weight: he has called for "creating a more effective, vibrant, and internationally influential carbon market" [43]. But the gap between central pronouncements and local implementation remains substantial. Provincial-level decision-making and capacity payments have continued to encourage coal power expansion, and the system's rapid expansion from power to steel, cement, and aluminum (adding 1,500 new entities) creates enormous MRV capacity challenges.

## 4. Methodological Challenges: Why Naive Cross-System Comparison Is Invalid

### 4.1 The Point of Regulation Problem

The three systems take fundamentally different approaches to where they apply the regulatory obligation, creating apples-to-oranges comparisons for most standard metrics.

California employs a uniquely **broad upstream approach**: fuel distributors are covered at the point where fuel enters commerce, meaning the program effectively prices emissions from millions of smaller sources (vehicles, homes, small businesses) through only ~400 entities. This gives California coverage of ~76% of state emissions from a small number of regulated entities [44].

The EU ETS operates **purely downstream**: each large installation (>20 MW thermal input for combustion plants) reports its own emissions. This means direct accountability at the source but coverage limited to ~35% of EU emissions because small sources and transportation (outside aviation/shipping) are excluded [45].

China's system is also downstream but on an enormous scale: originally covering only the power sector (~40% of national CO₂), it has expanded to cover steel, cement, and aluminum (now >60% of national CO₂). However, its intensity-based design means there is **no binding cap on absolute emissions** [46].

Simple comparisons of "percent of emissions covered" are deeply misleading. An upstream system covering 76% is not "more ambitious" than a downstream system covering 35% when the former prices transport fuel and the latter directly constrains industrial point sources.

### 4.2 The Price Comparability Fallacy

Raw carbon price differentials—~€78/tonne in the EU, ~$38/tonne in California, ~$12/tonne in China—convey little without understanding the structural determinants of each price [47].

| Factor | California | EU ETS | China |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Cap type | Absolute, declining ~4%/yr | Absolute, declining ~4.3%/yr | Intensity-based (no absolute cap) |
| Auction share | ~42.8% | Up to 57% | 0% (100% free allocation) |
| Price floor | $27.94 (2026) | None | None |
| Price ceiling | $102.52 (2026) | None | None |
| Banking | Allowed (with limits) | Allowed | Restricted |

A Chinese carbon price of ~$12/tonne operates in a system where allowance supply adjusts with output—the "cap" is derived from output × benchmark intensity. This is structurally incapable of producing scarcity-driven prices. An EU price of ~€78/tonre reflects the cumulative effect of binding absolute caps, auctioning, and the MSR's withdrawal of surplus allowances—institutional features that took nearly two decades to develop [48].

The marginal compliance cost also differs fundamentally. In China, where all allowances are free, the marginal cost is the opportunity cost of not selling surplus allowances—a much weaker signal. In the EU, where most allowances must be purchased at auction, the price directly represents a cash cost. In California's hybrid system, different entities face different effective carbon prices depending on their free allocation share.

### 4.3 The Counterfactual Problem

To determine whether a carbon market has been "effective," one must answer: *What would emissions have been without the carbon market?* This counterfactual is inherently unobservable, and establishing it is methodologically fraught in any single jurisdiction—let alone across three with fundamentally different economic structures [49].

The three jurisdictions differ so dramatically in structural conditions that attributing observed emissions trajectories to carbon market design is precarious:

| Dimension | California | EU | China |
|-----------|-----------|-----|-------|
| GDP growth | ~2-3% annually | ~1-2% annually | ~5%+ (slowing) |
| Industrial structure | Post-industrial; services dominant | Post-industrial; declining industrial share | Industrial; manufacturing ~40% of GDP |
| Energy mix | ~50%+ renewable electricity; declining gas | ~40% renewable electricity; declining coal | Coal-dominant (~60% of electricity) |
| Emissions trajectory | Declining (peak ~2004) | Declining (peak ~1979-1990) | Still rising (peak projected ~2030) |

California's emissions began declining before its cap-and-trade program (2013) due to the recession, energy efficiency policies, and the decline of in-state manufacturing. EU emissions have been decoupling from GDP since 1990—long before the EU ETS existed. China's emissions continue to rise with economic growth; the ETS's goal is to flatten the trajectory, not achieve absolute reductions.

The UCLA Emmett Institute report concluded that California's cap-and-trade program "appears to have had only a modest impact on emissions reduction to date," with most reductions driven by **complementary policies**—a finding with profound implications for cross-jurisdictional learning [50].

### 4.4 Temporal Incomparability

Comparing the three systems at any single point in time compares fundamentally different stages of development:

- **EU ETS** in its 20th year of operation (Phase IV) with mature market institutions, four phases of accumulated experience, and an evolving relationship with other EU climate policies
- **California** in its 13th year, with a well-established market but still evolving its post-2030 framework (recently extended to 2045)
- **China** in its 4th year of the national system, having only completed one full compliance cycle, without an absolute cap, without auctioning, and still building foundational legal and institutional capacity

This is akin to comparing a university professor's research output, a PhD student's dissertation progress, and a freshman's first semester grades—all three may be "in the education system," but direct comparison of outcomes is inappropriate.

The most valuable comparison is arguably **China today vs. the EU ETS in Phase I (2005-2007)**—both start-up phases characterized by over-reliance on free allocation, price discovery challenges, concerns about data quality, political sensitivity about carbon costs on industry, and absence of robust market stability mechanisms. The EU's catastrophic overallocation in Phase I (when verified emissions were ~6% below the cap) and subsequent reform trajectory offers a powerful cautionary tale for China.

## 5. The Portfolio Problem: Carbon Pricing vs. Complementary Policies

### 5.1 Evidence That Complementary Policies Drive Reductions

The most consequential finding across all three systems is that **carbon pricing has not been the primary driver of emissions reductions**. The evidence consistently shows that complementary policies—renewable portfolio standards, energy efficiency mandates, vehicle emission standards, industrial subsidies, and coal phase-out schedules—account for the majority of observed reductions.

For California, the California Department of Public Health's Health Impact Assessment found that "a cap-and-trade program would account for less than 20% of all emission reductions under AB 32" [51]. A University of San Francisco Master's Project concluded that "complementary policies are the primary drivers of GHG emission reduction in California" [52]. The California Air Resources Board itself estimated that complementary policies would achieve ~80% of reductions needed for the 2020 target, with cap-and-trade responsible for ~22.5% (18 MtCO₂e) [53].

For the EU ETS, the attribution question is complicated by the "waterbed effect"—the fixed aggregate emissions cap means that any additional reduction from complementary policies in covered sectors must be exactly offset by increased emissions elsewhere in the system. The Kleinman Center analysis found that the Market Stability Reserve "punctures" this waterbed effect over time, but the "climate effectiveness" of overlapping policies varies dramatically: the German renewable energy incentives achieved reductions beyond domestic decreases (high effectiveness), while aviation taxes showed a 50% leakage rate [54].

For China, the attribution is even more complex. A 2025 study in Humanities and Social Sciences Communications found that "the pilot ETS policy reduces carbon emissions and carbon intensity, whereas the national ETS policy increases carbon emissions and carbon intensity in the short term" [55]. This counterintuitive finding reflects the intensity-based design: by providing an implicit output subsidy to efficient coal plants, the national ETS may have actually *increased* absolute emissions by encouraging production at facilities that would otherwise have been uneconomic.

### 5.2 The California Paradox: Cap-and-Trade as Backstop

California's climate policy architecture positions the Cap-and-Invest program as a backstop mechanism alongside sector-specific regulations. Approximately 75% of emission reductions are expected from complementary policies (RPS, LCFS, ZEV mandates), while the cap-and-trade scheme acts as a guarantee to meet overall emissions caps [56].

This creates a paradoxical dynamic: as complementary policies succeed in driving reductions, allowance demand falls, prices drop toward the floor, and the cap-and-trade market appears "ineffective." Yet the cap ensures that total emissions stay within the declining trajectory regardless of which policy does the work. Critics argue this means the carbon price is too low to drive innovation; supporters counter that the cap guarantees the outcome while allowing flexibility in how reductions are achieved.

The Borenstein, Bushnell, and Wolak (2017) analysis found "a 34% probability of allowance prices reaching the $60 above-floor price ceiling by 2030, 47% of prices settling at the floor, and 19% falling in between" [57]. Variations in complementary policy effectiveness "significantly alter the probability distribution of prices and abatement levels." The finding that prices are likely to remain at or near the floor for extended periods confirms that complementary policies, not carbon pricing, are doing the heavy lifting.

### 5.3 The EU's Policy Portfolio Evolution

The EU ETS has evolved from a standalone instrument to one component within an increasingly complex policy portfolio. The Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) targets 42.5% renewable energy by 2030, the Energy Efficiency Directive targets an 11.7% improvement, and the Effort Sharing Regulation covers non-ETS sectors (transport, buildings, agriculture). The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) addresses leakage concerns that free allocation alone could not solve [58].

The interaction between these instruments is not always harmonious. The Florence School of Regulation notes that "renewable energy policies brought about significant emission reductions in the power sector but at higher costs compared to EU ETS carbon prices" [59]. The Effort Sharing Regulation provides flexibilities such as the use of EU ETS allowances by Member States, but Carbon Market Watch warned that "allowing countries to use forestry or ETS credits instead of actually reducing emissions undermines both environmental integrity and the low-carbon transition" and that "loopholes could reduce effective cuts to 23%" [60].

The 2023 revision of the EU ETS Directive increased the emissions reduction target to 62% below 2005 levels by 2030 and extended coverage to maritime transport from 2024. The new ETS2, covering buildings and road transport, will start in 2027 (delayed from 2027 to 2028 after political pushback), complemented by the Social Climate Fund mobilizing €86.7 billion between 2026-2032 [61].

### 5.4 What This Means for Policy Design

The consistent finding across all three systems is that **carbon pricing works best as part of a diversified policy portfolio, not as a standalone instrument**. The SUERF Policy Brief demonstrates this empirically: achieving the 2°C target through carbon taxation alone requires prices of approximately $474/tCO₂, triggering widespread capital divestment and severe transition costs. A diversified portfolio combining carbon taxes with clean-tech subsidies and R&D support can deliver climate stabilization at substantially lower carbon prices of $51-178/tCO₂ [62].

This has three implications for carbon market design. First, **carbon markets should be evaluated on their contribution to a portfolio, not on their standalone impact**. Second, **the risk of policy redundancy is real but manageable**: overlapping policies can create inefficiencies (the "waterbed effect") but also address market failures that carbon pricing alone cannot (technology lock-in, network effects, information asymmetries). Third, **political sustainability may depend on distributing the burden across multiple instruments**: no single policy bears the full weight of adjustment costs, and each instrument builds its own constituency of supporters.

## 6. Transferability: What Travels and What Doesn't

### 6.1 Universally Applicable Features

Certain carbon market design features have demonstrated transferability across diverse institutional contexts:

**Market Stability Reserves**: The EU's MSR has been studied for application in other systems, including China. The core principle—a rules-based mechanism to adjust allowance supply in response to predefined surplus or deficit thresholds—appears widely applicable. Washington State and Québec have adopted similar mechanisms. However, the MSR's specific parameters (24% intake rate, 400-1,096 Mt thresholds, invalidation provisions) must be calibrated to each market's size and volatility.

**Output-Based Benchmarking for Free Allocation**: This method, used by both the EU and California, "rewards companies with low emissions intensity, and is generally regarded as a superior method to grandfathering" [63]. It creates incentives for efficiency improvements while protecting against leakage, and has been adopted by Korea, China, and other emerging systems.

**Robust MRV Systems**: The lesson from early failures—the EU's Phase I overallocation (2005-2007), the Clean Development Mechanism's integrity problems, and China's pilot data quality issues—is that MRV infrastructure must precede market development, not follow it. The World Bank's Partnership for Market Implementation emphasizes that "systems, platforms, rules, and data flows determine whether markets can function, connect, and be trusted" [64].

**Banking with Holding Limits**: All major systems allow banking of allowances for future use but impose holding limits to prevent market manipulation and ensure liquidity. This simple feature provides flexibility while maintaining market integrity.

**Price Floors**: California's auction reserve price provides a minimum carbon price that gives investment certainty. RGGI has a similar mechanism. The EU ETS lacks a formal price floor, contributing to historically low prices during Phase II.

### 6.2 Context-Dependent Features

Other design features are deeply contingent on political economy, institutional capacity, and market structure:

**Auctioning vs. Free Allocation**: The EU's transition to auctioning as the primary allocation method took three phases and 15 years to achieve, and still covers only about 57% of allowances. California auctions ~42.8% of allowances. China has yet to auction any allowances. The political resistance to auctioning is not technical but distributional: it shifts costs from taxpayers to regulated entities, creating concentrated opposition. The Korea ETS, despite modeling itself on the EU, has maintained extensive free allocation due to its different political economy, with the study concluding that "institutional complementarity limits the feasibility of linking the EU ETS with the K-ETS in the foreseeable future" [65].

**Absolute vs. Intensity-Based Caps**: China's intensity-based system was a deliberate choice to "avoid hurting economic growth from restricting output" [66]. While intensity targets are widely criticized by carbon pricing advocates as weak, they served China's political needs during its start-up phase. The August 2025 policy shift toward absolute caps by 2027 represents a major transition, but its feasibility depends on resolving the MRV and enforcement challenges that made intensity targets attractive in the first place.

**Point of Regulation**: California's upstream fuel-distributor model "requires concentrated fuel distributors, robust electricity import monitoring, and cross-jurisdictional legal authority" [67]. This model has been transferred to Québec and Washington State, but may not function in jurisdictions with fragmented fuel supply chains or limited legal authority to regulate imported electricity.

**Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanisms (CBAM)**: The EU's CBAM entered its definitive phase on January 1, 2026, with the first official CBAM certificate price set at €75.36 per tonne [68]. CBAM addresses the fundamental political economy constraint that made free allocation necessary: domestic industry cannot bear carbon costs if foreign competitors do not. However, CBAM requires border customs authority, legal compatibility with WTO rules, and sufficient administrative capacity—conditions that few jurisdictions outside the EU currently meet.

### 6.3 Lessons from Failed Transplants

The history of carbon market transfer attempts reveals patterns of success and failure:

| Attempt | Outcome | Key Lesson |
|---------|---------|------------|
| EU ETS → Korea ETS | Diverged: K-ETS more regulatory, less market-based | Institutional complementarity prevents simple transplantation; energy market liberalization is key |
| California → Québec linkage | Successful (since 2014) | Harmonization on core features (price floors, compliance periods, holding limits) enables linkage despite differences |
| California → Washington State | Successful | APCR model transferable with similar political and legal context |
| EU → Australia linkage attempt | Failed: Australia repealed carbon pricing (2014) | Political sustainability requires building supportive interest group coalitions |
| China pilot ETS → National ETS | Partial: pilots informed national design, but national system initially weaker | Pilots build capacity, but political economy constraints may weaken national design |
| Kyoto CDM → Offset markets | Mixed: widespread controversies | Strict offset quality controls essential; questionable environmental integrity undermines entire system |

The Australia cautionary tale is particularly instructive. The carbon tax repeal illustrated that "the best way to ensure lasting climate change policy is to use that policy to build a strong network of interest groups that support climate change policy" [69]. Australia's economy dominated by primary extraction interests, especially coal, created concentrated opposition, while the visible costs of carbon pricing to consumers (rising energy costs) created diffuse but politically potent discontent. The lesson is that carbon pricing cannot survive politically if it imposes visible costs on a broad population while delivering diffuse environmental benefits and concentrated costs on powerful industries.

## 7. The 2024-2026 Watershed: Recent Developments Across All Three Systems

### 7.1 California: AB 1207, SB 840, and the $4 Billion Controversy

September 2025 marked a pivotal moment for California's program with the passage of AB 1207 and SB 840, extending cap-and-trade through 2045 and renaming it "Cap-and-Invest" [70]. Key provisions include:

- **Cap Trajectory**: CARB must remove approximately 118 million allowances between 2027 and 2030 to align the cap with emissions data, equating to an 11% annual reduction through 2030 and 7% thereafter [71].
- **Offset Reforms**: The 6% offset usage limit was extended through 2045. Offsets are now fully "under the cap"—CARB must retire an equivalent number of allowances from the following year's budget for each offset credit used. At least 50% of offsets must be sourced within California [72].
- **Allowance Allocation**: Industry Assistance Factors remain at 100% through 2030. Utility allowance allocations will shift from natural gas suppliers to electric utilities by 2031 [73].
- **Price Ceiling Revenues**: All revenues from price ceiling sales now flow into a new California Climate Mitigation Fund to support affordability measures [74].

However, the May 2026 CARB decision to create a $4 billion free permit pool for oil refineries threatens to undermine these gains. The free permit pool was capped at "the exact number of permits that must be retired to hit California's 2030 climate target," meaning it could completely offset the planned emissions reductions [75]. The Legislative Analyst's Office projects quarterly auction revenue will drop from ~$4 billion to ~$2 billion per year [76].

California's program now faces an existential question: if the cap is not binding because additional allowances can be created above the cap, what distinguishes it from a carbon tax with an adjustable rate? As economist Danny Cullenward stated, "The proposal is being presented as a compromise when in fact it is sacrificing both of the key goals of the program" [77].

### 7.2 EU ETS: CBAM Implementation, MSR Reform, and Industrial Revolt

The EU ETS entered 2026 facing contradictory pressures. On one hand, the system's fundamentals are strong: emissions have halved since 2005, the 2030 target of a 62% reduction remains on track, and auction revenues reached €43.2 billion in 2024 [78]. On the other hand, the ten-country industrial revolt and the Commission's proposal to halt MSR invalidations signal growing political resistance to further tightening.

**CBAM Implementation**: The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism entered its definitive phase on January 1, 2026. On April 2, 2026, the European Commission published the first official CBAM certificate price at **€75.36 per tonne** for Q1 2026 [79]. For 2026, CBAM prices will be determined quarterly, shifting to a weekly system from 2027. Importers must apply for authorized declarant status and declare embedded emissions for imports exceeding 50 tonnes. CBAM covers cement, iron and steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen, covering approximately 50% of EU ETS emissions, with full phase-out of free allowances for these sectors by 2034 [80].

The CBAM represents a significant innovation in carbon pricing governance: it extends the EU's carbon price to imports, addressing leakage concerns without requiring foreign jurisdictions to adopt equivalent policies. However, critics argue it "acts as a trade barrier and undermines common but differentiated responsibilities under the Paris Agreement" [81]. A study of the announcement found "statistically significant negative cumulative abnormal returns of approximately 1.3 percentage points for EU-based customer firms with suppliers in CBAM-regulated industries outside the EU," representing "an estimated loss over 1 billion euros in market capitalization among treated firms" [82].

**MSR Reform**: The Commission's April 2026 proposal to cease automatic invalidation of MSR allowances has been met with strong criticism. Over 100 companies and investors urged European leaders to maintain a strong and stable EU ETS, arguing that weakening it would erode investment certainty [83]. The reform maintains the EU's climate targets and overall emission caps but represents a significant retreat from the MSR's invalidation mechanism, which had been widely celebrated as the EU's most successful institutional innovation.

**2025 Emissions Data**: On April 10, 2026, the European Commission published verified 2025 emissions data showing a 1.3% reduction compared to 2024. Power generation emissions fell by 0.4% despite a 1.7% rise in electricity output. Solar power experienced the biggest growth at 24.6% year-on-year, surpassing hydropower as the EU's second-largest renewable electricity source. Industrial emissions decreased by 2.5%, mostly driven by cement and iron/steel production [84].

### 7.3 China: Absolute Caps Pledged, CBAM Pressure Mounts

China's carbon market has undergone the most dramatic transformation of the three systems in 2025-2026:

**Sectoral Expansion**: On March 20, 2025, the Ministry of Ecology and Environment published a work plan expanding the ETS to cement, steel, and aluminum smelting, adding approximately 1,500 new entities and increasing coverage from 40% to over 60% of national CO₂ emissions [85]. The expanded system covers approximately 8 billion tonnes CO₂ annually—about 15% of global emissions [86].

**Phased Implementation**: Phase 1 (2024-2026) focuses on familiarization, with entities receiving free allowances equal to verified emissions (grandfathering). From 2025-2026, allocation shifts to carbon-intensity based performance benchmarking. Phase 2 (from 2027) aims to tighten allocation and move toward capping total emissions [87].

**August 2025 Opinions**: The landmark "Opinions on Promoting Green and Low-Carbon Transformation and Strengthening the Development of the National Carbon Market," issued jointly by the Communist Party Central Committee and the State Council, set a roadmap for transitioning from intensity-based to absolute caps by 2027, with auctioning by 2030 [88]. This is China's first central-level document dedicated to carbon market development, signaling heightened governmental commitment.

**15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030)**: Adopted in March 2026, the plan sets a target to reduce carbon intensity by 17% over the 2026-2030 period (approximately 3.8% per year), slightly lower than the previous 18% target. Non-fossil fuels should cover 25% of primary energy consumption by 2030. China aims to double new energy capacity within ten years [89].

**CBAM Pressure**: The EU's CBAM directly drives Chinese ETS reforms. CBAM-covered products make up only about 1.8% of China's total EU exports, primarily in aluminum and iron/steel, but China holds the greatest market share for all products in the CBAM scope [90]. The EU CBAM imposes a 2034 deadline for the CN-ETS to mature fully. Without accelerated decarbonization, China's steel and aluminum exports to the European market could face significant cost disadvantages.

**EU-China Carbon Pricing Alliance**: On May 7, 2026, the EU, China, and Brazil launched the Open Coalition on Compliance Carbon Markets in Florence, with Brazil chairing and China and the EU as co-chairs. The coalition aims to improve the integrity, transparency, and effectiveness of nationwide compliance carbon markets [91]. Kurt Vandenberghe, European Commission's climate director general, stated: "We need to make sure that these emissions trading systems talk to each other so that it becomes much easier for trading these carbon credits" [92].

## 8. Conclusion: Actionable Insights for Policymakers

### 8.1 Carbon Pricing Is Necessary but Not Sufficient

The evidence across all three systems is clear: carbon pricing alone has not been the primary driver of emissions reductions. Complementary policies—renewable portfolio standards, energy efficiency mandates, vehicle emission standards, industrial subsidies—consistently account for the majority of observed reductions. This does not mean carbon pricing is ineffective; it means carbon pricing functions best as a backstop mechanism that guarantees emissions stay within a declining cap while complementary policies drive the technological transformation.

### 8.2 Political Sustainability Requires Broader Coalitions

The Australia carbon tax repeal and the 2026 EU industrial revolt demonstrate that carbon pricing cannot survive politically if it imposes concentrated costs on powerful industries without building countervailing coalitions. Successful systems have:

- **Recycled revenue visibly to affected communities**: California's mandated 35% of revenues to disadvantaged communities, the EU's Social Climate Fund (€86.7 billion for 2026-2032), and the California Climate Credit (utility bill rebates to consumers) build constituencies that benefit from the program.

- **Provided transitional support for industry**: Free allocation phase-out must be gradual and predictable. The EU's pairing of CBAM implementation (2026-2034) with free allocation phase-out creates a politically feasible transition path. California's maintenance of 100% industry assistance through 2030 reflects similar logic.

- **Distributed costs across multiple instruments**: No single policy bears the full weight of adjustment costs. The portfolio approach spreads political risk: if carbon prices fall, complementary policies still drive reductions; if complementary policies stall, the carbon cap provides a safety net.

### 8.3 Institutional Capacity Determines Design Feasibility

The most transferable insight from cross-system comparison is that **institutional capacity must precede market ambition**. The EU's Phase I failure (2005-2007) was caused by launching a market without reliable emissions data. China's decision to start with an intensity-based system and 100% free allocation reflected realistic assessment of its MRV capacity and political constraints. California's upstream model works because its concentrated fuel distribution system and legal authority enable point-of-regulation efficiency.

Policymakers considering carbon pricing should prioritize building MRV infrastructure, legal frameworks, and enforcement capacity before attempting ambitious cap setting or auctioning. The World Bank's Partnership for Market Implementation emphasizes that "systems, platforms, rules, and data flows determine whether markets can function, connect, and be trusted" [93].

### 8.4 Absolute Caps Are Not Always Superior

While the carbon pricing literature overwhelmingly favors absolute caps over intensity-based systems, China's experience suggests a more nuanced conclusion. Intensity-based systems can provide valuable learning-by-doing for jurisdictions with limited institutional capacity and political constraints on carbon costs. They avoid the political backlash of constraining economic growth while building MRV infrastructure, regulatory experience, and market familiarity. The EU's own evolution—from Phase I's free allocation and data collection to Phase IV's auctioning and declining caps—took 15 years.

The key is to use intensity-based systems as a transitional mechanism with a clear roadmap toward absolute caps. China's August 2025 Opinions, setting milestones for absolute caps by 2027 and auctioning by 2030, provide exactly this kind of staged transition pathway.

### 8.5 Leakage Concerns Drive Policy Innovation

Carbon leakage concerns have been the single most powerful driver of policy innovation across all three systems. The EU's CBAM, California's exploration of border adjustments, and China's expansion of ETS coverage to CBAM-covered sectors all demonstrate that leakage concerns—not environmental ambition per se—are what push systems toward more sophisticated design. This suggests a political strategy: framing carbon pricing reforms as competitiveness measures rather than environmental regulations may attract broader support.

### 8.6 The Ultimate Question: Can Carbon Pricing Survive Political Capture?

The most troubling finding across all three systems is the persistent pattern of **political capture by incumbent fossil fuel interests**. California's $4 billion free permit pool (May 2026), the EU's ten-country industrial revolt (March 2026) and MSR invalidation halt (April 2026), and China's decade-long resistance to absolute caps all reflect the systematic influence of industries that benefit from weak carbon pricing.

Carbon pricing advocates often assume that well-designed markets will naturally generate political support as compliance entities become accustomed to trading and benefits accumulate. The evidence suggests the opposite: as systems tighten and carbon prices rise, political resistance intensifies. California's 2026 CARB decision shows that even in the most climate-ambitious state in the US, a $6/gallon gasoline price creates irresistible pressure to dilute the cap.

The survival of carbon pricing as a meaningful climate policy instrument depends not on technical optimization but on **building countervailing political power**: revenue recycling that creates visible beneficiaries, complementary policies that distribute adjustment costs, and transitional support that reduces industry opposition. Without these political foundations, the most sophisticated design features—MSRs, APCRs, benchmarking, CBAMs—will be progressively hollowed out as costs become real.
