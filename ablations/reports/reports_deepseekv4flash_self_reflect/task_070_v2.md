## Part I: Threshold Procedural Issues

### 1. Statute of Limitations

**The Correction:** The prior analysis incorrectly assumed a two-year statute of limitations for promissory estoppel. Under Texas law, the limitations period for promissory estoppel claims is **four years**, governed by the residual statute codified at Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.051. This statute provides: "Every action for which there is no express limitations period, except an action for the recovery of real property, must be brought not later than four years after the day the cause of action accrues." [1][2][3]

Multiple Texas legal sources confirm this:

- The Adams & Reese LLP Quasi-Contract Claims guide states: "In Texas, the statute of limitations for a promissory estoppel claim is four years (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ann. § 16.051; Prestige Ford Garland v. Morales...)." [1]
- Houston-Opinions.com confirms: "The statute of limitations for promissory estoppel is four years. – Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code §16.051 (Vernon 1997)." [3]

**Accrual Date Analysis:**

Texas case law on the precise accrual date for promissory estoppel is limited, but the general rule is that a cause of action accrues when a wrongful act causes a legal injury, regardless of when the plaintiff discovers the injury. For promissory estoppel, the claim accrues when the promise is broken—i.e., when the promisor fails to perform the promised act, causing the promisee to suffer injury from their reliance. [2][4]

Applying this to Hayes's timeline:

| Event | Date | Significance for Accrual |
|-------|------|-------------------------|
| Verbal promise made | April 2024 | Promise made; no breach yet |
| Hayes relies (sells home, signs lease) | April–May 2024 | Reliance damages incurred |
| Company policy changes (3 days/week in office) | June 2024: policy announced; July 1, 2024: effective | Promise broken |
| Hayes refuses requirement, resigns | July 8, 2024 | Employment ends |
| Lawsuit filed | Early 2025 | Well within 4-year window |
| Current date | May 30, 2026 | Still timely under all accrual theories |

**Conclusion:** Under any reasonable accrual theory—whether the claim accrued when the promise was broken (late June/July 1, 2024), when reliance damages were incurred (April–May 2024), or when employment ended (July 8, 2024)—the four-year statute of limitations runs until at least April–July 2028. A lawsuit filed in early 2025 is comfortably within the limitations period. As of May 30, 2026, the claim remains timely, and this is not a defense issue for the company.

### 3. Statute of Frauds

Texas's Statute of Frauds, Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 26.01(b), requires agreements "not to be performed within one year from the date of making the agreement" to be in writing and signed. [5][6]

**The Critical Counterargument:** If the employment is at-will and the remote work arrangement could be terminated at any time by either party, then the agreement **IS performable within one year**, and the Statute of Frauds does NOT apply.

**Texas law supports this argument:**

- Contracts with indefinite duration generally fall outside the statute if performance could be completed within a year. [5][6]
- In *Badgett v. Northwestern Resources Co.*, 818 F. Supp. 998 (W.D. Tex. 1993), the court recognized that the statute of frauds does not render unenforceable an indefinite-term oral contract if termination could occur within one year. [7]
- This is distinguishable from *Holloway v. Dekkers* (Tex. App. 2014), where a one-year employment contract with a fixed term fell within the Statute of Frauds. Unlike Holloway, an at-will arrangement with a promise of remote work has no fixed duration—it can end at any time. [8]

**Application:** A promise of "full remote work" as part of at-will employment does not specify any particular duration. The company could require in-office work at any time (as it did), or Hayes could quit at any time. Because the arrangement could be terminated within one year, the Statute of Frauds does not apply.

**Additional Exception:** Even if the Statute of Frauds did apply, Texas law recognizes that promissory estoppel can bar application of the Statute of Frauds and allow enforcement of an otherwise unenforceable oral agreement when certain reliance conditions are met. [1][5]

## Part II: The Four Elements of Promissory Estoppel

### 4. Element (a): Clear and Definite Promise

**The Promise Itself:** The hiring manager verbally promised Hayes "full remote work" during April 2024 negotiations. The June 2024 confirmation email—stating "you have been approved to work fully remote"—serves as critical written evidence of this promise.

**Under Texas law,** the promise must be "clear and definite" to support a promissory estoppel claim. In *Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co.*, the Texas Supreme Court held that a written price quote provided evidence of a verbal promise. [9] Similarly, the confirmation email here provides powerful written evidence of a specific promise.

**The Conflicting Offer Letter:** The May 1, 2024 offer letter states "hybrid schedule per company policy" without defining remote frequency. This creates a factual dispute but does not make the verbal promise indefinite. The verbal promise of "full remote work" is specific on its face—it states a clear term (no office attendance required). The conflict with the written offer letter creates a dispute about which representation should be enforced, not about whether the verbal promise was clear.

**The Contract Bar Doctrine:**

The contract bar doctrine holds that promissory estoppel is unavailable when a valid written contract covers the same subject matter as the alleged promise. *Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco, Inc.*, 52 S.W.3d 671, 684 (Tex. 2000); *Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp.* (5th Cir. 2013). [10][11]

**Critical Analysis:** Does the offer letter's "hybrid schedule per company policy" language cover the same subject matter as the verbal promise of full remote work?

**Argument that it does NOT cover the same subject matter:**

- The phrase "per company policy" is **vague and indefinite**. It does not specify what the company policy on remote work is, nor does it promise any particular schedule.
- The verbal promise of "full remote work" is a specific, definite promise of a particular arrangement. The written offer letter's reference to "hybrid schedule per company policy" could be interpreted as:
    1. The company was disclosing its general policy (which might or might not allow full remote work), not making a contractual promise about Hayes's specific arrangement.
    2. The written contract was **silent or ambiguous** on the specific promise of full remote work, meaning the verbal promise covered a subject **not addressed** in the written contract.

**Texas law supports this analysis:**

- *El Paso Healthcare Sys., Ltd. v. Piping Rock Corp.*, 939 S.W.2d 695, 699 (Tex. App.—El Paso 1997): "Promissory estoppel is not applicable to a promise covered by a valid contract between the parties; however, it applies to a promise outside the contract." [3]
- Under *Clinton Growers*, if the specific promise (full remote work) is **not directly dealt with** in the written contract (which merely references "hybrid schedule per company policy" without defining it), then the promissory estoppel claim could proceed because the contract does **not cover that subject matter**. [11]

**Distinction from *Barkley v. Connally*:** In *Barkley*, the oral agreement to repurchase a farmhouse was inconsistent with the written Purchase and Sale Agreement. Here, the offer letter's vague "per company policy" language is not clearly inconsistent with the verbal promise—it is simply incomplete on the specific issue of remote work frequency. [12]

**Conclusion on Element (a):** The verbal promise was "clear and definite" in its terms. The confirmation email provides strong written evidence. The contract bar doctrine poses a challenge but the stronger argument is that the vague "per company policy" language does not cover the specific promise of full remote work. This is a genuine issue of material fact that would survive summary judgment.

### 5. Element (b): Reasonable Reliance

**The Merger Clause and Reasonable Reliance**

The offer letter includes a bolded merger clause: "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations." A reasonable employee reading this clause would be on notice that prior verbal promises may not be enforceable.

**Under *Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential Ins. Co. of America*, 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011):**

- Standard merger clauses do NOT bar fraud claims unless they contain a "clear and unequivocal" disclaimer of reliance. [13][14]
- The clause here states that the written agreement supersedes prior verbal representations—it disclaims the *existence* of other representations, but does not explicitly disclaim *reliance* on those representations or waive any claim based on them.
- Under *Italian Cowboy Partners*, a standard merger clause is insufficient to bar a promissory estoppel claim. [13]

**However**, the merger clause is relevant to the *reasonableness* of Hayes's reliance. A jury must determine whether it was reasonable for Hayes to rely on a verbal promise after signing a written agreement that explicitly supersedes prior verbal representations.

**Factors weighing in Hayes's favor:**

- The merger clause does not explicitly disclaim reliance.
- The verbal promise was made to induce relocation—a significant life decision.
- The confirmation email (sent after the offer letter) re-affirmed the promise, potentially creating a reasonable belief that the promise remained valid despite the written language.
- The company's subsequent conduct (accepting remote work through June 2024 without objection) suggested the promise was being honored.

**The Manager's Apparent Authority**

Under Texas agency law, **apparent authority** is the power of an agent to affect the legal relations of a principal by transactions with third persons, where the principal's conduct causes a third party to reasonably believe the agent has authority to act. [15]

**The Three Elements of Apparent Authority:**
(a) The principal (employer) holds out another as having authority.
(b) The third party (employee) reasonably and detrimentally relies on that holding out.
(c) The principal's conduct would lead a reasonably prudent person to believe the agent has authority. [15]

**Critical Rule:** "Only the actions of the principal may be considered; representations of authority made by the agent have no effect." [16] The principal must have "held out" the agent as authorized or knowingly permitted unauthorized actions.

**Also:** "Apparent authority is created by written words, spoken words or conduct by the principal to third parties and not by conduct to the agent." [16]

**Application to Hayes:**

- The employer designated this individual as the "hiring manager" and authorized him to conduct employment negotiations.
- The manager used a company email address and official title to send the confirmation email.
- The company did not inform Hayes of any limits on the manager's authority.
- Internal HR records showing the manager lacked actual authority to modify standard terms are **irrelevant** to apparent authority if Hayes never saw them. Texas law is clear: "Apparent authority is created by the principal to third parties and **not by conduct to the agent**." Internal policies Hayes never saw cannot limit apparent authority. [15][16]

**The "Power of Position" Doctrine:** A hiring manager, by virtue of their position conducting employment negotiations, has the apparent authority to make promises regarding terms and conditions of employment—including remote work arrangements. This is distinct from the extraordinary promise of lifetime employment in *Guerra v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc.*, which the court found was not within a manager's apparent authority. A promise of remote work is an ordinary term and condition of employment within the typical scope of a hiring manager's authority. [16]

**Conclusion on Apparent Authority:** Hayes has a strong argument that the hiring manager had apparent authority to promise full remote work. The internal HR records Hayes never saw are irrelevant.

### 6. Element (c): Foreseeable and Substantial Detriment

**Foreseeability Analysis:** The hiring manager made the verbal promise of full remote work specifically to induce Hayes to relocate from Seattle to Austin. Relocation is a significant life event that inherently involves substantial financial commitments. The manager should have foreseen that Hayes would sell his home (potentially at a loss), enter into a new lease, incur moving expenses, and leave prior employment.

**This element is the strongest for Hayes.** The very purpose of the promise was to induce action, making the resulting detriment highly foreseeable.

**The Actual Detriment:**

- **Home sale loss:** $40,000 loss on the sale of Hayes's Seattle home.
- **Moving expenses:** Costs of physically relocating from Seattle to Austin.
- **Lease-signing costs:** Security deposit, first month's rent, application fees, and other costs associated with signing a Texas lease.
- **Lost wages from prior job:** The wages Hayes gave up by resigning from his prior position in reliance on the promise.
- **Job search costs:** Expenses incurred after the promise was broken and Hayes was forced to seek new employment.

### 7. Element (d): Injustice Without Enforcement – With Deepened Disparate Treatment Analysis

The injustice element—whether injustice can only be avoided by enforcement of the promise—is a question of law for the court, not a question of fact for the jury. It requires the court to balance the equities between the parties. [17]

**Factors Weighing in Hayes's Favor:**

**A. The Company's Policy Change:** The company updated its policy on July 1, 2024, requiring three office days weekly—after Hayes had already relied on the promise. A policy change that retroactively eliminates a promised benefit weighs in favor of enforcement.

**B. Ratification Through Acceptance of Remote Work:** Hayes worked fully remote through June 2024 without objection from the company. Under the *BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen* "totality of circumstances" test, this conduct could constitute implied ratification. [18]

*BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen*, 629 S.W.3d 189 (Tex. 2021), established the standard for implied ratification:

- "Ratification is the adoption or confirmation by a person with knowledge of all material facts of a prior act which did not then legally bind him and which he had the right to repudiate." [18]
- "Acceptance of benefits is a quintessential indicator of ratification. But as with any objective inquiry, adjudicating a claim of implied ratification requires an examination of the totality of the circumstances." [18]

**Application:** The company accepted Hayes's labor for over one month while he worked fully remote. If the company knew (or should have known) that Hayes was working remotely based on the manager's promise, this acceptance of benefits without objection is strong evidence of ratification. The BPX court rejected a categorical rule that acceptance always means ratification, instead requiring the totality of circumstances. Critically, the BPX court noted that "continued objection by a lessor post-payment can negate implied ratification." [18] Here, the company raised no objection during the month of June 2024.

**C. Disparate Treatment of Similarly Situated Engineers (Key Factual Strength):** Two other engineers hired simultaneously received **written** remote approvals. This evidence is powerful for several reasons:

1. **Creates an inference of pretext or unfairness:** It shows the employer has the ability to grant full remote work, has a process for doing so, and has actually done so for similarly situated employees. This undercuts any argument that remote work was categorically unavailable or that the policy was uniformly applied.

2. **Undermines the injustice element:** The employer's own conduct in selectively granting remote work makes it unjust to deny the same to Hayes, who relied on a promise.

3. **Shows minimal burden on the employer:** Since the employer has already accommodated other engineers with written remote approvals, enforcing the promise would not impose an undue burden.

4. **Supports the reasonable reliance element:** If other engineers received written approvals for the same arrangement, it supports the argument that Hayes reasonably believed such an arrangement was available and that the manager had authority to promise it.

**D. Substantial Financial Harm:** The $40,000 home sale loss, combined with relocation costs and lease obligations, constitutes substantial financial harm that would not have occurred but for the promise.

**Factors Weighing Against Enforcement:**

**A. At-Will Employment Doctrine:** Texas is a strong at-will employment state. As one Texas appellate court held in *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc.*: "A promise to provide employment which is subject to termination at any time or for any reason [at-will] does not provide any assurances about the employer's future conduct, and does not provide a basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law." [19]

**B. The Merger Clause:** While not dispositive under *Italian Cowboy Partners*, the merger clause weighs against enforcement because Hayes signed an agreement that disclaimed prior verbal representations.

## Part III: The Roberts/Collins/Patterson Split – Forum Selection Is Outcome-Determinative

### 8. The Unresolved Split Among Texas Appellate Courts

There is a well-documented, unresolved split among Texas appellate courts on whether promissory estoppel can override the at-will employment doctrine. The Texas Supreme Court has **never definitively resolved this split**. [20]

**A. The Roberts Approach (1st Court of Appeals – Houston)**

*Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services, Inc.*, 757 S.W.2d 48 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1988).

Bobby Wayne Roberts, an oil drilling worker, relied on verbal and written assurances that he would be employed overseas. When Geosource later informed Roberts he would not be hired, he sued for promissory estoppel. [21]

**The court held:** "If the appellant/promisee acts to his detriment in reliance upon the promise of employment, or parts with some legal right or sustains some legal injury as the inducement for the employment agreement, we hold that there is sufficient consideration to bind the employer/promisor to its promise." [21]

**Key quote:** "It is no answer that the parties' written contract was for an employment-at-will, where the employer foreseeably and intentionally induces the prospective employee to materially change his position to his expense and detriment, and then repudiates its obligations before the written contract begins to operate." [21]

Under this approach, promissory estoppel serves as a substitute for consideration—detrimental reliance "clothes" the promise with consideration.

**B. The Collins Approach (14th Court of Appeals – Houston)**

*Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc.*, 871 S.W.2d 929 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1994).

Jeanne Collins and Craig Torry claimed enforceable three-year employment agreements with stock options. The court held the contracts were subject to the statute of frauds and explicitly repudiated *Roberts*. [19]

**The court held:** "A promise to provide employment which is subject to termination at any time or for any reason [at-will] does not provide any assurances about the employer's future conduct, and does not provide a basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law." [19]

**C. The Patterson Approach (Corpus Christi Court of Appeals – Most Restrictive)**

*Robert J. Patterson, P.C. v. Leal*, 942 S.W.2d 692 (Tex. App.—Corpus Christi 1997).

Norma Leal resigned from her prior job based on an oral indication she would be employed as a file clerk. When the position was filled, she sued for promissory estoppel. The appellate court reversed her trial court victory. [22]

**The court held even more restrictively:** "We hold that, in the context of employer and employee relations, no claim exists under Texas law for promissory estoppel when the underlying evidence consists of purely oral representations as to employment for an indefinite period." [22]

The court distinguished *Roberts* because it involved a written contract, and stated: "The Roberts decision was not tested on appeal and has been subsequently criticized as 'wrongly decided.'" [22]

The Texas Supreme Court denied a writ of error in *Patterson* on June 27, 1997. [22]

### 9. How Venue Selection Determines the Outcome

| Venue | Governing Precedent | Likely Outcome |
|-------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1st District (Houston – Harris County) | *Roberts* | Promissory estoppel can substitute for consideration. Hayes may prevail. |
| 14th District (Houston – Harris County) | *Collins* | At-will promise provides no basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law. Hayes likely loses. |
| Corpus Christi (Nueces County) | *Patterson* | No claim exists for purely oral indefinite employment. Hayes likely loses. |
| Other Texas counties (Austin/Travis County) | Unclear; would apply the closest precedent | Uncertain; but *Patterson* is from the Austin-based 3rd Court of Appeals, which applies restrictive approach. |
| Federal court (diversity jurisdiction) | *Erie* doctrine; federal court predicts how Texas Supreme Court would rule | Unpredictable. Federal courts have applied different approaches depending on which Texas appellate court's reasoning they find more persuasive. A federal district court in the Southern District of Texas (Houston Division) has held: "A promise to provide employment-at-will does not provide a reasonable basis for reliance as a matter of law." [23] |

**Practical Implications:** For Hayes, filing in a state court in the 1st District (Houston) is the most favorable forum. Filing in federal court under diversity jurisdiction is riskier and depends on which line of authority the federal judge finds more persuasive.

## Part IV: Damages Calculation

### 10. Recoverable Reliance Damages

Under Texas law, damages for promissory estoppel are limited to reliance damages—the amount necessary to restore the plaintiff to the position they would have been in had they not relied on the promise. Benefit-of-the-bargain or expectation damages (including anticipated future earnings) are not recoverable. [9][24]

**The governing cases:**

- *Fretz Construction Co. v. Southern National Bank of Houston*, 626 S.W.2d 478 (Tex. 1981): Foundational Texas Supreme Court case establishing promissory estoppel damages as reliance-based. [24]
- *Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co.*: The court upheld damages of approximately $40,000 to restore Geer's financial position, clarifying that promissory estoppel does not cover anticipated profits but compensates for losses caused by reliance on the promise. [9]
- *Hernandez v. UPS Supply Chain Solutions, Inc.* (W.D. Tex. 2007): A federal court applying Texas law granted summary judgment to an employee who relocated from Chicago to El Paso, awarding out-of-pocket moving expenses. [25]

## Part V: Summary Judgment Standards Under Texas Rule 166a

### 11. The March 1, 2026 Rule Changes

**Effective March 1, 2026**, the Texas Supreme Court implemented a comprehensive overhaul of Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a, which governs summary judgment practice. [26][27][28]

**Key Changes:**

- **Formal Definitions:** The rule now formally defines "traditional" and "no-evidence" summary judgment motions and allows combined motions.
- **Mandatory Titling:** Motions must be titled as "Traditional Motion for Summary Judgment," "No-Evidence Motion for Summary Judgment," or "Combined Motion for Summary Judgment."
- **Deadline Changes (Most Significant):** Deadlines now run from the motion filing date, NOT the hearing date. The nonmovant's response is due within 21 days after the motion is filed. The movant's reply is due 7 days after the response. The earliest hearing date is 35 days after the motion is filed.
- **Hearing and Ruling Deadlines:** Courts must set a hearing or submission date within 60 days of filing (extendable to 90 days with good cause). Courts must issue a written ruling within 90 days of the hearing or submission date.
- **Important:** The substantive legal standard for granting or denying summary judgment has **not changed**.

### 12. Traditional vs. No-Evidence Summary Judgment

**Traditional Motion (Rule 166a(c)):** The movant must show there is no genuine issue of material fact and that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law. The movant bears the burden of proof. If the movant produces evidence entitling them to summary judgment, the burden shifts to the nonmovant to produce controverting evidence raising a genuine issue of material fact. [26][27]

**No-Evidence Motion (Rule 166a(i)):** The movant challenges specific elements where the nonmovant lacks evidence. The movant does not need to produce evidence—they simply assert there is no evidence to support one or more essential elements. The burden shifts to the nonmovant to produce "more than a scintilla" of evidence—evidence sufficient to allow reasonable minds to differ. If the nonmovant fails, the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. [26][27]

### 13. Vulnerability of Each Element to Summary Judgment

| Element | Vulnerability to No-Evidence Motion | Analysis |
|---------|-------------------------------------|----------|
| **Clear and definite promise** | **Moderate** | The confirmation email provides strong evidence. But the employer could argue the promise was too vague or indefinite (e.g., a general statement about "flexibility"). Under *Allied Vista, Inc. v. Holt*, 987 S.W.2d 138, 141-42 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1999), "relying on a vague, indefinite promise of future business is unreasonable as a matter of law." However, the specificity of "you have been approved to work fully remote" likely constitutes a sufficiently definite promise. |
| **Foreseeability of reliance** | **Moderate** | Hayes must show the employer knew he was relocating from out of state. If Hayes can produce evidence the manager knew he was selling his home and moving from Seattle, this element is strong. |
| **Substantial reliance/detriment** | **HIGH** | **Most vulnerable element.** Hayes must produce evidence of: (a) specific actions taken in reliance, (b) that these actions were taken **because of** the promise of full remote work, and (c) specific damages incurred. Lack of documentation or failure to show a causal link would make this element vulnerable. Documentary evidence (home sale records, lease, moving receipts) is critical. |
| **Injustice** | **Low** | This is a question of law for the court, not a question of fact. The court makes an equitable determination based on the full factual record. Evidence of disparate treatment (two other engineers with written approvals) directly supports this element. |

**Strategic Conclusion for Hayes:** The most important goal is to survive the no-evidence summary judgment motion on the reliance element. Hayes must produce "more than a scintilla" of evidence—home sale records, lease documents, moving receipts, and the confirmation email—to show specific actions taken in reliance on the promise.

## Part VI: Alternative Causes of Action

### 14. Fraud / Fraudulent Inducement

**Elements:** Under Texas law, a fraudulent inducement claim requires: (1) a material misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of its falsity or reckless disregard for its truth, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting injury/damages.

**Viability:** Significant hurdles exist. The Texas Supreme Court in *Sawyer v. E.I. du Pont de Nemours & Co.*, 430 S.W.3d 396 (Tex. 2014), held that "an at-will employee cannot bring an action for fraud that is dependent on continued employment." [29][30] The Court explained: "Absent a specific agreement to the contrary, employment may be terminated by the employer or the employee at will, for good cause, bad cause, or no cause at all." [29]

**The Critical Exception:** *Sawyer* left an important exception: "At-will employees can still sue for fraud in cases unrelated to continued employment, such as recovery of expenses incurred in reliance on a fraudulent promise of prospective employment." [29]

**Application:** If Hayes frames the fraud claim as seeking recovery of **specific reliance expenses** (home sale loss, moving costs, lease costs)—not lost wages or continued employment—the claim may survive under the *Sawyer* exception.

**Proof Challenges:** Proving the manager "knew the promise was false" at the time it was made is difficult. Hayes would need evidence that the manager either knew the company would not approve full remote work or made the promise without any basis for believing it was true. Reckless disregard (making the promise without verifying) is easier to prove than actual knowledge.

**Damages:** Under Texas law, fraud allows both benefit-of-the-bargain and out-of-pocket damages. In severe cases, punitive damages may be available (requiring clear and convincing evidence of fraud, malice, or gross negligence, subject to constitutional caps).

### 15. Negligent Misrepresentation

**Elements:** Under Texas law (Restatement (Second) of Torts § 552): (1) a person makes a false material representation; (2) the person knew the representation was false or made it without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true; (3) the person intended to induce another to act; (4) the other person actually and properly relied and suffered harm. [2][31]

**Key Difference from Fraud:** Negligent misrepresentation does not require intent to deceive—only that the person making the false statement did not have reasonable grounds to believe it was true.

**Viability:** A promise of future remote work is generally a promise about future conduct, not a representation about a past or existing fact. Negligent misrepresentation requires a false statement about an existing fact.

**However:** If the manager stated "you have been **approved** to work fully remote" (present tense—a statement about an existing fact of approval), that could support a negligent misrepresentation claim if the statement was false and made without reasonable grounds.

**Damages:** Actual financial losses directly resulting from the misrepresentation. Benefit-of-the-bargain damages are not available.

**Statute of Limitations:** Two years for negligent misrepresentation in Texas. [2] If the misrepresentation occurred in April–May 2024, a lawsuit filed in early 2025 would be timely.

**Economic Loss Rule:** In Texas, the economic loss rule may bar negligent misrepresentation claims for purely economic losses in some contexts, though the rule's application is debated. [31]

### 16. Breach of Contract

**Viability:** This is the most difficult claim to prove. Texas courts strongly uphold at-will employment and require very clear and definite evidence that the parties intended to modify the at-will relationship.

**The confirmation email** might constitute a promise, but whether it creates an enforceable contract is questionable. Under *Sawyer*, "an employer and employee may modify their at-will relationship by agreement, but lest the general at-will rule be eroded, we have insisted that the parties be definite in expressing their intent." [29]

**Consideration Issue:** Even if the email constituted an offer, continued employment (or acceptance of the job) is generally not sufficient consideration for a modification under the at-will doctrine.

**Recommendation:** Breach of contract should be pleaded in the alternative but is unlikely to succeed.

### 17. Pleading Strategy – Alternative Causes of Action

Texas law allows plaintiffs to plead alternative causes of action. [1] Hayes should plead in the alternative:

1. **Promissory estoppel** (primary claim)
2. **Fraud/fraudulent inducement** (if the manager knew the promise was false)
3. **Negligent misrepresentation** (if the manager made the statement without reasonable grounds)
4. **Breach of contract** (if the email constitutes a contract)

**Important:** Recovery cannot occur under multiple theories for the same harm. The plaintiff must elect their best recovery at the end of trial. The *Fortune Production* decision confirms: "When a valid, express contract covers the subject matter of the parties' dispute, there can be no recovery under a quasi-contract theory." [10]

## Part VII: Practical Strategy for Both Sides

### 18. Current Position of the Case (May 30, 2026)

The lawsuit was filed in early 2025. Given the 16+ months since filing:

- **Discovery is likely nearing completion or has recently closed.** Depositions of the retired manager and the two other engineers with written remote approvals would be crucial.
- **Summary judgment motions are likely pending or about to be filed.** Under the new Rule 166a (effective March 1, 2026), motions would be governed by the expedited timeline.
- **Mediation may have occurred or be scheduled.**

### 19. Plaintiff's (Hayes's) Strategy

**Primary Goal: Survive Summary Judgment**

- The most critical objective is to defeat any no-evidence summary judgment motion on the reliance element.
- Produce all documentary evidence: confirmation email, home sale records, lease documents, moving receipts, correspondence with the manager.
- Be prepared with specific testimony about causation: "I would not have accepted the job, sold my home, or relocated but for the promise of full remote work."

**Leverage Comparator Evidence Aggressively:**

- The two other engineers with written remote approvals are the most powerful evidence.
- Conduct discovery to obtain: their written approvals, the approval process they followed, whether they had the same manager or different manager, and their job duties (to establish they are "similarly situated").
- Use this evidence to argue: (a) the company's policy was not uniformly applied, (b) the company could have granted the same to Hayes, and (c) enforcing the promise would not impose an undue burden.

**Forum Selection:**

- If the case was filed in a state court in the 1st District (Houston), emphasize *Roberts* and argue that detrimental reliance substitutes for consideration.
- If in a less favorable forum, argue that the Texas Supreme Court should adopt the Restatement approach (*Roberts*) and that *Collins* and *Patterson* were wrongly decided.
- Argue that the confirmation email distinguishes this case from *Patterson* (which involved "purely oral representations").

**Ratification Argument:** Emphasize that the company accepted Hayes's remote work through the entire month of June 2024 without any objection. Under *BPX*, acceptance of benefits with knowledge of material facts is a "quintessential indicator of ratification."

**Summary Judgment Response:** If the employer files a no-evidence motion challenging the reliance element, Hayes must produce "more than a scintilla" of evidence—specific documentation of expenses and testimony about causation. If the employer files a traditional motion, Hayes must demonstrate genuine issues of material fact on all elements.

### 20. Defendant's (Company's) Strategy

**Primary Argument: No-Evidence Summary Judgment on Reliance**

- Challenge whether Hayes actually relied on the promise. Was it reasonable to sell a home and relocate based on a verbal promise and a single email when the offer letter said "hybrid schedule per company policy"?
- Challenge causation: Can Hayes prove he would not have accepted the job or relocated but for the promise?
- Challenge damages: Are the claimed expenses fully documented? Are they causally connected to the promise?

**Emphasize the Merger Clause:**
- While not dispositive under *Italian Cowboy Partners*, argue that a reasonable person reading "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations" would not rely on a prior verbal promise.
- Argue that Hayes's reliance was unreasonable as a matter of law.

**Contract Bar Doctrine:**
- Argue that the offer letter covers the same subject matter (work location/attendance expectations) and thus bars the promissory estoppel claim.
- Argue that "hybrid schedule per company policy" is a definite term that Hayes accepted by signing.

**At-Will Employment Defense:**
- In a venue governed by *Collins* or *Patterson*, argue that promissory estoppel does not apply in at-will employment.
- Argue that any promise of remote work was inherently subject to change because employment was at-will.

**Manager's Lack of Actual Authority:**
- Emphasize that the manager lacked actual authority to modify standard employment terms.
- However, this argument is weakened if the manager had apparent authority (and internal records Hayes never saw are irrelevant to apparent authority).

**Settlement Considerations:**

Given the factual disputes and the unresolved legal split, both sides face litigation risk:
- **Low-end settlement value:** $40,000–$80,000 (documented out-of-pocket expenses).
- **High-end settlement value:** $100,000–$200,000 (including lost wages from prior job).
- **Key drivers:** Strength of the comparator evidence, whether the confirmation email is in writing, the amount of documented expenses, and the venue.
- **Recommendation:** Both sides should seriously consider mediation to avoid the uncertainty of summary judgment and the expense of trial.
