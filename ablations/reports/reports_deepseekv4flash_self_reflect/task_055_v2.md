# Critical Review and Revision of Research Report: Slavery and Legal Culture in the 1730s British Empire

## 1. Accuracy of Statutory Language

### 1.1 The Molasses Act (1733) — Confirmed: No Mention of Slavery

The Molasses Act of 1733 (6 Geo. II, c. 13), formally titled "An act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty's sugar colonies in America," contains **no mention whatsoever of enslaved people, slavery, or the slave trade**. This has been verified against the full text of the Act available from multiple authoritative sources [1][2][3][4].

The Act is purely a trade regulation and taxation statute. Its preamble states: *"Whereas the Welfare and Prosperity of Your Majesty's Sugar Colonies in America are of the greatest Consequence and Importance to the Trade, Navigation, and Strength of this Kingdom."* [5] The duties imposed were nine pence per gallon on foreign rum, six pence per gallon on foreign molasses or syrups, and five shillings per hundredweight on foreign sugar and paneles. Penalties involved forfeiture of goods and vessels, plus fines and imprisonment for resisting customs officers [5].

**Correction to the previous report:** The original report correctly identified that the Molasses Act contains no mention of slavery. This finding is accurate and does not require revision.

However, **critical context** is necessary: while the Act itself makes no reference to slavery, the molasses trade was integral to the Triangular Trade (Europe-Africa-Americas), which included the slave trade. The sugar plantations the Act protected were entirely dependent on enslaved African labor. This contextual point is essential for a novelist but must be clearly distinguished from the statutory text.

### 1.2 Jamaican Consolidated Slave Act (1733) — Confirmed: Does Not Exist

**The research confirms that no "1733 Jamaican Consolidated Slave Act" exists.** The earliest comprehensive consolidated slave act for Jamaica was the **"New Consolidated Act of 1788"** (also called "Code of laws for the government of the negro slaves in the island of Jamaica"), passed by the Assembly on November 6, 1788, and by the lieutenant governor on December 6, 1788 [6]. A later consolidated act was passed on December 22, 1826 [7].

**What DID exist in the 1733-1739 period:**

**The 1733 Act — Dividing the Island and Building Barracks:**
During the First Maroon War (1728–1739), the Jamaican Assembly "passed an act dividing the island in two. It called for the building of barracks in both" [8]. This act was part of the militarization of Jamaica during the Maroon War, a conflict between Jamaican Maroons and British colonial authorities.

**The 1739 Act — Treaty with Cudjoe and the Maroons:**
The Act titled **"An Act for confirming the Articles executed by Colonel John Guthrie, Lieutenant Francis Sadler, and Cudjoe the Commander of the Rebels; for paying Rewards for taking up and restoring runaway Slaves, and making Provision for four white Persons, residing, or to reside at Trelawney Town; and for granting Freedom to Five Negroes who were Guides to Parties"** was passed in 1739 [9]. This act confirmed the peace treaty between the British and Cudjoe, leader of the Leeward Maroons, granting the Maroons their freedom, land, hunting rights, and self-government in exchange for returning runaway slaves and helping suppress future rebellions [10][11].

The **1717 Act** — "An Act for the more effectual punishing of Crimes committed by Slaves" — remained in effect throughout the 1730s. It mandated mandatory death sentences for certain crimes by slaves, with compensation to owners capped at £40 per slave, prescribed "cutting off one of the Feet" for runaway slaves absent for over 30 days, prohibited gatherings of more than five "strange negroes" on plantations, banned drumming and signaling, and required free Mulattoes, Negroes, and Indians to carry certificates and wear "a publick Badge of a blue Cross to be worn upon the Right Shoulder" [12][13].

The 1696 Jamaican Slave Code designated enslaved individuals as real estate (real property), integrating them into economic transactions — this classification was in effect during the 1730s.

**Sources for verification:** The [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/locations/caribbean/jamaica) database, led by Stefanie Hunt-Kennedy at the University of New Brunswick, lists 105 laws related to Jamaica from the 17th to mid-18th century [14]. The published volume "Acts of Assembly, passed in the island of Jamaica; from 1681, to 1737, inclusive" (London: John Baskett, 1738/1739) covers all acts through 1737 [15].

### 1.3 South Carolina's 1740 Negro Act — Exact Statutory Quotation

The South Carolina 1740 Negro Act, officially titled **"An Act for the better Ordering and Governing Negroes and other Slaves in this Province"** [16][17], contains the following exact language establishing enslaved people as chattels personal:

> **"All negroes, Indians (free Indians in amity with this government excepted), mulattoes or mestizos ... shall be and they are hereby declared to be and remain for ever hereafter absolute slaves, and shall follow the condition of the mother; and shall be deemed, taken, and adjudged to be chattels personal in the hands of their owners and possessors, and their executors, administrators, and assigns, to all intents, constructions, and purposes whatsoever."** [16][18][19]

The Gilder Lehrman Institute version provides a slight variation: *"... law, to be chattels personal, in the hands of their owners and possessors, and their executors, administrators and assigns, to all intents, constructions ..."* [18]

The Duke Center for Firearms Law PDF of the 1814 South Carolina Code (which republished the 1740 Act) confirms: *"... slave are deemed to be chattels personal; and are, in every respect, as much the property of their owners, as any other [Criminal offenders]."* [20]

**Correction to the previous report:** The original report's quotation was substantially accurate. The more precise version above, verified against the primary source at the Laws of Enslavement and Freedom database, provides the complete wording. Note the archaic spelling "chattles" in some versions (the Laws of Enslavement and Freedom transcription uses "chattles") versus the modernized "chattels" in other sources.

### 1.4 Barbados 1661 Code — Exact Quotation

**Critical correction:** The original 1661 version of the Barbados Slave Code "has not survived" in its original form. The earliest surviving version is an amended 1667 version [21][22]. The code was "comprehensively rewritten in 1688, at which point Walrond's name and the date (1661) finally disappeared" [21].

The exact quotation from the preamble, as verified by multiple sources, states that the law's purpose was to **"protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels"** [21][23]. The full preamble text from the Studocu transcription reads: *"... goods and Chattels, and also somewhat further as being created Men though without the knowledge of God in the world, we have therefore upon mature and..."* [24]

**Correction to the previous report:** The original report correctly identified the "goods and Chattels" language. However, it incorrectly suggested that the code's preamble *opened* with this language. The language appears within the preamble, not as its opening. The report also should have noted that the original 1661 manuscript has not survived and that the earliest version is from 1667.

**Key provisions of the 1661/1667 code that were in force during the 1730s:**

- **Punishment for violence by slaves:** "If any Negro Man or Woman shall offer any Violence to any Christian ... the Negroe shall for his and their first offense ... be severely whipped ... for the second offense ... be burned in face ... and for his third offense ... greater Corporal punishments" [25][26]
- **Insurrection:** "If any Negro shall make Insurrection or rise in rebellion ... the Governor ... shall proceed by Martial Law and punish by death or other pain as their crimes shall deserve" [25][26]
- **Runaway slaves:** "All persons that are now possessed of any fugitive or Runaway Negroes do within six days ... bring them in to their proper owners or into custody ... upon pain of paying ten thousand pounds of good merchantable Muscavado sugar for damage unto the owner" [25]
- **Movement restrictions:** "No Master Mistress Commander or overseer shall give their negroes leave on sabbath days holy days or at any other time to go out of their plantations except the Negroes who usually wait upon them at home and abroad and them with a tickett [ticket]" [26]
- **The code allowed owners to punish slaves severely, including mutilation and burning alive, without fear of reprisal** by authorities.

### 1.5 1735 South Carolina Manumission Statute

The 1735 South Carolina law regulating manumission changed the earlier 1722 statute. The 1722 law had provided:

> "All owners of slaves, who, at any time hereafter, shall manumit or set free any slave, for any particular service, shall make provision for his departure out of this province; and such slave who shall not depart this province, by the space of twelve months next after such manumission, shall lose the benefit of such manumission, and continue to be a slave ... unless such manumission shall be approved of and confirmed by an order of both Houses of Assembly." [27]

**The 1735 law changed this as follows:**
- The departure period was shortened from **12 months to 6 months** [27][28][29]
- A new restriction added a **seven-year ban on return** — the freed person could not come back to South Carolina for seven years after departing [28][29]
- Slaves freed by act of the legislature were exempted from these requirements and could remain [28]

The 1740 Negro Act **omitted these banishment clauses**, tacitly accepting private manumission without forced exile [27].

## 2. British Parliamentary Law vs. Colonial Plantation Codes

### 2.1 The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729) — Legal Standing and Use

**Confirmed: The Yorke-Talbot Opinion was NOT a court judgment.** It was a formal legal opinion (a "law officer opinion") issued by England's two highest Crown law officers — Sir Philip Yorke (Attorney General, later Lord Chancellor Hardwicke) and Charles Talbot (Solicitor General, later Lord Chancellor) — to a deputation of West India merchants and planters [30][31][32].

The opinion was sought because Lord Chief Justice Holt's rulings in cases such as *Smith v. Gould* (1706) and *Chamberline v. Harvey* (1697) had "challenged the previously accepted notion that slavery was lawful" and "cast doubt on slave owners' rights" [32][33].

**The full text of the opinion** (preserved in Northumberland Archives) states:

> "We are of opinion, that a slave coming from the West-Indies to Great-Britain or Ireland, with or without his master, doth not become free, and that his master's property or right in him is not thereby determined or varied; and that baptism doth not bestow freedom on him, or make any alteration in his temporal condition in these kingdoms. We are also of opinion, that his master may legally compel him to return again to the plantations." [30][31][34]

**How it was cited and used in the 1730s:**

Despite having no formal legal authority as a court precedent, the opinion was **widely disseminated and cited**:
- It was cited in the **Boston Gazette in 1730** — just one year after it was issued [32]
- It was "widely published and relied upon" [30]
- It was "widely accepted by slave owners for nearly 40 years" [31]
- Nicholas Leah's paper notes: "The Opinion's significance rested not only in its widespread distribution in the colonies, but also in how it opened the door for English law to accommodate slavery" [32]

Travis Glasson's article in the *William and Mary Quarterly* (Vol. 67, No. 2, 2010), titled "'Baptism doth not bestow Freedom': Missionary Anglicanism, Slavery, and the Yorke-Talbot Opinion, 1701-30," analyzes how the opinion was shaped by Anglican missionary interests seeking to promote Christian conversion without threatening property rights [35].

### 2.2 Smith v. Gould (1706) — Status in the 1730s

**The ruling:** *Smith v. Gould* (also cited as *Smith v. Browne & Cooper*) was heard in the King's Bench around 1705–1706 before **Sir John Holt**, Lord Chief Justice. The case is reported at **2 Salkeld 666** or **2 Ld Raym 1274** [36][37].

Holt's holding was: **"As soon as a Negro comes into England, he becomes free; One may be a Villein in England, but not a Slave"** [36][37][38]. Holt also stated in the related case of *Chamberline v. Harvey* (1697): "By the laws of England one man cannot have an absolute property in the person of another man" [33][39].

**Was it still considered good law in the 1730s?**

**This is a complex and contested question.** The legal landscape in the 1730s was deeply divided:

**On one hand:** Holt's rulings were **never formally overruled by any court in the 1730s**. As King's Bench decisions, they carried substantial weight as common law precedent.

**On the other hand:** The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729) was **specifically designed to contradict Holt's position**. The opinion deliberately contradicted Holt's holding that enslaved people became free upon arriving in England.

**Later developments:** Holt's position was **effectively overruled** by the same Sir Philip Yorke (by then Lord Chancellor Hardwicke) in **Pearne v. Lisle (1749)** (Ambler 75, 27 E.R. 47). In that case, Hardwicke declared that an enslaved person "is as much property as any other thing" and explicitly argued that Holt had been wrong [40][41]. Wikipedia states directly: "Pearne v Lisle (1749) ... overturn[ed] earlier decisions such as the 1706 ruling by Chief Justice Sir John Holt in Smith v Gould, which had declared that no man could have property in another" [40].

**In the 1730s specifically:** Holt's ruling was thus **undermined in practice** by the Yorke-Talbot Opinion but not formally judicially overruled until 1749. The decade saw a **jurisprudential tension**: Holt's common-law position still technically stood, but the Crown's highest law officers had given contrary advice, and colonial practice increasingly ignored Holt.

### 2.3 Parliamentary Acts Concerning Colonial Slavery in the 1730s

**The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 (5 Geo. 2, c. 7) — A Critical Imperial Act**

This is the **most significant Parliamentary act of the 1730s concerning colonial slavery**, arguably more consequential for slave law than the better-known Molasses Act. Its full title is: "An Act for the more easy recovery of debts in his Majesty's plantations and colonies in America" [42][43].

The critical provision states:

> **"The houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates ... shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts ... and shall be assets for the satisfaction thereof in like manner as real estates are by the law of England."** [42][44]

Thomas Jefferson's annotated copy of this Act (preserved at Slavery Law & Power) shows his annotation of the key provision: "Houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates … shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts … and shall be assets for the satisfaction thereof" [44].

As Nicholas Leah argues: "The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 complemented the Opinion by legally recognizing slaves as chattel property for debt recovery in the colonies, further embedding slavery in imperial commerce" [32]. The Act made "houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates" liable as assets for all just debts.

**The Molasses Act (1733)** — As discussed above, this Act is silent on slavery, though it protected the slave-based sugar economy.

**No other significant Parliamentary acts** concerning colonial slavery or the slave trade were passed between 1730 and 1739 [45]. The major era of Parliamentary debate on slavery occurred much later, beginning in 1788.

### 2.4 Notable Court Cases in the 1730s Addressing Slave Status

**No major English reported cases from 1730-1739 specifically addressing slave status** have been identified [46]. The major English cases cluster around other periods:
- Pre-1730: *Butts v. Penny* (1677), *Chamberline v. Harvey* (1697), *Smith v. Gould* (1705/6)
- 1749: *Pearne v. Lisle* — Hardwicke overturns Holt
- 1772: *Somerset v. Stewart* — Lord Mansfield's ruling

The absence of major English cases 1730-1739 likely reflects the **dampening effect** of the Yorke-Talbot Opinion. As the opinion was widely accepted as authoritative (despite not being a judgment), there were fewer legal challenges to slavery's status in England during this period [32].

**However, the 1730s saw important colonial legal developments:**

**The 1736 Barnes/Bawow Case:** The trial of Captain John Barnes of the slave ship *Defiance* in Boston's Vice-Admiralty Court for murdering a ten-year-old enslaved boy named Bawow. Barnes was found guilty and sentenced to death (though granted a one-year respite to apply for a royal pardon). The case is significant because the court treated the boy "as a subject of the king" for purposes of the trial, drawing "on legal traditions based in natural law and Biblical authority" [47]. The case reveals the tension between treating enslaved people as property versus persons with some legal standing.

**Privy Council appeals:** A 1736 Court of Admiralty case involved the delivery of a sloop, slaves, and goods that had been libelled against, with claims by Henry Lory and Richard Todman asserting ownership of slaves and goods for settlement purposes in Tortola. The judgment on June 29, 1736, favored Lory and Todman, with the sloop condemned and forfeited [48]. This case demonstrates that slaves were treated as property subject to admiralty jurisdiction.

## 4. The Molasses Act Debate: Trade Regulation or Slave Control?

### 4.1 Scholarly Arguments (2020-2025)

**The most recent scholarship (2020-2025) does NOT argue that the Molasses Act was directly a "slavery control measure."** Instead, the dominant historiography treats the Act as a trade protection/navigation measure, with an increasing recognition that the distinction between "trade regulation" and "slavery protection" is artificial because the British sugar economy was entirely dependent on enslaved labor.

**No specific peer-reviewed article, book, or book chapter from 2020-2025 was found that explicitly argues the Molasses Act was fundamentally about controlling enslaved people or perpetuating slavery, rather than being a trade regulation** [66][67][68][69].

### 4.2 Verification of O'Shaughnessy, Burnard, and Koot Arguments

**Andrew O'Shaughnessy** treats the Molasses Act as part of the broader framework of British imperial trade regulation that systematically favored British West Indian sugar planters. In *An Empire Divided: The American Revolution and the British Caribbean* (University of Pennsylvania Press, 2000), he discusses the Molasses Act at pages 60-62, noting that "the plantation system of the islands was so similar to that of the southern mainland colonies that these regions had more in common with each other than with New England" [70][71]. His work emphasizes that the Caribbean planters' economic interests — based on enslaved labor and sugar monoculture — were aligned with Britain's mercantilist trade policies. O'Shaughnessy does **not** frame the Molasses Act as directly being about controlling enslaved people; rather, he sees it as an imperial trade regulation whose effects reinforced the slave economy.

**Correction to the previous report:** The original report's characterization of O'Shaughnessy's argument is substantially accurate but overstates his position on the Molasses Act specifically. O'Shaughnessy's emphasis on slave insurrection fears relates primarily to Caribbean planters' political loyalty during the American Revolution, not to the Molasses Act's purpose.

**Trevor Burnard** does not treat the Molasses Act as a standalone subject of analysis. His work situates the Act within the broader context of the British Atlantic plantation economy. In *Planters, Merchants, and Slaves: Plantation Societies in British America, 1650-1820* (University of Chicago Press, 2015), Burnard argues that "the plantations depended on relentless brutality toward slaves, and, nonetheless, were a spectacular financial success for whites in the plantation communities" [72][73]. His "Hobbesian" interpretation emphasizes that every policy that protected British planters was, by extension, a policy that protected and perpetuated chattel slavery as a system.

**Correction to the previous report:** The original report correctly characterized Burnard's emphasis on brutality and profit, but should have made clearer that Burnard does not directly argue the Molasses Act was a "slavery control measure." His framework connects trade protection and slavery maintenance implicitly, not explicitly.

**Christian Koot's** *Empire at the Periphery: British Colonists, Anglo-Dutch Trade, and the Development of the British Atlantic, 1621-1713* (NYU Press, 2011) covers the period ending in 1713, **before the Molasses Act**. However, his framework about informal/inter-imperial trade at the periphery is highly relevant to understanding the smuggling and evasion that the Molasses Act triggered. Koot's work helps explain *why* colonial merchants so readily evaded the Act: such inter-imperial (and often illegal) trade networks were already deeply entrenched [74].

### 4.3 The Current State of the Historiographical Debate

| Position | Core Interpretation | Key Scholars |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| **(A) Trade/Navigation Act** | The Molasses Act was a mercantilist trade regulation designed to protect British West Indian sugar planters from foreign competition, within the Navigation Acts system. It had implications for the slave economy *indirectly* because the sugar economy depended on slavery. | O'Shaughnessy, Sheridan, Britannica/Wikipedia consensus, mainstream economic historians |
| **(B) Slavery/Plantation System Protection** | The Molasses Act was fundamentally about protecting the British West Indian plantation/slave system. The distinction between "trade" and "slavery" is artificial since the sugar economy *was* the slave economy. Every protection of British planters was a protection of chattel slavery. | Burnard (implicitly, via his broader framework), Petley, and a growing number of scholars in the 2020-2025 period who emphasize the centrality of slavery to British imperialism |

**The current scholarly consensus** — particularly among economic historians and imperial historians — views the Molasses Act **primarily as Position A** (a trade protection measure). However, there is growing recognition that this distinction is partly artificial for the reasons noted above.

**Richard B. Sheridan's foundational article**, "The Molasses Act and the Market Strategy of the British Sugar Planters" (*The Journal of Economic History*, Vol. 17, No. 1, March 1957, pp. 62-83), established the economic history framework and remains the most important single treatment of the Act [75]. Sheridan argued that the Act must be understood "in terms of the international sugar economy, the changing nature of the British market for sugar and rum, and the planters' attempt to adapt the Navigation Acts to these changes" [75].

The most recent direct contribution is **Burnard and O'Shaughnessy's *Republic and Empire: Crisis, Revolution, and America's Early Independence*** (Yale University Press, 2025), which "offers an imperial history of the American Revolution by examining not just the thirteen rebelling colonies but also other British colonies" and argues that "the empire continued to rely on slavery and harshly suppressed indigenous peoples" [76].

## 5. Factual Corrections Needed

### 5.1 Stono Rebellion → 1740 Negro Act Causal Link

**The original claim is correct and universally accepted by historians.** The South Carolina 1740 Negro Act was passed directly in response to the Stono Rebellion of September 1739.

**Specific scholarly citations confirming this causal connection:**

The [Stono Rebellion Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stono_Rebellion) states: "In response, South Carolina passed the Negro Act of 1740, imposing strict regulations on slaves, restricting their freedoms, banning assembly, literacy, and controlling manumission" [1].

The [South Carolina Encyclopedia on Slave Codes](https://www.scencyclopedia.org/sce/entries/slave-codes) states: "The Stono Rebellion in 1739 led to the 1740 Negro Act, which denied slaves basic legal rights, prohibited literacy and certain gatherings, and allowed any white person to arrest and punish enslaved people" [77].

The [Equal Justice Initiative](https://calendar.eji.org/racial-injustice/may/10) states: "On May 10, 1740, the South Carolina General Assembly enacted the 'Bill for the better ordering and governing of Negroes and other slaves in this province,' also known as the Negro Act of 1740. The law prohibited enslaved African people from growing their own food, learning to read, moving freely, assembling in groups, and earning money" [78].

**Key scholarly works that establish this connection:**

**Peter H. Wood**, *Black Majority: Negroes in Colonial South Carolina from 1670 through the Stono Rebellion* (Norton, 1974, winner of the Albert J. Beveridge Award). Wood's analysis positions the Negro Act of 1740 as the immediate legislative consequence of the rebellion. Wood's chapters include "The Stono Rebellion and Its Consequences" [79].

**Mark M. Smith** (ed.), *Stono: Documenting and Interpreting a Southern Slave Revolt* (University of South Carolina Press, 2005/2019). Smith's volume assembles primary documents and scholarly interpretations, revealing "the fear it cast among lowcountry slaveholders and for the repressive slave laws enacted in its wake" [80].

**Jane Landers**, *Black Society in Spanish Florida* (University of Illinois Press, 1999). Landers situates the Stono Rebellion within the context of Spanish Florida's sanctuary policy for runaway slaves, noting that during the Stono revolt, several dozen Africans tried unsuccessfully to reach Spanish Florida [81].

**No scholar disputes the causal link** between Stono and the 1740 Negro Act. Scholarly disagreements exist about the *interpretation* of the rebellion's causes and meaning (e.g., John K. Thornton's argument about the African dimensions involving Kongo military experience and Catholic background; Edward A. Pearson's arguments about the symbolic meaning of beheadings and the religious significance of the date), but the legislative response is universally acknowledged [1][80].

### 5.2 1735 South Carolina Manumission Statute — Details Verified

The 1735 South Carolina law:
- Shortened the departure period for manumitted slaves from **12 months to 6 months**
- Added a **seven-year ban on return** to the province
- Exempted slaves freed by act of the legislature from these requirements
- Was superseded by the 1740 Negro Act, which omitted these banishment clauses [27][28][29]

## 6. Gaps in Coverage and Additional Legal Developments

### 6.1 Legal Status of Enslaved People in Transit (On Ships)

The legal framework governing enslaved people on British ships in the 1730s treated them as **"goods and merchandise" subject to admiralty law**. The chapter "Negroes Goods and Merchandizes: Legal Language and the Dehumanization of Slaves in British Vice Admiralty Courts, 1700-1763" demonstrates that "litigants, lawyers, and judges transformed local Vice Admiralty Courts into slave courts by analogizing human beings to cargo, using legal concepts designed for goods to justify the seizure, sale, and transfer of enslaved people" [82].

The **law of the flag** applied — British ships were subject to English admiralty law and, through that, to English common law classifications of property. The principle that slaves were goods, and admiralty law governed ships and their cargo, was well-established.

**The 1736 Barnes/Bawow Case** (discussed above in section 2.4) provides crucial insight into the tension between treating enslaved people as property versus persons on ships. Captain John Barnes was tried in Boston's Vice-Admiralty Court for murdering a ten-year-old enslaved boy, Bawow. The court found him guilty and sentenced him to death, treating the boy "as a subject of the king" for purposes of the trial. The case reveals that "seldom did the death of a slave in the transatlantic trade raise questions about the propriety of the captain's or crew's violence and even less often did such violence result in a criminal charge leading to a death sentence" [47]. The outcome was exceptional and reflected New England cultural values rather than standard imperial practice.

### 6.2 Laws Concerning Punishment of Enslaved People by Non-Owners

**South Carolina:** The 1740 Negro Act specifically allowed "any white person to arrest and punish enslaved people" [77]. The Act also created a formal patrol system: "all white inhabitants of patrol districts eighteen years or older were liable for patrol duty, and those who failed to serve or send a substitute to serve in their place were fined" [77]. The Act stated that "if an enslaved person died while being corrected or punished by a white person, the killer would not be charged with a felony as long as the punishment was considered reasonable" [78]. The Act further authorized white enslavers "to whip and kill enslaved Africans for being 'rebellious'" [78].

**Barbados:** The 1661 Slave Code authorized any white person to enforce punishments against enslaved people. Overseers were required to conduct regular searches for stolen goods and weapons. The Code established a summary trial system by two justices and three freeholders for capital crimes committed by enslaved people, without jury trial [25][26].

**Jamaica:** The 1664 Slave Code authorized any white person to punish enslaved people found off plantations without tickets. The 1717 Act's restriction on masters dismembering slaves at will (£100 penalty) did not extend to non-owners; the state performed judicial dismemberment (cutting off a foot for runaways absent over 30 days) [12][13].

**New York (Montgomerie's Act, 1730):** The 1730 New York slave code explicitly authorized cities, towns, and manors to "appoint a Common whipper" to administer punishment — creating a public, non-owner punishment mechanism. The Act also authorized any justice of the peace to order whipping of up to forty lashes for slaves meeting in groups of more than three [83][84].

### 6.3 Imperial-Level Legal Developments in the 1730s

**The Board of Trade and Privy Council Review:**

The Board of Trade, established in 1696, was responsible for reviewing colonial laws and nominating colonial officials. During the 18th century, the Board's activity fluctuated, with long periods of inactivity especially after 1714, contributing to "salutary neglect." However, a revival began in 1748 under George Montagu-Dunk, Earl of Halifax [85].

The royal disallowance was "an executive rather than a legislative act, performed not by the king but by the [Privy] Council as his executive agent." From 1696 to 1776, more than 8,500 colonial laws were reviewed, and over 400 colonial laws were disallowed for being repugnant to the laws of England [86][87].

**Specific instance in the 1730s:** The Privy Council disallowed Pennsylvania's acts imposing duties on slave imports as "a burden upon the British Trade and Navigation" [88]. This demonstrates that the Privy Council was actively reviewing colonial legislation affecting slavery and disallowing laws that interfered with British commercial interests in the slave trade.

**Colonial Governors' Instructions:**

Every governor of Great Britain's royal colonies received an official commission and a set of instructions from the Board of Trade that were to guide his actions while in office. The instructions were cumulative — "instructions sent to one governor were binding on his successors unless changed." Governors often had to disobey parts of their instructions to reach compromises with the colonial Assembly, which retained financial control [85].

**Holly Brewer's framework:** Brewer argues that by the 1730s, a well-established imperial common law framework for slavery already existed, within which colonial slave codes operated and were subject to review by the Board of Trade and Privy Council. Colonial slave laws were required to conform to English common law, enforced by royal governors and courts up to the Privy Council — "much like federal law in the United States" [54].

### 6.4 Other Significant Legal Developments in the 1730s

**New York's Montgomerie's Act (1730):**
Formally "An Act for the more Effectual Preventing and Punishing the Conspiracy and Insurrection of Negro and other Slaves; for the better regulating them and for repealing the Acts herein Mentioned Relating thereto." Key provisions included:
- Masters could punish slaves "at discretion not Extending to Life or Limb"
- Cities could "appoint a Common whipper" — creating public punishment mechanisms
- Prohibited gatherings of more than three slaves, with punishment up to forty lashes
- Disallowed slave testimony as evidence (except in conspiracy cases)
- Prohibited slaves from possessing weapons [83][84]

**The First Maroon War (1728–1739) and Jamaican Peace Treaties:**
The war between Jamaican Maroons and British authorities ended with the peace treaties of 1739-1740, which granted Maroons their freedom, land, self-government, and hunting rights in exchange for returning runaway slaves and helping suppress future rebellions. The 1739 Act confirming the treaty with Cudjoe is one of the most important legal documents of the period [8][9][10][11].

**The Maryland 1729 Act:**
"An Act for the more effectual Punishing of Negroes, and other Slaves; and for taking away the benefit of Clergy from certain offenders" empowered justices to impose extreme punishments: *"it shall and may be lawful for the Justices before whom such Conviction shall be, to give Judgment against such Negroe, or other Slave, to have the right Hand cut off, to be hang'd in the usual Manner, the Head severed from the Body, the Body divided into Four Quarters."* [89]

**The Bermuda 1730 Execution of Sarah "Sally" Bassett:**
In 1730, Sarah Bassett was executed by burning in Bermuda for alleged poisoning plots, reflecting the use of capital punishment (specifically death by burning) for enslaved people accused of poisoning — a common fear in slave societies [90].

**The Royal African Company and the Assiento:**
The business of the Royal African Company was largely increased by the Asiento contracts made for the supply of slaves to Spanish colonies. The 1713 Assiento treaty between England and Spain still shaped the slave trade during the 1730s [88].

**The New York Conspiracy of 1741:**
While just after the 1730s, this alleged slave and poor white plot to revolt and burn New York City resulted in 17 Blacks and 4 whites being hanged, 13 Blacks burned at stake, and many banishments. Stono's legacy directly influenced the hysteria [91].

## Summary for the Novelist

**For a novel set in the 1730s British Empire, the following legal landscape should be depicted:**

1. **In England itself:** The legal status of enslaved people was deeply contested. Lord Chief Justice Holt had ruled in 1706 that "as soon as a negro comes to England he is free," but the Yorke-Talbot opinion of 1729 (not a court ruling, but influential) claimed the opposite. This ambiguity was not resolved until Somerset's case in 1772. A character bringing an enslaved person to England could face genuine legal uncertainty.

2. **In Barbados (1661 code in force):** Enslaved people were explicitly classified as "goods and Chattels." Masters could kill their own slaves with only a fine. Punishments included whipping, nose-slitting, facial burning, and death for offering violence to Christians.

3. **In Jamaica (1664 code, 1696 real estate classification, 1717 act in force):** Enslaved people were classified as real estate. The 1717 act prohibited masters from dismembering slaves at will (£100 penalty), but the state could cut off a foot for runaways absent over 30 days. Owners were compensated for executed slaves (capped at £40). The First Maroon War (1728-1739) ended with the 1739 peace treaty with Cudjoe.

4. **In South Carolina (1735 manumission law; 1740 Negro Act passed just after the 1730s):** The 1740 Act contains the most explicit language — "deemed, taken, and adjudged to be chattels personal." This was passed in direct response to the Stono Rebellion of September 1739.

5. **The Colonial Debts Act of 1732** made "houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments" liable for debt recovery across the empire — a crucial legal mechanism that treated enslaved people as property at the imperial level.

6. **The Molasses Act of 1733** — while containing no mention of slavery — protected the slave-based sugar plantations of the British West Indies.

7. **On ships:** Enslaved people were treated as "goods and merchandise" under admiralty law, though the 1736 Barnes/Bawow case shows this classification was contested in some colonial courts.

8. **Any white person** could arrest and punish enslaved people in most colonies, with minimal legal consequences for killing enslaved people during "correction."
