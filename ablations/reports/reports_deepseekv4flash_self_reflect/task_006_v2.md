## 1. Spousal RRSP vs. Individual RRSP: Tax Comparison Under Both Scenarios

### 1.1 Understanding the Core Mechanics of a Spousal RRSP

A Spousal RRSP is a registered retirement savings plan owned by one spouse (the **annuitant**) but funded by the other spouse (the **contributor**) [1][4][8]. The contributor receives the immediate tax deduction for contributions made, while the annuitant spouse generally reports withdrawals as income in retirement [1][2][3]. The fundamental purpose is to shift future taxable income to the lower-income spouse, reducing overall household tax burden and smoothing retirement income [1][9].

The CRA's official guidance states: "Contributions you make to a spousal or common-law partner RRSP reduce your RRSP deduction limit. Although you cannot contribute to your RRSP after December of the year you turn 71 years old, you can still contribute to your spouse's or common-law partner's RRSP until the December of the year that they turn 71" [3][4][5].

### 1.2 The 3-Year Attribution Rule

The three-year attribution rule is the most critical feature governing spousal RRSPs. It is codified under subsection 146(8.3) of the *Income Tax Act* [1][3][2]. If the annuitant spouse withdraws money from the spousal RRSP **within three calendar years** of the contributor's most recent contribution to that spousal RRSP, the withdrawn amount is attributed back to the contributor and taxed at the contributor's marginal rate [1][2][4][8].

The rule looks back at contributions made in the **year of withdrawal and the two preceding calendar years** [1][2][4]. As the CRA explains: "If you made contributions to your spouse's RRSPs in 2023, 2024, or 2025 and your spouse withdrew amounts, you may have to include those amounts in your 2025 income" [3].

**Practical Example 1 — Attribution Triggered:**
- You contribute $10,000 to a spousal RRSP in December 2025.
- Your spouse withdraws $5,000 from the spousal RRSP in June 2027.
- Since the withdrawal is within the same calendar year (2025) and the two preceding calendar years (2026, 2027), the $5,000 is attributed back to you and taxed at your 31.48% marginal rate [1][2].

**Practical Example 2 — Attribution Avoided:**
- You contribute $10,000 to a spousal RRSP in December 2025.
- No further contributions are made in 2026, 2027, or 2028.
- Your spouse withdraws $5,000 in January 2029.
- Since the last contribution was in 2025, and more than three calendar years have elapsed, the withdrawal is taxed in your spouse's hands at their lower marginal rate [1][4].

### 1.3 Scenario (a): You Have a Spouse with Lower or No Income

If your spouse has lower or no income, a **Spousal RRSP is the optimal strategy**. Here's why:

**At Contribution:**
You contribute to a spousal RRSP and receive the immediate tax deduction at your **higher marginal rate of 31.48%** (Ontario combined rate at $95,000 income) [6][7]. This maximizes your tax refund.

**At Withdrawal (after the 3-year attribution period):**
Your spouse withdraws the funds in retirement at their **lower marginal rate** — typically 20.05% (Ontario's lowest bracket) if they have minimal other income [1][2][6]. This creates significant tax arbitrage.

**Numerical Comparison: Contributing $10,000**

| Metric | Individual RRSP | Spousal RRSP |
|--------|-----------------|--------------|
| Tax refund now (at 31.48%) | $3,148 | $3,148 |
| Tax on withdrawal (if you withdraw at 31.48%) | $6,296 on $20,000 grown | N/A |
| Tax on withdrawal (if spouse withdraws at 20.05%) | N/A | $4,010 on $20,000 grown |
| **Net tax saving** | $0 (same rate in/out) | **+$2,286 saved** |

Source calculations based on [1][2][6][8].

For every $10,000 contributed, the spousal RRSP generates a **net tax saving of $2,286** compared to an individual RRSP, assuming your spouse is in the 20.05% bracket in retirement while you would have been in the 31.48% bracket [1][6]. As Ferguson Financial Planning summarizes: "A spousal RRSP can help spread retirement withdrawals across 2 tax returns, instead of crushing just 1. Done well, it can lower lifetime tax and make retirement income feel smoother" [1].

### 1.4 Scenario (b): You Are Single or Your Spouse Has Equal or Higher Income

**If you are single**, a Spousal RRSP is not applicable. An individual RRSP is the appropriate vehicle [8]. Your full RRSP deduction room can be used in your own name.

**If your spouse has equal or higher income**, there is less tax arbitrage benefit from income splitting through a Spousal RRSP. In this case:

- **Individual RRSPs for both spouses** are generally more straightforward and appropriate [1][8].
- If both spouses are in the same tax bracket, the RRSP deduction and withdrawal are at the same rate, eliminating the arbitrage advantage.
- However, even with equal incomes, a Spousal RRSP may still be useful for:
  - **Pension income credit optimization**: Having a spousal RRSP/RRIF allows each spouse to withdraw $2,000 of eligible pension income to claim the pension income credit, which provides a maximum federal annual tax savings of $280 per spouse [9][10].
  - **Age-based planning**: If one spouse is older and must convert their RRSP to a RRIF earlier (by December 31 of the year they turn 71), a spousal RRSP in the younger spouse's name defers mandatory withdrawals [1][5].
  - **Home Buyers' Plan access**: Both spouses can access the Home Buyers' Plan for up to $60,000 each from their respective RRSPs [4][8].

### 1.5 Interaction with Pension Income Splitting in Retirement

Pension income splitting, available since 2006, allows couples to transfer up to **50% of eligible pension income** from the higher-income spouse to the lower-income spouse [9][10][11]. Couples file CRA Form T1032 annually to split pension income [9][10].

**How Spousal RRSPs Complement Pension Income Splitting:**

1. **Before age 65**: Pension income splitting is not available for RRSP/RRIF withdrawals until age 65 (only employer pension income qualifies for those under 65). Spousal RRSPs allow effective income splitting in early retirement (ages 55–64) [1][9].

2. **Beyond 50%**: Spousal RRSPs allow effectively splitting more than 50% of income, since withdrawals from the spousal plan are taxed entirely to the lower-income spouse [9].

3. **The $2,000 Pension Income Credit**: The federal pension income credit provides a non-refundable tax credit of **15% on the first $2,000 of eligible pension income**, for a maximum federal annual savings of **$280 per spouse** [9][10][11]. By ensuring both spouses have RRSP/RRIF balances, you can double this credit's utilization within a couple [10][11]. As BMO Private Wealth states: "The transferee spouse may be eligible for the $2,000 pension income tax credit, potentially doubling this credit's use within a couple" [11].

4. **OAS Clawback Mitigation**: Shifting income to a lower-income spouse can reduce or eliminate Old Age Security (OAS) clawbacks, which begin at net income of approximately $93,454 in 2025 [10][11].

However, the rate relevant to the RRSP comparison is **31.48%** once Ontario surtaxes are considered (though the surtax applies only to provincial tax exceeding certain thresholds) [6][7]. ## 2. RESP Contribution Strategy: Front-Loading vs. Spreading Evenly

### 2.1 CESG Rules and Grant Details (2026)

The Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) is the cornerstone of Canadian RESP planning, providing government matching on contributions [14][15][16].

**Basic CESG:**
- 20% match on the first **$2,500** contributed per child per year
- Maximum annual grant: **$500 per child**
- Available regardless of family income [14][15][16]

**Additional CESG (Income-Tested):**
Your family income of $95,000 in 2026 qualifies you for the **10% additional CESG rate** on the first $500 contributed. The income thresholds for 2026 are:
- Family income **$0 to $58,523**: 20% additional CESG (up to $100 extra/year)
- Family income **$58,523 to $117,045**: 10% additional CESG (up to $50 extra/year)
- Family income **above $117,045**: no additional CESG

Source: [14][17][18]

This means each child can receive **$550/year** in CESG ($500 basic + $50 additional) on a $2,500 contribution, or up to **$1,050/year** ($1,000 basic + $50 additional) on a $5,000 catch-up contribution.

**Lifetime CESG Maximum:**
$7,200 per child (combined basic and additional) [14][15][16][17]

**CESG Eligibility Deadline:**
CESG grants end **December 31 of the year the child turns 17** [15][16][18]. Contributions can still be made after age 17 (up to the $50,000 lifetime limit), but no CESG will be paid on those contributions.

**Age 16/17 CESG Conditions:**
For children aged 16 or 17 to receive CESG, the following conditions must be met by December 31 of the year the child turns 15:
- **Condition A:** At least $2,000 must have been contributed to the RESP; OR
- **Condition B:** At least $100 must have been contributed in at least 4 separate years before the child turns 16 [15][16]

Your children (aged 8 and 11) have ample time to meet either condition.

### 2.2 The Catch-Up Mechanism

Unused CESG grant room carries forward from prior years. The catch-up allows you to contribute **up to $5,000 in a single year** and receive **up to $1,000 in basic CESG** (catching up one missed year at a time) [15][16][18]. As Embark explains: "The CESG catch-up system allows up to $5,000 contribution in one year to claim $1,000 CESG, catching up one year at a time" [18].

**Critical constraint:** You can only catch up **one prior missed year per calendar year**. Multiple missed years cannot be recovered with a single large contribution [15][18].

### 2.3 Time Horizon and Missed Room Analysis

**Child aged 11 (eldest, turning 18 in ~2033):**
- CESG-eligible years remaining (from 2026): ages 11 through 17 = **7 years** (2026–2032)
- Prior missed CESG room (from birth): ~11 years of carryforward available
- Lifetime contribution room: $50,000

**Child aged 8 (youngest, turning 18 in ~2036):**
- CESG-eligible years remaining: ages 8 through 17 = **10 years** (2026–2035)

## 3. Optimal Monthly Savings Allocation Between TFSA, RRSP, and RESP

### 3.1 Emergency Fund: The Foundation

For a freelancer with variable income, the emergency fund is the **most critical first step** before any other investing [19][20][21].

**Recommended Size:**
- **Freelancers need 6–9 months of essential expenses** (vs. 3–6 months for salaried employees) [19][20]
- For a family of 3–4 in Toronto, essential monthly expenses (food, shelter, utilities, debt payments, insurance) are approximately **$5,000–$5,600/month** [22][23]
- **6-month target:** $30,000–$33,600
- **9-month target (recommended for freelancers):** $45,000–$50,400
- **Initial priority:** Build a $30,000 minimum emergency fund **before** starting any other savings

**Where to Hold the Emergency Fund:**

| Option | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| **High-Interest Savings Account (HISA)** | Instant access, no contribution limits, CDIC insured up to $100K | Interest is taxable as income | First 3–4 months of expenses |
| **TFSA (invested in HISA or GICs)** | Interest/growth is tax-free, contribution room returns next year after withdrawal | Limited by TFSA room ($7,000/year plus cumulative room) | Additional savings beyond initial HISA buffer |

**Recommended Strategy:** Start with a **HISA** for the first $15,000–$20,000 of emergency savings (for immediate accessibility), then use a **TFSA** holding high-interest savings or short-term GICs for the remaining $10,000–$30,000 (for tax-free growth). LifeMoney Canada confirms: "The best strategy is to start with a HISA for your first 3–4 months. Use a TFSA for additional savings if you have room. This maximizes both accessibility and tax efficiency" [19].

**CRITICAL WARNING:** Do not use an RRSP as an emergency fund. RRSP withdrawals incur withholding tax (10–30%), full income tax at your marginal rate, permanent loss of contribution room, and cannot be recontributed without using new room [19][21].

### 3.2 The Priority Order for Registered Accounts

Once the emergency fund is fully funded ($30,000 minimum), the order of savings priority is:

**Step 1: RESP — Guaranteed 20% Return**
The CESG provides a **guaranteed, risk-free 20% return** on the first $2,500 per child per year. This is the highest-return investment available to Canadian families [14][15][24].
- Annual RESP target: **$5,000** ($2,500 per child)
- Immediate CESG return: **$1,000/year**
- This is a higher guaranteed return than any other investment vehicle

**Step 2: TFSA — Maximum Flexibility for Freelancers**
The TFSA is uniquely suited for freelancers because of its **ultimate flexibility** — tax-free withdrawals for any purpose, with contribution room restored the following year [25][26][27].
- Annual TFSA limit (2026): **$7,000** [25][26][27]
- Use TFSA for: long-term growth investments + as an income smoothing buffer for variable freelance income
- Withdraw during low-income months, recontribute the following January

**Step 3: RRSP — Tax Deduction in High-Income Years**
The RRSP provides an immediate tax deduction at your marginal rate, but contributions are locked in until retirement [28][29][30].
- Annual RRSP room generated: **$17,100** (18% × $95,000) [28][29]
- Tax savings: $5,500 contributed × 29.65% = ~**$1,631/year refund**
- Contribute remaining savings after RESP and TFSA are funded

### 3.4 Why TFSA Takes Priority Over RRSP for Freelancers

For a freelancer with variable income at the $95,000 income level, the TFSA should be prioritized **after** the RESP but **before** the RRSP for several reasons:

1. **Variable cash flow needs**: The ability to withdraw tax-free during low-income months is critical for freelancers [25][26]
2. **No mandatory withdrawals**: Unlike RRSPs (which must be converted to RRIFs by age 71), TFSAs have no age-based withdrawal requirements [27]
3. **Contribution room restoration**: TFSA withdrawals restore contribution room on January 1 the following year, allowing you to "reset" your buffer annually [25][27]
4. **Tax bracket management**: At $95,000, your marginal rate is 29.65%. In a low-income year (e.g., $70,000), you might be better off skipping the RRSP deduction and carrying it forward to a high-income year (e.g., $120,000) when your marginal rate would be 37.16% [6][7][28]

Wealthsimple's Freelancer Guide advises: "Most freelancers can expect to owe roughly 30% on all income... so you'll want to put aside at least that much from every cheque. If you're earning a high income now and expect less in retirement, prioritize RRSP. If in a lower bracket or income is unpredictable, start with the TFSA" [30].

### 3.5 The "Reinvest the Refund" Strategy

A powerful strategy for freelancers is to **contribute to the RRSP for the immediate tax refund, then reinvest that refund into the TFSA** [27][28]. This leverages the best of both accounts:

**Example:**
1. Contribute **$5,500 to RRSP** in a year with $95,000 income
2. Receive **$1,631 tax refund** (at 29.65% marginal rate)
3. Reinvest **$1,631 into TFSA** for tax-free growth
4. Result: $5,500 growing tax-deferred in RRSP + $1,631 growing tax-free in TFSA

As Questrade notes: "The smartest strategy is often to use both: Contribute to your RRSP for an upfront tax deduction, then invest your refund into your TFSA for tax-free growth" [27].

## 5. Freelance-Specific Considerations for Variable Cash Flow

### 5.1 Budgeting with Irregular Income

Traditional budgeting methods (like 50/30/20) do not work for variable monthly income [33][34]. The recommended approach for Canadian freelancers involves five key adjustments:

**1. Budget Based on Your Lowest Income Month**
"Budget based on your worst month in the last 3–6 months... This is the absolute minimum you can count on" [33]. Calculate your average monthly income from the past 12 months, but budget your essential expenses based on your lowest month.

**2. Prioritize Expenses by Tiers**
Organize expenses in this order [33][34]:
- **Tier 1:** Survival (food, shelter, utilities)
- **Tier 2:** Taxes (25–30% of gross income set aside)
- **Tier 3:** Essential bills (debt payments, insurance)
- **Tier 4:** Savings (emergency fund, retirement)
- **Tier 5:** Business (software, supplies, professional development)
- **Tier 6:** Wants (discretionary spending)

**3. Set Aside 25–30% of Gross Income Immediately for Taxes**
"Set aside 25–30% of every dollar you earn. Put it in a separate savings account labeled 'TAXES.' Don't touch it" [33]. As a freelancer in Ontario earning $95,000, your combined federal + Ontario income tax (at ~29.65% marginal rate), plus CPP contributions (11.9% of net self-employment income between $3,500 and $71,300), means your effective tax burden is significant [35][36]. Keep this in a separate HISA (e.g., EQ Bank at 2.75–3.5% or Wealthsimple Cash at 2.25%) [37][38].

**4. Budget in Flexible "Payment Waves"**
Instead of calendar months, align your spending with when payments actually arrive. This means tracking "payment waves" rather than monthly cycles [33].

**5. Celebrate Good Months by Saving, Not Spending**
When you have a banner month (e.g., $15,000–$20,000), save the surplus into your TFSA buffer rather than increasing lifestyle spending [33].

### 5.2 Using TFSA as an Income Buffer

The TFSA is uniquely suited for freelancers because of its flexibility. Here is how to use it as an income smoothing vehicle:

**The Core Strategy:**
1. During high-income months, contribute surplus cash to your TFSA
2. During low-income months, withdraw from your TFSA to cover essential expenses
3. On January 1 of the following year, the withdrawn contribution room is restored, allowing you to rebuild the buffer [25][26][27]

**Critical Rules to Remember:**
- **Withdrawals are always tax-free and penalty-free** — no special forms, no income inclusion [25][26][27]
- **Withdrawals do not impact taxable income** — critical for maintaining income-tested benefits if applicable [26]
- **Do NOT recontribute in the same calendar year** — if you withdraw $4,000 in March, you cannot recontribute that $4,000 until January 1 of the next year, or you'll face a 1% per month penalty on the excess [25][26]
- **Keep your own records** — the CRA's TFSA information is updated only once per year (typically by April), so relying on CRA My Account can lead to accidental over-contributions [25]

**Practical Example:**
- **January 2026 (good month):** Gross income of $15,000. Set aside $4,500 for taxes, cover $5,500 in essential expenses, contribute **$5,000 to TFSA**
- **March 2026 (lean month):** Gross income of $3,000. Set aside $900 for taxes, need $5,500 for essential expenses. Withdraw **$3,400 from TFSA**
- **January 2027:** The $3,400 withdrawn in March 2026 is restored to your TFSA contribution room. You can now recontribute this amount

As Wealthsimple advises: "Set up automatic withdrawals from your chequing account to make saving effortless" [30].

### 5.4 Timing RRSP Contributions for Maximum Tax Benefit

For freelancers, when you contribute to your RRSP is as important as how much you contribute.

**The First 60 Days Rule:**
The RRSP first 60 days rule allows you to make contributions in January and February of the current year and choose whether to claim the deduction on the **previous year's or the current year's** tax return [41][42]. This is powerful for freelancers because:

- By March of Year N, you know your exact Year N-1 income
- You can decide which year gives the maximum tax deduction
- You receive the refund in approximately 2–4 weeks after filing

**Optimal RRSP Timing Strategy for Freelancers:**

1. **Contribute in high-income years, skip in low-income years:** Every dollar contributed reduces your taxable income. In a good year (e.g., $120,000), you save 37.16 cents per dollar contributed. In a lean year (e.g., $70,000), you only save 29.65 cents [6][7][28].

2. **Use a holding account:** Set aside 10–15% of each payment into a holding account (separate HISA). At the end of the tax year, make a lump-sum RRSP contribution based on your actual income [19][20].

3. **Defer deductions if needed:** If you contribute to an RRSP in a low-income year, you can choose **not** to deduct the contribution and carry the deduction forward to a future high-income year [41][42]. However, the contribution itself consumes RRSP room regardless of when you claim the deduction.

4. **Leverage the $2,000 over-contribution buffer:** The CRA allows a lifetime $2,000 grace amount for over-contributions without penalty [42]. This can be used strategically if you contributed slightly more than your room in a high-income year.

**Practical Example:**
- **2025 income:** $95,000 (good year)
- **2024 income:** $70,000 (low year)
- Your RRSP room for 2025 is based on 18% of **2024's earned income**: 18% × $70,000 = **$12,600**
- You contribute **$12,600** to your RRSP before March 2, 2026 (the deadline for claiming on 2025 taxes)
- You claim the deduction against your **$95,000 income**, saving **$3,736 in taxes** (at 29.65% marginal rate)
- You then reinvest the **$3,736 refund** into your TFSA for tax-free growth

### 5.5 CPP Considerations for Self-Employed

As a self-employed graphic designer, you must pay **both the employee and employer portions of CPP** — totaling **11.9%** of net self-employment income between $3,500 and $71,300 [35][36].

**2026 CPP Projections:**
- Base CPP (Tier 1): 11.9% on earnings between $3,500 and ~$71,300
- CPP2 (Enhanced Tier 2): 4% × 2 = 8% on earnings between ~$71,300 and ~$85,000
- **Total maximum CPP contribution:** ~$8,716 [35][36]

**Tax Treatment:**
- The "employer" half of CPP contributions is tax-deductible (Line 22200 of T1)
- The "employee" half provides a non-refundable tax credit (Schedule 8)
- This effectively reduces the after-tax cost by approximately 22% [35]

**Strategy to Reduce CPP:**
Maximizing legitimate business deductions reduces your net self-employment income, which in turn reduces your CPP contributions by 11.9 cents per dollar deducted [35]. Common deductions for graphic designers include:
- Home office expenses (proportion of rent, utilities, internet)
- Design software subscriptions (Adobe Creative Cloud, Figma)
- Computer hardware and equipment (CCA depreciation)
- Professional development and courses
- Marketing and website hosting
- 50% of business meals and entertainment [39][40][43]

### 5.6 HST/GST Registration and Input Tax Credits

If your freelance revenue exceeds **$30,000 in four consecutive calendar quarters**, you must register for and collect HST [39][40]. Ontario's HST rate is **13%** (5% federal GST + 8% provincial portion) [44].

**If Registered:**
- Charge 13% HST on all taxable services to Canadian clients
- File HST returns annually, quarterly, or monthly (CRA sets your filing period)
- Claim **Input Tax Credits (ITCs)** to recover HST paid on business expenses [45][46]
- ITCs can be claimed for: software subscriptions, equipment, home office expenses, professional fees, advertising, and more [45]

**If Not Yet Registered (revenue under $30,000):**
- You are not required to register
- You cannot charge HST or claim ITCs
- Consider **voluntary registration** if you have significant business expenses (you can recover the HST you pay on purchases) [40]

**Graphic Designer-Specific Deductible Expenses:**
- Adobe Creative Cloud subscription: fully deductible
- Stock photography and design assets: fully deductible
- Computer hardware (MacBook, monitor, tablet): depreciated via CCA
- Website hosting and domain fees: fully deductible
- Office supplies and printing: fully deductible
- Home office: percentage of rent/mortgage interest, utilities, internet, property taxes [43]

## 6. Integrated Strategy: Putting It All Together

### 6.1 Year 1 Action Plan (2026)

**Phase 1: Emergency Fund (Immediate Priority)**
- Build emergency fund to $30,000 minimum
- Hold first $15,000 in HISA (EQ Bank at 2.75–3.5%)
- Hold next $15,000 in TFSA-HISA (tax-free growth)
- Set up automatic monthly transfer of ~$2,500 until funded

**Phase 2: RESP (Start Immediately)**
- Open a Family RESP (allows flexible allocation between children)
- Contribute $5,000/year ($2,500 per child)
- Catch-up: Contribute $5,000 per child for the 11-year-old (using catch-up rules) to maximize CESG
- Use the hybrid strategy: $5,000/year for 4 years (ages 11–14), then $2,500/year for 3 years (ages 15–17)

**Phase 3: TFSA (After Emergency Fund)**
- Contribute $7,000/year to max out 2026 room
- Invest in a diversified portfolio (60–80% equities for long-term growth)
- Treat as dual-purpose: long-term savings + income buffer

**Phase 4: RRSP (Remaining Savings)**
- Contribute remaining $5,500/year
- Time contributions in high-income months
- Reinvest the tax refund (~$1,631) into TFSA

### 6.2 Dynamic Adjustments for Income Fluctuations

| Income Scenario | Recommended Adjustment |
|----------------|----------------------|
| **High-income month** ($15,000+) | Maximize RRSP contribution; add surplus to TFSA buffer |
| **Normal month** ($8,000–$12,000) | Maintain standard allocation (RESP $417 + TFSA $583 + RRSP $458) |
| **Low-income month** ($3,000–$6,000) | Reduce/skip RRSP contribution; withdraw from TFSA buffer if needed |

| **Lean year** ($70,000) | Reduce/skip RRSP; prioritize RESP and TFSA; carry forward RRSP room |

### 6.3 Key Takeaways

1. **Spousal RRSP is highly advantageous** if you have a spouse with lower income (saves ~$2,286 per $10,000 contributed). If single or both earn equally, individual RRSPs are sufficient.

2. **RESP hybrid strategy** maximizes CESG grants while balancing cash flow. For the 11-year-old, capture $5,850 in CESG; for the 8-year-old, capture the full $7,200 lifetime maximum.

3. **Savings priority: Emergency Fund → RESP → TFSA → RRSP** — with $17,500 annual disposable savings, allocate $5,000 to RESP, $7,000 to TFSA, and $5,500 to RRSP.

4. **Education-First strategy** achieves the highest after-tax net worth by 2032 (~$145,555), but the **Balanced strategy** is the most practical recommendation, providing strong after-tax wealth (~$134,850) with superior liquidity (~$49,600 in TFSA).

5. **Freelancers need 6–9 months of emergency savings** ($30,000–$50,400 for a Toronto family of 3–4), held in a HISA and TFSA. Use the TFSA as an income buffer, withdrawing in low-income months and recontributing the following January.

6. **Time RRSP contributions to high-income years** for maximum tax savings (37.16% if earning $120,000 vs. 29.65% at $95,000). Reinvest tax refunds into your TFSA for tax-free growth.
