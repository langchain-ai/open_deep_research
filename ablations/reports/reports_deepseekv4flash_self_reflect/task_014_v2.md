# Comprehensive Investment-Grade Analysis of NCD IPOs Open on May 30, 2026

## Executive Summary

This report provides a rigorous, investment-grade analysis of Non-Convertible Debenture (NCD) Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) open for subscription as of **May 30, 2026**, specifically evaluated for a retiree seeking fixed income. The analysis is designed to withstand scrutiny by institutional investment committees and compliance teams.

On May 30, 2026, **two NCD public issues are open for subscription**: Kosamattam Finance Limited (IND A/Stable by India Ratings) and Muthoot Mercantile Limited Tranche VIII (CRISIL BBB+/Stable). No AAA or AA rated NCD IPOs are open, representing a notable gap in the high-quality segment for risk-averse retirees.

**Primary Recommendation:** For risk-averse retirees specifically seeking fixed income, **Kosamattam Finance NCD Series VII (60-month, 10.00% monthly coupon, effective yield 10.47%)** offers the better risk-adjusted yield among the open issues, given its higher credit rating (IND A/Stable vs. CRISIL BBB+/Stable). Retirees should consider allocating no more than **10-15%** of their fixed-income portfolio to NCDs, with the remainder in safer instruments such as SCSS (8.2%), bank FDs (up to 7.05% for senior citizens), and AAA PSU corporate bonds.

**Positive Factors:**

**Key Constraints:**

## 3. Post-Tax Yield Analysis

### 3.1 Assumptions

**Tax Regimes for FY 2026-27:**

**Senior Citizen (Age 60+, Old Tax Regime):**
- Assumed taxable income above ₹10 lakh (30% tax bracket)
- Tax rate: 30% + 4% health and education cess = **31.2%**
- Section 80TTB: **NOT applicable to NCD interest** [17][18][19]

**General Investor (Age <60, New Tax Regime):**
- Assumed taxable income above ₹24 lakh (30% tax bracket)
- Tax rate: 30% + 4% cess = **31.2%**
- No deductions available for NCD interest under the new regime [17][18][19]

**Important Note:** Both investors face the same effective tax rate of **31.2%** on NCD interest income. The critical distinction is that senior citizens in the **old tax regime** can claim Section 80TTB deduction on bank/post office deposit interest (up to ₹50,000), but this deduction does NOT apply to NCD interest.

### 3.2 Section 80TTB Clarification

**Critical finding for senior citizen investors:** Section 80TTB of the Income Tax Act provides a deduction of up to ₹50,000 on interest income **ONLY from deposits with banks, post offices, and cooperative societies**. Interest from corporate fixed deposits, bonds, or Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs) is **explicitly excluded** from this deduction [17][18][19].

Multiple authoritative sources confirm:
- "Interest from company fixed deposits, bonds, or NCDs do not qualify" [18]
- "Interest earned on company fixed deposits, bonds, or NCDs does not qualify under Section 80TTB" [19]
- Section 80TTB "excludes interest from bonds and debentures" [17]

This means that **both senior citizens and general investors face identical tax treatment on NCD interest**, with no preferential deduction available to senior citizens.

### 3.5 TDS Implications

Under Section 193 of the Income Tax Act (now Section 393(1) under the Income-tax Act, 2025 effective April 1, 2026) [26][27]:

- **Standard TDS rate:** 10% on interest from listed NCDs
- **Threshold:** No TDS if annual interest from debentures is below ₹10,000
- **Without PAN:** TDS at 20%
- **Form 15H/15G:** Can be submitted to avoid TDS if total income is below taxable limit

Since both investors are in the 30% tax bracket (31.2% effective rate), which exceeds the 10% TDS rate:
- TDS of **10% will be deducted** at source by the issuer
- Additional **21.2% tax** will be due at the time of filing income tax return
- Investors must report gross interest income and pay the balance tax

### 5.2 Major NCD Default Events in India — Lessons Learned

#### IL&FS Crisis (September 2018)

**Amount Involved:** Approximately ₹91,000 crore in debt defaults [32][33]

**Timeline:**
- First defaults by group company IL&FS Transportation Networks Limited (ITNL) occurred in June 2018 but were concealed until August 2018 [33]
- In September 2018, IL&FS defaulted on commercial papers, triggering a systemic liquidity crisis [32]
- Government intervention appointed a new board led by Uday Kotak; Grant Thornton conducted forensic audit (Project Icarus) [33]

**Rating Agency Failure:** The forensic audit revealed collusion with rating agencies to maintain favorable credit ratings. IL&FS was AAA-rated until shortly before its collapse [33]. Possible kickbacks received by top management of rating agencies for favorable ratings [33].

**Key Misconduct:** Manipulation of loan provisioning, evergreening practices to mask NPAs (₹85,500 million in fresh loans to repay old loans), inter-company round-tripping of funds, inflated financial statements [33]. The company had 348 subsidiaries and associates, with substantial lending to group companies masked as external lending [33].

**Market Impact:**
- Wiped out approximately ₹8.48 lakh crore of investor wealth [32]
- NCD issuances declined ~50% year-on-year during H1 FY2019 [34]
- Credit spreads on NBFC AAA paper doubled to ~160 bps by February 2019 [35]
- Mid- and small-sized players (<₹30,000 crore loan book) found it difficult to raise money through NCDs [34]

**Lessons Learned:**
- Even AAA ratings can fail; governance and liquidity management are critical
- Demonstrated the dangers of asset-liability mismatch and over-reliance on short-term borrowing for long-term infrastructure projects
- Prompted tighter regulation of NBFCs by RBI, including the 'upper-layer' framework
- Primary NCD issuances declined ~50% in H1 FY2019 but recovered in H2 [34]

#### DHFL Default (June 2019)

**Amount Involved:** Total liabilities of ₹83,873 crore, including ₹41,431 crore to NCD holders and ₹6,188 crore to fixed deposit holders [36][37]

**Timeline:**
- June 4, 2019: DHFL defaulted on NCD repayments of ₹50 crore [36]
- Rating agencies CRISIL, ICRA, and CARE downgraded DHFL to 'Default' rating [36]
- November 2019: RBI superseded DHFL's board, initiated insolvency under IBC [37]
- Resolution: Piramal Group acquired DHFL at ₹37,250 crore; NCLT approved June 2021 [38]

**Retail Investor Impact:** Approximately ₹15,000 crore of retail investor money was stuck in DHFL's NCDs and FDs [37]. Secured NCD holders received payouts ranging from 5% to 100% of admitted claims depending on investment amount [39].

**Key Systemic Issue — Debenture Redemption Reserve (DRR):** Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman had proposed scrapping the obligation of DRR, which would have required at least 25% of NCD repayments to be reserved. After the DHFL default, investors rued the Centre's decision to scrap DRR [37].

**Lessons Learned:**
- Consumer commission held rating agencies (CARE, Brickwork) and debenture trustee legally liable for negligence [40]
- Demonstrated the inadequacy of investor protection for retail NCD holders
- The IBC resolution process can take years, leaving investors stranded
- Supreme Court (April 2025) approved the Piramal resolution plan, reinforcing primacy of Committee of Creditors' commercial wisdom [38]

#### Reliance Capital Default (September 2019)

**Amount Involved:** ₹20,511 crore total financial indebtedness; ₹17,000 crore debt downgraded to default grade 'D' [41][42]

**Timeline:**
- September 2019: CARE Ratings downgraded entire outstanding debt to 'CARE D' [42]
- October 2019: Failed to make interest payment on NCDs due October 16 and 17, 2019 [42]
- By February 2021: Defaulted for the 49th consecutive time on NCD payments [41]
- EPFO impacted with ₹535 crore interest default on ₹2,500 crore NCD investment [43]

**Lessons Learned:**
- The downgrade-to-default cascade: A single rating downgrade to 'D' triggered cross-default acceleration clauses, turning manageable liquidity into full-blown default
- Demonstrated contagion risk within the Anil Ambani group
- Showed the vulnerability of institutional investors (EPFO) holding NCDs
- Asset cover of outstanding debentures had fallen below 100%, impairing NCD holder security [41]

#### SREI Group Default (2021)

**Amount Involved:** Total debt of approximately ₹30,000 crore; ₹11,117.71 crore in bank loans plus ₹710.63 crore in bonds and NCDs rated 'D' [44][45]

**Timeline:**
- RBI flagged related-party lending issues as early as FY2020 [45]
- October 2021: RBI superseded boards of Srei Infrastructure Finance and Srei Equipment Finance [44]
- Only the second time such action was taken against an NBFC under the RBI Act (after DHFL) [44]

**Lessons Learned:**
- Delays in regulatory action were criticized — "What prompted RBI to take corrective action when a special audit had already pointed out a problem in the books?" [45]
- Demonstrated the danger of related-party lending and need for robust governance in infrastructure finance
- NCD holders in lower-rated infrastructure companies face significant risk of total loss

#### IBC Recovery Context (Overall)

- ICRA (May 2026): IBC recoveries against admitted claims halved to **23% in 2025-26** from 46% in 2024-25 [46]
- Successfully resolved plans achieve ~31% recovery; liquidation cases recover only ~4% [46]
- Average resolution time worsened to **744 days** as of March 31, 2026 [46]

## 8. Regulatory Landscape & Policy Implications

### 8.1 SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021

The NCD IPO market operates under the SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021 (NCS Regulations), effective August 16, 2021 [53][54].

**Key Provisions:**
- **Eligibility:** Issuer shall not make an issue if promoters or directors are debarred by SEBI or are willful defaulters or fugitive economic offenders [53]
- **Credit Rating:** Must obtain rating from at least one credit rating agency, disclosed in offer document [53]
- **Debenture Trustee:** Mandatory appointment for debt securities [53]
- **Issue Timeline:** Public issues shall be open for a minimum of two working days and a maximum of ten working days [53]
- **Listing Requirements:** NCDs must be listed on recognized stock exchanges within T+6 working days from issue closure [54]
- **Minimum Subscription:** 75% of the issue size [54]
- **Post-Listing Obligations:** Continuous disclosure of financial results, credit rating actions, and utilization of funds [54]

### 8.2 Recent Amendments to NCS Regulations

**SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) (Amendment) Regulations, 2025 (October 28, 2025):** [55]

- Issuers now required to execute the trust deed in such format and within such timelines as specified by SEBI
- Key deviations from the specified format can be accepted by debenture trustees if a key summary sheet capturing deviations with rationale is provided by the issuer in the General Information Document/Key Information Document or Shelf Prospectus
- Previous requirement to structure trust deed into two parts (Part A for statutory information, Part B for issue-specific details) removed to simplify documentation

**SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) (Amendment) Regulations, 2026 (January 20, 2026):** [56]

- **Definition Introduced:** "Retail individual investor" defined as an individual applying or bidding for debt securities up to ₹2 lakh
- **Incentives Allowed:** Issuers now permitted to offer incentives such as additional interest or discounts on issue price to specific categories of investors
- **Eligible Categories:** Senior citizens, women, serving or retired defence personnel and their widows/widowers, retail individual investors
- **Non-Transferable:** Incentives permitted solely for initial allottee and do not transfer with secondary sales
- **Objective:** Enhance retail participation and encourage public issuances in India's corporate debt market

### 8.3 Debenture Trustee Requirements

- Mandatory appointment of debenture trustee for NCDs with maturity beyond 18 months [53]
- Trustee monitors issuer compliance, enforces security on behalf of debenture holders
- Ensures adequate charged property is maintained
- **Nominee Director Provision (2023 Amendment):** All trust deeds must contain a provision mandating the issuer to appoint a nominee director, nominated by the debenture trustee, to its board upon specified defaults [54]
- **DHFL Consumer Commission Ruling (2025):** Established that trustees and rating agencies can be held legally liable for negligence, strengthening investor protection [40]

### 8.4 Tax Treatment

**TDS on NCD Interest (Section 193 / Section 393(1) of Income-tax Act, 2025):** [26][27]
- TDS at **10%** on interest from listed NCDs
- Threshold: No TDS if annual interest below ₹10,000
- Form 15H/15G can be submitted to avoid TDS if total income is below taxable limit
- Without PAN: TDS at 20%

**Capital Gains Tax on NCDs (Post-Finance Act 2024):** [57][58]

| Holding Period | Listed NCDs | Unlisted NCDs |
|---------------|------------|---------------|
| **Short-term (≤12 months)** | Taxed at slab rate (up to 31.2%) | Taxed at slab rate (up to 31.2%) |
| **Long-term (>12 months)** | **12.5% without indexation** | 20% with indexation (if held >36 months) |

**Finance Act 2024 Changes (Effective July 23, 2024):**
- LTCG rate on listed bonds/NCDs reduced from 10% to **12.5%** but indexation benefit removed
- Holding period for listed securities simplified to **12 months** (from 36 months previously)
- For unlisted NCDs held >36 months, LTCG taxed at 20% with indexation

**Budget 2026:** No changes to the LTCG tax rate structure introduced in 2024 [58].

**Section 80TTB Clarification (Reiterated):** [17][18][19]
- Provides deduction of up to ₹50,000 on interest income from deposits with banks, post offices, and cooperative societies only
- **NCD interest is NOT eligible** for this deduction
- Only available under the old tax regime

### 8.5 Proposed Regulatory Changes

**NITI Aayog Report (December 2025) — "Deepening the Corporate Bond Market in India":** [59]
- Enhanced inter-regulatory coordination (SEBI, RBI, MCA)
- Simplified disclosure norms to reduce reliance on private placements (98% of market)
- Corporate Bond Dealers (CBDs) to improve liquidity
- Lower minimum investment thresholds for retail investors
- Standardized green bond taxonomies
- Target: Expand corporate bond market to ₹100-120 trillion by 2030

### 9.3 Bond Market Implied Default Probabilities

Using the spread between NCD IPO yields and AAA PSU bonds:

| **Muthoot Mercantile NCD Series 7 (73mo)** | 12.07% | 7.80% | **427 bps** | **~4.27%** |

*Based on simplified assumption: Implied Default Probability ≈ Credit Spread / (1 - Recovery Rate), assuming 40% recovery rate

**Risk Premium Assessment:**
- **Kosamattam (IND A):** The market is pricing in a ~2.82% annual default probability. This is higher than the historical 1-year default rate for IND A (0.8%) but broadly consistent with the 3-year rate (~2.0%). The premium reflects investor caution about the high leverage (D/E >5.0x).
- **Muthoot Mercantile (CRISIL BBB+):** The market is pricing in a ~4.27% annual default probability. This is significantly higher than CRISIL's historical BBB default rate (0.4% 1-year, 2.5% 3-year). This suggests the market is demanding additional risk premium for the smaller scale, higher leverage, and lower credit rating.

**Conclusion on Pricing:** Both NCDs appear to offer **adequate credit spreads** relative to historical default probabilities. The spreads are not excessive (which would suggest bargain pricing) but are reasonable for the risk profiles. No systemic mispricing is evident.

## 10. Investment Thesis & Recommendations

### 10.1 Key Findings Summary

1. **Two NCD IPOs are open** as of May 30, 2026: Kosamattam Finance (IND A/Stable, up to ₹300 crore) and Muthoot Mercantile Tranche VIII (CRISIL BBB+/Stable, up to ₹150 crore). No AAA or AA rated NCD IPOs are available.

2. **Kosamattam Finance (IND A/Stable)** offers the stronger credit profile with coupons from 8.50% to 10.00% and effective yields up to 10.47%. The company has a larger scale, stronger funding profile (39 banking relationships), and a clean track record across 37 debt offerings.

3. **Muthoot Mercantile (CRISIL BBB+/Stable)** offers higher absolute yields (10.50% to 11.00%, effective yields up to 12.07%) but at a lower credit rating. The company has excellent asset quality (GNPA 0.23%) and strong profitability (RoA 4.2%) but smaller scale and concentrated funding profile.

4. **Both issuers share critical risk factors:** Very high leverage (D/E >5.0x), concentration in gold loans (>95% of income), and geographic concentration. These structural characteristics warrant caution for risk-averse retirees.

5. **Section 80TTB does not apply** to NCD interest, meaning senior citizens do not receive any tax preference on NCD income versus general investors.

6. **The current interest rate environment** (repo rate at 5.25%, 125 bps of cuts in CY2025) makes the available coupon rates attractive on a spread basis, particularly for locking in yields for longer tenures.

7. **Historical default studies** confirm that A-rated instruments have very low 1-year default rates (0.1-0.8%) and BBB-rated instruments have moderate rates (0.4-2.3%). However, the IL&FS, DHFL, Reliance Capital, and SREI precedents demonstrate that ratings can fail catastrophically when governance lapses occur.

### 10.4 Final Investment Thesis

**The primary recommendation for a retiree seeking fixed income is structured as follows:**

**Core Allocation (65-75% of fixed-income portfolio):**
- Sovereign-guaranteed instruments: SCSS (8.2%), Bank FDs (up to 7.05%), RBI FRB (8.05%)
- These provide capital protection, tax benefits via Section 80TTB on first ₹50,000 of interest, and liquidity

**Satellite Allocation (10-15% of fixed-income portfolio):**
- **Kosamattam Finance NCD Series VII (60-month, 10.00% monthly coupon, 10.47% effective yield, IND A/Stable)**
- This offers the best risk-adjusted yield among the open NCD issues

**Tactical Allocation (5-10% of fixed-income portfolio):**
- **Muthoot Mercantile NCD Series 5 (36-month, 10.75% monthly coupon, 11.31% effective yield, CRISIL BBB+/Stable)** or **Muthoot Mercantile NCD Series 7 (73-month, 11.00% cumulative, 12.07% effective yield, CRISIL BBB+/Stable)**

**Supporting Evidence for Kosamattam Recommendation:**

1. **Best risk-adjusted yield:** At 10.47% effective (7.20% post-tax for a 31.2% bracket investor), it offers a 282 bps spread over AAA PSU bonds and a 522 bps spread over the RBI repo rate, compensating adequately for the IND A credit risk.

2. **Lock-in at elevated rates:** With the RBI cutting rates by 125 bps in 2025 and rates likely to remain low (5.25% repo rate), locking in a 10.47% effective yield for 5 years is strategically attractive. The cumulative options eliminate reinvestment risk entirely.

3. **Adequate credit profile:** The IND A rating (upgraded from IND A- in July 2025) reflects stable asset quality (GNPA 1.0%), healthy capitalization (CRAR 18.7%), strong profitability (RoA 2.06%), and an improved funding profile with 39 banking relationships.

4. **Clean track record:** 37 consecutive debt offerings since April 2014 with no defaults or delays. This is a "frequent visitor of the debt market" with a demonstrated commitment to servicing obligations.

5. **Larger issue size:** Up to ₹300 crore total (including green shoe) provides better secondary market liquidity prospects than smaller NCD issues.

**Risk Mitigation Measures:**
- Limit total NCD allocation to **15-25%** of total fixed-income portfolio (combined Kosamattam + Muthoot)
- Hold to **maturity** to avoid interest rate volatility
- **Monitor** quarterly financial results for deterioration in GNPA, leverage (downgrade trigger >6.0x for Kosamattam), or profitability (downgrade trigger RoA <1% for Muthoot)
- **Diversify** across AAA PSU bonds and sovereign instruments for the core portfolio
- Consider **cumulative options** to eliminate reinvestment risk in a falling rate environment

**Key Watchpoints:**
- **Kosamattam Downgrade Triggers:** Leverage exceeding 6.0x, rising delinquencies, or regulatory challenges [4]
- **Muthoot Downgrade Triggers:** RoA declining below 1% or gearing exceeding 6.0x [10]
- **Sector Risk:** Both issuers are gold loan NBFCs — a sharp gold price decline or regulatory tightening on gold loan LTV norms could impact both simultaneously
- **Leverage Concern:** Both issuers have very high debt-to-equity ratios (>5.0x post-issue), which is the single biggest risk factor
