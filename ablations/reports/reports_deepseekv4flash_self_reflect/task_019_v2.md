# Comprehensive Literature Review: The Anthropology of Gift Economies – From Classical Foundations to Contemporary Digital Practices

## Introduction

The anthropology of gift economies represents one of the most enduring and generative frameworks in social theory. From Bronisław Malinowski's pioneering ethnography of the Kula ring in the Trobriand Islands to Marcel Mauss's foundational essay on *The Gift*, and from Marshall Sahlins's systematic typology of reciprocity to feminist anthropologists' critical interventions, the study of gift exchange has continuously evolved. This literature review synthesizes these classical foundations and examines how contemporary digital practices—open-source software communities, social media attention economies, and cryptocurrency governance structures—both challenge and reinforce these established models. Throughout, the review identifies points of continuity and rupture, and highlights how feminist critiques illuminate new dimensions of power, labor, and exclusion in modern gift-exchange contexts.

## 1. Malinowski's Trobriand Kula Rings: Critical Reassessment

### 1.1 The Mechanics of the Kula Ring

Bronisław Malinowski's *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922) remains the foundational ethnographic work on the Kula ring, a ceremonial exchange system conducted among 18 island communities in the Massim archipelago of Papua New Guinea [1][3]. The system involves the circulation of two types of shell valuables: *soulava* (veigun), long necklaces of red shell-discs that travel **clockwise** around the ring of islands, and *mwali*, white shell armbands made from *Conus millepunctatus* shells that travel **counterclockwise** [1][3][6]. Soulava are given with the left hand and mwali with the right [6].

A critical feature of the Kula is that valuables never permanently stop. Malinowski wrote: "Every article moves in one direction only, never comes back, never permanently stops... no one ever keeps any of the Kula valuables for any length of time" [3]. Keeping valuables for more than a year or two is viewed negatively. Participants form lifelong trading partnerships called *karayta'u*, establishing mutual obligations to trade, offer protection, hospitality, and assistance [3]. The system operates in trading cycles called *keda*, lasting from two to five years, with twice-annual overseas expeditions led by "Big Men" in highly decorated ceremonial canoes [7].

### 1.2 The *Vaygu'a*/Gimwali Distinction

Malinowski emphasized a crucial distinction between two categories of exchange. Kula valuables are classified as *vaygu'a*—objects of high social and symbolic value that are "of no practical use" [3]. These are "primarily ornaments," yet "the privilege of decorating oneself with them is not the real aim of possession" [3]. Each first-class arm shell and necklace has a personal name and history, and their value increases the further they travel [7]. By contrast, *gimwali* refers to ordinary utilitarian trade or barter, which involves haggling and is strictly separated from the ceremonial Kula exchange, where no bargaining is allowed [1][3].

As the Wikipedia summary notes: "The Kula ring is a classic example of Marcel Mauss's distinction between gift and commodity exchange" [1]. A regional saying from Papua New Guinea captures the lifelong commitment: "Once in the Kula, always in the Kula" [1][3].

### 1.3 *Kitoum* Ownership

The concept of *kitoum* (or kitoma) is central to understanding ownership within the Kula. *Kitoum* grants the owner the right to keep, sell, or destroy an item, but Kula valuables are generally expected to be passed along in the ring [3]. As the Wikipedia article notes: "The Kula valuables are inalienable and circulate continuously, with temporary possession conferring status. Ownership as kitoum grants rights to keep, sell, or destroy an item, but the Kula valuables are generally expected to be passed along in the ring" [3].

Keesing's later reinterpretation challenges Malinowski's emphasis on the non-utilitarian nature of Kula valuables, noting that they "function as convertible wealth and instruments of power, used in marriage, land, and mortuary transactions" [6][12]. This complicates any "pure gift" reading of the Kula, because the valuables remain tied to their original owners even as they circulate.

### 1.4 Nancy Munn's *The Fame of Gawa*

Nancy D. Munn's *The Fame of Gawa: A Symbolic Study of Value Transformation in a Massim (Papua New Guinea) Society* (1986/1992) offers a groundbreaking phenomenological/semiotic analysis of value creation in the Kula context [8]. Munn's work is "concerned with fundamental practices of value creation on Gawa, a small island off the southeast coast of mainland Papua New Guinea" [8].

Munn's central argument is that "it is through achieving fame in the wider inter-island world that the Gawan community asserts its own internal viability" [8]. She integrates "the sociocultural construction of space and time, self-other relations and the body, and moral and political problems of hierarchy and equality" [8]. The concept of "fame" serves as a significant representation of social value, influencing cultural practices and intersubjective relations within the community [8]. Munn's research reveals "the competitive nature between personally owned shell valuables (kitomu) and those within the Kula, contradicting Malinowski's emphasis on the unidimensionality of Kula exchange" [8].

### 1.5 Social Hierarchies and Political Alliances

Participation in the Kula is hierarchical, with hereditary chiefs often monopolizing the exchange and organizing overseas voyages [1]. The system reinforces status and authority distinctions while simultaneously creating peaceful inter-island relations and enabling utilitarian trade. Malinowski argued that "the gift received puts the recipient in debt to the donor, and failure to reciprocate lowers his reputation and status" [2].

Rolf Ziegler's game-theoretic analysis interprets the Kula as "a signaling system for peaceful relationships among potentially hostile communities, with inbuilt checks against cheating" [7]. Ziegler demonstrates that "the observed Kula ring therefore is a 'small world' network, characterized by high clustering of local neighborhoods with relatively short global distances among all points, facilitating the rapid diffusion of reputation and trust" [7].

### 1.6 Recent Reassessments

*Hann & James (2024) "One Hundred Years of Argonauts"*: This edited volume commemorates the centenary of Malinowski's *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* [5]. The 362-page volume contains 14 chapters divided into four parts, using "ethnographic studies from around the world to contextualize the work politically and intellectually, examining its gestation and influence from multiple perspectives" [5]. The volume includes chapters on "Cultural Capital and Economic Stringency," "The Archaeology of the Kula and Malinowski's Notion of Economy," and "Digital Argonauts: From Kula Ring to Bush Internet in the Western Pacific" [5].

*Keesing's "New Lessons from Old Shells"*: Keesing's chapter explores changing perspectives on the Kula, examining how "the Kula partners of the Melanesian Massim have been one of anthropology's most compelling and influential and enduring images of Otherness" [6]. His work examines how the ethnographic evidence of the Kula at different moments in history could be read as a series of transformations [6].

Recent research by Macintyre challenges the notion of the Kula as a timeless institution, contending that "the stable, permanent Kula ring required pacification and colonial influence to emerge, as precolonial relations were cyclical and war-prone" [12].

## 2. Mauss's Essay on *The Gift*: Critical Reassessment

### 2.1 The Triple Obligation

Marcel Mauss's *Essai sur le don* (1925) analyzed economic practices worldwide, demonstrating that early social exchanges are based on three fundamental obligations: to **give**, to **receive**, and to **reciprocate** [10][12][16]. As Mauss wrote, "in theory such gifts are voluntary, but in fact they are given and repaid under obligation" [10]. These obligations create social bonds and solidarity that extend far beyond material wealth.

The obligation to **give** initiates the exchange and establishes the relationship itself. The obligation to **receive** is equally powerful—refusing a gift constitutes a rejection of the social relationship and can be a hostile act. The obligation to **reciprocate** is the most important, creating an ongoing cycle of exchange that binds individuals and groups together [10][12].

Mauss observed that "the exchanges of archaic societies... are at the same time economic, juridical, moral, aesthetic, religious, mythological and sociomorphological phenomena" [10]. He argued that "We have lost much... by the substitution of a rational economic system for a system in which exchange of goods was not a mechanical but a moral transaction, bringing about and maintaining human, personal relationships" [10].

### 2.2 The Concept of *Hau* and the Scholarly Debate

Mauss drew on Maori ethnography—specifically the writings of the Maori elder Tamati Ranapiri—to develop his concept of the *hau*, which he described as a spiritual force inherent in gifts that compels their return to the original owner [10][14]. Mauss argued that "the objects are never completely separated from the men who exchange them" [10]. According to Mauss, "the thing given is not inert. It is alive and often personified, and strives to bring to its original clan and homeland some equivalent to take its place" [10].

The primary source material for Mauss's interpretation came from Maori letters written by Tamati Ranapiri to anthropologist Elsdon Best [14]. As Stewart (2017) documents, "the letters, which sparked the whole debate, are material artefacts of the cross-cultural educational relationship between Tamati Ranapiri as teacher, and Elsdon Best as student" [14].

*Stewart (2017) and Kaupapa Maori Reassessment*: Georgina Stewart's 2017 article "The 'Hau' of Research: Mauss Meets Kaupapa Maori" provides an indigenous Maori philosophical response to Mauss's Eurocentric interpretation [14]. Stewart argues that "common-sense Maori readings of Ranapiri find no mystery in what he wrote about hau, but reinforce the significance of his correspondence, from the perspective of Kaupapa Maori versions of the history of Maori education" [14]. The article brings a critical perspective by combining Critical Discourse Analysis with Kaupapa Maori theory and research principles [14].

*Tcherkezoff on Polynesian Gift-Giving*: Tcherkezoff's work explores Polynesian concepts related to *hau*, particularly the Samoan *sau* and fine mats (*toonga*), comparing them with the Maori *hau* and treasures (*taonga*) [15]. He presents the notion of *sau* as "a life-giving creative force linked to personhood and the obligation and happiness in gift-giving," emphasizing that "the happiness to make a gift… invades the person as a feeling imposed from outside, it 'strengthens one's life'" [15].

*Claude Levi-Strauss's Critique*: Levi-Strauss famously accused Mauss of identifying the motivating force in gift exchange with the Maori idea of *hau*, arguing that Mauss gave a "mystical" interpretation. Levi-Strauss instead sought underlying structural principles of reciprocity that did not rely on indigenous spiritual concepts.

*Sahlins on the Spirit of the Gift*: Rather than a mystical spirit, Sahlins argued the *hau* represents "its 'yield' or 'product,' and returns of such yield protect against unearned wealth accumulation" [17]. Sahlins emphasizes the wide semantic field of *hau* that extends beyond material goods to include natural elements like the forest and man, all imbued with spiritual essence [17].

### 2.3 Derrida's Deconstruction of the Gift

Jacques Derrida's *Given Time: I. Counterfeit Money* (1992) presents his famous deconstruction of the gift—what he calls the "aporia of the gift" [32]. Derrida argues that "for a gift to be a true gift, it must not be seen as a gift" [32]. If a gift is recognized as a gift—either by the giver, the recipient, or a third party—it annuls itself because it creates obligation, debt, or expectation of return [32].

Derrida argues that "Mauss's *The Gift* speaks of everything but the gift: It deals with economy, exchange, contract"—not the pure gift itself [32]. He argues: "On the one hand, Mauss reminds us that there is no gift without bond, without bind, without obligation or ligature; but on the other hand, there is no gift" that escapes this circle [32]. Derrida's work concludes with "a detailed deconstruction of Charles Baudelaire's short story 'Counterfeit Money,' a story of false money, and a story of tobacco; the latter is something we give freely to others, without the expectation of return" [32].

### 2.4 Godelier's Resolution

Maurice Godelier's *The Enigma of the Gift* (1999) offers a major theoretical resolution to problems in gift theory [33]. Godelier's central argument is that we must distinguish between three types of objects: alienable commodities (which can be freely exchanged and sold), inalienable possessions (which must be kept), and gifts (which must be given away) [33].

Godelier "reassesses the significance of gifts in social life by focusing on sacred objects, which are never exchanged despite the value they possess" [33]. He argues that "traditional theories are flawed because they consider only exchangeable gifts" [33]. Drawing on his fieldwork in Melanesia, Godelier shows that "by explaining gift-giving in terms of sacred objects and the authoritative conferral of power associated with them," he "challenges both recent and traditional theories of gift-giving" [33].

Godelier's resolution extends the concept of "keeping-while-giving" (from Weiner) by proposing "that social life depends not just on inalienable or alienable goods alone but on the interplay of 'keeping-for-giving and giving-for-keeping'" [33].

### 2.5 Parry's Critique

Jonathan Parry's seminal 1986 article "The Gift, the Indian Gift and the 'Indian Gift'" provides a powerful critique of Mauss's universalization of gift exchange [34]. Parry argues that "the Hindu 'law of the gift' is shown to have a key role in the structure of Mauss's argument, though in fact it is quite inconsistent with his central thesis" [34].

Parry demonstrates that in the Hindu context of *danadharma* (the law of the gift), "the gift contains some part of the spiritual essence of the donor, and this constrains the recipient to make a return" [34]. However, crucially, "the obligation to make a return is not therefore encoded in the danadharma" [34]. Unlike Melanesian and Maori gift exchanges that embed reciprocal obligations, Hindu gift-giving entails a one-way transfer that carries both spiritual contamination and moral risk for donor and recipient [34].

Parry's broader argument is that the "pure gift" ideology—the idea of a disinterested, non-reciprocal gift—is historically specific to modern capitalist societies and world religions [34]. He argues that "the ideological concept of the 'pure gift' emerges in differentiated societies, reflecting advanced social and economic structures" [34].

A 2017 symposium in the Australian National University's Open Research Repository features critical reflections on Parry's essay from five prominent anthropologists—James Carrier, Chris Gregory, James Laidlaw, Marilyn Strathern, and Yunxiang Yan—along with a "revisionist note" from Parry himself "identifying some of the limits of the Maussian approach for contemporary anthropology" [34].

### 2.6 Testart's Critiques

Alain Testart offers rigorous critiques of the gift theory tradition, arguing against the conflation of gift exchange with reciprocity and insisting on clear distinctions between various forms of transfer [35]. Testart's key intervention is to distinguish between gift and exchange based on "the legal exigibility of the counter-transfer" [35].

Testart defines a gift as "the transfer of a good implying the renunciation of any rights on this good and any rights that could arise from this transfer, especially the right to claim anything in return" [35]. In contrast, an exchange requires repayment—the counter-transfer is legally exigible or enforceable [35]. As Testart writes in his article "What is a gift?" (HAU: Journal of Ethnographic Theory, 2013): "'To give' is not the same as 'to make a gift'" [35]. He shows from Latin etymology that the notions of giving and gifting differ: "What marks an exchange is the requirement to repay with an appropriate counterpart of each of the transfers. With a gift, on the contrary, the counterpart (counter-gift) may be given, but cannot be required" [35].

Testart further identifies a third category of transfer: legally obligatory transfers without reciprocal exchange, such as taxes, tribute, or judicial payments [35]. This framework has significant implications for the study of exchange, since "objects do not preserve any trace of the rights exercised over them, nor of the manner in which these rights may have changed holders" [35].

### 2.7 Gift Exchange vs. Commodity Exchange

Subsequent scholars have elaborated on the distinction between gift and commodity exchange. **Chris Gregory (1982)** formulated that "what distinguishes commodity from gift exchange is the conceptualisation of kinship as a method of consumption" [12]. **Marilyn Strathern (1992)** argued that "In a commodity economy persons and things are reified... In a gift economy, persons and things are 'personified'... making people's relations visible" [21]. **James Carrier (1997)** challenged the sharp dichotomy, arguing that "it is too easy to become beguiled by differences, hard to see similarities among different types of societies, and hard to see differences within a single type" [12].

## 3. Sahlins's Analysis of Reciprocity

### 3.1 The Three-Part Typology

Marshall Sahlins, in his 1972 work *Stone Age Economics*, identified three main types of reciprocity [17]:

**Generalized Reciprocity** is the giving of goods or labor without expectation of an immediate or equivalent return. It is "the solidary extreme" of reciprocity, associated with what Malinowski called the "pure gift" [16][17]. The provider presumes the favor will be returned later but does not demand an immediate or equal reciprocal.

**Balanced Reciprocity** refers to direct, simultaneous or near-simultaneous exchange of equivalent value. It is "the direct exchange of customary equivalents" characteristic of social equals or allies, strengthening social ties through mutual trust [16][17].

**Negative Reciprocity** describes attempts to "get something for nothing"—transactions aimed at maximizing personal gain through haggling, theft, barter with outsiders, or other forms of unsociable exchange. It represents "the unsociable extreme" in Sahlins's framework [16][17].

### 3.2 The Sectoral Model: Social Distance and Kinship

Sahlins's core insight is that reciprocity types exist on a **continuum determined by social and kinship distance** between exchange partners [16][17]. He introduced "social distance" as a way to conceptualize the degree of familiarity and information transfer between exchange partners. The model maps as follows:

- **Within households and among close kin**: Generalized reciprocity predominates. Sahlins posited that "reciprocity is inclined toward the generalized pole by close kinship, toward the negative extreme in proportion to kinship distance" [17].
- **Within communities**: Balanced reciprocity occurs among community members.
- **With outsiders**: Negative reciprocity occurs "with outsiders, reflecting moral and social distances" [17].

The principle is that "different moral standards apply to transfers occurring in each sector" [17]. However, Rubinstein's 1975 study of urban poor in Mexico City partially validated the model while revealing that under acute deprivation, "typical kinship or residential predictors of reciprocity break down, shifting exchanges towards balanced or negative reciprocity" [17].

### 3.3 Relationship to Malinowski and Mauss

Sahlins built upon both Malinowski and Mauss by systematizing their observations into a formal three-part typology and linking exchange types explicitly to kinship distance and social structure—something neither had done systematically [16][17]. However, he diverged from his predecessors in key ways:

- He **reinterpreted the *hau***: Rather than a mystical spirit, Sahlins argued the *hau* represents "its 'yield' or 'product,' and returns of such yield protect against unearned wealth accumulation" [17].
- He **rejected the dichotomy** between gift and commodity, arguing instead for a continuum [17].
- He placed reciprocity within a broader theory of the **Domestic Mode of Production (DMP)**, integrating exchange analysis into a comprehensive account of non-market economic organization [17].

### 3.4 The "Original Affluent Society" Thesis

Sahlins's concept of the "original affluent society" argued that hunter-gatherer societies were "affluent" not through producing much but through desiring little: "There are two possible courses to affluence. Wants may be easily satisfied either by producing much or desiring little" [18]. Foragers follow "the Zen road to affluence: not by getting more, but by wanting less" [18].

Key claims include that "the hunter-gatherer only works three to five hours per adult worker each day in food production" and that "mobility and property are in contradiction. That wealth quickly becomes more of an encumbrance than a good thing" [18]. The thesis is intimately connected to Sahlins's theory of exchange: because hunter-gatherers produce for use rather than surplus accumulation, **generalized reciprocity** becomes the central mechanism for distributing resources and ensuring food security [18].

Sahlins also offers a powerful critique of poverty as a social relation rather than a material condition: "The world's most primitive people have few possessions, but they are not poor. Poverty is not a certain small amount of goods, nor is it just a relation between means and ends; above all it is a relation between people. Poverty is a social status. As such it is the invention of civilisation" [18].

### 3.5 Critiques of the "Original Affluent Society"

**Keane's "Sahlins's 'Original Affluent Society' 50 Years Later"** (2022): This essay commemorates the 50th anniversary of Sahlins's essay, noting that "the essay's significance lies less in empirical breadth and more in its conceptual challenge to contemporary capitalist consumption and bourgeois individualism, highlighting alternatives to endless consumerism" [19]. Keane notes that "Sahlins's work marked a shift in anthropology from positivist evolutionism toward pluralistic, meaning-centered approaches emphasizing culture as autonomous and foundational to human freedom" [19]. However, Keane acknowledges that "the piece has limitations, including less emphasis on colonial violence and some romanticization of foragers" [19].

**David Kaplan's "The Darker Side of the 'Original Affluent Society'"**: Kaplan's critical essay challenges the empirical basis of Sahlins's thesis. Kaplan argues that the data from Arnhem Land, Australia, and the !Kung Bushmen "face significant methodological flaws," including "artificial conditions, short study durations, and non-representative samples, including dependence on mission station foods and modern tools affecting behavior" [18]. Kaplan notes that "later analyses incorporating all subsistence-related tasks (tool-making, housework, childcare) suggest higher total work hours (around 40-45 per week), closer to industrial society household labor workloads" [18].

On nutritional status, Kaplan argues that "despite claims of abundant, balanced diets, medical and anthropological research notes chronic undernutrition, high childhood mortality, seasonal hunger, and health challenges linked to dietary limitations—particularly in carbohydrate and fat intake" [18]. Kaplan further notes that "in contemporary contexts hunter-gatherers... have apparently insatiable demands for shotguns, rifles, motor vehicles"—challenging Sahlins's claim of limited wants [18].

The Wikipedia article on the original affluent society summarizes critiques: "The 'Original Affluent Society' theory has been challenged for omitting time spent on activities such as collecting firewood and food preparation, and for overlooking issues like high infant mortality and frequent disease" [18].

## 4. Feminist Anthropological Interventions

### 4.1 Annette Weiner: Inalienable Possessions and Keeping-While-Giving

Annette Weiner's fieldwork in the Trobriand Islands produced a fundamental challenge to the male-centered accounts of Malinowski and Mauss. In *Women of Value, Men of Renown* (1976), Weiner demonstrated that women play vital roles in Trobriand society—roles that Malinowski had systematically dismissed as unimportant [20]. Islander women explicitly told Weiner: "Nununiga (banana leaf bundles) are just like your money" [20]. Her work revealed that women's production of banana leaf skirts and bundles constituted a form of wealth central to the Trobriand economy and critical for maintaining male prestige [20].

Weiner's 1992 book *Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While-Giving* introduced a paradigm-shifting concept [9]. She argued that "what makes a possession inalienable is its exclusive and cumulative identity with a particular series of owners through time... authenticated by fictive or true genealogies, origin myths, sacred ancestors, and gods" [9]. The central paradox is **"keeping-while-giving"**: these possessions are given as gifts yet maintain an enduring connection to their original owners, reinforcing status and kinship obligations [9].

Weiner argued that "The motivation for reciprocity is centered not in the gift per se, but in the authority vested in keeping inalienable possessions... [T]he authentication of difference rather than the balance of equivalence [is] the fundamental feature of exchange" [9]. This directly challenged the traditional anthropological view that reciprocity is the foundation of exchange. Weiner emphasized that societies conserve and hoard inalienable possessions—heirlooms and sacred objects imbued with ancestral power—that authenticate social difference and hierarchy [9].

Crucially, Weiner argued that "the idea of keeping-while-giving places women at the heart of the political process... for women possess a wealth of their own that gives them power" [9]. She highlighted that "Power is intimately involved in cultural reproduction... the degree of control over the production and distribution of cloth wealth coincides with women's rank and the development of hierarchy" [9].

### 4.2 Marilyn Strathern: The Gender of the Gift

Marilyn Strathern's *The Gender of the Gift* (1988) offers perhaps the most radical feminist rethinking of gift exchange theory [21][22]. She argues that "the concept of society is theoretically obsolete" and that "the relationship, rather than society as an entity or the individual as autonomous, is anthropology's subject" [21].

Central to Strathern's analysis is the concept of the **dividual** or **partible person**. Far from being regarded as unique entities, Melanesian persons are "as dividually as they are individually conceived," constituted as "the plural and composite site of the relationships that produced them" [21][22]. This conceptualization leads to viewing Melanesians as inherently androgynous rather than distinctly male or female [21].

Strathern argues that "gift exchange is not gender neutral in Melanesian culture; it is itself gendered, with men's and women's ability to transact stemming from the power structures this gendering creates" [21]. She asks: "To ask about the gender of the gift is to ask about the situation of gift exchange in relation to the form that domination takes in these societies" [21]. Gender is not simply about men and women as fixed categories but about how social relations and exchanges are gendered acts shaping power dynamics [21][22].

Strathern also critiques Western feminist theory, noting that feminist critiques have not been fully integrated into ethnographic practice and that "our own metaphors reflect a deeply rooted metaphysics" [21]. Her work has been influential far beyond Melanesian anthropology, shaping debates in science studies, intellectual property, and legal anthropology [21].

Strathern's argument about "the gender of the gift" shows that in Melanesia, gifts create gendered persons—persons are made and unmade through exchange. In the *moka* ceremony of Mount Hagen, "the exchange of pigs... forms part of the moka ceremony, a formal occasion... where one male would give a pig to another male and in doing so is awarded a name and public prestige" (Strathern 1988, 146-147) [21]. In this system, "women's labour remains embedded in the pigs"—a fact that Western Marxist analyses miss because they treat labor as an alienable commodity [21].

### 4.3 Gayle Rubin: The Traffic in Women

Gayle Rubin's 1975 essay "The Traffic in Women: Notes on the 'Political Economy' of Sex" applies Maussian and Levi-Straussian frameworks to analyze kinship, marriage exchange, and gender oppression [23]. Rubin introduces the concept of the **"sex/gender system,"** defined as "the set of arrangements by which a society transforms biological sexuality into products of human activity" [23].

Rubin argues that kinship systems "do not merely exchange women. They exchange sexual access, genealogical statuses, lineage names and ancestors, rights and people—men, women" [23]. Crucially, "the total relationship of exchange which constitutes marriage is not established between a man and a woman, but between two groups of men, and the woman figures only as one of the objects in the exchange" [23].

Rubin argues that "kinship systems are observable and empirical forms of sex/gender systems" and that "gender is a socially imposed division of the sexes... a product of the social relations of sexuality" [23]. The incest taboo, in this context, enforces exogamous marriage patterns reinforcing the exchange of women. Rubin famously asks: "What is a domesticated woman? A female of the species... She only becomes a domestic, a wife, a chattel, a playboy bunny, a prostitute, or a human Dictaphone in certain relations" [23].

### 4.4 Implications for Re-reading Classical Gift Theory

Collectively, these feminist critiques demonstrate that classical gift theory was built on a fundamentally male-centered perspective that systematically rendered women's contributions invisible. Weiner's challenge is explicit: would Malinowski have ignored Trobriand women's banana-leaf wealth if men had produced and exchanged it? [20]

The feminist interventions have permanently transformed the field by:
- Shifting focus from **reciprocity** to **inalienability** and the authority vested in keeping [9]
- Reframing gift exchange as being not about the circulation of objects but about the **creation and enactment of persons** [21][22]
- Establishing **gender as a central analytical category** for understanding exchange [21]
- Challenging Western assumptions about **autonomous individuals** and binary gender roles [21][23]

## 5. Contemporary Practices: Open-Source Software Communities

### 5.1 Gift Logics in Open-Source Development

David Zeitlyn's 2003 article "Gift economies in the development of open source software: anthropological reflections," published in *Research Policy*, is the key peer-reviewed source connecting open-source software (OSS) development to Maussian and Bourdieusian frameworks [26]. Zeitlyn argues that software engineers participating in open-source projects are motivated not purely by economic incentives but through social structures reminiscent of kinship groups [26].

As Bergquist and Ljungberg (2001) note, "Open source software development relies on gift giving as a way of getting new ideas and prototypes out into circulation" [26].

### 5.2 Maussian Obligations in Code Contribution

Zeitlyn highlights that "gifts entail ongoing obligations and create lasting social bonds, paralleling open source code contributions" [26]. This directly maps Mauss's three core obligations onto the open-source development cycle:
- The **obligation to give**: Developers contribute code, documentation, bug fixes
- The **obligation to receive**: Users adopt and use the software
- The **obligation to reciprocate**: Reciprocation occurs through bug reports, documentation, further code contributions, or community support

As Francois Zaninotto's 2011 blog post "Open-Source is a Gift" explains: "By giving you the phpUnit software, Sebastian created a bond between you and him. Reciprocity is not the goal—the social bond between giver and receiver has greater importance" [26].

### 5.3 Bourdieusian Symbolic Capital: GitHub Stars and Maintainer Status

Zeitlyn argues that "successful gift giving accumulates reputation, fitting Bourdieu's notion of symbolic capital" [26]. In contemporary OSS, this symbolic capital is visibly manifested on platforms like GitHub, where stars, commit histories, and contribution metrics function as quantifiable forms of reputation. Developers work "in order to enhance the reputation (of themselves and their group)" [26].

However, the symbolic capital economy on GitHub has been extensively gamed and commodified. A 2026 Carnegie Mellon University peer-reviewed study identified 6 million fake GitHub stars across 18,617 repositories using 301,000 accounts [19]. Fake stars are commercially traded, costing between $0.03 and $0.85 each on dozens of platforms [19]. The study found that stars are used by venture capitalists as indicators in startup sourcing decisions: "Redpoint shows that the median seed-stage startup repository has around 2,850 stars, highlighting the high return on investment for purchasing fake stars to boost fundraising appeal" [19].

Professor Bogdan Vasilescu (CMU) contextualizes this: "One way to think of the GitHub ecosystem as a whole is as an attention economy, much like social media. At their peak—to date, in July 2024—more than 16 percent of GitHub repositories were associated with fake star campaigns" [19].

### 5.4 Kinship Amity in OSS Communities

Zeitlyn proposes **"kinship amity"** —relationships characterized by non-economic, reciprocal support among family members—as a more fitting metaphor than Raymond's "bazaar" for understanding collaborative open-source relationships. He suggests that "open source projects can be seen as kin groups with hierarchies resembling familial structures, where long-term interactions through gift exchange establish social bonds" [26].

Dolfsma and van der Eijk's "Behavioral Foundations for Open Innovation: Knowledge Gifts and Social Networks" argues that "Gift exchange—following Mauss' dictum to 'give, receive and reciprocate'—creates informal obligations and social trust that explain persistence and dynamics of knowledge exchange" [26].

### 5.5 Gendered Participation Patterns

Research reveals significant gender disparities in OSS participation. Women represent approximately 9.8% of OSS contributors, with only 5% of projects having women as core developers, and women authoring less than 5% of pull-requests [26]. A 2022 study (Frluckaj et al., published in *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*) found that "Women participating in projects that they have been invited to, rather than initiating joining on their own, was a significant pattern" [26].

The well-known 2016 study analyzing nearly 1.4 million identified male and female users on GitHub found that women's code contributions had a higher overall acceptance rate (78.6%) compared to men's (74.6%), but when contributors' gender was visible, women's acceptance rates dropped from 71.8% (gender-neutral profiles) to 62.5%—indicating gender bias against women [26].

A 2021 systematic mapping study (Trinkenreich et al.) confirmed: "Women had lower code acceptance rates than men and had to wait longer to receive initial feedback for their code changes and to complete code reviews" [26].

### 5.6 Feminist Critique of Gift Ideology in OSS

Dawn Nafus's seminal 2012 article "'Patches don't have gender': What is not open in open source software" directly critiques the gift economy framing of OSS from a feminist perspective [26]. The article argues that the ostensible openness and meritocratic "gift" ideology of OSS masks exclusionary gender dynamics, with the title quoting the dismissive discourse that "patches don't have gender"—suggesting that technical merit alone should matter, while ignoring the social structures that produce gender disparities [26].

*Applying Weiner's Keeping-While-Giving*: In OSS communities, core code and governance authority function as inalienable possessions—maintainers and core contributors retain "inalienable" authority over project direction even as code is given freely. Weiner's analysis of how "the degree of control over the production and distribution of cloth wealth coincides with women's rank and the development of hierarchy" parallels OSS dynamics where control over code production and distribution aligns with gendered hierarchies.

*Applying Strathern's Relational Personhood*: OSS identities are constituted through reciprocal exchange (code reviews, issue discussions, commit histories) rather than pre-existing as fixed identities. This resonates with Strathern's argument that persons are "elicited" through gift transactions rather than pre-existing as autonomous individuals.

*Applying Rubin's Framework*: The gift exchange economy in OSS—with its emphasis on symbolic capital accumulation among predominantly male peer networks—may structurally reproduce gendered hierarchies in ways analogous to the kinship systems Rubin analyzed, where women are marginalized as full participants in the exchange system.

### 5.7 Tensions Between Gift Logic and Corporate Commodification

Kluttz and Fourcade's 2020 article "A Maussian bargain: Accumulation by gift in the digital economy" provides a crucial theoretical framework for understanding corporate extraction from OSS gift economies [26]. They argue that data harvesting is "often described as a process of 'accumulation by dispossession'" but find that "enrollment into digital systems is often experienced as a much more benign process: signing up for a 'free' service, responding to a 'friend's' invitation, or being encouraged to 'share' content" [26]. Their key concept: "The economic relation that develops as a result of a digital gift offering not only masks the structural asymmetry between giver and gifted but also permits the creation of the new commodity of personal data" [26].

Andrew Lilley Brinker's 2025 article "Open Source Software and Corporate Influence" provides detailed analysis of the tensions [26]:
- The term "open source" became "the focal point for a loose coalition of ideologues... who saw pursuit of corporate adoption as a means to achieve an impact which license isolationism would fail to reach"
- The 2014 Heartbleed vulnerability in OpenSSL exposed the risks: "OpenSSL had only two full time software developers working on it... Despite the use of OpenSSL by deep-pocketed businesses in mission-critical systems, OpenSSL had not received much if any investment back"
- Five ecosystem effects caused by corporate influence: (1) increased minimum expectations of projects, (2) greater relicensing risk, (3) fragmentation and privatization of governance, (4) increased modularization risk with companies controlling components as fiefdoms, and (5) project priorities shifting toward corporate needs over community ones

## 6. Contemporary Practices: Social Media Attention Economies

### 6.1 Attention as Symbolic Currency

Maxi Heitmayer's 2024 article "The Second Wave of Attention Economics: Attention as a Universal Symbolic Currency on Social Media and Beyond" argues that attention is becoming a "universal symbolic currency" that drives social exchanges [27]. The paper proposes a dual-stream model distinguishing between **"flow attention"** (immediate, transient focus) and **"calcified attention"** (accumulated, measurable attention such as likes and followers), which "can be translated into other symbolic currencies like money or influence at market rates" [27].

Heitmayer argues that "as attention is becoming an increasingly scarce and valuable 'resource,' it should be conceptualized as a symbolic currency" [27]. By integrating systems theory, the article shows that calcified attention functions as a form of symbolic capital that can be converted into money, influence, or political power [27].

### 6.2 Algorithmic-Affective Capital

Sebastian Hoyos-Torres's 2026 PhD dissertation examines how social capital affects the spread of information on Twitter, integrating "Bourdieu's concept of social and cultural capital with Gamson and Stuart's symbolic contest theory" [28]. The findings demonstrate that "likes and retweets were concentrated on a small group of accounts" and that social capital—measured in followers and network centrality—was "positively associated with an increase in likes and retweets" [28].

The concept of **"Algorithmic-Affective Capital"** has been proposed for "capital accrued through engagement metrics (likes, shares, followers, retweets). This is social capital made visible and calculable through platform algorithms" [27]. This extends Bourdieu's framework by emphasizing how algorithmic mediation transforms social capital into something quantifiable, visible, and subject to platform control.

Lundahl's "Algorithmic meta-capital: Bourdieusian analysis of social power through algorithms in media consumption" investigates how algorithms operate as a form of "meta-capital" that shapes what counts as valuable within digital fields [27]. Couldry's concept of "media meta-capital" argues that media exert a meta-capital which enables them to exercise power over other forms of power—not through fiat, but through complex interconnections between specialist fields and a central media field [27].

### 6.3 Reciprocity Norms vs. Algorithmic Mediation

Sahlins's reciprocity continuum maps onto social media behaviors [17]:
- **Generalized reciprocity**: Users give attention, likes, and content without expectation of direct return from specific others but hope for generalized community recognition
- **Balanced reciprocity**: Direct exchanges—"like for like," follow-for-follow, engagement pods
- **Negative reciprocity**: Trolling, harassment, engagement baiting, algorithmic exploitation by bad actors

The collapse of social distance on platforms means that Sahlins's continuum—where reciprocity type correlates with kinship distance—is flattened: all users, regardless of relational distance, are subject to the same algorithmic mediation. The expectation of balanced reciprocity is disrupted by algorithmic visibility—a user's gift of attention may not be reciprocated because the algorithm buries it. This creates a fundamental asymmetry in the digital gift economy.

### 6.4 Platform Capitalism's Extraction from Gift Exchanges

**Srnicek's *Platform Capitalism* (2017)** argues that contemporary companies such as Uber and Facebook no longer own traditional means of production but control customer interfaces, leveraging data as the new raw material driving the global economy [27]. Srnicek categorizes platforms into five types: advertising (e.g., Google, Facebook), cloud (e.g., Amazon Web Services), industrial (e.g., Siemens, GE), product (e.g., Zipcar), and lean platforms (e.g., Uber, Airbnb). Users beget more users, which leads to platforms having a natural tendency towards monopolization [27].

The transformation of Maussian social bonds into quantifiable metrics is the core mechanism: Users create content (give gifts of attention, creativity, information) → Platforms capture this value through advertising, data mining, and algorithmic ranking. This transforms Maussian social bonds (obligations to give, receive, and reciprocate) into quantifiable metrics (likes, shares, follower counts, engagement rates) that can be bought, sold, and algorithmically optimized.

The Center for Humane Technology notes that "algorithms are opinions embedded in code... optimized to some definition of success. If a commercial enterprise builds an algorithm to their definition of success, it's a commercial interest. It's usually profit" [31].

### 6.5 Gendered Hierarchies in Recognition

The LinkedIn gender reach disparity powerfully demonstrates algorithmic disruption of reciprocity: Women on LinkedIn who changed their profile gender to male reported significant increases in engagement—one saw her profile views jump by 1,600%, another reported a 400% spike [27]. A 2025 LinkedIn study revealed that posts by women reached only 0.6% of followers compared to men's 50% [27]. This demonstrates how algorithmic mediation disrupts the expectation of balanced reciprocity—women's gifts of content are systematically less visible.

Gender disparities in algorithmic amplification are further documented in a study on Twitter's gender inference, which found that Twitter's gender inference is correct in 81% of cases but misgenders nearly 19%, with misgendering disproportionately affecting LGBTQIA+ individuals and straight women, and non-binary users being misgendered 100% of the time [27].

*Applying Weiner's Keeping-While-Giving*: Social media users maintain their profile/identity (the "kept" self—their inalienable digital persona, the curated self that carries their identity and reputation) while giving content away (the "given" self—posts, likes, shares, data, attention). The self becomes both inalienable possession and gift. The platform, however, maintains inalienable control over algorithmic infrastructure even as users share "gifts" of content.

*Applying Strathern's Relational Personhood*: Social media profiles are composite, relational constructions—not bounded individuals but dividual selves composed of networked relations (followers, follows, mutuals, algorithmic recommendations, tagged content, shared histories). The social media user is a dividual node in a network of gift exchanges, not an autonomous individual.

*Applying Rubin's Framework*: The "traffic in women" framework illuminates how platforms traffic in the attention of marginalized users. Attention—like women in kinship systems—circulates through social media as a medium of exchange between groups, with the most valuable attention flows directed toward privileged users while the attention of marginalized users is extracted and devalued.

## 7. Contemporary Practices: Cryptocurrency Governance Structures

### 7.1 Blockchain as Gift Economy Experiment

Joseph Poon, blockchain researcher and co-author of the Lightning Network, has explored how blockchain technology might foster collaborative ecosystems through cryptocurrency gift economies. Interviewed on the Voices of VR Podcast (2018), Poon explains that distributing value via airdrops and faucets "acts as an incentive promoting cooperation rather than zero-sum competition" [29].

Poon emphasizes: "It's done as a gift without any expectation of direct return, irrespective of game theoretic tit-for-tat dynamics, that this can be done without any central authority when the platform is launched long-term" [29]. He further explains that "it's in your own self-interest to distribute out more value, as much as possible, given the competitive dynamics of other projects... the one that gives away more value has greater odds of success" [29].

### 7.2 Token Airdrops as Maussian Gifts

Cryptocurrency airdrops—the distribution of tokens to wallet addresses—function precisely as Maussian-style gifts. The triple obligation applies directly:
- The **obligation to give**: The protocol distributes tokens, often with no pre-announced terms or direct payment required
- The **obligation to receive**: Users accept the tokens by claiming them or simply by holding them in their wallets
- The **obligation to reciprocate**: Recipients are expected to use the protocol, participate in governance, contribute to the community, or promote the project

Surgence Labs' analysis of over $26 billion in airdrop value distributed since 2020 states: "Giving people ownership in a network makes them behave like owners, not customers" [29]. The Block3 Finance analysis notes: "Modern airdrops are behavioral experiments designed to identify users who contribute to ecosystem growth rather than those deploying capital opportunistically" [29].

### 7.3 Behavioral Analysis of Airdrop Reciprocity

A University of Washington paper titled "Altruistic and Profit-oriented: Making Sense of Roles in Web3 Community from Airdrop Perspective" collected over 71,000 transaction records from 20,148 members [29]. Users were classified into five broad roles:
- **Speculators (41.18%)**: Sell tokens quickly for profit
- **Diamond Holders (20.31%)**: Long-term holders who hold and stake tokens, contributing to community stability
- **Airdrop Hunters (22.24%)**: Addresses likely controlled by the same individual exploiting the system
- **Diversified Members (10.62%)**: Engage in multiple behaviors including staking and selling
- **Others**

The study found: "Users receiving more rewards tend to take positive actions towards the community" and "Reciprocity in the token transaction network gradually rises as interactions become more stable and robust." However, "Airdrop hunters exploit anonymity by registering multiple accounts to maximize profits, undermining long-term development" [29].

### 7.4 Smart Contracts as Programmatic Balanced Reciprocity

The 2024 paper "Cooperation Among Strangers: Algorithmic Enforcement of Reciprocal Exchange with Blockchain-Based Smart Contracts" published in the Academy of Management Review introduces the concept of **"programmed reciprocity"** [29]. The paper explains that smart contracts function as coded instructions to enforce the **automatic reciprocation of positive or negative actions** without human interference or central authority, and that algorithmic enforcement improves cooperation viability through programmed reciprocity, contingent upon contract complexity and users' confidence in the blockchain [29].

Sahlins's reciprocity typology maps directly onto smart contract functionality:
- **Generalized reciprocity**: Airdrops given freely with diffuse expectations
- **Balanced reciprocity**: Smart contract conditional execution—if X condition is met, Y outcome is automatically executed
- **Negative reciprocity**: Scam contracts, rug pulls, and exploitative DeFi protocols

Smart contracts represent a novel form of balanced reciprocity. Unlike generalized reciprocity (which is open-ended and trust-based) or negative reciprocity (which is exploitative), smart contracts enforce exact, simultaneous, or near-simultaneous exchange of equivalent value—the hallmark of balanced reciprocity. However, this enforcement is **automated** rather than socially negotiated, removing the ambiguity, strategic forgetting, and temporal flexibility central to Maussian gift exchange.

### 7.5 DAO Governance Evolution

A 2025 article in *Frontiers in Blockchain* examines the evolution of Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO) governance toward more regenerative and adaptive coordination systems [30]. The study identifies three generations:

**DAO 1.0 (Early DAOs)**: Immutable, rigid, token-weighted voting. Characterized by on-chain, binary voting with fixed rules, where all governance power was proportional to token holdings, leading to plutocracy and low participation.

**DAO 2.0 (Modular, Delegated)**: Introduction of modular voting, delegation systems, and role-based participation. This generation attempted to address the limitations of DAO 1.0 through delegated voting frameworks and more flexible governance structures.

**DAO 3.0 (Adaptive, AI-Integrated, Multi-Layer)**: Hypha's DAO 3.0 incorporates multi-layer modular voting, trust-based safeguards, real-time capital flow mechanisms, modular governance "spaces" enabling role-based participation, and AI-integrated decision support [30]. The guiding principle is "not to tell people what they cannot do—it was to give them the freedom to do what works in their context" [30].

The study identifies that "Traditional DAOs, often characterized by token-weighted voting and rigid structures, have replicated power asymmetries and exclusionary participation, limiting their effectiveness especially in emerging markets and low-income contexts" [30].

### 7.6 Power Asymmetries in Token-Weighted Voting

The Forbes article "DAOs Keep Centralizing—Decades Of Governance Research Explain Why" (April 4, 2026) reveals stark statistics: "As of 2026, DAOs manage over $25 billion in assets with more than 5,000 active entities, yet less than 1% of token holders control about 90% of voting power, with voter participation ranging from 5-15%" [29].

The paper "Concave is the New Linear: The Impossibility of Anti-Plutocratic DAO Governance" provides a formal proof: "No voting rule that derives power solely from wallet balance can succeed on a permissionless blockchain" [29]. The paper finds that under Quadratic Voting, Sybil amplification factors measured on five leading DAOs range between 1,172× and 4,039×, and exceeding 229,000× under steeper power rules. Attackers can control large votes at minuscule costs: "controlling a $300 million Uniswap vote for around $75,000" [29].

Bourdieu's concept of symbolic capital explains how crypto influencers, core developers, early adopters, and VIPs exercise governance power beyond their token holdings. Symbolic violence explains how token-weighted governance is accepted as fair despite its plutocratic nature—the mechanism obscures power relations by appearing procedurally neutral.

### 7.7 The Collapse of Gift/Commodity Distinctions

Cryptocurrency tokens are simultaneously **gifts** (airdropped, community-building, creating social bonds and obligations) and **commodities** (traded on exchanges, valued in fiat currency, held for speculative return). This dual nature represents a fundamental collapse of the analytical distinction between gifts and commodities that has been central to economic anthropology since Mauss.

The University of Washington airdrop study demonstrates this transformation empirically: 41.18% of airdrop recipients were Speculators who sell tokens quickly for profit—the gift of tokens is immediately converted into commodity cash. Only 20.31% were Diamond Holders who maintain a gift-relationship with the protocol [29].

The paradox of **"rational gift exchange"** —where gift logic is used instrumentally for financial return—is the defining feature of crypto gift economies. Airdrops are strategically designed to maximize network effects and token price appreciation. As Surgence Labs states: "Airdrops aren't broken. Most airdrop strategies are broken"—the gift is a strategy, not an end in itself [29].

This instrumentalization of gift logic represents what Bourdieu would call a field where different forms of capital (economic, social, symbolic, cultural) are all mutually convertible—and the conversion is explicitly acknowledged rather than disguised.

### 7.8 Feminist Analysis of Crypto Gift Economies

*Applying Weiner's Keeping-While-Giving*: Token distributions are given to community members, but founding teams and whales often retain inalienable governance power and foundational control over protocol development. Governance tokens function as inalienable possessions—they give voting power while being held, not traded. Weiner's concept of "keeping-while-giving" reveals the power asymmetry: founders and early investors "keep" control even as they "give" tokens to the community.

*Applying Strathern's Relational Personhood*: A wallet address is the composite site of all its transactions, holdings, and relationships. The wallet is the dividual person in digital form—constituted by the relationships and exchanges it is embedded in. This maps directly onto Strathern's concept of the dividual as "the plural and composite site of the relationships that produced them."

*Applying Rubin's Framework*: Crypto governance remains overwhelmingly male-dominated, with women significantly underrepresented. The "traffic in women" framework raises questions about who gets to represent, be recognized, and accumulate symbolic capital in crypto governance structures. The systematic devaluation of women's participation and the concentration of governance power among male-dominated networks echoes the structural patterns Rubin identified in kinship systems.

## 8. Theoretical Synthesis: The Future of Gift Exchange Theory

### 8.1 Smart Contracts and the Reconceptualization of Mauss's Triple Obligation

Smart contracts force a fundamental reconceptualization of Mauss's triple obligation. In classical gift economies, the obligations to give, receive, and reciprocate were socially embedded, flexible, and negotiated over time. The *hau*—whatever its ontological status—represented the binding force of social relationships themselves.

Smart contracts transform this tripartite obligation into programmatic, deterministic logic. The obligation to reciprocate is no longer a matter of social debt, honor, or shame but of computational enforcement. The "spirit of the gift" becomes code: if X, then Y. What is gained is certainty, trustlessness, and execution guarantees. What is lost is flexibility, context-sensitivity, the human relational dimension, and the social debt that actually builds relationships.

This transformation raises profound questions: Can there be a "gift" when reciprocity is guaranteed? Derrida's aporia—that the gift annuls itself when recognized as a gift—finds new expression in smart contracts: the moment reciprocity is automated, the gift ceases to be a gift in the Maussian sense. It becomes a conditional transfer. Yet the persistence of gift rhetoric in crypto communities (the discourse of community, sharing, mutual aid) suggests that the *experience* of gifting remains culturally significant even when the underlying mechanism is deterministic.

### 8.2 Algorithmic Curation and Sahlins's Social Distance Continuum

Algorithmic curation fundamentally disrupts Sahlins's social distance continuum. In classical gift economies, reciprocity type correlated with kinship distance: generalized reciprocity for close kin, balanced for community members, negative for strangers. The continuum was embedded in face-to-face social relationships where participants could assess kinship ties, reputation, and social standing.

In digital gift economies, algorithms flatten this continuum. Your gift of attention to a stranger on social media is mediated by the same algorithmic visibility structure as your gift to a close friend. The algorithm—not social distance—determines whose gifts are seen, valued, and reciprocated. This creates a new form of "algorithmic social distance" that is opaque, changeable, and controlled by platform owners.

The concept of "algorithmic meta-capital" (Lundahl) and "media meta-capital" (Couldry) captures this transformation: algorithms do not simply mediate gift exchanges but determine what counts as a gift, whose gifts are visible, and how reciprocity is structured. The platform becomes a "meta-giver" that controls the conditions of exchange itself.

### 8.3 Platform Capitalism and the Reconceptualization of Feminist Exchange Theory

Platform capitalism forces a reconceptualization of feminist theories of gendered exchange. Weiner's concept of "keeping-while-giving" takes on new meaning when platforms "keep" algorithmic control while users "give" content. The platform's inalienable possession is the infrastructure itself—the code, data, and algorithmic logic that determines visibility and value.

Strathern's dividual personhood finds new expression in the digital self: social media profiles are composite, relational constructions constituted through networks of exchange. The dividual is no longer a Melanesian particularity but a universal condition of platform-mediated existence. Every user is a node in a network of gift exchanges, their identity emerging through and constituted by algorithmic mediation.

Rubin's "traffic in women" framework illuminates how platforms traffic in the attention of marginalized users. The attention of women and marginalized groups is extracted and monetized by platforms, while the recognition and rewards disproportionately flow to dominant groups. The LinkedIn gender bias experiments provide empirical confirmation: women's content receives significantly less visibility, yet women's participation is essential to the platform's social fabric and data generation.

### 8.4 The Future of Gift Exchange Theory

These digital transformations suggest several directions for the future of gift exchange theory:

1. **From Social to Algorithmic Reciprocity**: The classical model of reciprocity as socially embedded and negotiated must be extended to account for algorithmic mediation. Reciprocity is no longer just social—it is *programmatic*, *scored*, and *optimized*.

2. **From Gift/Commodity to Hybrid Forms**: The analytical distinction between gifts and commodities, already challenged by Parry and Testart, collapses further in digital contexts. Tokens, attention, and code are simultaneously gifts and commodities. New analytical categories are needed to capture these hybrid forms.

3. **From Inalienable to Algorithmically Alienable**: Weiner's concept of inalienable possessions must be reconceptualized for digital contexts where personal data is both given as gift and extracted as commodity. The paradox of "keeping-while-giving" is reconfigured as users give content but platforms keep control.

4. **From Social Distance to Algorithmic Distance**: Sahlins's social distance continuum must be extended to account for algorithmic distance—the opaque, changeable forms of visibility and value that platforms impose on gift exchanges.

5. **From Gender to Algorithmic Stratification**: Feminist theories of gendered exchange must be extended to account for how algorithms reproduce and amplify gender hierarchies. The "traffic in women" framework finds new expression in the traffic in attention, data, and digital labor.

6. **The Enduring Relevance of Classical Theory**: Despite these transformations, the classical frameworks remain remarkably relevant. Mauss's insight that gifts create social bonds and obligations is as true for open-source code contributions as for Kula shell valuables. Sahlins's reciprocity typology maps onto distinct digital behaviors. Weiner's attention to power and inalienability reveals dynamics that simple reciprocity frameworks obscure. Strathern's relational personhood illuminates how digital identities are constituted through exchange.

The future of gift exchange theory lies not in abandoning classical frameworks but in extending and complicating them—insisting on attention to power, gender, and the political dimensions of gift exchange in an age of algorithmic mediation, platform capitalism, and digital hybridity.

## 9. Conclusion: Continuities and Ruptures

### 9.1 Enduring Continuities

The classical frameworks of Mauss, Malinowski, and Sahlins remain remarkably relevant for understanding contemporary digital practices:

1. **Mauss's triple obligation** (to give, to receive, to reciprocate) is visible across all three digital domains—code contribution, content creation, and token distribution.

2. **Symbolic capital** (Bourdieu, building on Maussian prestige) operates visibly as reputation in OSS (GitHub stars), attention metrics (likes/followers), and governance power (token holdings).

3. **Sahlins's reciprocity typology** maps onto all domains: generalized (open-source sharing, memes), balanced (smart contracts, mutual following), and negative (trolling, extraction, fraud).

4. **Gift exchange creates and maintains social bonds** and obligations, exactly as Mauss described.

5. **Prestige and honor systems** operate visibly (maintainer status, influencer status, whale status).

### 9.2 Fundamental Ruptures

However, contemporary digital practices introduce transformations that classical frameworks did not anticipate:

1. **Algorithmic Mediation**: Exchange is mediated by algorithms that determine visibility, distribution, and reciprocity, introducing a third party (the platform/protocol) absent in face-to-face gift economies.

2. **Scaled Asymmetry**: The global, asynchronous, anonymous scale of digital gift economies creates fundamentally different dynamics from small-scale societies.

3. **Programmatic Reciprocity**: Smart contracts automate and enforce reciprocity, removing the social ambiguity, strategic negotiation, and temporal flexibility central to Maussian exchange.

4. **Commodification of Gifts**: Platforms extract value from gift exchanges (advertising revenue from free content; corporate use of OSS without contribution), creating hybrid gift/commodity forms that challenge classical distinctions.

5. **Data as Inalienable/Alienable**: The paradox of "keeping-while-giving" is reconfigured as users give content but platforms keep control, and personal data circulates as both gift and commodity.

6. **The Collapse of Gift/Commodity Distinctions**: Cryptocurrency tokens are simultaneously gifts and commodities, representing a fundamental blurring of categories that classical anthropology held separate.

### 9.3 The Enduring Relevance of Feminist Critique

The feminist interventions of Weiner, Strathern, and Rubin are not simply historical critiques of classical theory—they offer essential tools for understanding contemporary digital gift economies:

- Weiner's **"keeping-while-giving"** reveals power asymmetries that simple reciprocity frameworks obscure: platforms keep algorithmic control while users give content; maintainers keep governance authority while code circulates; whales keep governance power while tokens are distributed.

- Strathern's **relational personhood** illuminates how digital identities are constituted through exchange: the social media user as dividual node; the wallet address as composite site of relationships; the open-source contributor as emergent through code reviews and commit histories.

- Feminist critiques reveal **gendered patterns of participation, recognition, and exclusion** across all three digital domains: women in OSS receive fewer contributions and slower code reviews; women content creators face algorithmic suppression; women in DAOs are marginalized in token-weighted governance.

These feminist contributions do not reject classical frameworks but extend and complicate them, insisting on attention to power, gender, and the political dimensions of gift exchange—insights that are as urgently needed for analyzing GitHub repositories, Twitter feeds, and DAO governance as they were for understanding Trobriand banana leaf bundles and Melanesian ceremonial exchanges.
