# COMPREHENSIVE INSTITUTIONAL-GRADE ANALYSIS OF NON-CONVERTIBLE DEBENTURE IPOs IN INDIA (DECEMBER 8, 2025)

## Executive Summary

As of December 8, 2025, the Indian Non-Convertible Debenture (NCD) market presented two primary institutional-grade offerings for investment consideration: Edelweiss Financial Services Limited's ₹250 crore NCD issue (open December 5-11, 2025) and Prachay Capital Limited's ₹150 crore NCD issue (opening December 11, 2025). This analysis addresses the comprehensive investment thesis for retirees and institutional clients across credit quality, tax-adjusted yields, comparative valuation, risk assessment, regulatory framework, and forward-looking indicators.

**Key Investment Findings:**

The Edelweiss Financial Services NCD offering represented the primary market opportunity on December 8, 2025, with CRISIL A+/Stable credit ratings, coupon rates ranging from 8.85% to 10.10% per annum, and tenors from 24 to 120 months. The issue demonstrated strong retail participation, achieving 3.30x oversubscription with 4.57x retail investor demand, indicating sustained retail appetite for mid-grade corporate debt. For a standard investor in the 20% income tax bracket, the post-tax yield on an 11% coupon NCD calculates to 8.712% after accounting for 4% health and education cess. Senior citizens with lower income levels benefit significantly from Section 87A rebate provisions under India's new tax regime, potentially achieving full after-tax yields equal to gross yields when total income falls below ₹12 lakh.

The regulatory environment has recently evolved favorably for debt market participants. SEBI's October 28, 2025 amendment to the SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021, standardized trust deed formats while maintaining flexibility for customization. The introduction of ESG debt securities framework (effective June 5, 2025) and mandatory listing provisions (effective January 1, 2024) have enhanced transparency and investor protection mechanisms. RBI's January 29, 2025 harmonization of Housing Finance Company NCD guidelines with NBFC frameworks has aligned regulatory oversight while reducing borrowing cost uncertainties for HFC issuers.

The macroeconomic backdrop supports fixed-income valuations in the short to medium term. The RBI has implemented a cumulative 125 basis point rate reduction since February 2025, bringing the repo rate to 5.25% as of December 5, 2025. Inflation has collapsed to historic lows (0.25% in October 2025, 1.33% in December 2025), well below the RBI's 4% target, providing policy rate flexibility through at least mid-2026. GDP growth remains robust at 8.2% for Q2 FY26, the highest in six quarters, with FY26 full-year growth projected at 7.3%. Bank credit growth remains elevated at 12-14.5% year-on-year, with NBFC credit growth outpacing at 20% year-on-year, indicating continued strong financial system growth and manageable credit stress risks in the near term.

### Edelweiss Financial Services Limited (EFSL) NCD IPO

**Issuer Profile and Corporate Structure**

**Total Issue Size and Structure**

The base issue size comprises ₹125 crore, consisting of 2,500,000 NCDs with a face value of ₹1,000 each. The Green Shoe Option permits additional issuance of ₹125 crore, aggregating to a total potential issue size of ₹250 crore [1][2]. The minimum application amount is ₹10,000 (10 NCDs), with allotment executed on a first-come, first-served basis [1].

**Subscription Period and Status**

The issue opened on December 5, 2025, and closed on December 11, 2025, with allotment scheduled for December 15, 2025, and listing on December 16, 2025 [1]. The offering achieved oversubscription of 3.30 times at closure, with substantial participation variation across investor categories:

- Institutional investors (QIB): 0.02 times subscription
- Corporate/Non-Institutional investors: Portion of base allocation
- High Net Worth Individuals: 2.69 times subscription
- Retail Individual Investors: 4.57 times subscription [1][4]

The significant concentration of demand in retail and HNI categories (7.26x combined subscription) relative to institutional interest (0.02x) reflects retail investor preference for mid-grade corporate debt in the prevailing interest rate environment.

**Coupon Rates and Tenor Structure**

The NCDs are offered across 10 series with tenors ranging from 24 to 120 months and coupon rates varying from 8.85% to 10.10% per annum depending on series and tenor [1][2]. Interest payments are offered on either annual or monthly bases depending on specific series [1]. The coupon structure reflects the credit profile of the issuer and market demand for different maturity segments, with longer-tenor instruments commanding higher yields as expected under normal yield curve conditions.

**Credit Rating Assessment**

Crisil Ratings Limited assigned a credit rating of CRISIL A+/Stable to the NCDs, indicating low credit risk and a high degree of safety regarding timely servicing of financial obligations [1][2]. The A+ rating from India's largest rating agency places EFSL in the upper-medium tier of non-AAA issuers, above the investment-grade/speculative-grade threshold (typically BBB-/Baa3). The "Stable" outlook indicates no anticipated rating change in the medium term, reflecting manageable debt service capabilities and adequate capitalization.

**Security Features**

The NCDs are issued as secured, redeemable, listed instruments [1][2]. The security features provide investors with recourse to company assets in case of default, representing a structural credit enhancement relative to unsecured debentures.

**Listing Exchange and Regulatory Compliance**

The NCDs are listed on the BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange), providing secondary market liquidity and regulatory oversight under SEBI's Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements (LODR) Regulations, 2015 [1][2].

**Use of Proceeds and Allocation Methodology**

Net proceeds will be allocated with at least 75% directed toward repayment or prepayment of existing borrowings, with up to 25% available for general corporate purposes [2]. This conservative allocation structure prioritizes debt reduction and deleveraging, reducing refinancing risk for debenture holders.

**Managing Institutions and Regulatory Framework**

Lead Managers include Trust Investment Advisors Private Limited, Nuvama Wealth Management Limited, and Tipsons Consultancy Services Private Limited. ## SECTION 3: TAX-ADJUSTED YIELD ANALYSIS FOR INVESTOR PROFILES

### Tax Treatment of NCD Interest Income Under Indian Income Tax Law

Interest received on Non-Convertible Debentures constitutes fully taxable income classified under the head "Income from Other Sources" per Section 56(2) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 [12][13]. No indexation benefit or special deduction applies to NCD interest income. Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) is automatically withheld at 10% when annual interest exceeds ₹10,000 for listed NCDs (Section 193) [12].

Investors may submit Form 15G/15H to avoid TDS withholding if their total income remains below applicable exemption limits. Capital gains realized through sale prior to maturity are taxed as short-term capital gains (STCG) if the holding period is less than 12 months (listed NCDs) or 36 months (unlisted NCDs), with gains taxed according to the investor's marginal income tax rate. Long-term capital gains (LTCG) on listed NCDs held exceeding 12 months are taxed at 12.5% plus surcharge and 4% health and education cess. Importantly, according to an ITAT ruling (May 6, 2024), premiums received upon NCD redemption (not transfer) are taxable as interest income rather than capital gains, as redemption constitutes realization of a debt obligation rather than a "transfer" triggering capital gains treatment [14][15].

## SECTION 5: HISTORICAL NCD MARKET PATTERNS AND DEFAULT CONTEXT

### Indian NCD Market Evolution and Issuance Cycles

**Market Size and Growth Trajectory**

India's corporate bond market expanded dramatically from ₹17.5 trillion in FY2015 to ₹53.6 trillion in FY2025, representing approximately 12% annual growth and reaching approximately 15-16% of GDP [20]. Fresh corporate bond issuances achieved a record ₹9.9 trillion in FY2025, demonstrating continued investor appetite for corporate debt [20].

**Market Structure Evolution**

The Indian NCD market has undergone significant structural evolution driven by regulatory enhancements:

- **FY2016-2018**: Market growth driven primarily by AAA-rated issuers (66% of market) with limited participation from lower-rated entities, NBFCs, and retail investors
- **FY2018-2019**: IL&FS default crisis (September 2018) created sharp market repricing, temporary reduction in NBFC issuances, and regulatory tightening
- **FY2019-2020**: COVID-19 pandemic triggered liquidity stress, RBI emergency interventions, and subsequent normalization through H2 FY2020
- **FY2021-2025**: Steady recovery with inclusion of India's Fully Accessible Route (FAR) bonds in Bloomberg Global Aggregate Index (April 2025), expansion of ESG debt securities, and mandatory listing requirements effective January 1, 2024

### Historical Default Rates by Rating Category

**Cumulative Default Statistics (2014-2024)**

The primary research-grade analysis indicates the following historical default frequencies across Indian debt market participants [11]:

- **AA-Rated Issuers**: 0.31% cumulative default rate (2014-2024 period)
- **A-Rated Issuers**: 0.69% cumulative default rate (2014-2024 period)
- **BBB and Below**: Approximately 3-5x higher default frequencies relative to A-rated entities (estimated 2-3% cumulative default for BBB-rated)

These figures imply that across a 10-year historical period, 3 of 1,000 AA-rated debentures and approximately 7 of 1,000 A-rated debentures defaulted. BBB-rated entities experience substantially higher default frequencies, placing Prachay Capital's BBB-/Stable rating in a higher-risk category relative to EFSL's A+/Stable rating.

**Default Implications for Current Offerings**

EFSL's A+/Stable rating implies historical default probability approximating 0.60-0.75% over a 10-year horizon, derived from interpolation between A-rated (0.69%) and AA-rated (0.31%) experience. Prachay Capital's BBB-/Stable rating implies default probability in the 2-3% range over similar horizons, representing 3-5x higher credit risk.

### IL&FS Default Case Study and Contagion Patterns

**IL&FS Crisis (September 2018) - Trigger and Propagation**

Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services Limited (IL&FS), one of India's largest infrastructure-focused NBFCs, triggered a systemic credit event through defaults on bank loans and short-term debt obligations in June 2018 [21][22]. The company's subsequent deterioration to complete operational failure created the first major NCD/short-term debt default contagion in modern Indian financial markets.

**Transmission Mechanisms and Market Impact**

The IL&FS contagion affected financial markets through multiple channels [22][23]:

1. **Money Market Stress**: IL&FS's substantial commercial paper (CP) and non-convertible debenture programs triggered sudden funding withdrawal from short-term debt markets, creating liquidity mismatches across the NBFC sector
2. **Mutual Fund Exposure**: Mutual funds with significant IL&FS short-term debt exposure faced redemption pressures and portfolio revaluation challenges
3. **Funding Channel Disruption**: The sudden withdrawal of wholesale funding (which NBFCs rely on through short-term debt and bank loans) created a systemic liquidity crisis
4. **Credit Spread Widening**: Non-IL&FS debentures experienced significant price deterioration as investors repriced credit risk across the NBFC sector

**RBI Policy Response and Stabilization**

The Reserve Bank of India implemented emergency stabilization measures including [23]:
- Large-scale liquidity injections via Open Market Operations (OMOs)
- Reduction of reserve ratio requirements to release system liquidity
- Regulatory tightening of NBFC lending standards and capital requirements
- Enhanced stress testing and credit quality monitoring requirements

**Market Recovery and Lessons Learned**

The IL&FS default proved relatively contained relative to systemic risk potential, with recovery beginning by Q4 FY2018-19 following RBI interventions. However, the crisis demonstrated that:

1. **Liquidity Mismatch Risk**: NBFCs' reliance on short-term wholesale funding creates vulnerability to sudden investor withdrawal
2. **Contagion Mechanisms**: Single entity defaults can create broader credit spreads across the sector
3. **Institutional Resilience**: RBI's rapid policy response and liquidity provision contained contagion and prevented systemic cascade

### DHFL Default (2019) and Secondary Contagion Wave

Following IL&FS's stabilization, Dewan Housing Finance Limited (DHFL) experienced similar funding stress in 2019, triggered by asset quality deterioration and fraud allegations. The DHFL crisis created a secondary wave of NBFC funding stress, with broader credit spread widening and mutual fund redemption pressures recurring.

### Asset Quality Review (AQR) and Banking System Stress (2015-2019)

Separate from NBFC defaults, the RBI's Asset Quality Review (2015-2017) identified elevated non-performing assets in public sector banks, leading to:
- Sharp decline in PSU bank lending growth (0-3% growth periods)
- Shift of credit growth to private sector banks and NBFCs
- Elevated NPA recognition across banking system (4-6% NPA ratios by 2017)

This historical period demonstrates that even systemically important bank defaults and credit stress do not typically cascade into NCD market collapses when managed with adequate policy response.

### Current Market Positioning (2025-2026) and Risk Considerations

The Indian financial system has evolved significantly post-IL&FS through [23]:
- Enhanced NBFC regulatory oversight and capital requirements
- Improved credit stress testing and early warning systems
- RBI liquidity management capabilities (demonstrated through COVID-19 and subsequent episodes)
- Expanded government bond market and reduced dependence on short-term funding

## SECTION 7: COMPREHENSIVE RISK ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK

### Credit Risk Quantification and Default Probability Modeling

**Structural Credit Risk Modeling (Merton Framework)**

Credit risk assessment employs structural models viewing equity as a call option on firm assets. The distance-to-default (DTD) framework suggests default occurs when firm asset value falls below debt obligations. For EFSL [32]:

- Total Assets: ₹416,225 million
- Total Debt Obligations: ₹375,082 million (implied from assets minus equity)
- Equity Value: ₹41,143 million (net worth)
- Asset Volatility (estimated): 15-18% annually (typical for diversified financial services)

Using Merton's framework with assumed 18% asset volatility, the distance-to-default of 0.95x standard deviations translates to an implied probability of default approximating 0.60-0.75% over a 1-year horizon, consistent with A+ rating band expectations [32][33].

**Reduced-Form Credit Models and Rating Transition Dynamics**

Reduced-form models treat default as a Poisson event with intensity (hazard rate) dependent on financial condition, market variables, and macroeconomic state. For EFSL, key drivers include:

- Asset quality deterioration (8.4% NPA ratio): Indicates elevated hazard rate relative to peers
- Revenue stability (1% YoY decline): Suggests operational headwinds
- Capital adequacy (9.1x implied leverage): Elevated debt relative to equity cushion
- Profitability (13% ROE): Adequate but not exceptional

**PD, LGD, and EAD Framework**

Expected Loss = Probability of Default × Loss Given Default × Exposure at Default [33]

For a ₹1,00,000 EFSL NCD position:

- PD (1-year): 0.65% (derived from A+ rating, adjusted for elevated NPA ratio)
- LGD: 40-60% (secured debentures imply higher recovery, typically 40-50% for A+ issuers)
- EAD: ₹1,00,000

Expected Loss = 0.65% × 50% × ₹1,00,000 = ₹325

This expected loss of ₹325 represents 0.325% of portfolio value, approximately 32 basis points annualized, representing the credit risk cost implicit in the NCD yield.

### Interest Rate Risk: Duration and Convexity Analysis

**Modified Duration Calculation**

For EFSL's maximum 120-month tenor NCD at 10.10% coupon with annual interest payments:

Using the Macaulay duration formula and adjusting for coupon-paying bonds:
- Years to Maturity: 10 years
- Semi-Annual Coupon Rate: 10.10%
- YTM (assumed): 10.10% (par value assumption)
- **Macaulay Duration ≈ 8.2 years** (time-weighted cash flows)
- **Modified Duration = 8.2 / (1 + 0.101/1) = 7.45 years** [34]

A modified duration of 7.45 years means the 120-month EFSL NCD experiences approximately 7.45% price depreciation for each 100 basis point increase in interest rates.

**Practical Interest Rate Scenarios**

Given the RBI's December 2025 repo rate of 5.25% and forward guidance suggesting potential additional 25-50 bps cuts, interest rate scenarios include:

1. **Bull Case** (RBI cuts additional 50 bps to 4.75%): 10-year G-Sec yield declines from 6.50% to 6.00%, NCD yields decline proportionately, EFSL NCD price increases approximately 3.7% (0.50% × 7.45 modified duration) = ₹3,700 per ₹1,00,000 position

2. **Base Case** (No further cuts, rates stable): 10-year yield remains 6.40-6.60%, NCD prices remain approximately stable around par value

3. **Bear Case** (Fed rate hikes, inflation surprise): 10-year G-Sec yields rise from 6.50% to 7.25% (+75 bps), EFSL NCD prices decline approximately 5.6% (0.75% × 7.45 modified duration) = ₹5,600 per ₹1,00,000 position

**Convexity Considerations**

Bonds exhibit negative convexity when option-embedded (call provisions) and positive convexity when option-free. EFSL's secured debentures lack explicit call provisions, implying positive convexity. For every 100 bps parallel shift in yields, positive convexity provides approximately 0.25-0.50% additional price benefit, reducing duration-calculated losses and enhancing gains.
