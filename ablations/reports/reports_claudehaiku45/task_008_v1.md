## Executive Summary

This analysis compares three heat pump dryer models suitable for a Melbourne household performing 6–8 loads of laundry weekly in a heritage-listed terrace with limited ventilation. Heat pump dryers are particularly advantageous for heritage properties because they operate on a closed-loop system requiring no external venting, eliminating the need for heritage permit approvals related to external modifications. 

**Key Finding on Victorian Energy Upgrades:** Despite the extensive Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU) program offering rebates for energy-efficient upgrades, heat pump clothes dryers are **not currently eligible** for rebates under this program. VEU rebates apply only to hot water systems, heating/cooling systems, lighting, insulation, and draught sealing. This is an important consideration for the cost-of-ownership analysis.

The analysis reveals significant differences in warranty coverage, reliability, maintenance requirements, and suitability for your specific installation constraints. The Bosch model has been discontinued from retail channels, making it increasingly unsuitable for purchase despite its historical efficiency. The LG model offers the strongest warranty coverage (10 years on motor components) and is recognised as Australia's Best Dryer Brand by Choice Magazine (2025). The Samsung model provides advanced AI-assisted drying and comparable efficiency but has more limited Australian availability and documentation.

## 2. Drying Performance

### AS/NZS 2442.1 Standard Testing Framework

The AS/NZS 2442.1 standard ("Performance of household electrical appliances – Rotary clothes dryers") is the Australian and New Zealand regulatory standard for clothes dryer testing and labelling. The most recent regulatory update is the Greenhouse and Energy Minimum Standards (Rotary Clothes Dryers) Determination 2024, effective from 4 October 2025, which implemented updates based on AS/NZS 2442.1:2021 and AS/NZS 2442.2:2021. [6]

The standard covers clothes dryers including heat pump condenser dryers. Key aspects of the testing methodology include:

- Testing under standard conditions (typically the Cotton/Cupboard Dry setting at testing facility temperature)
- Measurement of energy consumption over standardised load cycles
- Assessment under controlled humidity and temperature conditions
- Testing of drying efficiency and performance consistency
- Energy rating assignment based on standardised energy consumption

The 2024 determination introduced several modifications to testing procedures: expanded definitions of heating methods (now including electric resistance, heat pumps, and radio frequency devices), modified test methods that include cool-down periods, allowance for loads up to 20 kg (previously smaller loads), and standardised test voltage of 230V. [6]

### Noise Context and Shared-Wall Compliance

To contextualise these noise levels:

- 62–63 dB(A) is comparable to normal conversation at close range or background music in a restaurant
- Modern heat pump dryers are generally quieter than vented dryers, which often operate at 70–75 dB(A)
- For comparison, washing machines typically operate at 75–85 dB(A)

Victoria's planning regulations do not specify strict dB limits for domestic appliances within residential properties. However, the Environment Protection Act 1970 addresses noise pollution, with general guidance that noise should not be intrusive or unreasonably disturb neighbouring properties. For domestic appliances in residential areas, noise levels under 65 dB(A) are generally considered acceptable. [10]

The Bosch at 62 dB(A) and Samsung at 63 dB(A) both fall within acceptable ranges for shared-wall residential properties. The LG model's unspecified noise level represents a consideration—if it falls significantly above 65 dB(A), it could potentially violate Victoria's Environmental Protection Act in shared-wall scenarios.

### Advantages of Heat Pump Dryers for Shared Walls

Heat pump dryers offer significant advantages for terrace houses beyond noise considerations:

**No External Venting Required:** Unlike vented dryers that expel warm, moist air through external ducting, heat pump dryers operate on a closed-loop system. This eliminates the need for external vent penetrations through shared walls, avoiding both visible external installations that might violate heritage restrictions and potential neighbour complaints regarding warm air discharge or humidity impacts. [11]

**Moisture Management:** Heat pump dryers capture all condensed moisture internally, collecting it in a removable tank or draining it via a condensate hose. This prevents moisture accumulation that could damage external brickwork or mortar in shared walls—a particular concern in heritage structures where original lime mortar and breathable masonry are present. [11]

**No Heat Discharge to Adjacent Properties:** Traditional vented dryers can warm adjacent properties by venting hot air. Heat pump dryers release heat and humidity indoors (within the property owner's space), avoiding thermal impacts on neighbours. [11]

## 5. Installation and Compatibility with Heritage-Listed Properties

### Victorian Heritage Overlay Regulations

The three dryer models must be evaluated within the specific regulatory context of heritage-listed properties in Victoria. Understanding these requirements is crucial for choosing a suitable installation solution.

#### Heritage Classifications in Victoria

Victoria has two primary classifications for heritage homes: [16]

1. **Victorian Heritage Register:** Rare properties with strict preservation requirements and limited tolerance for modifications
2. **Heritage Overlay:** More common classification applied by local councils with planning permit requirements for visible modifications

Heritage-listed properties in Victoria are subject to additional regulations to preserve historical significance. If your property is covered by a Heritage Overlay, a planning permit is required to carry out most works including construction, alterations, demolitions, window replacement, and re-roofing. [17]

#### Planning Permit Requirements for Dryer Installation

**Critical Finding for This Analysis:** The absence of external venting requirements for heat pump dryers provides a substantial regulatory advantage for heritage properties.

If your property is located in a Heritage Overlay, the installation of external Service Units (such as traditional dryer venting ducts or air conditioning units) will require a Planning Permit if visible from a street or public park. [18] However, heat pump dryers do not require external Service Units because they operate on a closed-loop system with no external venting. [19]

Internal installations located within the property and not visible from public spaces typically do not require separate heritage permits beyond standard building code compliance. Rear room installations—particularly in utility areas, kitchens, or laundries not visible from the street—are generally more acceptable under heritage guidelines without requiring explicit heritage approval. [17]

**The Regulatory Advantage of Heat Pump Dryers:**

The three dryer models compared in this analysis—all heat pump models—require no external venting. This contrasts sharply with traditional vented dryers, which would require:
- External duct penetration through external walls (visible modification requiring permit)
- External duct termination and weather hood (visible from street, likely requiring heritage permit)
- Potential concerns about thermal and moisture impacts on heritage fabric

By selecting a heat pump dryer, you effectively eliminate the most significant heritage regulatory barrier to dryer installation, as no visible external modifications are necessary.

#### Council Pre-Application Consultation

Heritage Victoria and local councils recommend consultation before undertaking modifications to heritage properties. The process is straightforward:

- **Free Heritage Advisory Service:** Consult with your local council (in Melbourne, this is typically the City of Melbourne, Stonnington, Moreland, or applicable local council depending on your location) regarding planned installations
- **Pre-Application Meetings:** Many councils offer free pre-application meetings to clarify what is and is not permitted in your area
- **Due Diligence:** Thorough research and preparation are the best way to ensure compliance

For laundry room internal installations of heat pump dryers, most councils will confirm that no heritage permit is required, as the installation is internal and involves no visible external modifications.

### Heat Pump Dryer Suitability for Limited Ventilation Environments

Heat pump dryers are specifically designed and well-suited for properties with limited external ventilation options. This makes them ideal for heritage terraces.

#### Closed-Loop Operation

Heat pump dryers operate via a closed-loop system that heats air, circulates it through the tumbling drum to remove moisture from clothes, cools the moist air to condense the water, and recycles the dried air back through the process. [19] Unlike traditional dryers that expel warm, moist air through an outdoor vent, heat pump dryers circulate warm air within a closed system, eliminating the need for external venting. [20]

The three models in this analysis all employ this closed-loop technology:
- **Bosch:** ActiveAir heat pump technology with efficient heat reuse
- **LG:** Eco Hybrid Technology with inverter control heat pump system
- **Samsung:** AI Control heat pump technology with OptimalDry sensors

#### Condensate Management for Heritage Properties

The moisture extracted from clothes during the drying process is collected as liquid condensate inside the dryer unit. This condensate must be managed in one of several ways:

**Option 1: Manual Emptying**
The dryer collects condensate in a removable tank (typically 3–5 litres capacity). The tank must be manually emptied after each drying cycle or when full. This is the simplest installation method and requires no plumbing modifications. For a household performing 7 loads weekly, the tank would typically require emptying 1–2 times per week.

**Option 2: Drain Hose Connection**
The dryer can be connected to an external drain via a small-diameter condensate hose (approximately 1/4 inch or 6mm diameter, much smaller than washing machine drain hoses). The hose can be directed to:
- An existing washing machine drainage point
- An external drain or gutter
- An indoor grey-water collection system

The drain hose length specification typically allows up to 1.5 metres (5 feet) maximum without a pump. If longer distances are required or the outlet is lower than the dryer (creating back-pressure risk), a non-return valve must be installed. [21]

**Advantage for Heritage Properties:** This drainage system requires no external wall penetration if connected to existing internal plumbing. The small hose diameter and flexible routing mean installation can be achieved within heritage constraints without visible external modifications.

#### Indoor Heat and Humidity Release

Heat pump dryers release the heat and humidity they extract into the internal environment of the property. This is a significant advantage for heritage terraces during winter (providing incidental heating) but requires consideration during summer:

- **Winter Advantage:** In Melbourne's winter, the dryer contributes approximately 1–2 kW of heat to the laundry room, reducing space heating requirements by an estimated 10–15 therms annually (equivalent to approximately $50–$100 in heating cost savings)
- **Summer Consideration:** During summer, the internal heat release may increase air conditioning requirements if the laundry room is within the conditioned space

For heritage properties with period room configurations, the laundry room is often a rear utility space that can be isolated thermally from living areas, minimising summer cooling impacts.

### Moisture Management for Heritage-Sensitive Environments

Heritage buildings are particularly sensitive to uncontrolled moisture, which can cause severe damage to historic materials:

**Heritage Building Humidity Requirements:**
Heritage sites generally aim to maintain relative humidity levels between 40–65%, with an ideal compromise range of 35% in winter to 60% in summer. Excess moisture leads to issues including mould, dry rot, and structural deterioration such as masonry spalling and timber warping. [22]

**Advantages of Heat Pump Dryer Condensate Systems:**

The heat pump dryer's closed-loop condensate management provides a significant advantage over traditional vented dryers:

1. **Moisture Capture:** All moisture extracted from clothes is captured as liquid condensate inside the dryer unit, not released into room air
2. **Controlled Discharge:** Condensate is either manually removed (preventing any moisture entry to heritage fabric) or directed via drain hose to external drainage (again, no indoor moisture accumulation)
3. **Stable Indoor Humidity:** Unlike traditional dryers that vent 5–10 kg of moisture-laden air into the home per cycle, heat pump dryers maintain more stable indoor humidity levels
4. **Heritage Fabric Protection:** Stable humidity prevents the damaging humidity fluctuations that cause timber joinery to swell and shrink, plaster finishes to crack, and mortar to deteriorate

For heritage terrace houses with original lime mortar, timber frame construction, and period plaster finishes, this humidity stability is a significant advantage.

### Heritage Property Installation Advantages

The closed-loop, no-venting design of heat pump dryers provides several specific advantages for heritage-listed properties:

3. **No Visible External Modifications:** The absence of ductwork, vents, or weather hoods maintains the property's external appearance and heritage character
4. **Flexible Interior Placement:** The closed-loop design allows placement in any room with electricity and a water drainage option, not limited to locations near external walls
5. **Humidity Control Benefits:** Moisture capture prevents the humidity damage that threatens heritage timber and masonry
6. **No Thermal Impact on Neighbours:** Heat release occurs internally, not affecting shared walls or adjacent properties
7. **Simplified Regulatory Compliance:** By avoiding external modifications, the installation typically avoids the most complex heritage approval requirements

## 7. Victorian Energy Upgrades Program - Important Finding

### VEU Rebate Eligibility for Heat Pump Dryers: NOT ELIGIBLE

A critical research finding emerged regarding Victorian government support for heat pump dryer purchases. **The Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU) program does NOT provide rebates for heat pump clothes dryers.**

#### What VEU Does Cover

The Victorian Energy Upgrades program provides rebates and discounts on the following energy-efficient products and upgrades: [24]

- **Heating and Cooling Systems:** Reverse cycle air conditioning (up to $500+ rebates depending on configuration)
- **Hot Water Systems:** Heat pump hot water systems (up to $1,000 rebates, potentially combined with Federal incentives for $0 upfront cost)
- **Lighting:** LED lighting and efficient lighting systems
- **Insulation:** Ceiling insulation (new for 2026)
- **Draught Sealing:** Weather sealing and draught prevention
- **Solar Power Systems:** Primarily for businesses

#### Clothes Dryers Specifically Excluded

Despite heat pump clothes dryers being among the most energy-efficient appliances available, they are not included in the VEU product list. [24] Extensive searching of Victorian Energy Upgrades official documentation, eligible product lists, and program information yielded no mention of heat pump clothes dryers as eligible for rebates.

#### Implications for Cost-of-Ownership Analysis

The cost-of-ownership calculations provided in Section 1 of this analysis do **not include any VEU rebate**, as none is available. Some households may have mistakenly assumed rebate eligibility based on the general VEU programme for energy-efficient appliances. This finding is important for accurate financial planning.

#### Alternative Government Support

Households in Victoria may be eligible for other supports:
- **Victorian Power Savers:** Some local councils offer additional energy efficiency grants beyond VEU
- **Federal Support:** Federal schemes for solar and certain energy systems may apply
- **Council-Specific Programs:** Some municipalities offer localised rebate schemes

Prospective buyers should contact their local council directly to inquire about any additional local-level rebate programmes beyond the state-level VEU scheme.

## Installation Pathway for Heritage Properties: Summary Process

For installing a heat pump dryer in your heritage-listed terrace, the recommended process is:

1. **Identify Local Council:** Determine which Melbourne local council has jurisdiction over your property (City of Melbourne, Stonnington, Moreland, Darebin, etc.)

2. **Heritage Status Confirmation:** Verify whether your property has Heritage Overlay designation through the local council's planning database

3. **Council Pre-Application Consultation:** Contact your local council's Heritage Advisory Service or planning department and request advice regarding heat pump dryer installation. Emphasise the internal-only installation with no external modifications. Most councils will confirm no heritage permit is required for this type of installation.

4. **Installation Arrangements:** Once council guidance is confirmed, arrange professional installation through an electrical contractor. Ensure the contractor:
   - Verifies electrical circuit capacity and installs proper 10–15A circuit breaker protection
   - Complies with AS3000 (Australian Electrical Standards)
   - Allows 1-hour post-installation stabilisation before initial operation
   - Tests condensate drainage functionality

5. **Condensate Management Selection:** Decide whether to:
   - Manually empty condensate tank (lowest cost, minimal modifications)
   - Connect condensate hose to existing washing machine drainage (minimal modifications, recommended)
   - Install new drainage if required

6. **Maintenance Planning:** Establish a maintenance schedule based on the dryer model, including:
   - Monthly/quarterly filter cleaning (most models)
   - Annual professional inspection if desired
   - Condensate tank emptying (if manually managed)

## Final Considerations

### Drying Time Expectations

Prospective buyers should reset expectations regarding drying time compared to traditional vented dryers. Heat pump dryers are substantially slower:

- **Heat Pump Dryers:** Typically 180–240 minutes for full loads of mixed items
- **Traditional Vented Dryers:** Typically 120–180 minutes for equivalent loads
- **Trade-off:** Extended drying time is the cost of 50% lower energy consumption

For a household performing 6–8 weekly loads, the extended drying time translates to approximately 20–30 additional hours per week of dryer operation time. However, the dryer operates unattended, making actual impact on household workflow minimal compared to the energy cost savings.

### Noise Consideration for Shared Walls

All three models operate within acceptable noise ranges (62–63 dB(A)) for shared-wall residential properties. However, the LG model's unspecified noise level remains a consideration. If precise noise specifications are essential for your decision, contacting LG directly or requesting this specification from retailers is recommended before purchase.

### Maintenance Commitment

Heat pump dryer ownership requires genuine commitment to maintenance, particularly condenser/heat exchanger cleaning. The "ventless" design does not eliminate maintenance—it relocates it from external ducting to internal components. Households unwilling or unable to maintain these internal components regularly should carefully consider whether a heat pump dryer is suitable.
