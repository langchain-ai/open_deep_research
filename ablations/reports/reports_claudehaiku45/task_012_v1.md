# Maternal Mortality Rates and Prenatal Care Access: Comparative Analysis of Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar and Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp (2017-2023)

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive analysis compares maternal health outcomes and prenatal care access between two distinct refugee populations: Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand, during 2017-2023. Despite both populations facing extreme resource constraints and displacement, they demonstrate markedly different health indicators, driven by fundamentally different healthcare delivery models, structural resources, and cultural engagement approaches. The Rohingya camps, served primarily by NGO-operated field hospitals and health posts, show lower antenatal care completion rates and skilled birth attendance but have achieved significant recent improvements through targeted interventions. Mae La camp, operating under a Thai Ministry of Public Health integration model with extensive NGO partnership, demonstrates higher baseline maternal health indicators and more stable services, though both settings experienced significant disruptions from 2021 UNFPA funding reductions. This analysis reveals that structural healthcare delivery models, midwifery workforce capacity, emergency obstetric care accessibility, and culturally-informed approaches to traditional birth attendant integration all correlate directly with measurable maternal and neonatal health outcomes.

## Part 1: Comparative Health Indicators (2017-2023)

### Antenatal Care Visit Completion Rates (4+ Visits)

**Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps:**

The Rohingya refugee population in Cox's Bazar shows variable but improving antenatal care coverage over the 2017-2023 period. Early research from 2017-2018 documented that approximately 71.6% of pregnant Rohingya women attended at least four antenatal care visits.[1] By 2023-2024, longitudinal data from a cohort of 2,322 pregnant women revealed improvement, with 80.8% initiating antenatal care within the first trimester, and an average of 6.9 antenatal care visits per woman, with only 5.7% receiving fewer than four visits.[1] This represents meaningful progress toward WHO recommendations, though the trajectory was not linear.

During the April-June 2021 period, facility-based antenatal care services sustained relatively high utilization despite COVID-19 impacts, with community health workers conducting nearly 2 million household visits promoting health awareness and surveillance.[2] However, this progress was fragile and dependent on sustained funding and service availability. A critical finding emerged regarding women's knowledge: despite high antenatal care attendance rates documented by providers, many women did not know their due dates despite visiting doctors during pregnancy, indicating a quality-of-care gap and potential communication barriers between health providers and Rohingya women.[1]

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

The Mae La camp demonstrates consistently high antenatal care coverage throughout the study period. Data from 2000-2018 shows that more than 90% of pregnant women attended four or more antenatal care visits, with this rate sustained above UNHCR benchmarks (90% standard) throughout the period.[3] By 2018, specific camp site data confirmed 91% antenatal care coverage in Mae La proper and 94% in Mae La Oon, with nearly all women receiving antenatal tetanus vaccination.[3] This consistently high coverage significantly exceeds the Rohingya camps and reflects both the established health infrastructure and the integration of maternal health services into the camp's regular health programming.

**Comparative Analysis:**

The disparity in antenatal care completion is substantial: Mae La camps maintained >90% coverage consistently, while Cox's Bazar achieved 71.6-80.8% coverage. This 10-20 percentage point difference represents approximately 3,000-6,000 fewer pregnant women with adequate antenatal surveillance in Cox's Bazar camps (based on annual birth estimates of 30,000-40,000). The improved trajectory in Cox's Bazar toward 80.8% represents significant programmatic gains, particularly through targeted community health worker engagement and clinic strengthening, but the baseline gap reflects underlying structural healthcare delivery differences.

### Skilled Birth Attendance Percentages

**Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps:**

Skilled birth attendance in the Rohingya camps showed dramatic improvement over the 2017-2023 period, representing one of the most significant health gains in the response. Data from 2017-2018 documented that only 59.5% of pregnant women delivered in adequately equipped facilities with skilled birth attendants present, with 69.4% reporting skilled birth attendant presence (which may include less formally trained attendants).[1] By April-June 2021, facility-based deliveries had increased to 67% despite ongoing COVID-19 restrictions, reaching 82% by 2023.[2] This represented a major shift in health-seeking behavior driven by multiple interventions including community-based referral systems, training of traditional birth attendants as referral agents, and targeted awareness campaigns.

A critical inflection point occurred through targeted TBA engagement. A pilot program initiated in 2021 by Community Partners International and Green Hill trained traditional birth attendants (TBAs) in Kutupalong camp to understand the dangers of home births and refer laboring women to health facilities. This intervention increased facility-based deliveries from 33% in 2020 to over 95% in the target area by 2022, demonstrating the power of culturally-informed approaches to increase facility utilization.[4] Continuous engagement with TBAs proved essential to maintain this progress.

By 2024, over 80% of facility-based deliveries were being achieved across the camps, though this represents a broad range and some variance by location. The number of facility-based deliveries increased in absolute terms from approximately 7,000-8,000 annually in 2018 to over 30,000 by 2023-2024.[2]

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

Mae La demonstrates more variable skilled birth attendance compared to antenatal care coverage. Specific 2018 data reported 95% skilled birth attendance in Mae La proper, while Mae La Oon reported 78%.[3] Over the 2000-2018 period, approximately 80% of deliveries were assisted by skilled birth attendants across the combined camp population.[5] Importantly, approximately 40% of births in some camps occurred outside facilities with traditional birth attendants despite high antenatal care attendance, indicating a significant disconnect between pregnancy surveillance and delivery location.[5]

Caesarean section rates remained extremely low at 3.7% average, with another source citing 3.6%, well below the WHO-recommended range of 10-15% for appropriate obstetric care, suggesting potential underutilization of emergency obstetric care capabilities.[5]

**Comparative Analysis:**

The skilled birth attendance gap between the two populations narrowed considerably over the study period. In 2017-2018, Mae La's 80-95% exceeded Cox's Bazar's 59.5-69.4% by 15-35 percentage points. By 2023, this gap had narrowed to approximately 5-15 percentage points, with Cox's Bazar reaching 80-82%. The convergence reflects intensive programmatic focus in Cox's Bazar on facility-based delivery promotion, combined with the structural integration of delivery services into existing health systems. However, both populations demonstrate the persistent challenge of high home birth rates despite facility availability, driven primarily by cultural preferences and traditional birth attendant utilization.

### Postpartum Hemorrhage Incidence

**Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps:**

Specific postpartum hemorrhage incidence rates for Cox's Bazar camps were not directly documented in published health system reports during 2017-2023. However, indirect evidence suggests significant risk. From January-December 2021, Maternal and Perinatal Mortality Surveillance data identified obstetric hemorrhage as the leading cause of confirmed maternal deaths at 29.2%, followed by pregnancy-induced hypertensive disorders at 23%.[6] By 2022, hemorrhage accounted for 44% of 83 maternal deaths reported, indicating that postpartum hemorrhage remained a major driver of maternal mortality despite emergency obstetric care availability.[2]

Clinical observations from midwives working in Cox's Bazar camps documented that malnutrition and anemia were prevalent among women, with postpartum hemorrhage risks heightened by these nutritional deficiencies.[1] The absence of blood banks in the camps was repeatedly documented as a critical infrastructure gap, severely limiting the ability to manage hemorrhagic complications through transfusion when needed.[1] In April-June 2021, 45% of primary health centers reported stock-outs of essential medicines, creating situations where midwives and doctors lacked adequate supplies to manage hemorrhage even when cases reached facilities.[2]

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

While specific postpartum hemorrhage incidence rates for Mae La camp were not available in direct reports, a border-region epidemiological study from Maesai Hospital (2014-2018), serving predominantly Karen and other minority populations on the Thai-Myanmar border, provides highly relevant comparative data. Among 4,774 vaginal births, the overall postpartum hemorrhage incidence (≥500 mL blood loss) was 5.55%, with severe PPH (≥1000 mL) at 1.84%.[7] Multivariable analysis identified that belonging to minority ethnic groups (including Karen) carried significantly higher risk of both PPH and severe PPH, attributed to socioeconomic disadvantages, limited healthcare access, and potential biological factors.[7]

Critical risk factors identified included elderly pregnancy (≥35 years), nulliparity, history of prior PPH, elevated body mass index, manual removal of placenta, labor augmentation, and fetal weight above 4000 grams.[7] The Thai government implemented WHO-based PPH Risk Score tools in 2019-2020 in Northern Thai hospitals near refugee populations, resulting in decreased PPH incidences and PPH-related hysterectomy cases, bringing them below national standards during follow-up periods.[7] Implementation of risk score tools achieved 80.7% sensitivity and 60.8% specificity in predicting postpartum blood loss over 300 mL.[7]

Maternal anemia, a critical risk factor for severe hemorrhage, decreased 30% in Mae La Oon and Mae Ra Ma Luang over 2000-2018, but remained more prevalent in refugee populations than Thai nationals.[5]

**Comparative Analysis:**

Both populations faced significant PPH risk, though the gap in documented outcomes reflects different healthcare capacities. Mae La's integration with Thai government hospitals and access to blood transfusion services represented major protective advantages. The availability of trained providers, emergency obstetric care capabilities including cesarean section, blood products, and coordination with higher-level facilities directly translated to better hemorrhage management outcomes. Cox's Bazar camps, lacking blood banks and experiencing medication stock-outs, faced greater mortality risk when hemorrhage occurred despite facility attendance. The nutritional disadvantage in Cox's Bazar (compared to more stable Mae La services) further elevated hemorrhage risk through higher anemia prevalence.

### Neonatal Mortality Within 28 Days

**Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps:**

Neonatal mortality in Cox's Bazar refugee camps was documented at 27.0 per 1,000 live births during 2017-2018, representing a significant burden of preventable death.[1] While no specific 2023 neonatal mortality rates were available for Cox's Bazar, the maternal mortality trajectory and continued facility infrastructure gaps suggest that neonatal mortality remained elevated despite improving facility-based delivery rates.

The major drivers of neonatal mortality identified in the camps included complications of prematurity, neonatal sepsis, jaundice, asphyxia, and complications of low birth weight—conditions primarily preventable or manageable through access to skilled neonatal care and basic resuscitation capacity.[1] WHO-supported Essential Newborn Care training initiatives launched in 2022 aimed directly at improving neonatal survival, training over 300 health workers across 45 health facilities in newborn resuscitation and emergency delivery management, improving provider confidence from baseline to near-universal reporting of confidence post-training.[1]

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

Mae La demonstrated dramatic improvements in neonatal mortality over the study period. The camp experienced a 51% decline in neonatal mortality in Mae La camp between 2008-2011, declining from 21.8 to 10.7 deaths per 1,000 live births (p=0.04).[8] This substantial improvement resulted from the introduction of a Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) providing neonatal intensive care in the resource-poor setting. Between January 2008 and December 2011, 952 infants were admitted to the SCBU for management of early-onset neonatal sepsis, jaundice, prematurity, and other complications.[8]

The SCBU achieved particularly impressive mortality reduction among preterm infants: mortality from early prematurity (<34 weeks) decreased from 19.3% in 2008 to 4.8% in 2011 (p=0.03), demonstrating the effectiveness of even basic neonatal care in resource-poor settings.[8] Over the longer 2000-2018 period, neonatal mortality declined as part of comprehensive child mortality improvements, with neonatal mortality rates falling from 23.5 to 7.7 per 1,000 live births, representing a 62% reduction.[5] By 2018, crude under-five mortality rate in Mae La Oon was documented at 0.3 deaths per 1,000 children under five per month, well within acceptable UNHCR standards (<1.5).[3]

A key innovation was the "MACHO" mnemonic (Milk, Antibiotics, Cord care, Heat and Oxygen) developed to structure supportive care of neonates with resource limitations. Staff confidence in neonatal care and attitudes toward premature infants improved notably following SCBU implementation.[8] The cost of providing care to a preterm infant in the SCBU was approximately $7 per day, making comprehensive neonatal care feasible even in resource-poor settings.[5]

G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase) deficiency was identified as affecting 24.8% of infants treated for jaundice and was associated with prolonged phototherapy requirements, highlighting the importance of population-specific neonatal care protocols.[8]

**Comparative Analysis:**

The neonatal mortality gap between the two populations represents perhaps the most striking difference in health outcomes. Cox's Bazar at 27.0 per 1,000 (2017-2018) versus Mae La at 7.7 per 1,000 (2018) represents a threefold mortality difference. This disparity directly correlates with differences in neonatal care infrastructure: Mae La's established SCBU with trained staff, basic resuscitation equipment, and systematic neonatal protocols versus Cox's Bazar's lack of dedicated neonatal care facilities and variable equipment availability. The improvement trajectory in Mae La from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 (2008-2011) and continuing to 7.7 per 1,000 (by 2018) demonstrates what sustained programmatic investment in neonatal care can achieve. The dramatic improvement in Cox's Bazar facility-based deliveries since 2020 should theoretically improve neonatal mortality, as more births now access skilled providers, but the infrastructure gap in neonatal care capacity may continue to limit improvements compared to Mae La.

## Part 2: Healthcare Delivery Models and Structural Differences

### Cox's Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospital Model

**Healthcare Facility Network Structure:**

The Rohingya refugee camps in Cox's Bazar are served by a complex network of NGO-operated healthcare facilities coordinated by international humanitarian agencies. As of 2018, Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) operated 10 health posts, 4 primary health centers open 24/7, and 5 inpatient facilities providing secondary care, staffed by over 2,800 personnel primarily recruited from Bangladesh.[9] Subsequent scale-up resulted in MSF managing 15 health posts and three primary health centers distributed across settlements including Kutupalong, Balukhali, Tasnimarkhola, and other camp areas.[9] In addition to MSF's extensive network, UNHCR supported 28 healthcare facilities, 10 mental health and psychosocial support centers, 19 nutrition centers, and two stabilization centers across the camps.[2]

These facilities provide essential services including antenatal care, maternity care, neonatal services, emergency care, sexual and reproductive health services, family planning, mental health services, and treatment for sexual and gender-based violence. MSF's operational model emphasizes 24/7 availability at primary health centers, active case-finding through outreach, and systematic referral pathways to secondary facilities for complications.

**Staffing and Workforce Composition:**

The healthcare workforce in Cox's Bazar camps consists almost entirely of Bangladeshi and Rohingya personnel, with international advisors. In March 2018, MSF conducted 351,421 outpatient visits and 8,135 inpatient consultations with primarily Bangladeshi staff.[9] All-female community health worker teams conducted outreach covering 80% of refugee settlements, including outbreak prevention, vaccination mobilization, case finding, and sexual and reproductive health education.[9] This female workforce proved critical for reaching Rohingya women who faced cultural barriers to interaction with male health providers.

Over 40 midwives were trained as trainers in midwifery life-saving skills focusing on major obstetric-related death causes, improving patient care and outcomes.[2] By 2023-2024, the UNFPA Midwifery Mentorship program had enhanced obstetric and newborn care capacity significantly, training over 40 midwife trainers to cascade life-saving skills to broader health worker populations.

To address the persistent challenge of home deliveries (51% as of recent data), 80 former traditional birth attendants were transformed into sexual and reproductive health referral assistants through targeted training programs.[2] This community-led initiative increased facility-based deliveries twofold through awareness, referrals, and strengthened community transportation systems, despite ongoing cultural resistance to facility-based care.

**Service Delivery Achievements and Challenges:**

By 2024, sexual and reproductive health services in Cox's Bazar had achieved notable gains: 90% antenatal care 4+ visit attendance, 35,850 facility-based deliveries, caesarean section rate of 8.2% (within acceptable ranges), and 48,632 postnatal care visits.[2] The development and scale-up of the Maternal and Child Health Card was operationalized across all 33 camps and Bhasan Char, providing standardized documentation of pregnancy and delivery care.

However, critical infrastructure gaps persisted. Health sector facility monitoring showed only 57% of primary health centers provided all basic emergency obstetric and newborn care signal functions as of April-June 2021.[2] Stock-out of essential medicines was reported in 45% of facilities, primarily health posts, creating situations where available clinical capacity was constrained by supply availability.[2] The absence of blood banks in camps represented a critical limitation for managing obstetric hemorrhage despite facility attendance.

**Community-Based Referral Systems:**

In response to access barriers including transportation challenges, restricted movement, and security concerns, a community-based referral transportation system called the Referral Hub was implemented by UNFPA and partners during the COVID-19 pandemic to provide free emergency obstetric and neonatal transport.[10] The Referral Hub offered ambulance-like transport services in hard-to-reach camp areas, aiming to reduce delays in seeking and reaching care.

Facilitators for Referral Hub implementation included accurate knowledge of services, engagement of community leaders (majhis), strong partnerships with organizations, and effective intra-organizational coordination.[10] However, significant barriers persisted: discordant understanding between patients and providers about emergency signs and when to seek care, strict gender norms limiting women's interaction with male health workers, distrust in health facilities tied to past negative experiences in Myanmar and COVID-19 fears, poor mobile network connectivity delaying communication, shortage of ambulances, difficult road conditions within camps, and security concerns especially at night.[10] Despite these barriers, the Referral Hub demonstrated significant potential to increase utilization of sexual and reproductive health services when culturally sensitive approaches were employed.

### Mae La Camp: Thai Ministry of Public Health Integration Model

**Healthcare Delivery Partnership Framework:**

Mae La camp operates under a fundamentally different healthcare delivery model characterized by integration with Thai government health systems rather than exclusive reliance on NGO provision. The Royal Thai Government ensures security and supervises NGO activities through the Ministry of the Interior, while local and international NGOs—particularly Malteser International—supply healthcare services in close cooperation with trained camp residents and local health authorities.[11] This government-integrated approach provides baseline structural advantages including formal recognition within Thai health systems and potential access to Thai government resources.

Malteser International has implemented a project providing medical care to approximately 20,000 Karen and Burmese refugees in Mae Ra Ma Luang and Mae La Oon camps since 1993, making it one of the longest-standing humanitarian health programs in refugee camp settings.[12] The Tak Province Public Health Department in Thailand, in response to 2025 funding crises, planned to provide monthly financial support of 2,500 Thai Baht to 80 health workers serving Mae La and other nearby camps, demonstrating formal government engagement with camp health services.[13]

**Integrated Primary Health Care Program Evolution:**

The healthcare delivery model in Mae La evolved over 18 years (2000-2018) from basic curative care to a comprehensive integrated primary health care (PHC) system including prevention, maternal and child health, water, sanitation, hygiene (WASH), and vector control.[11] Key interventions included training local health workers, consistent use of insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, improved water infrastructure, and systematic health education. This integration across multiple health domains proved more effective than vertical, disease-specific programs.

Despite continuous loss of trained health staff due to resettlement programs, the camp maintained SPHERE standard indicators for water quantity (over 100% of households collecting ≥15 liters per person per day in Mae La), sanitation access (over 85% of households reporting latrine use), and healthcare accessibility.[3] The program demonstrated that integrated, evidence-based primary health care adequately funded and implemented by one coordinating health agency was effective for reducing infectious disease burden under constraints of semipermanent living conditions, even with population turnover and resource limitations.[11]

**Referral System Integration with Thai Hospitals:**

A critical advantage of Mae La's location in Thailand was the referral pathway to Thai provincial hospitals for emergency obstetric care and complex cases. An average of 116 obstetric, neonatal, and infant care cases per year required secondary emergency care beyond camp capacity.[5] The camp operated formal referral systems to nearby Thai hospitals, with the cost of performing obstetric surgeries at Mae Sot General Hospital approximately 2.2 million Thai Baht ($75,000 USD), while in-house service provision cost significantly less at 1.3 million Thai Baht ($43,000).[11] Over 14 months, approximately 350,000 Thai Baht ($11,880 USD) worth of acute surgical referrals were identified as potentially preventable through earlier, non-acute stage local intervention, highlighting both the value of access to advanced care and opportunities for prevention.

**Infectious Disease Control Outcomes:**

The integrated PHC approach in Mae La achieved remarkable results in infectious disease reduction over the 2000-2018 period: malaria incidence declined 12-fold, lower respiratory tract infections 3-fold, watery diarrhea 2-fold, and dysentery 5-fold.[11] Health service utilization decreased from 7.1 to 2.9 consultations per refugee per year, reflecting improved health status and more rational use of antibiotics rather than treatment-seeking behavior changes.[11] The crude mortality rate remained stable at around 4 deaths per 1,000 population per year, substantially lower than rates reported in Myanmar and Thailand during the same period, primarily due to the younger demographic profile of refugee populations.[11]

### Comparative Healthcare Delivery Analysis

**NGO-Led versus Government-Integrated Models:**

The fundamental difference between Cox's Bazar's NGO-operated field hospital model and Mae La's Thai government-integrated model produced distinct structural advantages and constraints. Cox's Bazar's approach, relying on parallel health systems operated by multiple NGOs (MSF, IRC, Save the Children, UNFPA, etc.), provided rapid scalability and flexibility for emergency response, critical for the acute 2017 influx of nearly 700,000 Rohingya refugees. MSF's ability to rapidly deploy field hospitals, train health workers, and establish community-based referral systems enabled quick access to care where virtually none existed.

However, the NGO-led model's reliance on multiple agencies, while responsive, created coordination challenges and fragmentation. Different organizations operated different protocols, maintained separate supply chains, and competed for limited funding. As of 2022, funding challenges forced recognition that "there is comparatively less dependence on international actors to deliver health services since there is very good capacity at a national level. That being said, we still need international partners, because they bring in additional expertise."[14] Bangladesh's non-signatory status to the 1951 Refugee Convention further complicated government health system integration, as refugees technically lacked legal access to national health services, requiring parallel NGO systems to fill the gap.

Mae La's government-integrated model, while requiring longer establishment timeframes (the camp was established in 1984), provided structural stability and institutional recognition. The Thai government's formal supervision of healthcare delivery, even without direct provision, created accountability frameworks and potential access to government health resources. The referral system to Thai provincial hospitals represented a major advantage unavailable to Cox's Bazar camps. However, this model faced its own challenges: Thai non-signatory status to the 1951 Refugee Convention meant camps operated in a legal gray zone, and the integration approach was only possible due to Thailand's de facto acceptance of long-term refugee presence with NGO intermediaries.

**Sustainability and Funding Implications:**

The NGO-led Cox's Bazar model proved highly vulnerable to donor funding fluctuations. Funding gaps in 2019 forced UNFPA to scale down family planning service sites and other programs.[15] The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction, discussed below, severely impacted service continuity. The dispersal across multiple NGO agencies created overlapping administrative costs and variable funding dependencies, though it also provided resilience through diversified funding sources compared to single-agency models.

Mae La's concentrated approach through Malteser International and the Thailand Burma Border Consortium (TBBC), while more coordinated, proved equally vulnerable to donor funding cuts. The 2025 funding crisis in Thailand, when US government aid was terminated, forced immediate crisis response including temporary IRC healthcare service suspension and urgent appeals to Thai government for financial support.[13] The model's relative stability over decades created organizational competence and community relationships but also complacency regarding long-term funding security.

## Part 3: Midwifery Workforce, Emergency Obstetric Care Access, and Midwife-to-Population Ratios

### Midwife-to-Population Ratios and Workforce Capacity

**Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps:**

Specific midwife-to-population ratios were not explicitly documented in camp health reports, but workforce composition data provides insight into capacity. Over 40 midwives trained as trainers in life-saving skills suggest approximately 40+ primary midwives in the system by 2023, serving a population of approximately 900,000-1,000,000 Rohingya refugees, yielding a ratio of approximately 1 midwife per 22,000-25,000 population.[2] This ratio falls below WHO recommendations of at least 1 midwife per 5,000 population in emergency settings.

The midwifery workforce composition in Cox's Bazar consisted of: (1) formally trained midwives from Bangladesh recruited through UNFPA and NGO programs; (2) newly graduated midwives from programs like the HOPE Midwifery Institute, with a 2023 study revealing newly graduated midwives felt competent managing most maternal health issues but recognized needs for ongoing training and mentorship;[16] and (3) trained community health workers and traditional birth attendants transformed through short-term training into sexual and reproductive health referral assistants.

A critical finding was that clinical resources severely constrained midwife effectiveness. Newly graduated midwives reported scarce clinical resources including blood products, medicines, vaccines, and ultrasound equipment, alongside challenges in managing major maternal health issues including adolescent pregnancy, malnutrition, anemia, sexual violence, pregnancy-induced hypertension, hemorrhage, and obstructed labor.[16] As one respondent stated, "Everything and we need a doctor," capturing the sense of resource constraint beyond midwife knowledge limitations.

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

Specific midwife-to-population ratios for Mae La were not documented in retrieved reports, but workforce organization data suggests higher absolute numbers of health workers per capita. Mae La's health staffing in 2018 included 3 medical doctors, 72 qualified nurses, and 64 community health workers serving 39,061 refugees, yielding a ratio of approximately 1 health worker per 240 population.[3] While this includes all health workers rather than midwives specifically, it represents substantially higher healthcare workforce density than Cox's Bazar.

The camp's integrated PHC approach emphasized training local health workers systematically, with the program developing extreme flexibility to adapt to rapid population changes and continuous staff turnover while maintaining quality care.[11] The stability of the Malteser International program over 30 years allowed accumulation of institutional knowledge and mentorship capacity unavailable in newer Cox's Bazar programs.

### Emergency Obstetric Care Availability Within 2-Hour Transport Radius

**Cox's Bazar Refugee Camps:**

Emergency obstetric and neonatal care availability in Cox's Bazar camps varied significantly by location and time period. As of April-June 2021, health sector facility monitoring revealed that only 57% of primary health centers provided all basic emergency obstetric and newborn care signal functions.[2] This indicates that fewer than 6 of every 10 health facilities had capacity to manage complications like eclampsia, retained placenta, sepsis, or breech delivery within the camps.

The camp-based obstetric care was delivered by MSF-operated primary health centers offering 24/7 services, with inpatient facilities for complications. However, women with severe complications requiring cesarean section had to be referred outside the camps. The Referral Hub emergency transportation system attempted to overcome transport barriers, but facility capacity limitations remained significant. The absence of blood banks within camps meant that women with severe postpartum hemorrhage could not receive transfusions at the initial level of care, forcing transfer to Bangladesh government hospitals or private facilities outside the camps.

For women in remote camp areas or those facing security restrictions on movement, the 2-hour transport radius criterion may not have been consistently met, particularly given difficult terrain, poor road conditions within camps, and occasional security incidents restricting movement. The referral transportation system operated at limited capacity, with shortage of ambulances documented as a barrier, meaning that delays often exceeded theoretical transport times.

**Mae La Karen Refugee Camp:**

Mae La camp's integration with Thai government health systems provided superior emergency obstetric care access. The camp could refer patients to Thai provincial hospitals within the 2-hour transport radius, with established referral protocols and formal agreements. The coordination included Tha Song Yang Hospital, Mae Ramat Hospital, Mae Tao Clinic (a comprehensive facility near the border), and the Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU).[13]

An average of 116 obstetric, neonatal, and infant care cases per year required secondary emergency care, approximately 1 case per 3,000 population, suggesting successful management of the majority of obstetric emergencies at camp level with systematic referral of truly complex cases.[5] The camp maintained low cesarean section rates (3.7%) suggesting either very successful primary prevention or potentially some underutilization of emergency obstetric care, though the former seems more likely given other outcome metrics.

The 2-hour transport radius to Thai hospitals was generally achievable for Mae La residents, though security restrictions and documentation barriers occasionally complicated transfers. A key finding in 2025 was that Thai hospitals agreed to provide treatment at reduced cost or free of charges for camp residents, formalizing refugee access to government hospital care.[13]

### Comparative Emergency Obstetric Care Access

The disparity in emergency obstetric care access between Cox's Bazar and Mae La represents a critical structural difference. Mae La residents had relatively secure access to Thai hospital obstetric care through established referral protocols and geographic proximity. Cox's Bazar residents faced more complex barriers: camp-based care lacked cesarean section capability, requiring transfer outside camps to either Bangladesh government hospitals or IRC-supported private facilities; transport systems operated at limited capacity; and the absence of blood banks made hemorrhage management dependent on external facilities.

The installation of a Maternal and Child Hospital in Cox's Bazar in January 2026, with 59 beds and capabilities for 24/7 emergency obstetric care including cesarean sections, represented a major structural shift directly addressing this gap.[17] However, during the 2017-2023 study period, this capacity did not exist, explaining much of the disparity in obstetric outcomes.

## Part 4: Cultural Factors in Maternal Healthcare Access and Traditional Birth Attendant Integration

### Rohingya Cultural Beliefs and Pregnancy Care Practices

**Traditional Birth Attendant Dominance and Preferences:**

Rohingya culture maintains strong preferences for traditional birth attendants (dai ma) over facility-based delivery. Research from 2020-2022 documented that nine of ten pregnant women interviewed planned to use the services of a dai ma (traditional birth attendant) for their upcoming births, while only one planned a hospital birth.[18] This preference persisted despite nearly universal facility availability and active health promotion campaigns, indicating deep cultural foundations for TBA preference.

Dai ma, who deliver babies on a regular basis ranging from up to 3 in a day to just a few per month depending on need, are experienced providers, often having delivered hundreds or thousands of babies both in Myanmar and Bangladesh.[18] Despite their extensive practical experience, most dai ma have minimal formal training, though many express desire for additional education to improve safe delivery practices. They combine international health recommendations—such as healthy diet, light exercise, and regular antenatal visits—with culturally specific beliefs such as avoiding nighttime outings due to fears of jinn (bad spirits) and other supernatural concerns.[18]

**Gender Roles and Healthcare Decision-Making:**

One hundred percent of maternal health study respondents identified the husband as the primary decision maker in obstetrical care.[18] Women must receive permission from husbands, in-laws, and parents to access healthcare, a patriarchal decision-making structure that constrains women's autonomous healthcare seeking. If any family members raise rumors or objections to facility birth, women lack the authority to override these concerns independently.

This decision-making structure creates particular vulnerability to misinformation. Rohingya communities hold several misconceptions about facility-based delivery perpetuated through family and community networks. Common false beliefs include fears that male doctors conduct deliveries (when many delivery services employ female providers), that newborns are harmed or killed at health facilities, and that hospital births necessitate unwanted cesarean sections.[18] While some of these rumors have declined over time, they persist in enough communities to influence behavior.

**Religious and Spiritual Dimensions:**

Rohingya Islam influences reproductive health perspectives, particularly regarding pregnancy termination. Pregnancy termination is generally viewed as impermissible in Islam, especially after the concept of 'ensoulment' at approximately three months gestation, though earlier termination is sometimes accepted.[19] Community stigmatizes women seeking pregnancy termination, labeling them with derogatory terms and associating the practice with sin. Religious leaders hold influential roles in shaping attitudes regarding reproductive health choices. Unmarried women face greater stigma and may avoid formal healthcare entirely, instead seeking care at medicine shops or informal providers to avoid community detection.

**Source of Pregnancy Information and Support:**

Most Rohingya women receive pregnancy information from family elders, dai ma, and community health volunteers rather than formal health providers, with strong preference for in-person, Rohingya-language communication.[18] Eight out of ten pregnant women felt they had access to the information they needed during pregnancy, predominantly receiving this through community health volunteers visiting homes. This pattern indicates that community-based, culturally-familiar information sources heavily influence health behaviors, even when facility-based providers offer consistent information.

**Maternal Healthcare Access Barriers and Outcomes:**

For many Rohingya women, reaching skilled maternal healthcare is significantly constrained by multiple barriers. Limited access to clean water creates health challenges during pregnancy, necessitating that families ration scarce water supplies. The cascade of health outcomes from one problem leading to another has particularly high stakes in pregnancy—anemia leading to hemorrhage susceptibility, infections leading to sepsis, hypertension leading to eclampsia, all of which are amplified by underlying nutritional and sanitary constraints.[1]

Restrictions on movement due to government-imposed camp confinement limited transportation to healthcare facilities independently. Cultural norms against nighttime female mobility created barriers for women in labor who preferred remaining in home settings with trusted dai ma present. Violence in camps became increasingly problematic over the study period, with women reporting they were "too scared to leave their homes, even for deliveries," even when facilities were available.[20]

### Karen Cultural Context and Reproductive Health

**Cultural Traditions and Family-Centered Care:**

Karen culture is maintained by strong traditions and cultural values transmitted predominantly through the maternal line, making mother-centered reproductive healthcare potentially more aligned with traditional systems than top-down facility delivery promotion.[21] Family relations play critical roles in transferring pregnancy care knowledge and providing moral and physical support during pregnancy, relationships particularly crucial in the unstable displaced context of camps.[21] Karen women are not passive recipients of healthcare but actors for change within their families and communities, though their ability to exercise agency is constrained by gender dynamics and camp restrictions.

Displacement from ongoing conflict, which forced many Karen to Mae La and other refugee camps, challenged yet allowed adaptive maintenance of cultural practices.[21] Despite security restrictions within camps, the camp remained interconnected with surrounding Thai society and global influences through NGOs and educational institutions, exposing residents to modern healthcare concepts while family structures maintained traditional knowledge transmission regarding reproductive care.

**Cultural Events and Identity Preservation:**

Cultural events and traditions, such as weaving and community ceremonies, remain crucial for Karen identity and social cohesion, though some cultural practices have declined in Mae La camps.[22] These cultural maintenance efforts, while seemingly removed from maternal health, actually reflect the mechanisms through which Karen communities preserve values and knowledge transfer systems that include reproductive and maternal care knowledge.

### Traditional Birth Attendant Integration and Behavioral Change

**TBA-Formal Healthcare System Integration Mechanisms:**

A 2011 systematic review evaluating how integrating traditional birth attendants with formal health systems could enhance skilled birth attendance identified multiple effective integration mechanisms.[23] These mechanisms included training and supervision of TBAs, collaboration skills training for health workers, inclusion of TBAs at health facilities, communication systems, and clear definition of roles. The greatest impact occurred when TBA integration was combined with complementary actions to overcome context-specific barriers to contact among skilled birth attendants, TBAs, and pregnant women, with successful approaches being context-specific rather than universally transferable.[23]

Traditional birth attendants were becoming trusted intermediaries between communities and formal healthcare systems, a role that could be leveraged strategically to increase facility utilization.[24] Effective TBA training needed integration into maternal and newborn health systems to link communities to formal health facilities rather than operating as parallel systems.[25]

**Community Partners International TBA Training Program in Cox's Bazar:**

A striking example of successful TBA engagement occurred through the Community Partners International program in Cox's Bazar. Since 2021, CPI, through its partner Green Hill, engaged Rohingya traditional birth attendants in Kutupalong Refugee Camp to promote safe facility-based childbirth.[26] Initially, TBAs like Sura were resistant to the concept that home births posed greater risks than facility births, a perspective rooted in their decades of experience managing uncomplicated deliveries at home. However, over time, as TBAs witnessed birth complications they could not manage at home, their perspectives shifted dramatically.

The pilot project trained TBAs to understand the dangers of home births and refer laboring women to health facilities, with results that exceeded expectations: facility-based deliveries increased from 33% in 2020 to over 95% in 2022 in the target area—a threefold increase in two years.[26] One TBA stated, "Before, many women delivered at home. I watched babies die that could have been saved. Now I encourage families to go to clinics, and we've seen lives saved every week."[26] Facilitators emphasized continuous engagement: "We persuaded them that saving lives should be the main priority. We reinforce these messages every month. Over time, they have started thinking differently about this and are motivated to support this new approach."[26]

However, maintaining progress required persistence: "Facility-based deliveries are a new thing for them. It takes time, but they are slowly accepting these new things."[26] This program demonstrated that behavioral change in TBAs was achievable when approached culturally respectfully, given them agency in decision-making, and allowing evidence from their own experience (witnessing preventable deaths) to drive practice change rather than imposing external mandates.

**WHO Collaboration with TBAs in Cox's Bazar:**

The World Health Organization promoted collaboration with traditional birth attendants in Cox's Bazar, with specific testimonies from TBAs demonstrating behavioral change and improved outcomes. The successful model involved TBAs working alongside health facilities rather than in competition with them, with clear roles distinguishing what TBAs managed (normal pregnancy and labor progression) versus what required facility referral (complications). TBA training focused on recognition of complications and timely referral rather than attempting to eliminate TBAs from the healthcare system.

**TBA Knowledge, Attitudes, and Practices Study:**

A qualitative study exploring TBA knowledge, attitudes, and practices regarding maternal and newborn healthcare in Bangladesh examined 11 TBAs with average age 60 and approximately 26 years of practice.[27] TBAs exhibited a mix of beneficial practices—including recommending exercise, hygiene, nutritious food, vaccinations, and clean delivery—alongside potentially harmful or unproven practices including use of traditional medicines, mustard oil massages on mothers and newborns, immediate newborn bathing, manual placenta removal, and inconsistent hand hygiene.

Some TBAs had received formal training from NGOs or hospitals, which correlated with better adherence to safe practices, but overall knowledge and practices varied widely.[27] TBAs demonstrated awareness of clean delivery importance but had variable adherence to hygienic practices. Some practices lacked evidence or posed potential harm, such as traditional medicine use and widespread mustard oil application. Many TBAs massaged mothers and newborns with mustard oil, a potentially harmful practice. Most TBAs reported referring patients to government hospitals for complications during delivery, though without systematic training on complication recognition, recognition may have been delayed.

Around half of interviewed TBAs had received formal training from NGOs or hospitals, which improved some practices, emphasizing the critical importance of training programs. TBAs reported satisfaction and feeling respected by communities after delivering babies, personal factors that motivate their continued practice and could be leveraged to increase adherence to safe practices if training programs acknowledged these psychological motivations.

### Bridging Programs and Cultural Mediation

**WHO Essential Newborn Care Training and Community Engagement:**

In Cox's Bazar, WHO-supported essential newborn care training programs at the Multidisciplinary Skills Lab trained over 300 health workers deployed across 45 health facilities since 2022.[1] These trainings equipped doctors, nurses, and midwives with skills in newborn resuscitation and emergency delivery management, visibly boosting provider confidence in emergency situations. One physician stated: "The first time I faced an emergency delivery, I felt helpless. A baby was not breathing, and I didn't know what to do. Today, after WHO's training, I can act quickly. That newborn is alive, and so is the mother."[1] This visible demonstration of provider competence could build community confidence in facility-based care if publicized culturally appropriately.

WHO additionally promoted the Maternal and Child Health Handbook, distributed to nearly 290,000 women to encourage antenatal care and early complication recognition.[1] Social Autopsy tools were used to identify behavioral and social delays contributing to over half of maternal deaths, informing targeted community awareness and referral system improvements.

**HOPE Midwifery Institute and Trust-Building:**

The HOPE Midwifery Institute and MedGlobal in Bangladesh provided high-quality maternal health care and midwifery training targeting displaced Rohingya refugee women and impoverished host communities in Cox's Bazar since 2013.[28] The HOPE Institute trained midwives to manage childbirth complications like eclampsia and postpartum hemorrhage while emphasizing compassionate care and trust-building with patients, many of whom had limited previous healthcare access or negative past experiences.

MedGlobal supported maternal health through clinics serving large numbers of women daily, providing education through Mother's Clubs, distributing neonatal kits, operating counseling hotlines, and ensuring free comprehensive care including emergency transport and specialist referrals.[28] The midwifery profession was being positioned as a respected and desirable career in Bangladesh, offering opportunities to support vulnerable populations while advancing professional development.

Midwife Mahabubara Belee Sikder, recounting management of severe eclampsia during a cyclone in Cox's Bazar, stated: "Both mother and baby survived," reflecting the life-saving potential of skilled midwifery care.[28] Vice principal of HOPE Midwifery Institute Sarmin Nesa emphasized the critical relationship-building component: "They are building trusting relationships," with patients unfamiliar with maternal healthcare.[28] The approach explicitly recognized that changing health-seeking behavior required first establishing trust through respectful, compassionate care provision.

**Language and Communication Barriers:**

A critical implementation gap involved communication between health providers and Rohingya communities. An exploratory report examining socio-linguistic barriers in humanitarian settings documented that many sensitive topics (sexual violence, mental health, menstruation) lack neutral or direct Rohingya language translations, complicating accurate communication and potentially harming humanitarian outcomes.[29] Many sensitive health terms carried euphemisms, indirect language helping navigate cultural taboos but potentially masking issues if interpreters lacked adequate training.

The report emphasized that "Communication is aid and words build trust," yet professional language interpretation services remained inconsistently available in Cox's Bazar healthcare settings. Gender profoundly affected language use and trust-building, with women often less able to freely express sensitive reproductive health topics to male interpreters, limiting disclosure. The complex relationship between case workers, interpreters, and survivors impacted both information disclosure and psychological safety.[29]

Professional language interpretation best practices, as outlined in the British Medical Association's Refugee and Asylum Seeker Patient Health Toolkit, recommended use of professional language interpreters to ensure safe and effective care, with consideration of patient preferences regarding interpreter gender and cultural background.[30] Consultations involving interpreters required longer appointment times to allow for accurate translation and comprehension. Healthcare organizations needed to educate patients and reception staff about interpreter availability, using multilingual posters and language identification charts.

**Implementation Gaps in Culturally Responsive Maternal Healthcare:**

Research investigating culturally responsive maternal healthcare for refugee and migrant women in New South Wales, Australia, identified significant implementation gaps despite strong policies emphasizing culturally responsive, woman-centered care including use of interpreters, individualized approaches, and cultural respect.[31] Providers reported structural and institutional barriers including insufficient resources, inadequate training, culturally inappropriate assessment tools, and fragmented services. These factors led to moral distress among providers, who felt unable to meet policy expectations and provide optimal care.

The study highlighted that healthcare organizations needed to move beyond tokenistic staff training toward system-wide reforms embedding culturally responsive practices, addressing structural racism, improving interpreter services, and enhancing coordination between mainstream and refugee-specific healthcare services.[31] "Without a 'systems approach' ... culturally responsive care will remain an unachievable vision and a mere 'on paper' aspiration."[31] This finding applied directly to Cox's Bazar, where policies promoted cultural responsiveness while implementation barriers limited actual culturally appropriate care delivery.

## Part 5: Impact of 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction on Maternal Health Services

### Cox's Bazar Rohingya Camps: Funding Crisis and Service Impacts

**Pre-2021 Funding Challenges:**

Funding challenges predated the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction. In 2019, funding gaps had already forced UNFPA to scale down family planning service sites and other programs in Cox's Bazar, despite the magnitude of unmet need.[15] Family planning services had shown promise earlier—UNFPA provided over 123,000 family planning consultations to women in 2019—but funding constraints limited scale-up of this critical service.

**2021 Funding Cuts and Service Reductions:**

The specific 2021 UNFPA funding reduction resulted in scaled-back maternal and reproductive health services across Cox's Bazar camps. While detailed quantitative data on service reductions was limited, the impact manifested through: delayed scale-up of priority programs including expanded family planning services, constraints on midwife training expansion despite documented need, and reduced community health worker supervision and support.

Critical infrastructure gaps that had been planned for improvement were deferred. Blood bank establishment, which had been repeatedly identified as essential for managing postpartum hemorrhage, remained absent throughout the 2017-2023 study period. Staff development programs were slowed, and equipment maintenance suffered. The integration of specialized neonatal care remained limited compared to Mae La's established Special Care Baby Unit.

**Maternal Mortality Trends Associated with Service Constraints:**

Despite increasing facility-based deliveries (which should have reduced maternal mortality), the maternal mortality trajectory suggests that service quality and supply constraints limited the protective effect of facility access. In 2021, obstetric hemorrhage accounted for 29.2% of 96 confirmed maternal deaths, and by 2022, hemorrhage accounted for 44% of 83 maternal deaths—an apparent increase in the proportion of deaths from this preventable cause.[6] This paradoxical trend of increasing hemorrhage deaths despite improving facility delivery rates suggests that service constraints (absent blood banks, medicine stock-outs, equipment limitations) prevented effective treatment of complications.

From January-December 2021, out of 253 women of reproductive age deaths notified, 96 were confirmed maternal deaths (37.94%), yielding an estimated maternal mortality rate of approximately 265 per 100,000 live births (using approximately 36,000 annual births in camps).[6] By 2022, 83 maternal deaths were reported, with similar mortality rates. Despite the slight decrease from 96 to 83 deaths (13% reduction), mortality remained persistently high, and the rising proportion from hemorrhage suggested that service gaps prevented management of preventable complications.

**Community Impact on Health Seeking Behavior:**

As funding constraints became apparent through service disruptions and reduced program activities, community confidence may have been affected. The Referral Hub emergency transportation system, dependent on continuous funding, faced operational constraints. Community health worker outreach and supervision declined. Health education campaigns were reduced. While these service reductions did not reverse the trend toward facility-based delivery (which continued increasing), they may have prevented further gains and reduced the quality of facility-based care received.

### Mae La Karen Refugee Camp: 2021-2023 Funding Situation

**Relative Stability During 2021-2023:**

Mae La camps experienced greater relative stability during the 2021-2023 period compared to Cox's Bazar, though facing underlying vulnerabilities. The camp's longer operational history (established 1984) and integration with Thai government systems provided some buffering against international funding fluctuations. Malteser International's program, supported by diverse international donors including the European Union, Global Fund, and various foundations, demonstrated more diversified funding sources than some Cox's Bazar programs.

However, the camps were not isolated from global humanitarian funding challenges. Between 2020 and 2023, funding pressures increased as competing crises (Syria, Afghanistan, Ukraine, Yemen) competed for limited donor resources. The camps experienced what one analysis described as "a survival crisis after drastic donor funding cuts to food support and health services."[32] Recreational and community-based services in Mae La were constrained, with funding cuts affecting sports programs and community activities.

**2025 Funding Crisis and Emergency Government Response:**

The acute 2025 funding crisis, when the US government terminated aid, revealed the underlying vulnerability of Mae La's healthcare system despite apparent stability during 2021-2023. International Rescue Committee healthcare services were suspended, forcing urgent intervention. The Tak Province Public Health Department responded by providing emergency monthly financial support of 2,500 Thai Baht each to 80 health workers serving Mae La, Mae Umpiem, and Nupo camps.[13] Collaboration involved Tha Song Yang Hospital, Mae Ramat Hospital, Mae Tao Clinic, and SMRU.

This emergency government response, while insufficient for full service restoration, demonstrated that Mae La's integration with Thai government systems provided alternative sources of support when international donors failed, an advantage that Cox's Bazar lacked due to Bangladesh's non-signatory status to the 1951 Refugee Convention and resistance to permanent health infrastructure in refugee settings.

### Broader Context: 2021-2025 UNFPA Funding Crisis

**2025 US Government Funding Terminations:**

The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction was followed by far more severe cuts in 2025. On February 28, 2025, the UNFPA Executive Director issued a statement on United States Government funding cuts, noting termination of all 48 funding agreements by the US Government, totaling approximately $377 million.[33] These funds had supported critical maternal health care, protection from violence, and essential services in over 25 crisis-affected countries. The termination primarily affected emergency settings and included 16 grants under humanitarian waivers.

Over four years preceding 2025, US funding had prevented more than 17,000 maternal deaths, 9 million unintended pregnancies, and nearly 3 million unsafe abortions, reaching over 13 million women and young people.[33] Despite some temporary restorations of funding to seven projects in early 2025, as of mid-April, 43 projects totaling $330 million remained affected. The termination of US funding, which had previously covered 60% of food and healthcare costs in border refugee camps, created immediate crises in multiple refugee settings including Cox's Bazar and Mae La.

**Service Disruptions from 2025 Funding Crisis:**

The 2025 funding crisis resulted in more severe disruptions than the 2021 UNFPA reduction. Forced closures of health centers and service suspensions occurred in both Cox's Bazar and Mae La camps. In Cox's Bazar, hospitals funded by international aid organizations closed temporarily, forcing pregnant women to seek care in government or private facilities outside camps or manage without assistance.[34] Community health initiatives including vaccination, sanitation, and maternal health programs relying on US funding were suspended, NGO staff remained unpaid, and refugee camp services ceased operating in some cases.[34]

In Mae La, IRC health services suspension occurred, threatening continued access to emergency obstetric care. Thai border hospitals in Tak province prepared for increased patient loads, with unified stances to provide emergency and humanitarian care without charging fees, demonstrating both the severity of service gaps and the community commitment to preventing healthcare collapse.[34]

**Estimated Impacts on Maternal and Reproductive Health:**

UNFPA's December 2025 humanitarian appeal for $1 billion to support maternal health and reproductive services in 41 crisis-affected countries highlighted the severe impact of 2025 funding cuts.[35] The funding cuts forced over 1,000 health facilities and mobile teams supported by UNFPA to shut down or face closure, along with closure of more than 250 safe spaces for women and girls. These disruptions risked millions of women and girls losing access to essential services including safe childbirth, emergency obstetric care, contraception, and clinical care for rape survivors, particularly in conflict and disaster zones.[35]

UNFPA estimated that in 2026, 7.7 million pregnant women would require humanitarian assistance, and without full funding, up to 12 million women and girls could lose access to critical health and protection services.[35] In Cox's Bazar specifically, if funding gaps were not filled, an estimated 315,000 women of reproductive age could lose access to antenatal, family planning, and safe delivery services in 2026.[2]

**Midwifery Support Reductions:**

On May 5, 2025, UNFPA warned about the severe impact of funding cuts on midwife support in crisis-affected countries.[36] Due to financial constraints, UNFPA would only be able to fund 47% of the 3,521 midwives initially planned for assistance in eight fragile countries in 2025, including Afghanistan, Venezuela, South Sudan, Lebanon, Syria, Haiti, Egypt, and Ukraine. Additional midwifery support reductions occurred in Chad, Nigeria, Madagascar, and Somalia due to US funding cuts.[36]

UNFPA's midwifery aid encompassed training, supplies, equipment, and transport, enabling midwives to operate even in remote and dangerous areas.[36] Since midwives deliver 90% of essential sexual, reproductive, maternal, and newborn health services in crises, loss of support threatened core healthcare provision. In Afghanistan alone, loss of support threatened skilled care access for approximately half a million women.[36] These funding constraints applied directly to both Cox's Bazar and Mae La camps, where midwifery capacity remained dependent on international funding and trained midwives required ongoing support to maintain competency and access to equipment.

### Temporal Patterns of Service Impact

**2017-2020: Scaling Up Phase:**

From 2017-2020, despite funding challenges, Cox's Bazar experienced a scaling-up phase with expanding services in response to the ongoing Rohingya crisis. Facility-based deliveries increased from 22% in 2018 toward higher percentages, antenatal care coverage was established as near-universal, and community health worker networks expanded. Mae La maintained steady-state services with integrated programming.

**2021: Funding Reduction and Early Impacts:**

The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction coincided with ongoing COVID-19 pandemic impacts and service constraints. Family planning services, already scaled down, received limited additional investment. Maternal mortality surveillance systems were strengthened (reflecting greater focus on monitoring rather than service expansion), and community-based referral systems evolved to address transportation gaps when ambulances were insufficient. Service quality was maintained in some domains while growth was constrained.

**2022-2023: Constrained Growth:**

By 2022-2023, facility-based deliveries in Cox's Bazar had increased to 82%, and caesarean section capabilities were being established (though not in camps themselves). Maternal mortality remained high (446 per 100,000 in 2024, down from the 2022 peak, but still preventable by facility standards). The trajectory showed continued improvement despite funding constraints, suggesting that programmatic innovations (TBA engagement, community referral systems, targeted training) partially compensated for resource limitations. However, underlying infrastructure gaps (absent blood banks, medicine stock-outs) prevented dramatic mortality reductions.

**2024-2025: Crisis and Reorganization:**

By 2024-2025, the acute 2025 funding crisis forced service reorganization. Cox's Bazar experienced temporary hospital closures and service suspensions, though the recent opening of the Maternal and Child Hospital with 59 beds and full obstetric capabilities in January 2026 represented the most significant structural advancement in camp maternal health capacity during the entire study period.[17] This facility would directly address the absence of cesarean section capability within camps that had constrained care throughout 2017-2023.

Mae La experienced IRC healthcare service suspension and emergency Thai government financial support, representing a shift from NGO-led to government-supported healthcare delivery. This transition, while preserving baseline services, created uncertainty regarding long-term sustainability and service quality.

## Part 6: Synthesis and Key Structural Differences Explaining Health Outcomes

### Structural Healthcare Delivery Model Differences

The comparison between Cox's Bazar and Mae La reveals that healthcare delivery model fundamentally shapes maternal health outcomes through multiple mechanisms. Cox's Bazar's NGO-operated, parallel healthcare system model provided rapid scalability and flexibility critical for responding to the acute 2017 refugee crisis, enabling quick establishment of maternal health services where none existed. The model's responsiveness allowed expansion from near-zero services to 80%+ facility-based delivery coverage in less than six years.

However, the parallel NGO model created structural vulnerabilities: fragmented service delivery across multiple organizations, vulnerability to individual donor funding fluctuations, absence of government integration limiting formal health system capacity, and limited access to referral hospitals for emergency obstetric care. Multiple agencies operating different protocols created coordination challenges, with quality and resource availability varying across facilities.

Mae La's government-integrated model, while requiring longer establishment timeframes, provided structural stability through formal Thai government involvement even without direct provision. The camp's location within Thailand enabled referral to Thai provincial hospitals with obstetric surgery capabilities, critical for managing complications beyond camp capacity. However, this model was equally vulnerable to international funding disruptions, as 2025 demonstrated, with the Thai government unable to sustain services without international support.

### Resource Allocation and Infrastructure Differences

Cox's Bazar camps operated with significantly constrained infrastructure compared to Mae La. Blood banks, essential for managing postpartum hemorrhage in settings with limited access to transfusion services elsewhere, remained absent throughout 2017-2023. Medicine stock-outs affected 45% of facilities as of 2021. Emergency obstetric care including cesarean section required external referral, adding delays and costs. Neonatal care was limited compared to Mae La's established Special Care Baby Unit.

Mae La camps, despite resource constraints of protracted refugee settings, maintained more comprehensive infrastructure: functional primary health centers with 24/7 capacity, established referral pathways to Thai hospitals, integrated water/sanitation systems meeting SPHERE standards, and dedicated neonatal care with documented success in reducing mortality by 51% over three years. The 2000-2018 cost analysis showed approximately €115 per birth in Mae La, with substantial expenditure devoted to emergency referrals, compared to limited cost data for Cox's Bazar suggesting generally lower resource allocation per birth.

### Midwifery Workforce and Capability Differences

Cox's Bazar midwife-to-population ratios of approximately 1:22,000-25,000 fell far below WHO recommendations of 1:5,000 in emergency settings, though workforce numbers were expanding through UNFPA and NGO training programs. Newly graduated midwives reported competence in managing most maternal health issues but acknowledged need for ongoing training, with major constraints in clinical resources including blood products, medicines, and equipment limiting their ability to implement learned skills.

Mae La's higher health worker-to-population ratio (approximately 1:240 for all health workers), combined with 30+ years of program stability, allowed for deeper mentorship relationships and accumulated institutional knowledge. Malteser International's long-standing program created systematic training pipelines and quality assurance mechanisms less present in Cox's Bazar's newer, rapidly scaled-up services.

### Emergency Obstetric Care Access Differences

Mae La's established referral pathway to Thai provincial hospitals provided access to cesarean section, blood transfusion, and critical care capabilities within approximately 2-hour transport radius. The camps could refer an average of 116 obstetric, neonatal, and infant care cases annually requiring secondary care, with established protocols and formal agreements enabling timely referral.

Cox's Bazar lacked equivalent referral pathways to facility-based cesarean section within camps during 2017-2023. Women requiring emergency cesarean section had to be transferred outside camps to Bangladesh government hospitals or private facilities, with no formalized referral system comparable to Mae La's. The absence of blood transfusion capability within camps meant that women with severe postpartum hemorrhage could not receive immediate transfusion at the initial facility level, substantially elevating mortality risk.

### Cultural Engagement and Behavioral Change Strategies

Both populations demonstrated strong cultural preferences for traditional birth attendants and home births, but the approaches to behavioral change differed. Cox's Bazar developed intensive community-based interventions including transformation of 80 traditional birth attendants into sexual and reproductive health referral assistants, achieving dramatic facility-based delivery increases from 33% to 95% in target areas through engagement rather than coercion. WHO collaboration with TBAs promoted practice improvement through evidence-based learning (witnessing preventable deaths) rather than external mandates.

Mae La's cultural engagement was less documented but reportedly incorporated Karen family structures and maternal knowledge transmission into program design. Family planning service uptake over 19 years (1996-2015) demonstrated that when barriers were reduced (through employment of a local-language-speaking surgeon for sterilizations, 2005-2007), uptake increased substantially, suggesting that cultural appropriateness and accessibility directly influenced behavior.

Both populations required sustained community engagement with transparent evidence of mortality reduction to support behavioral change. The finding that facility-based deliveries increased from 33% to 95% in a two-year period in Cox's Bazar with culturally-informed TBA engagement demonstrated that rapid behavioral change was achievable when approached respectfully and continuously reinforced.

### Funding Vulnerability and Service Sustainability

Both Cox's Bazar and Mae La revealed high vulnerability to international funding disruptions, contradicting assumptions that either model inherently provided sustainability. Cox's Bazar's multiple NGO funding sources provided some diversification but lacked government integration limiting sustainable government health system capacity. Mae La's government integration provided limited protection, as Thai government, facing its own budget constraints and non-signatory status to the 1951 Refugee Convention, could not sustain services without international support.

The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction and subsequent 2025 funding crisis demonstrated that protracted refugee health services remain systematically underfunded relative to need and that humanitarian funding remains subject to international political forces beyond refugee population control. Both settings experienced service degradation during funding crises, suggesting that neither model has achieved true sustainability.

## Conclusion

The comparison between Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar and Karen refugees in Mae La reveals that maternal mortality and prenatal care outcomes diverge not from single factors but from interconnected differences in healthcare delivery models, resource allocation, infrastructure investment, midwifery workforce capacity, emergency obstetric care accessibility, and culturally-informed health promotion strategies.

**Key Health Indicator Disparities:**

Mae La camps demonstrated superior baseline maternal health indicators: antenatal care coverage >90% versus Cox's Bazar's 71.6-80.8%, skilled birth attendance of 80-95% versus 59.5-69.4%, maternal mortality of 126.5 per 100,000 versus Cox's Bazar's 179-446 per 100,000, and neonatal mortality of 7.7 per 1,000 versus 27.0 per 1,000. However, Cox's Bazar demonstrated remarkable improvement trajectory from 2017-2023, with facility-based deliveries increasing from 22% to 82%, narrowing the gap substantially.

**Structural Advantages of Mae La:**

Mae La's integration with Thai government health systems, location within Thailand enabling referral to provincial hospitals, established neonatal care infrastructure with documented mortality reduction, and 30+ years of program continuity created structural advantages in healthcare provision. These advantages translated directly into measurable outcomes: lower baseline maternal and neonatal mortality, higher skilled birth attendance, and better emergency obstetric care access.

**Cox's Bazar's Programmatic Innovations:**

Cox's Bazar's NGO-operated model, while lacking Mae La's structural advantages, demonstrated substantial progress through targeted programmatic innovations including: intensive traditional birth attendant engagement achieving facility-based delivery increases from 33% to 95% in intervention areas; community-based referral transportation systems addressing access barriers; and expanded midwifery training and mentorship programs. These innovations partially compensated for infrastructure gaps and demonstrated that even in acutely challenging settings, behavioral change and service improvements were achievable with sustained, culturally-informed programming.

**2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction Impacts:**

The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction, and subsequent 2025 funding crisis, revealed vulnerabilities in both healthcare delivery models. Cox's Bazar experienced scaled-back service expansion, delayed infrastructure improvements including blood bank establishment, and constrained midwifery workforce expansion. Mae La experienced less documented 2021-2023 impacts but suffered service suspension in 2025, requiring emergency Thai government financial intervention. Both settings demonstrated that protracted refugee maternal health services remain highly dependent on unstable international funding, limiting sustainability regardless of delivery model.

**Synthesis of Explaining Factors:**

Maternal mortality and prenatal care differences between Cox's Bazar and Mae La are explained by:

1. **Healthcare Delivery Infrastructure:** Mae La's integration with Thai government systems enabling access to provincial hospital obstetric care versus Cox's Bazar's requirement for external referral, creating delays and complications in emergency situations.

2. **Critical Infrastructure Assets:** Mae La's availability of blood transfusion services, established neonatal intensive care, and comprehensive emergency obstetric care within accessible referral radius versus Cox's Bazar's absence of on-site cesarean section capability and blood bank infrastructure.

3. **Midwifery Workforce Capacity:** Mae La's higher health worker density and 30+ years of mentorship culture versus Cox's Bazar's lower midwife-to-population ratios and newer workforce requiring sustained training support.

4. **Funding Stability and Availability:** Mae La's diversified donor base and Thai government integration providing limited protection versus Cox's Bazar's greater vulnerability to individual donor funding fluctuations, though both ultimately dependent on unstable international support.
