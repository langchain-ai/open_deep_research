# Comprehensive Literature Review: The Anthropology of Gift Economies and Contemporary Digital Applications

## Introduction

Gift exchange has long served as a fundamental organizing principle of human societies, structuring social relationships, economic transactions, political alliances, and religious practices in ways that fundamentally differ from market-based commodity exchange. Since Marcel Mauss's seminal 1925 essay "The Gift," anthropological scholarship has grappled with understanding how gifts create obligations, establish reciprocal relationships, and constitute the social fabric that binds communities together. This comprehensive literature review examines classical anthropological frameworks of gift exchange—particularly those developed by Malinowski, Mauss, and Sahlins—while centering feminist critiques that expose the gendered labor obscured in traditional analyses. The review then extends this theoretical apparatus to contemporary digital contexts, analyzing how open-source software development, social media attention economies, and cryptocurrency governance structures both reinforce and fundamentally challenge classical models of gift versus commodity exchange.

The central argument emerging from this analysis is that contemporary digital gift economies operate according to principles of obligation and reciprocity identifiable within classical anthropological frameworks, yet they simultaneously introduce new dimensions that require theoretical modification. These dimensions include geographically dispersed, anonymous participation without kinship ties; algorithmic mediation of exchange; the extraction of unpaid affective and attention labor; gendered hierarchies in recognition and compensation; and the intimate entanglement of gift and commodity logic within profit-seeking corporate platforms. Feminist perspectives prove essential for illuminating how these contemporary systems perpetuate invisible labor, particularly from women and marginalized groups, while classical models often failed to account for such gendered dimensions of exchange.

## Part I: Classical Anthropological Frameworks of Gift Exchange

### Bronislaw Malinowski and the Kula Ring: Ceremonial Exchange as Social Bond

Bronislaw Malinowski's ethnographic research on the Kula ring among Trobriand Islanders, documented in his 1922 work "Argonauts of the Western Pacific," initiated modern anthropological study of gift exchange systems. The Kula ring represents a ceremonial exchange system in the Milne Bay Province of Papua New Guinea, where red shell-disc necklaces (veigun or soulava/bagi) circulate clockwise while white shell armbands (mwali) circulate counterclockwise among 18 island communities. Participants undertake dangerous ocean voyages spanning hundreds of miles to exchange these valuables, not for economic gain but to establish lifelong partnerships, reinforce peaceful relations, and enhance social status within their communities.

Malinowski's central ethnographic puzzle was why men would risk life and limb traveling across dangerous ocean expanses to give away what appear to be worthless trinkets. His answer fundamentally reframed how anthropologists understood exchange: the Kula operated according to distinct principles from market trade. These exchanges were "ceremonial and formal, facilitating peaceful relations and prestige" rather than generating economic profit. The valuables themselves possessed no practical utility; their value derived entirely from their role in establishing and maintaining social relationships. Each valuable received had to be reciprocated with an alternate type of similar prestige, creating a continuous ring of obligation that returned objects to their original owners or successors within a cycle spanning years or decades.

Critically, contemporary scholarship has challenged Malinowski's romanticized portrayal of the Kula as a timeless, closed system operating outside historical processes. Roger Keesing's essay "New Lessons from Old Shells: Changing Perspectives on the Kula" reveals that the Kula ring involves multiple types of valuables beyond shells, functions as both symbolic practice and convertible wealth linked to wider economic systems, and developed relatively recently—approximately 500 years ago—shaped significantly by precolonial warfare and colonial pacification rather than existing as an ancient, unchanging institution. The concept of "kitoma" (kitoum, kitomwa) demonstrates that valuables can be held free of obligation, contradicting Malinowski's view of perpetual circulation. Most significantly, research by Macintyre argues that "the kula as closed circuit is a modern institution" that required generations of peace to establish—a condition that only emerged through colonial pacification. This historical critique demonstrates how anthropology's typification process is situated in time and space, with cultures represented as dynamic systems embedded in material and ideological processes rather than timeless entities.

### Marcel Mauss's "The Gift": Obligation, Reciprocity, and the Spirit of the Gift

Marcel Mauss's 1925 essay "The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies" became foundational to anthropological and sociological theory, offering a systematic comparative analysis of gift exchange across diverse societies including the Trobriand Islanders, potlatch ceremonies among Northwest American tribes, and Polynesian and Melanesian cultures. Mauss posed the central theoretical question that animated his entire essay: "What force is there in the thing given which compels the recipient to make a return?" His answer revolutionized economic anthropology by arguing that gifts are never truly free; they embed social, moral, religious, and political obligations that bind individuals and collectives together in perpetual cycles of reciprocity.

Mauss identified three core obligations structuring gift exchange systems: the obligation to give, the obligation to receive, and the obligation to reciprocate. These obligations operate simultaneously as economic transactions and as moral imperatives. Crucially, Mauss emphasized that gifts create what he termed "total prestations"—exchanges that involve entire collectivities rather than isolated individuals. A gift between two persons represents an exchange between their families, clans, or tribes, embedding economic transactions within broader systems of kinship, political alliance, and spiritual obligation. This concept of total prestation distinguishes gift exchange fundamentally from commodity exchange, where impersonal transactions between individual buyers and sellers dominate.

Central to Mauss's analysis is the concept of "hau," drawn from Māori traditions, which represents the spirit or essence of the gift. This spiritual force compels reciprocation not through legal or economic calculation but through a quasi-magical compulsion. The gift, in Mauss's formulation, carries within it the essence of the giver and maintains an ongoing connection between giver and receiver even after the object passes into the recipient's possession. Gifts therefore create perpetual social debt—not in the sense of financial obligation but as enduring relationship and commitment. As Mauss observed, gifts exchanged under obligation contain an intrinsically magical power (mana) that compels repatriation, explaining why recipients feel compelled to return gifts without explicit contractual terms.

Mauss explicitly linked his anthropological analysis to contemporary political concerns. Writing in the aftermath of World War I, he positioned gift exchange systems as models for how modern societies might achieve peace and solidarity through principles of obligatory reciprocity. He wrote: "The clan, the tribe, and the peoples have learned—as tomorrow, in our so-called civilized world, classes and nations and individuals too will have to learn—how to confront one another without massacring each other, and to give to each other without sacrificing themselves to the other." This political intent reveals that Mauss's essay was not merely academic description but a deliberate intervention in post-war European politics, suggesting that modern social insurance and welfare systems echoed premodern gift-based reciprocity.

However, subsequent scholarship has contested Mauss's interpretation of hau and challenged aspects of his theoretical framework. The concept of hau itself has become subject to intense scholarly debate, with some arguing that Mauss misunderstood or misinterpreted Māori concepts, projecting Western spiritual assumptions onto indigenous knowledge systems. Maurice Godelier's work on hau provides alternative interpretations, while indigenous Māori scholars have offered critical perspectives on how Eurocentric anthropology has appropriated and reinterpreted the hau concept. Additionally, the universality of Mauss's three obligations has been questioned, with scholars noting that non-reciprocal gifts (such as Hindu dan, pure almsgiving) complicate his framework.

Jane Guyer's expanded 2024 edition of "The Gift" has restored the essay to its original historical context by including Mauss's memorial tributes to colleagues lost in World War I and his critical reviews of contemporaries like Malinowski and Boas. This contextualization reveals the political and intellectual environment shaping Mauss's thought, demonstrating how anthropological theory emerges from specific historical moments rather than existing as timeless wisdom. Guyer herself notes the essay has been "the most misunderstood work in the history of anthropology," with previous English translations removing crucial historical context.

### Marshall Sahlins and the Classification of Reciprocity

Marshall Sahlins's 1972 work "Stone Age Economics" provided a systematic classification of reciprocity that became enormously influential in economic anthropology. Sahlins identified three types of reciprocity based on the relationship between exchange parties: generalized reciprocity, balanced reciprocity, and negative reciprocity. This typology proves analytically useful not only for understanding tribal economies but also for examining contemporary digital exchange systems.

**Generalized Reciprocity** refers to exchanges where givers do not explicitly expect immediate or specific returns. These exchanges occur typically among close kin and intimate community members where "giving without expecting a specific thing in return" operates based on the "axiom of kinship amity"—direct economic calculation is inappropriate within intimate relationships. In generalized reciprocity, parties keep only loose records of exchanges, operating on the assumption that "each is 'there' for the other." The timing of return is indefinite, and the amount returned need not strictly equal the original gift. Generalized reciprocity sustains relationships based on trust and shared identity rather than transactional balance.

**Balanced Reciprocity** involves more precise exchanges with explicit expectation that something of roughly equal value will be returned within a defined timeframe. These exchanges characterize relationships among acquaintances and community members outside one's intimate circle. Unlike generalized reciprocity, balanced exchanges keep careful account of what was given and what is owed. The Kula ring, in Sahlins's analysis, exemplifies balanced reciprocity—while not instantaneous, exchanges follow predictable cycles with expected returns of equivalent prestige value. Balanced reciprocity establishes mutual obligation while maintaining some distance and formality in the relationship.

**Negative Reciprocity** represents attempts to maximize gain at minimal cost, typified by haggling, theft, or outright exploitation. These exchanges characterize relationships among strangers or adversaries where parties strive to get something for nothing. Negative reciprocity does not create social bonds but rather reflects distrust and antagonism. Nigerian email scams, gambling, and commercial haggling exemplify negative reciprocity in contemporary contexts—exchanges aimed at extracting value without reciprocal obligation.

Critically, Sahlins linked reciprocity types to kinship distance and social rank. The principle that "the degree of social distance—kinship in particular—affects the kind of reciprocity" became foundational to understanding economic organization in non-market societies. Close kin engage in generalized reciprocity, creating horizontal relationships of mutual support. Community members engage in balanced reciprocity, maintaining both cooperation and some competitive distinction. Strangers and outsiders engage in negative reciprocity, which may reflect genuine economic transactions or exploitative relations.

Sahlins embedded reciprocity within his broader framework of the "domestic mode of production"—the household or kinship group as the fundamental economic unit in pre-capitalist societies. He argued that kinship structures organize labor distribution, determine access to resources, and establish the social contexts within which different reciprocity types operate. This framework challenged formalist economics, which treated all economic behavior as rational individual maximization of utility regardless of cultural context.

Sahlins's "Original Affluent Society" thesis further complicated evolutionary narratives about economic development. He argued that Paleolithic hunter-gatherers lived not in conditions of scarcity and hardship but rather achieved affluence through what he termed "limited wants." Drawing on ethnographic studies of modern hunter-gatherers like the Bushmen and Australian Aborigines, Sahlins demonstrated that such societies often had sufficiency in material needs, high leisure time, and cultural systems valuing limited desires rather than continuous accumulation. This thesis challenged the assumption that gift economies represented economically inefficient alternatives to market systems—rather, they represented sustainable responses to environmental and social conditions.

However, Sahlins's classification system has faced significant criticism regarding its definitional clarity and universal applicability. A 2023 study by Guevara Beltran and colleagues reviewing 34 definitions of reciprocity across disciplines found that after decades of research, "there is still no consensus about the meaning of the term." The study revealed that experts disagreed substantially on which definitions and hypothetical scenarios exemplify true reciprocity, though they did reach near-unanimous agreement that "unconditional transfers lacking expectation of repayment should not be classified as reciprocity." The researchers proposed four alternative categories—Balanced Transfers, Reputation-based Transfers, Debt-based Transfers, and Unconditional Transfers—suggesting that Sahlins's three-part framework may not adequately capture all relevant dimensions of reciprocal exchange.

### Annette Weiner's Inalienable Possessions and the Paradox of Keeping-While-Giving

Annette Weiner's groundbreaking work, particularly her 1992 book "Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While Giving," fundamentally challenged both Malinowski's and Mauss's frameworks by introducing the concept that certain possessions maintain permanent connections to their owners even while circulating as gifts. Weiner conducted ethnographic research at Kwaibaga village in the Trobriand Islands, directly revisiting Malinowski's fieldwork site. Where Malinowski had emphasized male valuables circulating in the Kula ring, Weiner discovered that Trobriand women actively engaged in elaborate mortuary ceremonies distributing massive quantities of wealth items—bundles of dried banana leaves and woven fiber skirts. On her first day in the village, two young girls took Weiner to a mortuary ceremony where women distributed thirty thousand bundles in a single day, each valued at approximately one cent Australian.

Weiner's critical insight was that these women's wealth items constituted a distinct form of exchange that Malinowski had largely ignored. The paradox Weiner identified—keeping-while-giving—describes a mode of exchange where objects are given but remain symbolically and legally tied to their original owners. What makes a possession inalienable is "its exclusive and cumulative identity with a particular series of owners through time. Its history is authenticated by fictive or true genealogies, origin myths, sacred ancestors, and gods." In Trobriand mortuary exchanges, women distribute items that must eventually return to their matrilineage to maintain the status and identity of the kin group. "To give in this instant means to transfer without alienating... to cede the right of use without ceding actual ownership."

This framework directly challenged Mauss's emphasis on reciprocity as the primary dynamic in gift exchange. Weiner argued that "reciprocity is only the superficial aspect of exchange, which overlays much more politically powerful strategies of keeping-while-giving." The most politically significant exchanges, in her analysis, are not those involving reciprocal circulation but those involving strategic keeping. Women's control over inalienable possessions placed them at the heart of political processes: "The idea of keeping-while-giving places women at the heart of the political process, however much that process may vary in different societies, for women possess a wealth of their own that gives them power."

Power, Weiner demonstrated, is intimately involved in cultural reproduction. The degree of control over cloth wealth and other inalienable possessions coincided directly with women's rank and the development of hierarchy in the community. This insight revealed that gender and economic power were inextricably linked—understanding social hierarchies required understanding women's control over valuable resources and their roles in maintaining kinship identities across generations.

Maurice Godelier extended Weiner's framework, proposing the concept of "keeping-for-giving and giving-for-keeping" to emphasize the reciprocal nature of the paradox. Godelier argued: "Maintaining society thus requires not keeping-while-giving, but 'keeping-for-giving and giving-for-keeping.'" This formulation sophisticatedly captures how social reproduction depends on complementary but asymmetrical processes: certain actors (typically men) give to establish political authority and prestige, while others (typically women) keep inalienable possessions that anchor kinship identity and ensure the continuity of the group. Neither process alone sustains society; rather, the interaction between them produces social stability.

Weiner's work proved transformative for anthropology precisely because it recentered women's agency and wealth in systems previously analyzed through male-dominated frameworks. By demonstrating that women's wealth and women's exchange systems were fundamental to understanding Trobriand society, she provided a model for feminist reanalysis of other societies. The paradox of keeping-while-giving became applicable far beyond Oceania, with Weiner herself extending the analysis to Western history, suggesting that the concept illuminates inheritance systems, property law, and family dynamics across cultures.

### The Gift-Commodity Distinction and Its Theoretical Limitations

Christopher A. Gregory's work "Gifts and Commodities" offered a systematic analytical framework distinguishing gift and commodity exchange. Gregory argued that gifts involve inalienable goods and dependent actors (creating relationships of dependence and obligation), while commodities involve alienable goods and independent actors (creating impersonal transactional relationships). This distinction proved influential in economic anthropology, offering what appeared to be a clear categorical boundary between gift economies and market economies.

However, subsequent scholarship has complicated this dichotomy. David Graeber established that "no pure barter economy has ever been described ethnographically," challenging conventional economic evolutionary theories that assumed gift economies preceded barter, which preceded money and markets. Graeber distinguished "open" reciprocity (implying permanent mutual commitment) from "closed" reciprocity (resembling balanced, competitive exchanges), suggesting that Sahlins's three-part categorization may not adequately capture reciprocal dynamics.

More fundamentally, scholars including James Carrier and Susanne Cheal argued that the gift-commodity distinction itself reflects an ideological distinction rather than an empirical one. Western capitalist societies, they demonstrated, extensively engage in gift exchanges that manage emotional relationships and cannot be dismissed as residual or subordinate to market transactions. Japanese society, despite being clearly a capitalist commodity economy, sustains powerful gift exchange traditions that transform commodities into gifts to sustain intimate social worlds. The binary between gift and market economies proved inadequate for understanding actual economic practices.

Costas Lapavitsas, writing from a Marxist political economy perspective, argues that the distinction between commodity and gift is fundamentally misleading for analyzing capitalist societies. Commodities themselves, in his analysis, "give rise to a wealth of non-market relations such as trust, custom, power, and hierarchy," which are integral to capitalist economic activity. Trade credit among capitalist enterprises, for example, depends heavily on trust and moral obligation despite involving commodities. Many economic activities in capitalism assume commodity form without reflecting produced commodities or value substance, demonstrating the complex interplay between market and non-market factors. Lapavitsas concludes that understanding capital as "a set of social relations characterized by use value and exchange value" provides a richer framework than the gift-commodity dichotomy.

## Part II: Feminist Anthropological Critiques and Reframings

### The Development of Feminist Anthropology and Gender as Analytical Category

Feminist anthropology emerged in the 1970s as a direct response to the androcentrism that had characterized the discipline. The Association for Feminist Anthropology (AFA) was formally established in 1988 to promote politically engaged research across anthropology's subdisciplines. The field has progressed through distinct phases: the 1970s focused on salvage ethnography and documenting women's roles; the 1980s analyzed gender as a culturally constructed category distinct from biological sex, emphasizing comparative analysis of gender power and acknowledging intersectional identities; and from the 1990s onward, the field has emphasized diverse gender ideologies, systemic inequalities, and the interconnection between gender, race, class, and other dimensions of social organization.

A major breakthrough recognized that gender—the social meanings and expectations attached to sex categories—matters far more than sex (biological differences) for understanding how people behave and organize societies. As documented in the 2012 publication "Introduction: Tidemarks and Legacies of Feminist Anthropology," feminist anthropologists "established an anthropology of women, bringing women and gender to the forefront of ethnographic inquiry, challenging universal notions of womanhood and emphasizing gender as an analytic category tied to social inequality."

Key foundational scholars shaped the field's development. Sherry Ortner's "Is Female to Male as Nature is to Culture?" (1974) argued that women's universal devaluation is tied to associations of women with nature versus men with culture, applying structuralist and feminist analyses to show how "Pan-culturally women hold a secondary status in comparison to their male counterparts." Michelle Rosaldo and Louise Lamphere co-edited the foundational 1974 volume "Women, Culture, and Society," which highlighted the public/private sphere gender divide where men dominate public life and women remain confined to domestic roles, yet retain indirect political influence. Rosaldo argued that "When there is less distinction between the public and domestic spheres, women have the most power."

Eleanor Leacock connected gender inequality to capitalist class structures, arguing that male dominance emerges from "the monogamous family as an economic unit, at the heart of class society." She warned against capitalist co-optation of feminist rhetoric. June Nash produced superb ethnography foregrounding women's roles and challenging dominant theories by integrating gender and class analysis. Carol Stack developed a women-based theory of family structure and kinship in African American communities to write against the culture of poverty ideology. Later scholars including Ifi Amadiume documented gender fluidity in Igbo kinship systems, while Sylvia Yanagisako and Jane Collier analyzed how "social systems are, by definition, systems of inequality," with gender functioning as a key mechanism through which inequality is organized and maintained.

### Annette Weiner and the Recentering of Women's Wealth in Gift Economies

Annette Weiner's ethnographic work in the Trobriand Islands directly challenged Malinowski's male-centered analysis by demonstrating women's central role in exchange systems. Her 1976/1983 work "Women of Value, Men of Renown: New Perspectives in Trobriand Exchange" revealed that on her first full day in Kwaibaga village, two young girls took her to a mortuary ceremony where women exchanged bundles of dried banana leaves and fiber skirts—not unusual for women to distribute thirty thousand bundles in one day, each valued at about one cent Australian. These ceremonies revealed women as significant agents of economic exchange and social identity.

Weiner found Malinowski's writings profoundly lacking in descriptions of women's wealth and roles in mortuary rituals. The study emphasized different responses and relationships between men and women throughout the life cycle, including life events, death rituals, and wealth distribution. The work "makes an interesting comparison with the work of pioneer ethnographer Bronislaw Malinowski" by "revisiting and contrasting earlier ethnographic research conducted by Bronislaw Malinowski between 1915 and 1918, which primarily emphasized men's roles and often dismissed the economic and social importance of women's work."

Her 1992 work "Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While Giving" extended this analysis to argue that women possess wealth of their own that gives them power. The book explicitly challenged anthropology's "traditional assumptions about kinship, economics, power, and gender in an exciting challenge to accepted theories of reciprocity and marriage exchange." Weiner demonstrated that "Power is intimately involved in cultural reproduction," with the location of power in each society determined by control over the production and distribution of cloth wealth, which coincided with women's rank and the development of hierarchy in the community.

### Marilyn Strathern and Gender Relations in Melanesian Societies

Marilyn Strathern's 1988 work "The Gender of the Gift: Problems with Women and Problems with Society in Melanesia" offered a sophisticated critique of how both Western anthropologists and feminist scholars had made "unexamined assumptions" about gender relations. The work represents "the most original and ambitious synthesis yet undertaken in Melanesian scholarship," treating "with equal seriousness—and with equal good humor—the insights of Western social science, feminist politics, and ethnographic reporting, in order to rethink the representation of Melanesian social and cultural life."

Strathern's central argument critiques Western categories as inadequate for interpreting Melanesian cultures. She "proposes that Melanesian social life should be understood through relationships rather than conventional concepts of society or the autonomous individual." She criticizes Western categories—such as domination, individualism, and societal structure—as fundamentally misaligned with Melanesian concepts. Strathern emphasizes Melanesian concepts like "the divisible person, sociality, and gift exchange," arguing that these require "a new anthropological vocabulary and perspective."

The book explicitly critiques both Western anthropology and Western feminism for imposing inappropriate frameworks. Strathern states: "I have not authored 'a perspective' on Melanesian society and culture; I have hoped to show the difference that perspective makes... I have not presented Melanesian ideas but an analysis from the point of view of Western anthropological and feminist preoccupations." She argues: "The concept of society is theoretically obsolete" and "The relationship, rather than society as an entity or the individual as autonomous, is anthropology's subject."

Her key theoretical contributions include: "Melanesian 'persons' are multiple and divisible; sociality is the crux of social action, not individual autonomy" and "Feminism and anthropological scholarship endorse different approaches to the nature of the world open to investigation." By critiquing Western feminist assumptions as much as Western anthropological ones, Strathern models a more reflexive approach to cross-cultural analysis, acknowledging how the researcher's own cultural categories shape interpretation.

### Invisible Labor: Emotional Labor and Unpaid Work in Gift Economies

Feminist scholarship has illuminated how gift economies systematically obscure and devalue women's labor, particularly emotional and reproductive labor. The concept of emotional labor, coined by sociologist Arlie Hochschild, describes the behind-the-scenes emotional support work that women often perform—tasks such as listening to others' problems, managing social calendars, and providing care, often without recognition or compensation.

Research demonstrates that "Women – and other people that society labels 'feminine' – are socialized to provide a vast array of emotional services for other people (usually men), most often without acknowledgement or pay." The concept emphasizes that "Instead of labor, we're taught that the work we do to care for others is an act of love which must be given freely, even when it comes at the cost of our own well-being and self-expression." One scholar notes that "Emotional labor is therefore one of the last frontiers of feminism."

Quantitative research demonstrates the scale of unpaid labor. According to a 2018 United Nations Women study, "Women complete 2.6 times more unpaid domestic care or emotional work than men do." Furthermore, "Women reportedly undertake 2.6 times more unpaid domestic and emotional work than men," with the effects on mental health severe: "The odds of developing emotional distress increase by 22 percent in response to a ten-hour increase in unpaid work time" for women, while "there is no statistically significant relationship for men."

Research on unpaid work in the U.S. shows that "Total work time is positively related with emotional distress for women, while there is no statistically significant relationship for men. For women, the relationship between work time and mental health is primarily driven by unpaid work rather than paid work." This suggests that the unpaid voluntary nature of gift exchange labor, while celebrated in gift economy theory, has differential psychological and health impacts on women compared to men.

The concept of "wages for housework," articulated by feminist activists in the 1970s, directly challenged the assumption that reproductive and care work should be unpaid. As Silvia Federici argued: "To say that we want wages for housework is to expose the fact that housework is already money for capital, that capital has made and makes money out of our cooking, smiling, fucking." She further stated: "The Wages for Housework movement would not only expose women's hidden work in the home as labor, but would also begin to unite the working class... the state... should pay wages for housework." This framework challenges gift economy theory by arguing that the gift-framing of women's reproductive labor functions as a mechanism for capitalist extraction of unpaid value.

### Women's Wealth Systems and Female Exchange Networks

In the Trobriands, doba—banana leaf skirts and bundles—represent historically significant women's wealth central to demonstrating matrilineal clan strength. The manufacture of these items "is a task that defines Trobriand womanhood. To lack these skills is to be considered a lazy woman." Critically, "Doba continues to have economic value but, more importantly, it has social or symbolic value as representing a woman's work, knowledge, and ability to reproduce the matrilineage."

However, modernization pressures have transformed these systems. Since the mid-1980s, particularly through evangelical church influence, some villages have abandoned doba production in favor of cash, calico cloth, and store-bought goods, reflecting an ideological shift valuing productivity, individualism, and Christian modernity over traditional practices. "Evangelical churches have advocated that women should stop wasting their time making doba and instead focus on more 'useful' activities such as church fellowship and economic success." This reflects tensions where "Christianity is growing stronger than culture," demonstrating how women's traditional wealth systems face pressure from competing value systems.

Bride price systems, distinct from dowry, provide another example of how women's wealth and value are culturally determined. A bride price (also known as bride-dowry, bride-wealth, bride service, or bride token) involves the transfer of money, property, or wealth from a groom or his family to the bride or her family at the time of marriage. The practice is common in East Asia, Eastern Europe, the Middle East, parts of Africa, and Pacific Island societies. "A bride price is intended to demonstrate that the bride's family has agreed to the marriage and accept that any resulting children will be exclusively a part of the groom's lineage." In many cultures, the act is largely symbolic, intended to show respect for families' ethnic traditions and the woman's honor and value.

Bride prices have significant demographic implications. Studies in Indonesia and Zambia found that traditional bride price practices increased incentives for educating daughters significantly, indicating that women's economic value shapes investment in their development. However, contemporary bride price escalation in countries like China demonstrates how these systems can become exploitative under market pressure. Throughout most of the twenty-first century, the average bride cost in China was 10,000 to 20,000 yuan until 2007; by the mid-2020s, the average price started at 140,000 yuan—a dramatic increase partially driven by demographic imbalances created by the one-child policy, which caused a skewed male-to-female ratio, leading to skyrocketing bride prices often reaching multiples of annual male income.

### Feminist Perspectives on Gender and Power Dynamics in Gift Exchange

The fundamental feminist contribution to gift economy theory is shifting the central question from "what are gender roles in gift exchange?" to "how is inequality organized through gender in gift economies?" Feminist anthropology emphasizes that "differential power is constituted as gender" should be a central analytical concern. As the 2012 publication on feminist anthropology notes: "Feminist scholarship is that there is no single 'truth' in science – there are multiple truths that are, in part, the consequences of different experiences of and in the world by both the researcher and the researched."

Feminist scholars have successfully challenged male-dominated narratives in ethnography. Feminist anthropologists "called into question the supremacy of male interlocutors and worked to elevate the voices of female informants." This has resulted in revised understanding of major ethnographic sites, particularly the Trobriands and Melanesia, where "women's economic roles are now recognized as central rather than peripheral." Contemporary feminist anthropology includes approaches such as "Black feminist abolitionist anthropology committed to dismantling institutional racism and sexism, decolonial feminist archaeological fieldwork practices in Mexico, and the creation of the AFA Writes collective for feminist scholarly writing."

## Part III: Open-Source Software Development as Gift Economy

### Gift Exchange Principles in Open-Source Communities

Open-source software development communities function according to demonstrable gift exchange principles identifiable within classical anthropological frameworks. The foundational work in this domain is Matthias Studer's analysis in "The Commoner," which directly applies gift exchange theory developed by Marcel Mauss and the M.A.U.S.S. group to free software. Free software, pioneered by Richard Stallman with the GNU project in 1984 and expanded by Linus Torvalds' Linux kernel in the 1990s, challenges capitalist social relations by promoting cooperation and liberty rather than proprietary ownership.

Software constitutes a fundamentally distinct type of good compared to physical commodities: it is non-scarce, meaning it can be copied infinitely without depriving others of its use. This characteristic fundamentally alters exchange dynamics. The free software ethos centers on four key freedoms—to run, study, redistribute, and improve programs—often safeguarded legally by copyleft licenses like GPL. In open-source contexts, developers like Sebastian Bergmann, creator of phpUnit, give their work freely, creating a bond of gratitude and obligation with users. This bond extends through community interaction, such as contributing to projects, reporting bugs, and collaborating on code.

Studer emphasizes that development is motivated by "pleasure, creativity, and social recognition rather than capitalist profit motives." As he states: "Creation is a pleasure, to be given, not exchanged" and "Free software is the affirmation of four liberties: to run, study, redistribute, and improve the program." Furthermore, "Work among hackers is pleasure with a capital P, the pleasure that gives meaning to life." The hacker culture embodied in open-source development differs fundamentally from capitalist work ethics by valuing passion over utilitarian labor.

Mauss's three obligations (to give, to receive, and to reciprocate) apply directly to open-source contexts. Developers give code freely, users receive it, and reciprocation occurs through various non-monetary mechanisms including bug reports, code contributions, reputation enhancement, and community support. Yet the reciprocation is typically not immediate or precisely specified, aligning with generalized reciprocity dynamics. Modern platforms like GitHub facilitate these exchanges by accelerating collaboration and tie developers together in a philanthropic community. "By facilitating free collaboration so much, GitHub is a social bond maker."

Social capital—resources embedded within social structures and relationships that facilitate social interaction and exchange—arises from gift exchange mechanisms in open-source communities. Gift exchange is characterized by principles of giving, receiving, and reciprocating, where reciprocity is deferred and imprecise, creating social obligations and trust. Frequent gift or favor exchanges lead to positive emotions and uncertainty reduction, generating cohesion and commitment to exchange relations. Unlike legal contracts, social capital relies heavily on trust, mutual obligations, and repeated interactions, which are fostered through gift exchange. These exchanges are embedded in cultural rituals and signals, serving both instrumental and altruistic purposes. Through cycles of giving and reciprocating, social capital emerges and is maintained within communities.

However, gift exchange in open-source contexts faces significant tension regarding mismatched expectations. David Heinemeier Hansson, creator of Ruby on Rails and co-owner of Basecamp, discusses the nature of open source contributions as a "gift exchange" rather than a commercial transaction. He emphasizes that "open source work is primarily motivated by self-interest before benefiting others, making sustainability possible over decades." He distinguishes open source from commercial services: "The first recipients of these programming gifts are almost always myself and my company. It's an intentionally selfish drive first, then a broader benefit second."

Critically, Hansson identifies a fundamental contradiction in how many developers and users approach open-source: "To expect specific forms of reciprocity from acts of altruism is a contradiction in terms." Open-source exchanges involve vague terms causing mismatched expectations and resentment. Users sometimes mistakenly expect commercial-level customer service from open source maintainers, a phenomenon Hansson terms "vendoritis." He explains: "The terms of commercial reciprocity when it comes to open source are usually far more vague, and thus open to illusions of agreement." To align expectations, Hansson suggests viewing open source contributions as gifts given without strings attached: "If you're a recipient of a gift, one offered with no strings attached, you're not entitled to much beyond the freedom of choice of whether to accept it or not. And if you're the giver of a gift, you'd be foolish to expect specific reciprocity in return."

### Status Hierarchies and Reputation Mechanisms

Status and reputation function as alternative currency to monetary compensation within open-source communities. A developer's reputation level is determined by multiple factors: coding quality, deviation of commitment behavior, community experience, and community integration. The gift economy "pays in status, and status pays in many ways." Good work is noticed, admired, and rewarded by those who benefit from it.

Notable contributors like Richard Stallman, Theo de Raadt, and Eric Raymond illustrate how status translates into various forms of compensation beyond direct payment, including opportunities, donations, hardware, consulting roles, or eventual financial gains. Leadership in open source communities differs fundamentally from corporate leadership, emphasizing openness, consensus, and code contributions over titles and hierarchy. Leadership is earned through credibility, trust, and consistent, meaningful contributions. As stated in Linux Foundation guidance: "The only way to gain leadership is to earn the role within the community. And the only way to do that is to gain credibility and make contributions."

Leadership roles in open source range from technical (committers, maintainers) to governance (executive committees) and operational (project managers, community advocates), but differ fundamentally from corporate roles, promoting transparency and collaboration. Becoming a leader involves earning respect by contributing valuable code, participating actively in community roles, and understanding group dynamics, often supported by attending conferences and mentorship. Companies seeking influence must adapt: "Companies often go through a phase of thinking 'Oh, well, we're huge. Why can't we pound our fist on the table and just make the community do what we want?' They soon come to realize that tactic won't work." Instead, "In an open source community, you have people who work for different companies who may have lots of areas of shared purpose and shared outcome and some areas of contention and cross purposes."

Recognition functions as a valuable form of contribution itself. Maintainers and contributors operate within a system where recognition is essential. The intense emotional labor of open-source maintainers, especially solo maintainers, is significant—they are "stuck in a cycle of reviewing PRs, supporting community, navigating spam and angry messages, coding, project management, product marketing, and more." One major contributor to maintainer burnout is loneliness. When maintainers feel seen and heard, they're more likely to have positive interactions with the community, which can create a ripple effect, establishing a culture of recognition and support. "Recognition is a form of contribution too—one that can make all the difference in a maintainer's journey."

### Copyleft and the Structure of Obligatory Reciprocity

The free software ethos is safeguarded legally by copyleft licenses like GPL, which mandate that derivative works must also be shared under the same license terms. This creates a perpetual structure of obligatory reciprocity. This contrasts fundamentally with proprietary software's use of patents and secrecy to restrict collaboration.

A distinction exists between permissive licenses (such as MIT) and copyleft licenses. Permissive licenses allow free use, modification, and redistribution without the obligation to share derivative works, while copyleft licenses like GPL mandate such sharing. Permissive licenses have no restrictions: "You may copy, alter and share the source code and anything built with it as you please." While permissive licenses enable broader adoption, they raise questions about reciprocal obligation.

Software licenses function as carriers of gift meaning. A distinction exists: "A gift comes with no attachments. Software licenses add attachments; they allow you to keep your intellectual property." Licenses structure the social obligations embedded within software gifts, determining whether recipients must reciprocate by sharing derivatives or may use the software with minimal reciprocal obligation. Permissive licenses like MIT are likened to "non-toxic gifts" that promote wider use and potential future contributions, where "People may adapt and contribute back if they wish – or choose not to use it at all." However, some argue that "Permissive licenses create an ecosystem of freeloading corporations, but the alternative is likely worse, with less good tools available."

Python is cited as a successful example of permissive licensing fostering dominance and openness in data science: "Would Python be as dominant in data science today if it hadn't been given away, with no license headaches? I doubt it would." The licensing choice fundamentally shapes ecosystem development.

### How Open-Source Challenges and Reinforces Classical Models

Open-source communities reinforce classical anthropological models by demonstrating that gift exchange mechanisms persist in technologically advanced, capitalist societies. The parallels between gift economies and open source software reflect cultural histories of economic exchange. Zeitlyn suggests that sub-groups within the open source community function similarly to kinship lineages, where gift giving occurs without direct or immediate reciprocation, following the "axiom of kinship amity," where economic calculations are not appropriate. Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic capital" explains how people work to enhance their and their group's reputation.

However, open-source communities also fundamentally challenge classical models in crucial ways:

**Scale and Geographic Distribution:** Unlike traditional gift economies operating within bounded kinship groups, open-source communities operate at global scale with geographically distributed, largely anonymous participants. This challenges assumptions about the necessity of face-to-face interaction and kinship ties for gift exchange to function.

**Mediation Through Technology:** Open-source exchanges are mediated through technological platforms (GitHub, email lists, issue tracking systems) rather than embodied ritual performance. This raises questions about whether gift exchange can function effectively through disembodied, asynchronous communication.

**Distinction Between Sacred and Secular:** Mauss emphasized the spiritual essence (hau/mana) compelling reciprocation in archaic gift exchanges. Open-source gift exchange operates without explicit spiritual or religious framing, suggesting that reciprocal obligation can arise from secular social and professional motivations.

**Hybrid Gift-Market Economy:** Open-source communities do not operate in pure gift economy mode but rather blend aspects of gift and market economies. Organizations like the Open Source Development Lab support this ecosystem by providing resources to contributors, emphasizing a quid pro quo relationship rather than free giveaways. This hybrid model challenges pure gift economy frameworks.

### Gender Dynamics and Unpaid Labor in Open-Source

Women are significantly underrepresented in open-source software development. Research from the 2018 Stack Overflow Developer Survey shows that only 6.9% of respondents identified as female, with women even more underrepresented specifically in the open-source software (OSS) community than in software development generally. A 2006 study noted that women comprised only about 1.5% of OSS developers globally. The Digital Impact Alliance's 2018 report "Towards a More Gender-inclusive Open Source Community" identified this as a critical issue, involving 44 in-depth interviews across diverse geographic and socio-cultural contexts (India, Kenya, Zambia, Francophone Africa, Europe, and the US) with women contributors and stakeholders.

Barriers to women's participation include pervasive implicit bias, a culture of meritocracy perceived as gendered, feeling outnumbered and alienated, care and family responsibilities, lack of awareness and access, and insufficient mentorship and support structures. Research reveals that "pull requests by women are more accepted when their gender isn't identifiable, demonstrating structural bias." One woman even confessed to having a male profile on Stack Overflow to avoid facing bias. "Cultures that describe themselves as meritocracies tend to be male-dominated cultures that seem unfriendly to women." A 2010 study found that 72% of women surveyed felt outnumbered by men, though only 24% said this caused them to feel alienated; however, approximately 50% had witnessed discrimination or harassment.

Critically, a 2019 study comparing women who contribute to open-source versus those who do not found that "personal and demographic characteristics are remarkably similar," suggesting that the gender gap is not due to women lacking skills or confidence but rather reflecting structural, cultural, and institutional barriers. The study found that "perceived competence did not differ significantly between groups," indicating that "the gender gap in open-source is not due to women lacking skills or confidence but rather reflects structural, cultural, and institutional barriers."

Care and family responsibilities profoundly impact women's participation in open source. "Family and motherhood roles significantly impact women's participation in open source, especially in resource-poor environments." Women bear disproportionate unpaid work burdens, and open-source participation competes for scarce time and energy. Research on unpaid labor shows that for women, "The odds of developing emotional distress increase by 22 percent in response to a ten-hour increase in unpaid work time."

Gender bias also appears in OSS tools and infrastructure. GitHub and Stack Overflow, critical infrastructure for open-source work, have been found to "inadvertently perpetuate gender biases and newcomer barriers." The GenderMag method—using gendered personas embodying five cognitive facets statistically associated with gender differences—has revealed both gender inclusiveness issues and newcomer challenges in OSS tools.

Maintenance labor in open-source projects shows gendered patterns. While code contributions are visible and recognized, maintenance work—documentation, issue triaging, community support, and emotional labor—often goes unrecognized. These forms of invisible labor are disproportionately performed by women and marginalized groups. As research on maintainer burnout notes, "Maintainers, especially solo maintainers, are stuck in a cycle of reviewing PRs, supporting community, navigating spam and angry messages, coding, project management, product marketing, and more."

The DIAL report provides concrete recommendations for addressing gender barriers: "Organizations supporting women in coding should foster mentoring and vocational training; open source communities must implement codes of conduct and create inclusive peer networks; employers should ensure equitable pay and support; conferences should engage men in diversity efforts and provide women-focused sessions; governments and funders ought to require diversity audits and fund inclusion initiatives." Successful interventions include safe, women-only spaces like AkiraChix in Kenya and Grace Hopper conferences, which empower women through community and skill-building.

### Corporate Exploitation of Open-Source Labor

A critical dimension of open-source as gift economy is how capitalist corporations exploit unpaid labor without reciprocal obligation. Don Goodman-Wilson presents a sharp critique of how capitalist interests have exploited open-source's gift economy structures. Open source has been transformed "into a tool for maintaining a status quo that favors the already powerful." The open source movement, initially a grassroots effort for freedom, has been co-opted by "moneyed and powerful interests."

A central problem Goodman-Wilson identifies is that "Open source as marketed by the OSI is literally about exploiting unpaid labor." Companies disproportionately benefit from unpaid labor by maintainers without fair reciprocity. The principle of software freedom has led to a situation "where we openly tolerate unethical use of open source software as a necessary cost for the freedoms of developers."

Goodman-Wilson cites specific examples of open-source software being used for unethical purposes without reciprocation. Open source software has been employed by organizations like ICE (Immigration and Customs Enforcement) in ways contributing to human rights violations: "ICE has been repeatedly accused of violating immigrants' human rights, exposing detainees to COVID-19 through neglect, and illegally violating the privacy of American citizens." Open-source tools have enabled creation of unethical applications: "Avatarify is an open source software tool that allows the user to impersonate other people live in a Zoom video conference, given only a photograph of them."

Many companies rely on open-source software as critical infrastructure for their products while maintaining asymmetrical relationships with open-source communities. Organizations often approach open-source with an extractive mentality: "If you've got an open source project that is super critical to your product and you've only got one person from your company making upstream contributions, you have a single point of failure." Yet many corporations fail to invest resources in open-source communities, extracting value without reciprocal investment.

Goodman-Wilson advocates for shift toward a "post-open source" world that centers ethics and human relationships rather than unrestricted freedom and availability. He suggests adopting "just licenses" that restrict software use based on ethical standards: "Licenses like the Hippocratic License place conditions on access to software licensed with them, requiring that licensees not engage in human rights violations." Foundations like Code for America provide models for fostering ethical, impact-driven software development. These models represent attempts to rebalance the gift economy by adding ethical obligations to recipients.

## Part IV: Social Media Attention Economies and Digital Labor

### The Attention Economy: Foundational Concepts

The attention economy is an economic concept that views human attention as a scarce and valuable resource. Theorized by economist Herbert A. Simon in 1971, it emphasizes that in an information-rich world, attention becomes a limiting factor due to information overload. "Attention is a resource—a person has only so much of it," and in information-rich contexts, "the wealth of information means a dearth of something else: a scarcity of whatever it is that information consumes."

The digital attention economy monetizes user attention primarily through advertising, allowing services to be offered free or at reduced cost. While legacy media have long used such models, digital platforms, particularly social media, have intensified attention capture via technical features like infinite scrolling and algorithmic content curation, supported by network effects that increase user and advertiser engagement. In France alone, online advertising revenue totaled approximately €9 billion in 2023, with induced sales approximating €32 billion annually, alongside potential productivity gains from digital tools.

However, substantial negative externalities exist. The attention economy contributes to reduced productivity, poorer mental health, and most critically, a decline in cognitive abilities among children exposed to intensive digital media. Estimates suggest this cognitive decline could reduce French GDP by 1.4 to 2.3 percentage points by 2060. Mental health implications include increased depression, anxiety, and stress linked to high smartphone use, potentially costing €5 billion annually. Productivity losses from work interruptions related to digital platforms are estimated at about €10 billion (0.4% of GDP).

### The "Like Economy" and Reciprocal Currency

Social media platforms have developed their own currencies for exchange, distinct from traditional money but functioning according to gift economy principles. The "Like economy" describes the intersection of gift exchange and market economy operating daily online. Likes, follows, shares, and comments function as reciprocal currency within social media platforms, where posting content, receiving responses, and reciprocating engagement establish social bonds and status hierarchies.

Romele and Marta Severo's article "The Economy of the Digital Gift: From Socialism to Sociality Online" critiques common interpretations of online gift exchange as an alternative to the market economy, arguing that the internet no longer serves as a counter to capitalism due to its renewed territorialization. They redefine "gift" in anthropological terms, emphasizing mutual recognition rather than goods exchange, and illustrate this through Facebook's "Like" button and Twitter's "Mention" feature. The concept is that "Gift exchange has nothing to do with goods exchange, but with mutual recognition," highlighting the "importance of gift circulation through Facebook's 'Like' button and the Twitter feature called 'Mention.'"

Mark Zuckerberg's initial vision of Facebook explicitly invoked gift economy language. He stated: "A more transparent world creates a better-governed world and a fairer world" and "The whole culture works on this framework of mutual giving." However, critical analysis reveals important caveats. "One of the anthro 101 points about potlatch is that it demonstrates the way giving is an aggressive, differentiating act, not an agape-filled solidarity-making one." Facebook is "somehow more insidious" because "many users don't view FB as a business, but rather as some kind of neutral organization." Critical observers note: "Gifts (as gestures on Facebook) are given new life via the dissipation of intention, calculation, anticipation of return" while simultaneously "Gift economies can be just as agonistic and exploitative, deceptive and crafty, as economies governed by market imperatives."

### Parasocial Relationships and Emotional Labor

Social media has fundamentally transformed the nature of relationships between content creators and audiences through mechanisms of parasocial relationships—one-directional relationships where fans feel emotionally closer to creators than the relationship actually warrants. "Comfort media has emerged as a way for individuals to navigate an oversaturated and often negative media environment." "Comfort creators are people who provide relief amid an online environment full of AI slop, mis/disinformation, the overconsumption of influencer culture, and more." Through instilling comfort in audiences, comfort creators "cultivate parasocial relationships with their followers, which means their fans feel closer to them than they actually are."

However, this dynamic leads to severe consequences for content creators. The study "Comfort Creators: Gender, Parasocial Relationships, and Emotional Labor" reveals that the study leads to "increased emotional labor and burnout for content creators, who become unable to meet their audiences' constant demands for comfort." The research identifies seven triggers of negative engagement: misinformation/disinformation, content quality, conflict between words and actions, violation of social norms, relationships, paid collaborations, and physical appearance/mannerisms. Ethical and value-related issues prominently surfaced in negative engagement.

Emotional labor in platform contexts is particularly intense. Research from MedieKultur examining emotional labour experienced by professional gamers, influencers, and gig and crowd workers within the context of digital work platforms, drawing on 29 qualitative interviews with young Nordic platform workers aged 18-30, explores how the affordances of platforms—specifically their capacity to transcend physical space and the immediacy and multiplicity of information distribution—shape and intensify emotional labour demands.

Emotional labour is conceptualized as "the regulation and management of feelings to meet the expectations of customers and followers, often leading to exhaustion and complex boundary negotiations between private and professional spheres." Examples include streamers who must simultaneously engage in gameplay and emotionally connect with followers in live chat, online workers who exert extra politeness and maintain relationships to secure future gigs, and influencers who navigate fan intimacy and harassment.

Direct quotes from platform workers reveal the strain: "It takes a lot of me. You can have a bad day, but that will not work [on the stream]. Bad days aren't a possibility. You have to stay alert." (Rasmus, professional gamer). Another worker states: "You try to choose your words carefully and have a 'nice tone of voice'. You think more than once when it is a digital team. Everybody knows that it requires extra effort to collaborate." (Vera, digital marketing consultant). An influencer expressed: "I reject it if somebody requests friendships on Facebook or Snapchat. They should not be able to follow my life. I want the professional and the private to be kept apart." (Karen, babysitter on a gig platform).

Platform features such as rating systems, real-time feedback, and digital communication broaden the scope and intensity of emotional labour, often requiring workers to constantly monitor and adjust their emotions to sustain income and social capital. The study highlights how platform affordances co-produce emotional labour, affecting workers' mental health, identity management, and work-life boundaries across diverse types of digital platform-mediated work.

### Influencer Labor and Economic Precarity

Influencer labor represents a distinct form of digital work characterized by significant precarity, emotional demands, and gendered dimensions. Momblogging exemplifies post-Fordist manifestation of domestic labor that foregrounds affective labor and articulates the discursive tension between brand identity and commodity value. The article "We Connect With People Through Stories: Gender and Affective Labor in Momblogging" examines the commercialization of social media influencer labor, revealing tensions between brand identities and commodity values.

Influencers face constant anxiety about revenue streams and their ability to maintain longevity on social media platforms. Most people don't start out with the goal of becoming rich and famous; rather, "Influencers increasingly drive the entire advertising infrastructure of the internet." Angèle Christin, an associate professor at Stanford University, conducted extensive research into the influencer economy through over one hundred interviews, participation in an influencer agency internship, quantitative income analysis, and personal content creation. She challenges common media stereotypes that often depict influencers—particularly young, white females—as vapid or trivial, emphasizing that "influencers include people of all genders, races, and ages, and are central to a booming economy that now drives internet advertising more effectively than traditional banner ads."

Despite their economic impact, influencers often face financial instability and anxiety over revenue and platform algorithm changes. Content creation is shaped by economic pressures: "reliance on brand partnerships fosters 'safe' content that may exclude socially or politically charged themes, with white male influencers generally earning more." Conversely, influencers monetizing via merchandise and fan support might adopt more extreme or niche positions to meet audience expectations, reinforcing certain gender and racial norms.

A doctoral dissertation by Nesli Urhan Güneş, submitted to Middle East Technical University in August 2025, critically examines the precarious nature of digital labour experienced by Instagram influencers in Türkiye. Grounded in Christian Fuchs' theory of digital labour and Brooke Erin Duffy's concept of aspirational labour, the study explores how influencer work is "simultaneously economically exploitative and emotionally demanding, with distinct gendered and generational implications." The study found that "despite the allure of creative autonomy, flexible work, and digital visibility, influencers frequently face unstable income, opaque algorithms, emotional strain, and lack institutional protections."

The dissertation emphasizes that "Influencer work is both economically exploitative and affectively demanding, with significant gendered and generational dimensions." The "apparent autonomy and fame of influencer work masks deeper forms of exploitation, unpaid labour, and dependency on platform infrastructures." The study highlights the pressing need for regulatory frameworks that formally recognize and protect platform-based labour.

### Algorithms and Platform Governance

Algorithmic governance reshapes gift exchange dynamics in social media contexts. Algorithms are understood not as purely technical systems but as "sociotechnical systems involving continuous human cultural decisions." The design of recommender systems embeds ideas about user psychology akin to animal traps guiding behavior. These systems "are embedded with human cultural decisions," illustrated through examples such as music recommender systems and Facebook newsfeeds.

The datafication of everyday life, particularly affecting children and families, highlights ethical issues around surveillance, bias, and social justice. Datafication has reorganized everyday life to collect and analyze personal data extensively, often coercively. Current AI governance emphasizing transparency and accountability is insufficient; a social justice–oriented ethnographic approach is necessary to address real-world impacts on marginalized groups. Anthropology's rich history studying bureaucracy, structural violence, and cultural knowledge offers essential insights into algorithmic violence and AI ethics.

Platform governance at the margins reveals how algorithmic (in)visibility disproportionately affects marginalized creators. The article "Platform governance at the margins: Social media creators' experiences with algorithmic (in)visibility" explores how marginalized social media creators perceive and experience algorithmic visibility and invisibility across platforms like TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook, and Twitch. Drawing from in-depth interviews with 30 creators from historically marginalized identities and stigmatized content genres, the study reveals that platforms enforce governance unevenly through formal (content moderation, account suspensions) and informal (shadowbans, algorithmic biases) mechanisms.

Creators commonly experience frustration and fear due to ambiguous, inconsistent, and often discriminatory moderation, leading to practices such as self-censorship, strategic circumvention, and experimentation with content. The findings highlight "perceived racial, gender, and sexuality-based biases embedded in platform algorithms and moderation systems, which disproportionately disadvantage marginalized creators while privileging normative, mainstream voices." Direct quotes from creators reveal these dynamics: "TikTok has been shadowbanning Black creators and anyone that mentions Black Lives Matter"; "Algorithms are built by people, and tech is still largely cis white male and they code their biases into the code"; "If you're a male, and you're white, and you're straight, and you're cis, you can basically do anything on TikTok and get away with it"; and "It feels kind of like gambling... Maybe one of your videos goes viral... but if they shadowban you right after you go viral, it just makes you want it more."

### The Political Economy of Attention and Cognitive Autonomy

The article "The Political Economy of Attention: Capitalist Extraction of Human Time" explores how late digital capitalism has transformed human attention into a key commodity, effectively monetizing everyday cognitive activities such as scrolling, liking, and watching on digital platforms. Drawing on Marxist theory, particularly surplus value and alienation, the paper posits that "late capitalism is no longer centered on factories, machinery, and wage labour alone. It has entered a new regime where attention—the most intimate resource of human consciousness—has become the critical site of extraction."

The metaphors used describe this extraction vividly: "The scrolling thumb becomes the new conveyor belt, continuously generating the data that fuels algorithmic accumulation." "Time which once belonged to life itself has become a commodity." "Emotional reaction—outrage, desire, envy, admiration—each becomes economic input." Case studies from India's rapidly expanding platform economy illustrate how leisure time and emotional states have become domains of capitalist extraction, with over 7.7 million gig workers and 820 million internet users contributing to data-driven profits usually captured by global tech corporations, exacerbating neo-colonial dynamics.

The article discusses the erosion of boundaries between labour and life, the role of algorithmic surveillance capitalism, and the emotional commodification of feelings to keep users engaged. It identifies "a new form of alienation where individuals lose control over their own consciousness and mental time." The challenges of fragmented digital labour, the risks of cognitive fatigue, and the urgency for political and legal frameworks to recognize users as data labourers with rights are emphasized.

The article frames the struggle to reclaim attention as pivotal to autonomy, democracy, and humanity's future, making cognitive liberation "a central class struggle in the age of digital capitalism." With India representing one of the world's largest attention markets, the conditions are unprecedented: "People are labouring every moment they are awake—without wage, without recognition. This is the proletariat of attention." Critically, "When algorithms dictate what we must notice, we lose the power to shape our own consciousness," and therefore "Reclaiming attention becomes resistance against domination." The ultimate statement captures the stakes: "The battle of our era is not for wages alone but for the right to own our minds. To protect attention is to protect the human soul."

### How Attention Economies Challenge Classical Gift Models

The attention economy fundamentally challenges classical gift economy theory in several ways:

**Anonymity and Scale:** Unlike Malinowski's Kula ring operating within bounded communities with face-to-face relationships, social media platforms connect billions of anonymous users. This challenges the assumption that gift exchange requires personal relationships and direct social connection.

**Algorithmic Mediation:** Platforms use algorithmic systems to determine who sees what content, fundamentally reshaping reciprocal dynamics. Likes, follows, and comments are mediated by algorithms rather than emerging from direct social choice, introducing opacity into gift exchange.

**Commodity Entanglement:** Social media operates as a hybrid system where gift-like interactions (sharing content, receiving likes) simultaneously generate data extracted for corporate profit. This entanglement of gift and commodity logic challenges the classical distinction.

**Affective Labor Extraction:** The emotional labor required to maintain parasocial relationships is unpaid and often invisible, yet generates enormous corporate value. This resembles more the gendered labor dynamics feminist anthropologists identified in traditional gift economies than pure gift exchange.

**Attention as Commodity:** Rather than exchanging physical goods or establishing social bonds, social media platforms commodify user attention itself. This represents a new form of exchange that classical models did not anticipate.

## Part V: Cryptocurrency, Blockchain, and Decentralized Economic Systems

### Blockchain as Decentralized Gift Economy Infrastructure

Cryptocurrency and blockchain technology have been imagined as alternatives to traditional capitalist economic organization, with some theorists and developers explicitly framing blockchain systems as gift economy experiments. Joseph Poon, a blockchain scalability researcher and co-author of the Lightning Network and Plasma, discusses how blockchain technology alters economic and social dynamics toward gift-based distribution.

Poon argues: "The threat of forking a blockchain project is a catalyst for cooperation, which means there are parallels between blockchain and open source development dynamics." A blockchain operates as "a ledger stored in a network of decentralized nodes, and all recorded transactions are transparent to each member of the network." This transparency contrasts with traditional banking and financial systems. Cryptocurrencies emerged as "a means of payment that does not depend on the authority of the central bank, courts or police—where the algorithm itself guarantees the execution and the reliability of transactions."

The techno-anarchist/libertarian genealogy of blockchain brought revolutionary expectations: "the days of the state and the central bank would be over as the 'neutral' authority of algorithms takes precedence." However, the practical development of blockchain systems has involved experiments with gift-like distribution mechanisms. Poon explains that "the future could involve distributing coins and value of the blockchain widely to developers, users, and wider humanity, ideally as gifts without expectation of return."

Critically, Poon identifies an emerging game in blockchain economics: "There's an emerging game evolving in the blockchain space via mechanisms such as airdrops and faucets, whereby it is in your own self-interest to distribute out more value given the competitive dynamics." This creates an incentive structure where "it is in your own self-interest to distribute out more value, as much as possible, given the competitive dynamics of other projects." The logic inverts traditional capitalist accumulation: to build a successful blockchain ecosystem, one must distribute tokens widely as gifts, creating a "vibrant ecosystem of participants who are exchanging value with each other."

Poon emphasizes that "The ultimate potential is to remove the need for a lot of centralized authorities and disintermediate the notion of the corporation itself." However, he acknowledges that practical implementation faces complexity: "When you attach a bounty system, there's an inherent complexity... it looks a lot like a company." The tension between distributed gift-based systems and organizational structure that reproduces capitalist hierarchies remains.

### Mutual Credit Systems: Alternative Cryptocurrency Models

Paul d'Aoust explores an alternative concept of cryptocurrency known as "mutual credit," rooted in anthropological understanding of how humans have organized economies. The standard narrative of money evolution goes from barter to metal coins to bank-issued credit, but anthropology reveals human societies first used IOUs (promises) as currency, with metal money emerging later mainly to settle unrepayable debts. This historical sequence challenges conventional economic narratives.

Mutual credit systems operate by exchanging promises recorded through double-entry bookkeeping, where each participant's ledger reflects credits and debits, resulting in a net money supply of zero but a circulating money supply equal to outstanding promises. This system avoids the pitfalls of centralized money creation and interest charges found in traditional banking by distributing obligations across the economy and enabling money supply to "breathe"—expanding and contracting with economic activity.

D'Aoust explains: "When it's designed well, mutual credit can help match the money supply to the economy. It 'breathes', expanding and contracting with the creation and settlement of promises as goods and services are exchanged." A key distinction is that "banks lend out their money at interest, and I've never seen a mutual credit system that does that. Interest seems to create a vicious cycle of wealth drainage." The system avoids centralized control while enabling economy-wide coordination through distributed mutual obligation.

D'Aoust highlights mutual credit as a bridge between current economic models and more regenerative, community-driven forms, recommending technologies like Holochain for building distributed mutual credit applications. However, the article warns about risks like inflation if promises are unbacked, noting that design measures such as credit limits can mitigate these. This represents an attempt to institutionalize gift-like exchange principles—mutual obligation without centralized authority—through technological means.

### Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) and Governance

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) represent blockchain-based organizations operating through smart contracts and community voting, designed to manage voting and financial processes without centralized control. A DAO is a "software system for organization of other computer programs handled through a decentralized ledger technology like a blockchain." The concept originated with the 2016 launch of The DAO, a system built to manage an Ethereum-based venture capital fund that amassed 3.6 million ether, then worth more than US$70 million.

DAOs are collaborative, blockchain-enabled organizations managed via smart contracts and community voting, with challenges including technical complexity, centralized influences despite decentralization goals, voting limitations, and engagement difficulties. A framework emphasizes five dimensions: sustainable decentralization, decision accountability, centralization risk, voting limitation, and community engagement. The framework suggests applying theories such as Agency, Stewardship, Sociomateriality, and Institutional Analysis and Development to inform DAO governance design.

As of April 2023, the top three DAOs by governance token market capitalization are Uniswap Foundation (USD 3.4 billion), Lido DAO (USD 2.1 billion), and Ape Foundation (USD 1.5 billion). Utah's Decentralized Autonomous Organizations Act, effective 2024, allows DAOs to be treated equivalently to domestic limited liability companies, beginning to establish legal recognition for these organizational forms.

DAOs strive to be virtual, transparent, democratic, and autonomous, with decision-making logged on the blockchain to ensure accountability and transparency. However, challenges persist. Governance tokens, which entitle holders to DAO membership and voting power proportional to tokens held, create potential for token concentration leading to centralized power. The concentration of power is "crucial for the long-term health of DAOs given known risks
