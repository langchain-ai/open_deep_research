# Independent Directors Under NASDAQ Listing Standards: Comprehensive Revised and Deepened Research Report

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive, technically precise, and thoroughly revised analysis of independent director requirements under NASDAQ listing standards. It addresses all areas identified in the research brief, including definitional criteria, disqualification provisions, exemption scenarios, phased compliance timelines, cross-referenced SEC regulatory standards, and recent regulatory developments. The analysis is based exclusively on primary sources, including the official NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook (Rule 5600 Series), SEC releases approving NASDAQ rule changes (including SR-NASDAQ-2019-049 and SR-NASDAQ-2024-019), SEC Rule 10A-3, SEC staff interpretation letters, and authoritative legal practice guides. All rule numbers, thresholds, time periods, and exceptions are explicitly enumerated with precise rule citations.

## Section 1: Eligibility Criteria — The Core Definition Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 1.1 The Two-Part Test for Independence

Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2), an "Independent Director" is defined through a two-part test that requires satisfaction of both an objective component and a subjective component (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook, Rule 5600 Series [1]):

**Part A — The Subjective Affirmative Determination by the Board:**
"Independent Director" means a person other than an Executive Officer or employee of the Company **or any other individual having a relationship which, in the opinion of the Company's board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment** in carrying out the responsibilities of a director.

**IM-5605** elaborates: "The board has a responsibility to make an affirmative determination that no such relationships exist through the application of Rule 5605(a)(2)" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

**Part B — The Objective Bright-Line Criteria:**
Rule 5605(a)(2) continues by listing paragraphs (A) through (G), which set forth per se disqualifications. These are objective measures that "provide transparency to investors and Companies, facilitate uniform application of the rules, and ease administration" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

### 1.2 Board Process Requirements

NASDAQ expects a robust board process for making independence determinations, typically including (SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]; Latham & Watkins [3]):
- Annual director and officer questionnaires
- A relationship inventory
- A committee overlay review (to assess additional criteria for audit, compensation, and nominating committees)
- A board resolution reflecting the affirmative determinations

**Critical Note:** The bright-line tests are not determinative. A director who meets all bright-line criteria is not automatically presumed independent — the board must still make an affirmative determination that no material relationship exists (SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]).

### 1.3 Factors Considered in the Materiality Determination

In assessing whether a "material relationship" exists, the board should consider all relevant facts and circumstances, including (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]):
- **Stock ownership:** Ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board finding of independence. "Because Nasdaq does not believe that ownership of Company stock by itself would preclude a board finding of independence, it is not included in the aforementioned objective factors" (IM-5605 [1]).
- **Family relationships:** Any familial relationship that could create a sense of obligation or loyalty must be evaluated
- **Business relationships:** Direct or indirect business relationships between the director (or an affiliate of the director) and the company or its affiliates
- **Professional relationships:** Service as legal counsel, consultant, or other professional service provider
- **Charitable relationships:** Significant charitable contributions by the company to an organization with which the director is affiliated
- **Past relationships:** The board considers both current and past relationships through the lookback periods codified in the bright-line rules

### 1.4 Scope of "Company" and "Executive Officer"

Per IM-5605 (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]):
- **"Company"** includes any parent or subsidiary of the Company. "The term 'parent or subsidiary' is intended to cover entities the Company controls and consolidates with the Company's financial statements as filed with the Commission (but not if the Company reflects such entity solely as an investment in its financial statements)."
- **"Executive Officer"** means those officers covered in Rule 16a-1(f) under the Act.

### 1.5 Definition of "Family Member" (Post-2020 Amendment)

Per Rule 5605(a)(2), as amended effective February 13, 2020 (SR-NASDAQ-2019-049) (SEC Release 34-88210 [4]; Cooley PubCo [5]; Federal Register September 19, 2019 [6]):

"Family Member" means a person's **spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers and fathers-in-law, sons and daughters-in-law, brothers and sisters-in-law, and anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home.**

The complete enumerated list of all relationships constituting a "Family Member" is:
- Spouse
- Parents
- Children (excluding stepchildren — see 2020 Amendment below)
- Siblings
- Mothers and fathers-in-law
- Sons and daughters-in-law
- Brothers and sisters-in-law
- Anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home

**The 2020 Amendment (SR-NASDAQ-2019-049, effective February 13, 2020):** The amendment made two key changes to the prior definition (which included "a person's spouse, parents, children and siblings, whether by blood, marriage or adoption, or anyone residing in such person's home"):

- **Change 1 — Stepchildren Excluded:** The new definition excludes stepchildren from the per se Family Member definition. Nasdaq's rationale: "Nasdaq believes that a director's relationship with their stepchildren may or may not interfere with the director's exercise of independent judgment based on the particular facts and circumstances of the situation." However, if a stepchild shares the director's home, they are still captured under the "anyone who shares such person's home" prong (SEC Release 34-88210 [4]; Cooley PubCo [5]).
- **Change 2 — Domestic Employees Excluded:** The new definition excludes domestic employees who share a director's home. Nasdaq's rationale: "Nasdaq believes that the definition of a Family Member should not include a domestic employee who shares a director's home because this definition is intended to capture familial, not commercial, relationships" (SEC Release 34-88210 [4]).

The amended definition is now "identical to NYSE's definition of an immediate family member" (Cooley PubCo [5]).

**Critical Interaction with SEC Rule 10A-3:** The amendment filing explicitly states: "the proposed rule change to Listing Rule 5605(a)(2) will not affect the additional independence criteria for audit committee members set forth in Listing Rule 5605(c)(2)" (SEC Release 34-88210 [4]). This means that for audit committee independence determinations under Rule 10A-3, the SEC's own family member definition continues to apply separately and independently.

## Section 2: Disqualification Criteria — The Complete Bright-Line Disqualifications Under Rule 5605(a)(2)

The following persons shall **not** be considered independent under Rule 5605(a)(2), paragraphs (A) through (G) (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]):

### 2.1 Employment Relationships — Paragraph (A)

Rule Text: "(A) a director who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the Company"

**Lookback Period Mechanism:** This is a **fixed, retrospective three-year period measured backward from the date of the board's independence determination.** It is not a rolling period but a backward-looking static period (Latham & Watkins [3]; IPO GO Nasdaq-NYSE Comparison [7]).

IM-5605 clarifies: "The three year look-back periods referenced in paragraphs (A), (C), (E) and (F) of the Rule commence on the date the relationship ceases. For example, a director employed by the Company is not independent until three years after such employment terminates" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

**Exception — Interim Executive Officer Employment:** IM-5605 provides: "For purposes of paragraph (A) of the Rule, employment by a director as an Executive Officer on an interim basis shall not disqualify that director from being considered independent following such employment, provided the interim employment did not last longer than one year. A director would not be considered independent while serving as an interim officer" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

### 2.2 Compensation Threshold — Paragraph (B)

Rule Text: "(B) a director who accepted or who has a Family Member who accepted any compensation from the Company in excess of $120,000 during any period of twelve consecutive months within the three years preceding the determination of independence, other than the following:
- (i) compensation for board or board committee service;
- (ii) compensation paid to a Family Member who is an employee (other than an Executive Officer) of the Company; or
- (iii) benefits under a tax-qualified retirement plan, or non-discretionary compensation.
Provided, however, that in addition to the requirements contained in this paragraph (B), audit committee members are also subject to additional, more stringent requirements under Rule 5605(c)(2)" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

**Exact Testing Mechanism for "Any 12 Consecutive Months":** This is a **"rolling window" test** — the most stringent interpretation of a lookback. The board must examine **all possible overlapping 12-month periods** within the three years preceding the determination date (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; SEC Release 34-71037 [8]).

- **Example:** A director receives $50,000 in consulting fees in June 2023 and another $80,000 consulting fee in May 2024. The 12-month period from June 2023 to May 2024 includes $130,000 in compensation, exceeding the $120,000 threshold, disqualifying the director.

**What IS Considered Indirect Compensation:** "Political contributions to the campaign of a director or a Family Member of the director would be considered indirect compensation under paragraph (B)" (IM-5605 [1]).

**Additional Excluded Payments (Non-Compensatory):** Per IM-5605, the following are not considered disqualifying compensation:
- Non-preferential payments made in the ordinary course of providing business services (such as payments of interest or proceeds related to banking services or loans by a Company that is a financial institution, or payment of claims on a policy by a Company that is an insurance company)
- Payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities
- Loans permitted under Section 13(k) of the Act

### 2.3 Family Member of Executive Officer — Paragraph (C)

Rule Text: "(C) a director who is a Family Member of an individual who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the Company as an Executive Officer"

**Lookback Period Mechanism:** Fixed three-year retrospective lookback measured from the determination date, operating the same way as paragraph (A) (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

### 2.4 Business Relationships — Paragraph (D)

Rule Text: "(D) a director who is, or has a Family Member who is, a partner in, or a controlling Shareholder or an Executive Officer of, any organization to which the Company made, or from which the Company received, payments for property or services in the current or any of the past three fiscal years that exceed 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues for that year, or **$200,000, whichever is more**, other than the following:
- (i) payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities; or
- (ii) payments under non-discretionary charitable contribution matching programs" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

**Two-Threshold Test Mechanism:** This is a two-threshold alternative test:
1. **Threshold 1:** Payments exceed **5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues** for the recipient's most recently completed fiscal year
2. **Threshold 2:** Payments exceed **$200,000** in absolute dollars

If **either** threshold is met, the director is disqualified (Director Independence Questionnaire [9]; IPO GO Comparison [7]).

**Lookback Period Mechanism:** Unlike the rolling periods in (A) and (B), this is a **fixed-period fiscal year lookback**. The rule evaluates payments in the "current or any of the past three fiscal years" — a completed fiscal year test, not a rolling 36-month lookback.

**What Qualifies as "Recipient's Consolidated Gross Revenues":** Measured for the recipient's most recently completed fiscal year, this is the total revenue of the recipient organization as reported in its financial statements on a consolidated basis. For private organizations without published financial statements, the board must make reasonable inquiry.

**Treatment of Charitable Organization Relationships:** "Under paragraph (D), a director who is, or who has a Family Member who is, an Executive Officer of a charitable organization may not be considered independent if the Company makes payments to the charity in excess of the greater of 5% of the charity's revenues or $200,000" (IM-5605 [1]). However, per NASDAQ Staff Interpretation Letters (Nasdaq Listing Center [10]), charitable contributions are treated differently from commercial payments — the board has more discretion to find independence despite exceeding the thresholds, provided the board documents its reasoning.

### 2.5 Interlocking Directorates / Compensation Committee Interlocks — Paragraph (E)

Rule Text: "(E) a director of the Company who is, or has a Family Member who is, employed as an Executive Officer of another entity where at any time during the past three years any of the Executive Officers of the Company serve on the compensation committee of such other entity"

**Lookback Period Mechanism:** Three years, commencing on the date the interlocking relationship ceases (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

**Explanation of the Mechanism:** This is the "cross-compensation committee" disqualification (the "interlocking directorate" rule for compensation committees). It prevents a "you-scratch-my-back-I'll-scratch-yours" arrangement where executives of two companies sit on each other's compensation committees and set each other's pay (Latham & Watkins [3]; IPO GO Comparison [7]).

### 2.6 Auditor Affiliation — Paragraph (F)

Rule Text: "(F) a director who is, or has a Family Member who is, a current partner of the Company's outside auditor, or was a partner or employee of the Company's outside auditor who worked on the Company's audit at any time during any of the past three years"

**Lookback Period Mechanism:**
- **Current partners:** Always disqualified (no lookback needed for current status)
- **Former partners or employees who worked on the audit:** Three years from cessation of the audit work

**Key Distinction:** This disqualification applies only if the director **personally worked on the company's audit**. A partner of the outside auditor who worked on other clients would not be automatically disqualified under this subsection (though the board must still consider whether the relationship impairs independence under the general standard).

### 2.7 Investment Company Special Rule — Paragraph (G)

Rule Text: "(G) in the case of an investment company, in lieu of paragraphs (A)-(F), a director who is an 'interested person' of the Company as defined in Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act of 1940, other than in his or her capacity as a member of the board of directors or any board committee"

IM-5605 confirms: "Paragraph (G) of the Rule provides a different measurement for independence for investment companies in order to harmonize with the Investment Company Act of 1940" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

### 2.8 Audit Committee-Specific Additional Disqualifications — Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)

Under Rule 5605(c)(2)(A), each audit committee member must meet heightened requirements beyond the general independence criteria (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; SEC Rule 10A-3 [11]; Latham & Watkins [3]):
- (i) be an Independent Director as defined under Rule 5605(a)(2);
- (ii) meet the criteria for independence set forth in **Rule 10A-3(b)(1) under the Act** (subject to exemptions in Rule 10A-3(c)) — meaning they must not accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee from the Company other than for board service, and must not be an affiliated person of the Company;
- (iii) **not have participated in the preparation of the financial statements** of the Company or any current subsidiary of the Company at any time during the past three years; and
- (iv) **be able to read and understand fundamental financial statements**, including a Company's balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement.

Additionally, each Company must have at least one member who has "past employment experience in finance or accounting, requisite professional certification in accounting, or any other comparable experience or background which results in the individual's financial sophistication, including being or having been a chief executive officer, chief financial officer or other senior officer with financial oversight responsibilities" (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]).

### 2.9 Exceptional and Limited Circumstances Exception for Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)(2)(B)

One director who is not independent may be appointed to the audit committee if (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]):
- Not currently an Executive Officer or employee or a Family Member of an Executive Officer;
- The board determines membership is required by the best interests of the Company and its Shareholders;
- Disclosure is made under Item 407(d)(2) of Regulation S-K;
- The member serves no longer than two years and may not chair the committee.

## Section 3: Operational Mechanics of Lookback Periods

### 3.1 Summary Table of All Lookback Periods and Their Mechanics

| Disqualification Type | Rule Citation | Lookback Period | Type of Period | Key Calculation Point |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Employment (A)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(A) | 3 years | Fixed retrospective period from termination date | 3 years forward from termination of employment |
| **Compensation (B)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(B) | Any 12 consecutive months within the 3 years preceding determination | Rolling 3-year window, testing all possible 12-consecutive-month periods | Date of independence determination |
| **Family Member of Executive Officer (C)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(C) | 3 years | Fixed retrospective period from cessation date | 3 years forward from cessation of family member's employment |
| **Business Relationships (D)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(D) | Current or any of the past 3 fiscal years | Fixed fiscal year periods (completed fiscal years) | Current FY + 3 preceding completed FYs |
| **Compensation Committee Interlocks (E)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(E) | At any time during the past 3 years | Fixed period from cessation date | 3 years forward from when interlock ceased |
| **Auditor Affiliation (F)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(F) | At any time during any of the past 3 years | Current: always disqualified; Former: 3 years from cessation | 3 years forward from when audit work ceased |
| **Financial Statement Preparation (audit committee only)** | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(iii) | At any time during the past 3 years | Rolling 3-year lookback from determination date | Date of independence determination |

### 3.2 How Lookback Periods Interact With Each Other

Based on IM-5605 (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]), the lookback periods operate **independently** but can run **concurrently**. A person may have multiple disqualifying relationships simultaneously, and each lookback period is measured independently.

**Critical Operational Rule:** A director **cannot be deemed independent until ALL lookback periods have expired for ALL disqualifying relationships.** The longest period must elapse before independence is possible.

**Example:** If a director was employed by the company until June 1, 2023, AND received a $150,000 consulting fee in December 2022, the director cannot be deemed independent until:
- **June 1, 2026** (3 years from last employment date), AND
- **December 2025** (3 years from the last 12-month period in which compensation exceeded $120,000 — the 12-month period ending December 2022 would have included the $150,000 fee)

The **longest** period (June 2026) must elapse before independence is possible.

## Section 4: Exemption and Transition Scenarios — Controlled Companies

### 4.1 Definition and Threshold

Under **NASDAQ Rule 5615(c)(1)**, a "Controlled Company" is defined as **"a Company of which more than 50% of the voting power for the election of directors is held by an individual, a group or another company"** (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

**Critical Distinction — Voting Power vs. Share Ownership:** The rule measures **voting power for the election of directors**, not share ownership. Under dual-class stock structures, a holder of super-voting shares could control more than 50% of voting power while holding far fewer than 50% of equity shares. Conversely, someone with >50% of shares carrying no votes would not qualify.

**What Constitutes a "Group":** The term follows the SEC concept under Section 13(d)(3) of the Exchange Act. A group consists of two or more persons acting together through voting agreements, written or oral understandings, pooling arrangements, or concerted actions to elect directors. The group is treated as a single "person" for the 50% threshold.

### 4.2 Available Exemptions — What Controlled Companies CAN Exempt

Under Rule 5615(c)(2), a Controlled Company is **exempt** from:
- **Rule 5605(b)** — Majority independent board requirement (can have zero independent directors if desired)
- **Rule 5605(d)** — Independent compensation committee requirements (full board can handle compensation decisions)
- **Rule 5605(e)** — Independent nominating committee requirements (controlling stockholder can directly determine nominations)

**Important Clarification on Compensation Committee:** If a controlled company relies on the controlled company exemption for compensation committees, it does **NOT** need to have a compensation committee at all. The full board can handle executive compensation decisions. There is **no residual requirement** that the compensation committee have two independent members. SEC Rule 10C-1 (the federal compensation committee independence standard) also exempts controlled companies (Duane Morris [14]; SEC Final Rule 33-9330).

### 4.3 What CANNOT Be Exempted (Mandatory Compliance Items)

The following requirements apply to **ALL** Nasdaq-listed companies, including controlled companies. There is **no exemption available**:

| Requirement | Rule Reference | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit Committee Independence** | Rule 5605(c) AND SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1) | Controlled companies must have an audit committee of at least three fully independent members meeting both 5605(c) criteria and 10A-3(b)(1) standards |
| **Executive Sessions of Independent Directors** | Rule 5605(b)(2) | Exemption language specifically preserves this requirement (but moot if zero independent directors) |
| **Code of Conduct** | Rule 5610 | Must adopt code of conduct applicable to all directors, officers, and employees |
| **Related Party Transaction Review** | Rule 5630 | Must have appropriate review by audit committee or independent body |
| **Notification of Noncompliance** | Rule 5625 | Must promptly notify Nasdaq upon discovery of noncompliance |
| **Annual Meeting and Quorum** | Rule 5620 | Must hold annual meeting and provide for quorum of at least 33 1/3% |
| **Shareholder Approval Requirements** | Rule 5635 | Required for equity compensation plans, issuances exceeding 20%, and change of control |
| **Voting Rights** | Rule 5640 | Prohibition on disparately reducing voting rights of existing shareholders |

### 4.4 Disclosure Obligations

A controlled company relying on the exemption must disclose (NASDAQ Listing Center FAQ [15]; Perkins Coie [16]):
1. **Its status as a controlled company** — that it is relying on the controlled company exemption
2. **The basis for that status** — that more than 50% of voting power is held by a specified individual, group, or company
3. **A description of the specific exemptions being relied upon**

The disclosure must appear in:
- **Proxy Statement** for the annual meeting (in the corporate governance section)
- **Annual Report** (Form 10-K for domestic companies)
- FPI: Annual report on Form 20-F or Form 40-F

### 4.5 Transition Schedule When Company Ceases to Be a Controlled Company

Under Rule 5615(c)(3) (renumbered as Rule 5615(b)(7) by 2024 Amendments):

**General Rule:** When a company ceases to be a controlled company, it has **one year (12 months) from the date it ceases to be controlled** to comply with previously exempted requirements (Winston & Strawn [13]; Latham & Watkins [3]).

| Requirement | Phase-In Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)(1))** | 12 months from date of cessation |
| **Compensation Committee Independence (Rule 5605(d))** | 12 months from date of cessation |
| **Nominating Committee Independence (Rule 5605(e))** | 12 months from date of cessation |
| **Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c) + Rule 10A-3)** | **Already in compliance** — never exempted |
| **Executive Sessions (Rule 5605(b)(2))** | **Already in compliance** — never exempted |

**Impact of 2024 Amendments on Phase-In:** If the company demonstrates compliance during the phase-in period but falls out before the phase-in expires, it will not be considered deficient until the end of the phase-in period. However, if the company never achieved compliance during the phase-in period, **no cure period is available** after expiration, and Nasdaq will issue a Staff Delisting Determination (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

## Section 5: Exemption and Transition Scenarios — Foreign Private Issuers

### 5.1 Definition of Foreign Private Issuer

Under **17 CFR § 240.3b-4** (SEC Rule 3b-4), a "foreign private issuer" is any foreign issuer other than a foreign government, except for an issuer meeting two conditions as of the last business day of its most recently completed second fiscal quarter:
- **(1)** More than 50% of the issuer's outstanding voting securities are directly or indirectly held of record by residents of the United States; **AND**
- **(2)** Any of the following: Majority of executive officers or directors are U.S. residents; More than 50% of assets are located in the United States; Business is administered principally in the United States

(17 CFR § 240.3b-4 [17]; Aduro Clean Technologies [18])

### 5.2 Home Country Practice Exemption — What CAN Be Exempted

Under **NASDAQ Listing Rule 5615(a)(3)**, a Foreign Private Issuer is permitted to follow its **home country practice in lieu of** the requirements of the Rule 5600 Series and Listing Rule 5250(d), subject to conditions (Aduro Clean Technologies [18]; Baker McKenzie [19]; Legal & Compliance [20]).

The term "home country" is defined as the jurisdiction of legal incorporation and principal listing if different (Baker McKenzie [19]).

**Specific Rules That CAN Be Exempted:**
- **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)(1)):** FPI is not required to have a majority of independent directors (Legal & Compliance [20]; Cooley PubCo [21])
- **Executive Sessions of Independent Directors (Rule 5605(b)(2)):** FPI is not required to have regularly scheduled meetings at which only independent directors are present (Legal & Compliance [20])
- **Compensation Committee Independence (Rule 5605(d)):** May follow home country practices regarding executive compensation decisions and committee composition (Baker McKenzie [19]; Basel Medical Group [22])
- **Nominating Committee Independence (Rule 5605(e)):** May follow home country practice in lieu of independent nominating committee requirements (Legal & Compliance [20])
- **Shareholder Meeting Quorum Requirements:** May follow different quorum thresholds (e.g., 25% under TSX rules vs. Nasdaq's 33-1/3%) (Aduro Clean Technologies [18])
- **Shareholder Approval for Securities Issuances:** May follow different thresholds (e.g., 25% under TSX vs. 20% under Nasdaq) (Aduro Clean Technologies [18])
- **Annual Meeting Timing:** May follow home country requirements under Rule 5620(a) (Legal & Compliance [20])

### 5.3 What CANNOT Be Exempted (Mandatory Compliance Items)

The home country practice exemption does **not** apply to the following items. FPIs **must** comply:

| Requirement | Rule Reference | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit Committee Independence** | Rule 5605(c)(3) AND SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1) | Must have audit committee of at least three members meeting 10A-3 independence criteria. At least one member must be financially sophisticated from listing date |
| **Notification of Noncompliance** | Rule 5625 | Must promptly notify Nasdaq upon discovery of noncompliance |
| **Voting Rights** | Rule 5640 | Prohibition on unfairly discriminatory voting rights unless prohibited by home country law |
| **Code of Conduct** | Rule 5610 | Must establish code of conduct to same extent as domestic company; waivers must be disclosed |
| **Related Party Transaction Review** | Rule 5630 | Must have transactions scrutinized and approved/reviewed by audit committee or independent body |
| **Listing Fees and Timely Filing** | Rule 5250 | Must submit annual US Share Certification Form; file Form 6-K with interim financials within six months after fiscal quarter-end |

### 5.4 Conditions for Relying on the Home Country Practice Exemption

**Home Country Counsel Certification:** The FPI must submit a **written statement from an independent home country counsel** certifying that the company's practices are **not prohibited by home country law** (Aduro Clean Technologies [18]; Baker McKenzie [19]; Cooley PubCo [21]).

Requirements for the certification:
- Must be from "independent home country counsel" — independent legal counsel in the issuer's jurisdiction of incorporation
- Must state that the FPI's home country does not have an equivalent requirement
- Must state that the company's current practice is both legal and an accepted business practice in the home country
- Typically submitted once at initial listing or before adopting non-conforming practices

**Disclosure Requirements:** The company must **publicly disclose each requirement that it does not follow** and include a **brief statement of the home country practice** followed instead (Aduro Clean Technologies [18]; Baker McKenzie [19]).

The disclosure must be made:
- In the company's **annual SEC filings** (Form 20-F or Form 40-F for Canadian issuers); **OR**
- **On the company's website** (publicly accessible)

Failure to provide required disclosures can result in delisting unless promptly cured (Nasdaq Listing Center [1]).

### 5.5 Transition Schedule When an FPI Ceases to Be an FPI

Under **NASDAQ Rule 5615(b)** (as amended and approved by the SEC on August 26, 2024):

"A Foreign Private Issuer that ceases to qualify... shall have six months from the determination date to comply with the applicable domestic company requirements" (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]).

**What Is the "Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date"?** The date when the company fails to qualify as an FPI at the end of its most recently completed second fiscal quarter. The six-month clock begins at this date (17 CFR § 240.3b-4 [17]).

**Requirements That Must Be Met Within the Six-Month Phase-In:**
- Majority independent board (Rule 5605(b)(1))
- Independent compensation committee (Rule 5605(d))
- Independent nominating committee (Rule 5605(e))
- All other Rule 5600 Series requirements previously exempted

**CRITICAL CAVEAT — Rule 10A-3 Audit Committee Independence: NO Phase-In Permitted:** The most important caveat is that **audit committee independence requirements under Rule 10A-3 must be continually complied with** — there is **NO** phase-in for this specific requirement (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]).

The SEC explicitly stated: "The phase-in rule for Foreign Private Issuers makes clear that the company must continue to have an audit committee meeting these requirements during any phase in for other corporate governance requirements" (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]).

This means that even during the six-month transition period:
- The company must already have an audit committee satisfying SEC Rule 10A-3 independence requirements
- At least one audit committee member must be independent and financially sophisticated
- The company cannot "phase in" audit committee independence

## Section 6: Phased Compliance Timelines — Complete Schedule

### 6.1 IPO Companies — Rule 5615(b)(1)

Companies listing in conjunction with an Initial Public Offering may phase in compliance on the following schedule (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]):

| Requirement | Deadline | Rule Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit Committee — At least one independent member** | **By Listing Date** | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) |
| **Audit Committee — At least two members** | **Within 90 days of Listing Date** | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) |
| **Audit Committee — All three members fully independent** | **Within one year of Listing Date** | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) |
| **Audit Committee — Financial sophistication (at least one member)** | **By Listing Date** (no phase-in) | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) |
| **Compensation Committee — At least one independent member** | **By earlier of IPO closing or 5 business days from Listing Date** | Rule 5605(d) |
| **Compensation Committee — Fully independent / at least two members** | **Within one year of Listing Date** | Rule 5605(d) |
| **Nominating Committee — At least one independent member** | **By earlier of IPO closing or 5 business days from Listing Date** | Rule 5605(e) |
| **Nominating Committee — Fully independent** | **Within one year of Listing Date** | Rule 5605(e) |
| **Majority Independent Board** | **Within one year of Listing Date** | Rule 5605(b)(1) |

**Critical Note on Audit Committee Independence:** The Phase-In Amendments do **NOT** grant an exemption or phase-in period with respect to Rule 5605(c) or Rule 10A-3. From the Listing Date, at least one audit committee member must be independent and meet the financial sophistication requirement. The phase-in is for audit committee **size**, not independence itself (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

### 6.2 Carve-Out and Spin-Off Transactions — Rule 5615(b)(4)

Companies listing in connection with a carve-out or spin-off transaction are permitted to phase in on the same schedule as IPO companies, with one key difference (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]):

**Key Difference from IPOs:** For carve-out/spin-off companies, the first independent director on compensation and nominations committees must be appointed **by the date the transaction closes** (not by 5 business days post-listing as with IPOs), accommodating board meetings between listing and transaction close (Cooley PubCo [21]).

| Requirement | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board** | 12 months from Listing Date |
| **Audit Committee (Independence and Size)** | Same as IPO schedule (1 by Listing Date, 2 within 90 days, 3 within one year) |
| **Compensation Committee** | 1 independent by close of transaction, fully independent within one year |
| **Nominating Committee** | 1 independent by close of transaction, fully independent within one year |

### 6.3 Companies Emerging from Bankruptcy — Rule 5615(b)(2) (renumbered as 5615(b)(3))

Companies emerging from bankruptcy **must** comply with audit committee composition requirements **by the Listing Date** unless an exemption is available pursuant to Rule 10A-3 (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

**No phase-in is permitted for audit committee compliance for bankruptcy emergence companies.**

Other requirements (majority independent board, compensation committee, nominating committee) may be phased in over the same timeline as IPO companies (one year from Listing Date).

### 6.4 Companies Transferring from Other National Securities Exchanges — Rule 5615(b)(3)(A)

The phase-in period applies **only** to companies that transfer securities registered under Section 12(b) of the Act from another national securities exchange to Nasdaq (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]):

- **If the prior exchange had a substantially similar requirement:** The company is afforded the **balance of any grace period** afforded by the other market
- **If the prior exchange did NOT have a substantially similar requirement:** The company is afforded **one year from the date of listing on Nasdaq** to comply

### 6.5 Section 12(g) Companies — Rule 5615(b)(3)(B)

Companies with securities registered under Section 12(g) that list on Nasdaq (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]):

**Audit Committee (Independence):** Must satisfy audit committee requirements except for the three-member minimum **by the Listing Date** (unless an exemption under Rule 10A-3 applies). Only directors who are independent under Rule 5605(a)(2) and meet Rule 10A-3(b)(1) criteria may serve during the transition period.

**Audit Committee (Size) Phase-In:**
- At least **one** independent member as of the Listing Date
- At least **two** independent members within **90 days**
- At least **three** independent members within **one year**

**Other Requirements:**
- Majority independent board: 12 months from Listing Date
- Compensation committee: One independent upon listing, fully independent within one year
- Nominating committee: One independent upon listing, fully independent within one year

### 6.6 Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs) Ceasing to Be SRCs

Under NASDAQ Rule 5615(b) as amended, when a company ceases to qualify as an SRC (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Perkins Coie [16]):

- Must comply with compensation committee requirements under Rule 5605(d)(3) regarding compensation committee responsibilities and authority **within six months** of ceasing to be an SRC
- The six-month phase-in applies to compensation committee composition and adviser independence requirements

**Phase-in schedule for compensation committee independence:**
- **Within 6 months:** One member meeting enhanced independence requirements
- **Within 9 months:** A majority of independent members
- **Within 12 months:** All members fully independent

### 6.7 Controlled Companies Ceasing Controlled Status — Rule 5615(b)(7)

When a company ceases to be a controlled company (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Latham & Watkins [3]):

| Requirement | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board** | 12 months from date of cessation |
| **Compensation Committee — At least one independent member** | By date of cessation or 5 business days (same as IPO schedule) |
| **Compensation Committee — Fully independent** | 12 months from date of cessation |
| **Nominating Committee — At least one independent member** | By date of cessation or 5 business days |
| **Nominating Committee — Fully independent** | 12 months from date of cessation |

### 6.8 FPIs Ceasing FPI Status — Rule 5615(b)(6)

| Requirement | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board** | 6 months from Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date |
| **Compensation Committee Independence** | 6 months from Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date |
| **Nominating Committee Independence** | 6 months from Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date |
| **All other Rule 5600 Series requirements** | 6 months from Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date |
| **Audit Committee Independence (Rule 5605(c) + Rule 10A-3)** | **NO phase-in — must be continually complied with** |

### 6.9 The 2024 Amendments — Cure Period Unavailability Following Phase-In Periods

The 2024 Amendments (SR-NASDAQ-2024-019, effective August 26, 2024) codify several critical rules regarding cure periods after phase-in periods (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]):

**The Core Rule:** A company relying on any phase-in period in Rule 5615(b) is **not eligible for a cure period** immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period, **unless** the company complied with the applicable requirement during such phase-in period but fell out of compliance after having complied before the end of the phase-in period.

**Applicable to these specific requirements:**
- Audit committee composition requirement in Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)
- Compensation committee composition requirement in Rule 5605(d)(2)(A)
- Majority independent board requirement in Rule 5605(b)(1)

**The "Compliance During Phase-In" Exception:** If a company demonstrated compliance during the phase-in period but subsequently fell out of compliance:
1. The company would **not be considered deficient** until the end of the phase-in period
2. For computing the cure period, the event causing the deficiency is the event causing the fall-out of compliance (not the end of the phase-in period)

**Consequence of Non-Compliance:** If a company allows the phase-in period to run out without demonstrating compliance, Nasdaq will issue a **Staff Delisting Determination letter** to delist the company's securities — no cure period is available.

## Section 7: Standard Cure Periods for Noncompliance

### 7.1 General Cure Period — One Vacancy or Loss of Independence

For companies that become noncompliant with independent director or committee requirements due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control (e.g., death, resignation, or disqualification of a director):

**General Rule:** The company has until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting** or **one year from the occurrence of the event** that caused the failure to regain compliance (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

**Minimum Guarantee:** If the next annual shareholders meeting occurs **less than 180 days** after the event, the company has **180 days from the event** to cure.

### 7.2 Majority Independent Board — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(b)(1)(A))

If a Company fails to comply with the majority independent board requirement due to **one vacancy, or one director ceases to be independent due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control** (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; Winston & Strawn [13]):

- The Company shall regain compliance by the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event
- **Provided, however, that if the annual shareholders meeting occurs no later than 180 days following the event**, the Company shall instead have **180 days from such event to regain compliance**
- The Company must provide notice to Nasdaq immediately upon learning of the event

### 7.3 Audit Committee — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(c)(4))

**(A) Loss of Independence:** If an audit committee member ceases to be independent for reasons outside the member's reasonable control, the member may remain on the audit committee until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

**(B) One Vacancy:** If the Company fails to comply due to **one vacancy** on the audit committee, the Company has until the **earlier of the next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event. If the annual shareholders meeting occurs no later than 180 days following the event, the Company has **180 days** from the event.

### 7.4 Compensation Committee — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(d)(4) and Rule 5810(c)(3)(E))

If a Company fails to meet the compensation committee composition requirement under Rule 5605(d)(2)(A) due to **one vacancy, or one compensation committee member ceases to be independent due to circumstances beyond the member's reasonable control** (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; Winston & Strawn [13]):

- The Company has until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event
- If the annual shareholders meeting occurs sooner than **180 days** after the event, the Company has **180 days** from the event
- This cure period is available only if the vacancy was caused by death, disability, resignation, or removal, or if a director who was independent at appointment ceases to be independent for reasons outside the director's control

### 7.5 Unavailability of Cure Periods After Phase-In (2024 Amendments)

As codified in the 2024 Amendments, companies relying on phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) **cannot use cure periods to extend the phase-in period** if they never achieved compliance during the phase-in. This applies to (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]):
- Majority independent board (Rule 5605(b)(1)(B))
- Audit committee composition (Rule 5605(c)(4)(C))
- Compensation committee composition (Rule 5605(d)(4)(B))

## Section 8: Cross-Referenced Regulatory Standards — SEC Rule 10A-3 and NASDAQ's Independence Framework

### 8.1 SEC Rule 10A-3 — Core Independence Criteria

Under **17 CFR § 240.10A-3(b)(1)**, each member of the audit committee must be a member of the board of directors and otherwise be independent based on two core criteria (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]; 17 CFR § 240.10A-3 [23]):

**(i) No Acceptance of Compensatory Fees (Rule 10A-3(b)(1)(i)):** Audit committee members may not accept directly or indirectly any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee from the issuer or any subsidiary thereof, other than in the member's capacity as a member of the board of directors and any board committee.

The SEC's adopting release clarifies: "To prevent evasion of the requirement, disallowed payments to an audit committee member includes payments made either directly or indirectly... including payments to spouses, minor children or stepchildren or children or stepchildren sharing a home with the member" (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]).

**(ii) No Affiliation with the Company or Subsidiary (Rule 10A-3(b)(1)(ii)):** Audit committee members may not be an affiliated person of the issuer or any subsidiary thereof.

**Comparison with NASDAQ's General Independence Definition:**

| Aspect | NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2) | SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1) |
|---|---|---|
| **Compensation Threshold** | $120,000 threshold in any 12 consecutive months (excludes director fees) | **Any** consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee is prohibited (no threshold) |
| **Affiliation Standard** | Specific categorical disqualifications (employment, family, business relationships) | "Affiliated person" concept with 10% safe harbor |
| **Scope of Application** | All independent directors (general standard) | Audit committee members only |
| **Lookback Period** | Three years (varying mechanisms) | No explicit lookback; continuous compliance |

### 8.2 Rule 10A-3 "Affiliated Person" Definition and Safe Harbor

Under Rule 10A-3, an "affiliated person" is defined by reference to the definitions of "affiliate" and "control" (17 CFR § 240.10A-3(e) [23]; SEC Release 33-8220 [11]):

- **"Affiliate"** of a person means a person that directly, or indirectly through one or more intermediaries, controls, or is controlled by, or is under common control with, the person
- **"Control"** means the possession, direct or indirect, of the power to direct or cause the direction of the management and policies of a person, whether through the ownership of voting securities, by contract, or otherwise

**SEC Safe Harbor (10% Threshold):** A person who is NOT an executive officer and who beneficially owns **LESS than 10%** of any class of voting equity securities of the issuer is presumed **not** to be an "affiliate" of the issuer. Conversely, a person who beneficially owns **10% or more** (or is an executive officer) is presumed to be an affiliate. These are rebuttable presumptions (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]).

### 8.3 Interaction Between SEC Rule 10A-3 and NASDAQ Family Member Definitions

**SEC Rule 10A-3 Family Member Definition:** The SEC identifies the following as family members for audit committee independence purposes (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]):
- Spouse of the audit committee member
- Minor children or stepchildren
- Children or stepchildren sharing a home with the member (regardless of age)
- Other immediate family members sharing the member's home

**NASDAQ's Post-2020 Family Member Definition (Rule 5605(a)(2)):**
- Spouse, parents, children, siblings, in-laws, and anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home
- Stepchildren who do NOT share the home are excluded from automatic disqualification
- Domestic employees sharing the home are excluded

**Critical Interaction:** The SEC expressly stated in approving the 2020 amendment that it "does not affect the stricter independence requirements for audit committee members under NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2) and SEC Rule 10A-3" (SEC Release 34-88210 [4]).

This means: **For audit committee independence determinations**, the SEC's own family member definition under Rule 10A-3 continues to apply as a separate, non-waivable standard. A stepchild who does NOT share the director's home would still be a "minor child" under the SEC's Rule 10A-3 for purposes of the indirect compensatory fee prohibition, even though such stepchild would not be a "Family Member" under NASDAQ's amended general definition.

### 8.4 Rule 10A-3(c) Exemptions

**Rule 10A-3(c)(2) — Controlled Companies: NO Exemption Available:** Rule 10A-3 does **NOT** have a controlled company exemption for audit committee independence. The audit committee independence requirements of Rule 10A-3 apply to **ALL** listed issuers, regardless of controlled status (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]; Latham & Watkins [3]).

**Rule 10A-3(c)(3) — Foreign Private Issuers Limited Exemptions:** FPIs have limited exemptions to accommodate differing international corporate governance structures (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]; Cleary Gottlieb [24]):
1. **Home Country Board of Auditors Alternative:** If an FPI has a board of auditors (or similar body) established under home country law, that body may satisfy audit committee requirements
2. **Employee Representative Exemption:** FPIs may include non-management employee representatives on the audit committee if required by home country law
3. **Government Designee Exemption:** FPIs may include government designees on the audit committee if required by home country law
4. **Two-Tier Board Structures:** FPIs with two-tier board systems may have audit committee members drawn from the supervisory board

**Rule 10A-3(c)(4) — Investment Companies:** For investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940, the independence standard is tied to the definition of "interested person" under Section 2(a)(19) of the 1940 Act (17 CFR § 240.10A-3(c)(4) [23]; SEC Release 33-8220 [11]).

**Rule 10A-3(c)(5) — Asset-Backed Issuers:** Exempt from audit committee requirements.

### 8.5 The Critical Rule: Rule 10A-3 Independence Must Be Continually Complied With — No Phase-In Permitted

**This is a critical distinction that applies across all company types:** Even when companies are entitled to phase-in periods for general independence requirements (majority independent board, compensation committee, nominating committee), **Rule 10A-3 audit committee independence must be satisfied from the listing date**, with no phase-in permitted (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]).

**Confirmation for Controlled Companies:** Controlled companies are NOT exempt from Rule 10A-3. The controlled company exemption under NASDAQ Rule 5615(c) does NOT apply to audit committee independence requirements (Latham & Watkins [3]).

**Confirmation for FPIs:** FPIs ceasing FPI status must continue to have an audit committee meeting Rule 10A-3 independence requirements during the six-month phase-in for other requirements (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]).

**Confirmation for IPOs:** At least one audit committee member must be independent under Rule 10A-3 from the Listing Date. The phase-in is for the number of members meeting the independence requirement, not for independence itself (Winston & Strawn [13]).

**Confirmation for Bankruptcy Emergence:** Companies emerging from bankruptcy must comply with audit committee composition requirements by the Listing Date unless exempted under Rule 10A-3 (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]).

### 8.6 Other SEC Rules Incorporated by NASDAQ

**Rule 16a-1(f) — Definition of "Executive Officer":** Rule 16a-1(f) under the Exchange Act defines "officer" (for purposes of Section 16) as:
- The president
- Principal financial officer
- Principal accounting officer (or if no such accounting officer, the controller)
- Any vice-president in charge of a principal business unit, division, or function
- Any other officer who performs a policy-making function

NASDAQ Rule 5605 uses the term "executive officer" in its independence criteria, and the definition is consistent with Rule 16a-1(f) (Williams Mullen [25]; Perkins Coie [16]).

**Regulation S-K Item 407 — Disclosure Requirements:**

**Item 407(a) — Director Independence:** Requires registrants to disclose (17 CFR § 229.407 [26]):
- Which directors and nominees are independent
- The criteria used to make independence determinations
- Any transactions, relationships, or arrangements considered in determining independence

**Item 407(d) — Audit Committee Disclosures:** Requires:
- Disclosure of whether audit committee members are independent (using the applicable exchange definition, which incorporates Rule 10A-3)
- Whether at least one audit committee financial expert serves on the committee
- The audit committee report

**Item 407(e) — Compensation Committee Disclosures:** Requires disclosure regarding compensation committee independence and any conflicts of interest involving compensation consultants. The compensation committee must consider six independence factors before engaging compensation advisers, as required by Rule 10C-1(b)(4).

## Section 9: Investment Companies vs. Business Development Companies (BDCs)

### 9.1 Registered Management Investment Companies — NASDAQ Rule 5615(a)(2) Exemptions

Under NASDAQ Rule 5615(a)(2), management investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940 are exempt from (SEC Release 34-86072 [27]; Federal Register Vol. 75 No. 59 [28]; NASDAQ Reference Library [29]):

| Requirement | Exemption Status |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b))** | **EXEMPT** |
| **Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c))** | **EXEMPT** from Rule 5605(c), but **MUST** comply with SEC Rule 10A-3 |
| **Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d))** | **EXEMPT** |
| **Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e))** | **EXEMPT** |
| **Code of Conduct (Rule 5610)** | **EXEMPT** |
| **Annual Meeting (Rule 5620(a))** | **EXEMPT** |

**Audit Committee Rule 10A-3 Compliance for Investment Companies:** Under SEC Rule 10A-3(c)(4), investment companies use the "interested person" standard under Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act of 1940 for audit committee independence determinations, rather than the general "affiliated person" standard (17 CFR § 240.10A-3(c)(4) [23]; SEC Release 33-8220 [11]).

### 9.2 Business Development Companies (BDCs) — Fully Subject to All Rule 5600 Series

**BDCs Are NOT Classified as Registered Management Investment Companies for These Purposes.** The Nasdaq Reference Library explicitly states: "Yes. Business development companies are required to comply with all of the provisions of the Rule 5600 Series, including compensation committee requirements in Rule 5605(d)" (NASDAQ Reference Library, Identification Number 1107 [29]).

BDCs are fully subject to:

| Requirement | BDC Status |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b))** | **MUST COMPLY** |
| **Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c) + Rule 10A-3)** | **MUST COMPLY** |
| **Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d))** | **MUST COMPLY** |
| **Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e))** | **MUST COMPLY** |
| **Code of Conduct (Rule 5610)** | **MUST COMPLY** |
| **Annual Meeting (Rule 5620(a))** | **MUST COMPLY** |

### 9.3 The Key Distinction Under the Investment Company Act

The Investment Company Act of 1940 creates distinct categories (15 U.S. Code § 80a-2(a)(48) [30]; 15 U.S. Code § 80a-53 [31]; Mayer Brown BDC Guide [32]):

**Registered Management Investment Companies:** Registered under Section 8 of the 1940 Act, subject to the full regulatory scheme, including comprehensive provisions applicable to open-end and closed-end funds.

**Business Development Companies:** Separate category of closed-end investment companies that elect BDC status under Section 54 of the 1940 Act. Defined in Section 2(a)(48). Subject to the modified regulatory scheme in Sections 54 through 64. BDCs are **not** classified as "registered management investment companies" under the 1940 Act regulatory framework.

For NASDAQ purposes, when Rule 5615(a)(2) refers to "management investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940," it refers to the specific statutory category of registered management investment companies, not to BDCs.

## Section 10: Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs) vs. Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs)

### 10.1 Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs) — Definition and NASDAQ Treatment

**Definition (SEC Rule 12b-2):** An SRC is an issuer that has (SEC Rule 12b-2 [33]; SEC.gov [34]):
- A public float of less than $250 million as of the last business day of its most recently completed second fiscal quarter; OR
- If no public float or public float less than $700 million, annual revenues of less than $100 million during its most recently completed fiscal year

The SEC amended the SRC definition on June 28, 2018, raising the public float threshold from $75 million to $250 million and the annual revenue threshold from $50 million to $100 million (SEC.gov [34]).

**NASDAQ Treatment of SRCs:**
- **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)):** **Must comply fully.** No exemption (SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]).
- **Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c) + Rule 10A-3):** **Must comply fully.** No exemption. Must have at least three independent members (SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]; SEC Release 34-68013 [35]).
- **Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d)):** **Modified requirements.** Must have at least two independent directors, but SRCs have certain relaxed requirements including phase-in schedules for full compliance and exemption from enhanced independence considerations under Rule 5605(d)(2) (SecuritiesLawyer101.com [2]; Hunton [36]).
- **Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e)):** **Must comply fully.** No specific SRC exemption. May use majority of independent directors to discharge nominating functions (Baker McKenzie [19]).

**Transition When Ceasing to Be an SRC:** The company must comply with compensation committee requirements under Rule 5605(d)(3) **within six months** of ceasing to be an SRC (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]).

### 10.2 Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs) — Definition and NASDAQ Treatment

**Definition (JOBS Act Section 2(a)(19)):** An EGC is an issuer with total annual gross revenues of less than $1.235 billion during its most recently completed fiscal year (for fiscal years ending before December 2025, adjusted for inflation) (SEC.gov [37]; Gibson Dunn [38]).

EGC status continues until the earliest of:
1. Last day of fiscal year with revenues ≥ $1.235 billion
2. Last day of fiscal year following the five-year anniversary of first equity sale
3. Date on which the issuer has issued more than $1 billion in non-convertible debt in the previous three years
4. Date on which the issuer becomes a large accelerated filer

**CRITICAL: EGCs Do NOT Receive Exemptions from NASDAQ's Independence Requirements.** EGCs only receive SEC disclosure relief under the JOBS Act (scaled executive compensation disclosures, fewer years of audited financials, exemption from auditor attestation on internal controls, ability to "test the waters"). They do **NOT** receive any relief from exchange listing requirements (SEC.gov [37]; Wilson Sonsini [39]; Winston & Strawn [13]).

| Requirement | EGC Status |
|---|---|
| **Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b))** | **Must comply fully** — no exemption |
| **Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c) + Rule 10A-3)** | **Must comply fully** — no exemption |
| **Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d))** | **Must comply fully** — no exemption |
| **Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e))** | **Must comply fully** — no exemption |

### 10.3 Comparison Chart: SRC vs. EGC

| Feature | SRC | EGC |
|---|---|---|
| **Legal Basis** | SEC Rule 12b-2 | JOBS Act Title I |
| **Financial Threshold** | Public float < $250M OR revenues < $100M | Annual gross revenues < $1.235B |
| **Duration** | Re-determined annually | Up to 5 years post-IPO, then lost permanently |
| **Majority Independent Board** | Full compliance required | Full compliance required |
| **Audit Committee** | Full compliance required | Full compliance required |
| **Compensation Committee** | **Modified requirements** (exempted from enhanced independence) | Full compliance required |
| **Nominating Committee** | Full compliance required | Full compliance required |
| **SEC Disclosure Relief** | Scaled disclosure under Reg S-K and S-X | JOBS Act scaled disclosure (404(b) exemption, 2 years financials, reduced executive comp) |

## Section 11: Committee Composition Requirements — Complete Summary

### 11.1 Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)

**Size:** At least three members (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; SEC Rule 10A-3 [11]).

**Independence Standards:**
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2)
- Each member must meet the heightened independence criteria of SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1) (no consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees; no affiliation with the company or subsidiaries)

**Financial Qualifications:**
- Each member must be "able to read and understand fundamental financial statements" (financial literacy)
- At least one member must have "financial sophistication" — past employment experience in finance or accounting, professional certification in accounting, or other comparable experience (including being a CEO, CFO, or senior officer with financial oversight responsibilities)
- A director who qualifies as an audit committee financial expert under Item 407(d)(5)(ii) and (iii) of Regulation S-K is presumed to qualify as financially sophisticated

**Additional Disqualification (Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(iii)):**
- Must not have participated in the preparation of the company's financial statements at any time during the past three years

**Charter Requirements (Rule 5605(c)(1)):**
- Formal written audit committee charter
- Must specify scope of responsibilities, including structure, processes, and membership requirements
- Must specify responsibility for overseeing the outside auditor's independence
- Must specify responsibilities and authority set forth in Rule 10A-3(b)(2), (3), (4) and (5)

**Committee Responsibilities (Rule 5605(c)(3)):**
- Directly responsible for the appointment, compensation, retention, and oversight of the independent auditor (Rule 10A-3(b)(2))
- Must establish procedures for receiving and handling complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, and auditing matters (Rule 10A-3(b)(3))
- Authority to engage independent counsel and other advisers (Rule 10A-3(b)(4))
- Appropriate funding as determined by the audit committee (Rule 10A-3(b)(5))

### 11.2 Compensation Committee — Rule 5605(d)

**Size:** At least two members (NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook [1]; Hunton [36]).

**Independence Standards:**
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2)
- The board must consider all factors specifically relevant to determining whether a director has a relationship to the Company which is material to that director's ability to be independent from management, including:
  - (i) the source of compensation of such director, including any consulting, advisory or other compensatory fee paid by the Company to such director
  - (ii) whether such director is affiliated with the Company, a subsidiary of the Company or an affiliate of a subsidiary of the Company

**Exception for Non-Independent Member (Rule 5605(d)(2)(B)):**
- If the committee has at least three members, one non-independent director may be appointed
- Maximum of two years
- Company must disclose the nature of the relationship and reasons

**Charter Requirements (Rule 5605(d)(1)):**
- Formal written compensation committee charter
- Must specify scope of responsibilities
- Must specify responsibility for determining or recommending CEO and Executive Officer compensation
- Must specify that the CEO may not be present during voting or deliberations on his or her compensation
- Must specify responsibilities and authority under Rule 5605(d)(3)

**Committee Responsibilities and Authority (Rule 5605(d)(3)):**
- Authority to retain compensation consultants, legal counsel, and other advisers
- Directly responsible for appointment, compensation, and oversight of advisers
- Company must provide appropriate funding
- Six independence factors to consider before selecting a compensation consultant

**SRC Treatment (Rule 5605(d)(5)):**
- SRCs are exempt from enhanced independence requirements for compensation committee members
- SRCs are exempt from the responsibility to assess independence of compensation committee advisers
- SRCs must still have a compensation committee of at least two independent directors
- SRCs may adopt a board resolution specifying committee responsibilities instead of a formal charter

### 11.3 Nominating Committee — Rule 5605(e)

**Composition Requirements (Rule 5605(e)(1)):**
Director nominees must either be selected, or recommended for the board of directors' selection, either by:
- (i) a **nominating committee comprised solely of Independent Directors**; or
- (ii) **Independent Directors constituting a majority of the board's independent directors in a vote in which only Independent Directors participate**

**Exception for Non-Independent Member (Rule 5605(e)(3)):**
- In exceptional and limited circumstances, if the committee has at least three members, one non-independent director may be appointed
- Maximum of two years

**Charter Requirements:**
Must address, by written committee charter provision or board resolution:
- The process for board selection of nominees for election by shareholders
- Policy regarding consideration of director candidates proposed by securityholders

## Section 12: Key Regulatory Developments

### 12.1 2024 Amendments to Phase-In and Cure Periods (SR-NASDAQ-2024-019)

On August 26, 2024, the SEC approved amendments to Nasdaq Rules 5605, 5615, and 5810 (SEC Release 34-100208 [12]; Winston & Strawn [13]; Cooley PubCo [21]).

**Key Changes:**
1. **Codification of Cure Period Unavailability Following Phase-In Periods:** A company relying on any phase-in period is not eligible for a cure period immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period unless it complied during the phase-in period but fell out of compliance
2. **Codification of Phase-In Compliance Policy:** A company demonstrating compliance during a phase-in but falling out of compliance before its expiration is not considered deficient until the end of the phase-in period
3. **IPO Phase-In Clarifications:** Specific schedule for audit committee size phase-in (1 member by Listing Date, 2 within 90 days, 3 within one year)
4. **Bankruptcy Emergence Codification:** Companies emerging from bankruptcy must comply with audit committee requirements by the Listing Date
5. **Section 12(g) Companies:** Specific phase-in schedules for companies listing after Section 12(g) registration
6. **Carve-Out and Spin-Off Transactions (new Rule 5615(b)(4)):** Phase-in schedules for companies listing via carve-out or spin-off
7. **FPI Cessation Transition:** Six months from Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date to comply with domestic requirements
8. **Compensation Committee Cure Period (Rule 5810(c)(3)(E)):** Specific cure period for one vacancy or one member ceasing to be independent

### 12.2 SEC Rule 10A-3 Requirements

SEC Rule 10A-3, adopted pursuant to Section 301 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, establishes the baseline independence requirements for audit committee members that NASDAQ must enforce through its listing standards (SEC Release 33-8220 [11]; 17 CFR § 240.10A-3 [23]):
- No acceptance of any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company (other than director or committee fees)
- No affiliation with the company or any of its subsidiaries
- These requirements are in addition to the general independence standards under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 12.3 Diversity Rule Vacatur (December 11, 2024)

On December 11, 2024, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, sitting en banc, vacated the SEC's approval of Nasdaq's board diversity-related requirements set forth in Nasdaq Listing Rules 5605(f) and 5606 (Alliance for Fair Board Recruitment v. SEC [40]; Harvard Law School Forum [41]; Covington [42]).

**Case Background:** In Alliance for Fair Board Recruitment v. SEC, a narrow 9-8 en banc Fifth Circuit decision vacated the SEC's August 2021 approval of Nasdaq's board diversity rules.

**Court's Holdings:**
- The SEC **exceeded its authority under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934** by approving the rules
- The SEC's action was **"arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law"** under the APA
- The SEC **failed to adequately explain** how the diversity rules were connected to the purposes of the Exchange Act
- The court applied the **major questions doctrine**, requiring the SEC to point to "clear congressional authorization"

**Current Status:**
- Nasdaq-listed companies are **no longer required to comply** with Rule 5605(f) (board diversity requirement) or Rule 5606 (diversity matrix disclosure)
- Nasdaq has stated it "does not intend to appeal the Fifth Circuit's ruling"
- The SEC is "reviewing the decision" but an appeal is considered highly unlikely
- Companies may **voluntarily** continue to disclose board diversity information
