# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms: California Cap-and-Trade, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market (2020-2024)

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of three major carbon pricing systems—California's Cap-and-Trade Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China's national carbon market—across seven critical dimensions from 2020 to 2024. Each system operates within distinct political and economic contexts, with unique design features that shape their effectiveness. The EU ETS represents the most mature system with the highest carbon prices (averaging €60-84), while California's program generates substantial revenue ($34.5 billion cumulative) for climate investments. China's newer national market, launched in July 2021, has achieved remarkably high compliance rates (99.98%) and is rapidly expanding sectoral coverage to become the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage.

## 1. Allowance Price Trajectories (2020-2024)

### California Cap-and-Trade

California's allowance prices evolved significantly over the 2020-2024 period, shaped by economic recovery, regulatory expectations, and the program's price containment mechanisms.

**Quarterly Auction Settlement Price Evolution:**
The California Air Resources Board (CARB) conducts quarterly joint auctions with Québec. Allowance prices experienced notable fluctuations [1]:
- **2020:** Pandemic-induced economic disruption led to auction undersubscriptions, with the self-ratcheting mechanism removing unsold allowances from circulation
- **February 2023 onward:** Prices increased due to post-pandemic industrial recovery and expectations of stricter future regulations
- **May 2024:** Allowance prices decreased by 11% in the quarterly auction, breaking the recent upward trend amid uncertainty about regulatory amendments and possible linkage with Washington State's carbon market. All 51.6 million allowances offered were sold at a settlement price of $38.35 [2]

**Price Floor (Auction Reserve Price):**
CARB sets an annual auction reserve price that increases by 5% plus inflation annually [8][9]:
- The auction reserve price in effect is determined as the higher of the Annual Auction Reserve Prices established in U.S. and Canadian dollars
- In the May 2024 auction, the floor price stood at $24.04 per allowance
- The 2026 Annual Auction Reserve Price is set at USD 27.94

**Price Containment Mechanisms:**
California's program redesigned its price containment structure under AB 398 (effective 2021) [8][9]:

**Allowance Price Containment Reserve (APCR):**
- A certain number of allowances from the cap is set aside annually into a Reserve
- CARB offers allowances through Reserve sales when a quarterly auction settlement price reaches 60% of the lowest Reserve tier price

**Two-Tier System:**
- Tier 1: $41.40 in 2021, increasing annually by 5% plus inflation to $65.31 in 2026
- Tier 2: $53.20 in 2021, increasing annually by 5% plus inflation to $83.92 in 2026

**Price Ceiling:**
- Started at $65 per allowance in 2021, rising by 5% plus inflation to $102.52 in 2026
- Activated when entities lack sufficient compliance instruments and no allowances remain in the Reserve

**Secondary Market Dynamics:**
Current Carbon Credit Allowance (CCA) prices were approximately $36.25 as of the latest reporting [3]. The market responded positively to CARB's introduction of two new alternative cap trajectories consistent with the 48% emissions reduction target by 2030 and the 85% target by 2045, with prices rallying to $39 before settling at $36.25 [20].

### European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

The EU ETS witnessed dramatic price evolution under Phase IV, reflecting the system's maturity and the impact of structural reforms [2]:

**Price Trajectory:**
- **Early 2020:** Prices around €25 per tonne of CO2
- **2021:** Carbon prices surged above €50 per tonne, influenced by market reforms and geopolitical energy crises
- **2021-2023:** Prices hovered around €80 on average
- **2022:** EUA prices rose significantly to as high as €97.59, attributed to the removal of structural carbon allowance surpluses
- **February 2023:** EU Carbon Permits reached an all-time high of €105.73
- **2023:** Annual average price of €84; prices declined toward year-end due to decreased industrial activity, mild weather reducing energy demand, and falling gas prices
- **2024:** Expected average EUA price of €60 based on early year trends; €43.2 billion raised in auction revenues
- **May 2026:** Price rose to €77.10, up 7.44% year-on-year

**Market Stability Reserve (MSR) Mechanism:**
The MSR serves as the primary price stability tool in the EU ETS, operating through volume adjustments rather than direct price intervention [2][3]:

- **Established:** By Decision (EU) 2015/1814, operational from January 2019
- **Function:** Adjusts auction volumes based on the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation (TNAC)

**MSR Thresholds:**
- Surplus exceeds 1,096 million tonnes (Mt): MSR withholds 24% of the surplus
- Surplus between 833 Mt and 1,096 Mt: Variable percentage withheld
- Surplus between 400 Mt and 833 Mt: No action taken
- Surplus below 400 Mt: MSR releases 100 Mt annually into the market

**MSR Impact on Surplus Reduction:**
- 2013: Surplus exceeded 2.1 billion allowances
- 2020: 1.58 billion excess permits
- 2022: TNAC approximately 1,134 million
- 2023: TNAC approximately 1,111 million (about 50% less than the 2013 peak)
- 2024: Surplus decreased to 1.14 billion allowances

**Significant Invalidations:** Starting in 2023, allowances held in the MSR above certain thresholds are permanently removed: 2.5 billion (2023), 381 million (2024), and 271 million (2025)

The MSR cut auction volumes by around 30-40% in 2019 and 2020, contributing to the subsequent price rally. The 2023 review (Directive (EU) 2023/959) maintained the doubled intake rate of 24% and minimum of 200 million allowances until 2030 [3].

**Price Forecasts:** By 2030, EU ETS carbon prices are forecasted between €90 and €200 per tonne of CO2 [2].

### China National Carbon Market

China's national carbon market, launched on July 16, 2021, has experienced steady price growth since its inception [1][6]:

**China Carbon Allowance (CEA) Price History:**

**First Compliance Cycle (2021):**
- Spot carbon price ranged from 38.5 to 62.29 yuan per tonne
- Closed the first cycle at 54.22 yuan
- Prices initially around RMB 50 per tonne, roughly half of European levels
- Displayed a "smiling curve" pattern (high-low-high) over the period
- First cycle traded 178.79 million tonnes valued at 7.66 billion yuan ($1.20 billion)

**2022-2023:**
- Prices remained in a relatively stable range, gradually increasing
- Trading activity remained moderate

**2024:**
- The CEA price averaged 98 yuan (€13) in 2024
- On April 24, 2024, the closing price exceeded 100 yuan per tonne for the first time
- By end of 2024, carbon prices reached nearly $14/tonne (approximately 97.49 yuan/tonne)
- Annual transaction value reached a record high of 18.11 billion yuan
- Average daily trading volume increased 43.55% over the previous cycle

**Price Formation and Market Structure:**
China's national ETS uses an intensity-based cap rather than an absolute emissions cap, allocating allowances based on output-based benchmarks tied to production levels [1][8]. The system features:
- Two-step allowance allocation: pre-allocating 70% of previous year's verified emissions and later adjusting ex-post based on verified current year output
- Spot trading only (no derivatives yet)
- Trading primarily through the Shanghai Environment and Energy Exchange (SEEE)
- Price limits and position restrictions to regulate volatility
- 100% free allocation during Phase 1 (2021-2025)

**Price Projections:**
- ClearBlue Markets projects the average carbon price will reach 100 yuan (€13) in 2025 and increase steadily to 200 yuan (€25) by 2030
- EDF projects prices up to $42/tonne (approximately 300 yuan) by 2030 as the system tightens and expands
- Market balance forecasts suggest that by 2026, the current surplus may transition into a deficit, requiring emission reductions or government intervention

**Regional Pilot Prices:**
China's seven regional pilot markets (Beijing, Chongqing, Guangdong, Hubei, Shanghai, Tianjin, Shenzhen) demonstrated significant price variation. The 2016 auction in Guangdong set a price of $1.48 per ton. Emissions prices in Hubei, Guangdong, Shenzhen, and Tianjin are highly correlated with the national prices [5][10].

### Comparative Analysis

| Metric | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **2024 Price Level** | ~$38/tonne | ~€60/tonne ($65) | ~98 yuan/tonne ($14) |
| **Price Floor** | Yes ($24 in 2024) | No (volume-based MSR) | No formal mechanism |
| **Price Ceiling** | Yes ($65-102) | No | No |
| **2020-2024 Trend** | Moderate increase | Volatile, peaked at €106 | Steady increase |
| **Market Phase** | Mature (since 2012) | Highly mature (since 2005) | Early (since 2021) |

The EU ETS has the highest carbon prices globally but also the most volatility, while California's system provides the most comprehensive price containment through floors, ceilings, and reserves. China's low prices reflect its early stage of development and intensity-based cap design.

## 2. Emissions Reductions vs. Baseline Projections

### California Cap-and-Trade

**Coverage:**
The California Cap-and-Invest Program puts a carbon price on approximately 76% of the state's GHG emissions [5][9]. Verified ETS emissions stood at 272.3 MtCO2e in 2023 [11].

**Cap Trajectory:**
- Started at 162.8 MtCO2e in 2013
- Expanded to 394.5 MtCO2e in 2015 with sector inclusion
- Cap declines by about 13.4 MtCO2e each year from 2021 to 2030 (approximately 4% annually)
- Set to reach 200.5 MtCO2e by 2030 [5][9]

**Achieved Reductions:**
- The Cap-and-Trade Program was created by AB 32 (2006) with the aim of reducing GHG emissions to 1990 levels by 2020—a target met in 2016 [2][10]
- Statewide GHG emissions decreased 5.3% from 2013 to 2017
- Since the program began, California's overall GHG emissions have declined by 14%
- Emissions across tracked sectors dropped 8% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels, including a 2.4% decrease from 2021 to 2022 [9]

**CARB's 2022 Scoping Plan:**
Adopted on December 15, 2022, this plan targets [4][36]:
- Carbon neutrality by 2045 or earlier
- 85% reduction below 1990 levels by 2045
- 48% reduction below 1990 levels by 2030 (more aggressive than the statutory 40% target)

However, the Legislative Analyst's Office (LAO) found that the 2022 Scoping Plan "lacks a clear strategy for meeting 2030 GHG goals" and that the "Cap-and-Trade program is not currently positioned to close 2030 emissions gap" due to an oversupply of allowances and the ability to bank unused allowances [34][39].

**Economic Efficiency:**
California's cap-and-trade program costs about $30 per ton of CO2 equivalent reduced compared to $193 for zero-emission vehicle programs [6]. The presence of an Emissions Containment Reserve (ECR) could have gained nearly $1.5 billion in benefits for ratepayers and the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund since 2023 [7].

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Long-term Reduction Achievements:**
- Emissions from stationary installations declined by **51%** between 2005 and 2024, driven by carbon pricing, fuel switching, renewable energy policies, energy efficiency improvements, and global events [2]
- The EU ETS achieved a **21%** reduction of emissions by 2020 relative to 2005
- The carbon intensity of EU ETS1 sectors decreased by **62%** since 2005
- Since 2005, EU-wide emissions reduction was approximately 48% [2]

**2023 Data (Most Recent Full Year):**
- EU ETS emissions decreased by **15.5%** in 2023—the largest single-year decline on record
- Total emissions from all installations in 2023: 1,126,557,459 tonnes of CO2
- Emissions from stationary installations: 1,096 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent (16.5% decrease from 2022)
- Emissions from electricity production saw a **24%** decrease compared to 2022, partly due to increased wind, hydro, solar, and nuclear power
- Industrial emissions fell by **7%** year-on-year
- Stationary emissions were 26% below the ETS cap of 1,486m tCO2e

**2024 Data:**
- Emissions from stationary sources decreased by **5.7%** year-on-year in 2024
- Emissions remained well below the emissions cap

**Linear Reduction Factor (LRF) Evolution:**
The cap tightening mechanism has accelerated significantly [3]:
- 2013-2020: LRF of **1.74%** annual reduction (~38 million allowances per year)
- 2021-2023: LRF increased to **2.2%**
- 2024-2027: LRF increased to **4.3%** per year (Fit for 55 package)
- 2028-2030: LRF increased to **4.4%** per year
- The cap in 2026 is set at 1,185 MtCO2e
- The current cap aims for a **62%** reduction by 2030 compared to 2005 levels

**Aviation and Maritime Emissions:**
- Aviation emissions surged by over 30% since 2013 and nearly returned to pre-pandemic levels in 2024
- Aviation accounted for 5% of total EU ETS emissions in 2024
- Maritime transport included in EU ETS since 2024, contributing 7% of total EU ETS emissions
- Maritime operators must surrender increasing allowances: 40% in 2024, 70% in 2025, and 100% in 2026

**ETS2 (Buildings and Road Transport):**
The new ETS for buildings and road transport has LRF rates of 5.10% initially, decreasing by 5.38% from 2028. Its start has been delayed from 2027 to 2028 [3].

### China National Carbon Market

**Coverage:**
- Launched July 2021, initially covering the power generation sector with over 2,200 entities responsible for ~5 billion tons of CO2 emissions annually (~40% of China's total emissions) [3][12]
- By 2024: 2,257 key emitting entities across the power sector, representing over 5.1 billion tonnes of annual CO2 emissions [13]
- By 2025: Expanded to approximately 3,600 enterprises across four major sectors (power generation, iron and steel, cement, and aluminum smelting), collectively accounting for about 8 billion tonnes of GHG emissions annually, making it the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage [2][8]

**Quantified Emissions Reductions:**
- The ETS achieved a **2.38%** reduction in carbon intensity in the thermal power sector by 2023 relative to 2018 [8]
- Overall electricity generation emissions intensity decreased by **8.78%** [13]
- A **10.8%** reduction in carbon intensity in fossil-fuel power generation by 2024 compared to 2018 levels [2][4]
- An academic study found that China's ETS reduced carbon emissions by up to **18.2%**, reduced inequality, and improved welfare. Around a third of all health benefits from air pollution reduction were achieved by the ETS [5]
- The ETS lowered power sector emissions by **13%** from 2013-2020 [5]

**Important Caveat:**
China's total emissions continue to rise—emissions are projected to increase by 1.9% annually through 2025. The ETS uses intensity-based benchmarks rather than absolute caps, meaning total emissions can rise with higher production even as emissions intensity declines [12]. The system has been described as having "generous allowances, lack of an absolute emissions cap, allowance surpluses, and limited trading liquidity" which has limited its immediate emissions impact [4].

**NBER Modeling Results:**
A multi-sector, multi-period general equilibrium model found that the Tradable Performance Standard's (TPS) climate-related benefits exceed its economic costs by a factor of more than five, rising substantially when accounting for health benefits from improved air quality [1]. The study also found that current benchmark stringency is below the efficiency-maximizing level, suggesting benchmarks 9% tighter could double emissions reductions over 2020-2035.

**Future Trajectory:**
In November 2025, China submitted its 2035 NDC, committing to reduce economy-wide GHG emissions by 7-10% from peak levels by 2035—the first time China has committed to reducing its absolute GHG emissions [2][4]. This marks a significant shift from intensity-based targets to an absolute reduction commitment.

### Comparative Analysis

| Metric | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Coverage** | ~76% of state emissions | ~35-37% of EU emissions | ~40-60% of national emissions |
| **Cap Type** | Absolute (declining) | Absolute (declining) | Intensity-based |
| **2023 Reduction vs. Baseline** | 8% below 2019 levels | 15.5% year-on-year decline | 10.8% intensity reduction (since 2018) |
| **Long-term Target** | 85% below 1990 by 2045 | 62% below 2005 by 2030 | Carbon neutrality by 2060 |
| **Methodology for Baseline** | CARB Scoping Plan modeling | Verified emissions vs. cap | Output-based benchmarking |

## 3. Revenue Generation and Allocation

### California Cap-and-Trade

**Total Auction Proceeds:**
California's cap-and-trade program has generated substantial revenue [5][9][10]:
- **USD 34.5 billion** in cumulative auction revenue since program inception
- **USD 3.13 billion** expected in 2025
- The program generates roughly $7-8 billion per year

**Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF):**
Revenues from the program are deposited into the state's GGRF and then appropriated to state agencies for GHG reduction programs [11]:
- $4-5 billion annually deposited into the GGRF
- $2-3 billion annually rebated to electricity customers as the California Climate Credit
- $28.7 billion appropriated since 2013-14 across 90 programs
- $15.4 billion implemented to date, resulting in an estimated 113 million tons of CO2-equivalent reductions

**Allocation Breakdown:**
- **Continuous appropriations (60%, $17.5 billion):** High-Speed Rail, Affordable Housing
- **Discretionary appropriations (40%, $11.2 billion):** Low Carbon Transportation and other programs
- **35% of revenues** required by law to benefit disadvantaged and low-income communities (SB 535)
- Approximately **50%** of investments benefit disadvantaged communities

**California Climate Credit:**
In October 2024, Governor Newsom announced that more than 11 million Californian households would automatically receive savings on their electricity bills through this credit, amounting to ~$10 monthly for customers of major utilities like PG&E and SCE [6].

**Electrical Distribution Utility Allocations:**
$16 billion in allocated allowance value from 2013-2023 for EDUs, and $6.9 billion for Natural Gas Suppliers [3][8].

**County-Level Allocation Patterns:**
Research has found that democratic-controlled counties receive significantly higher investments relative to generated auction revenues compared to republican-controlled counties, suggesting political bias in fund allocation. Investments also peak during senate election years despite auction revenue remaining stable or lower [15].

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Total Auction Revenues:**
The EU ETS has generated the largest carbon pricing revenues globally [2]:
- **Over €230 billion** since 2013
- **€265.7 billion** (~USD $297.1 billion) since inception
- **€43.5 billion** in 2023 (517.6 million EUAs auctioned at average price of €84)
- **€38.8-43.2 billion** in 2024

**Revenue Allocation Requirements:**
Since June 2023, Member States are required to spend **all** their EU ETS revenues (or an equivalent amount) in support of the green transition, including measures to address social impacts [2]. From 2024, 100% of proceeds must be used for sustainable projects. Between 2013 and 2022, Member States dedicated 76% of their EU ETS revenues to sustainability projects.

**Key Funds:**

**Innovation Fund:**
- Funded by 450 million allowances using carbon price revenues
- Supports breakthrough innovation in clean technologies

**Modernisation Fund:**
- Supports energy system modernisation and energy efficiency in 13 lower-income EU Member States
- Beneficiary states: Bulgaria, Czechia, Estonia, Greece, Croatia, Latvia, Lithuania, Hungary, Poland, Portugal, Romania, Slovenia, and Slovakia
- Expected to generate €57 billion from 2021 to 2030 (assuming €75/tCO2)
- Finances six priority areas: renewable energy, energy efficiency, energy storage, energy networks, and just transition

**Social Climate Fund:**
- Starting in 2026 (ETS2 related)
- Will allocate up to €65 billion between 2026 and 2032
- Funded by auctioning over 200 million emission allowances
- Supports vulnerable groups in the energy transition

**Country Examples:**
- **Spain:** Generated €3.3 billion in 2023 but allocated only 33% (€1.117 billion) toward climate and energy actions
- **Germany:** Allocates all proceeds to climate projects including building retrofitting and electric vehicle grants
- **France:** Channels revenues into the 'ma prime renov' program for energy renovations
- **Italy:** Funds government building retrofits and sustainable transportation

### China National Carbon Market

**Current Revenue Generation:**
Allowances in the China National Carbon Market are 100% freely allocated using an output-based approach during Phase 1 (2021-2025) [2][4]. The government has generated minimal direct revenue from allowance sales. Primary revenue comes from transaction fees from trading [8].

**Planned Auctioning in Phase 2 (Post-2025):**
The State Council regulations published in February 2024 explicitly state that "auctioning is to be introduced and gradually expanded as a method of allowance allocation" [6]:

- **IEA Recommendation:** Phased introduction starting with 5-10% in the power sector, increasing to about 25% by 2035
- **IEA Estimate:** Auctioning could generate an estimated USD 39 billion annually by 2035
- **IMF Estimate:** Partial auctioning could generate substantial fiscal revenues (~0.2% of GDP by 2030, approximately $40 billion), which can be used to compensate low-income households and address competitiveness concerns
- **NBER Study:** Introducing allowance auction could reduce economy-wide costs by 30-43%, particularly if auction revenues finance reductions in capital and labor taxes

**Regional Pilot Revenues:**
Regional pilots have experimented with partial auctioning (e.g., Guangdong's 2016 auction at $1.48/ton), but the national system has not yet followed suit. Pilots generated cumulative trading value of CNY 7.68 billion by 2019 [7].

### Comparative Analysis

| Metric | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|

| **Annual Revenue (2024)** | ~$3-8 billion | ~€40 billion | Transaction fees only |

## 4. Compliance Rates

### California Cap-and-Trade

**Near 100% Compliance:**
Compliance has been near 100% since program inception, bolstered by strict penalties and public enforcement transparency [10]. Entities emitting over 25,000 MTCO2e annually are subject to these requirements.

**Compliance Period Structure:**
The Fourth Compliance Period (CP4) ran from January 1, 2021 to December 31, 2023 [13]:
- November 1, 2022: Surrender obligations for 2021 emissions (first annual compliance obligation)
- November 1, 2023: Surrender obligations for 2022 emissions
- November 1, 2024: Full Compliance Period obligation (remaining balances for 2021 and 2022 plus all 2023 emissions)

**Penalties and Enforcement:**
- Covered entities must surrender one compliance instrument per tCO2e covered emissions
- For noncompliance: penalty of **three additional allowances per missing unit**
- CARB operates under "strict liability" doctrine—any prohibited act constitutes a violation regardless of intent
- Maximum penalty can exceed $10,000 per violation, per day, with higher penalties for intentional violations
- CARB's 2022 Enforcement Activities Annual Report documented **over $21 million in penalties demanded** from businesses [9]
- In 2025, CARB settled 4,683 enforcement actions totaling over $21 million in penalties
- A major action included settling with Hino Motors, Ltd. for use of unapproved emission control devices on heavy-duty engines, resulting in $82.5 million in state penalties and $111.8 million towards mitigation projects [12]

**Compliance Instruments:**
- Offsets allowed with quantitative limits: 4% per year for 2021-2025 emissions and 6% for 2026-2045 emissions
- Offsets must meet direct environmental benefit standards with strict verification
- Banking of allowances is permitted within holding limits; borrowing is not allowed

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Compliance Framework:**
The EU ETS covers approximately 10,000 installations (8,883 stationary installations and 334 aviation operators in 2023) [2]. All covered entities must surrender allowances equivalent to their verified emissions annually by **30 September** [2].

**Compliance Cycle:**
- **By 31 March:** Operators submit their Verified Annual Emissions Report for the previous year
- **By 30 September:** Surrender allowances to cover reportable emissions of the previous calendar year
- Starting in 2025 (maritime): Shipping companies submit verified aggregated emissions data by 31 March annually
- Verifiers must verify emissions reports by end of February for maritime

**Penalties:**
- **Excess Emissions Penalty:** €100 per tonne of CO2 for which allowances have not been surrendered, increased in line with the European Consumer Prices Index
- Failure to surrender allowances by 30 September results in automatic liability for this penalty
- The penalty for non-compliance is €100 per tonne of CO2 plus continued liability for allowances
- Repeated non-compliance can lead to a ban on ships trading in the EU (for maritime sector)

**Enforcement Structure:**
National competent authorities ensure compliance in each Member State. The EU ETS operates under a comprehensive regulatory framework with strict MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) procedures. The EU ETS data viewer aggregates EUTL data by country, activity, size, and year to make compliance data accessible [2].

### China National Carbon Market

**First Compliance Cycle (2021):**
- Achieved a compliance rate of **99.5%** [6]
- First cycle traded 178.79 million tonnes of allowances valued at 7.66 billion yuan ($1.20 billion)
- Regulated entities were predominantly state-owned enterprises in the power generation sector

**Second Compliance Cycle (2021-2022):**
- Compliance rates of **99.61%** for 2021 and **99.88%** for 2022 [13]
- Accompanied by a 31.79% rise in active participants compared to the first cycle

**Third Compliance Cycle (2023):**
- **99.98%** of covered entities surrendered their compliance units for the 2023 compliance year [2][4]
- The 2025 MEE Progress Report states this achieved a historic high, reflecting robust enforcement and market operation [15]
- Total allowance issuance of 5.22 billion tonnes with verified emissions requiring 5.24 billion tonnes, maintaining a slight deficit of 0.55%

**Current Compliance Cycle:**
Covers emissions from January 1, 2024 to December 31, 2024, with the surrender deadline set for December 31, 2025 [3][14]. Compliance cycles have been shortened from two years (2019-2022) to one year (since 2023) [2][1].

**Enforcement Mechanisms:**
- **Original penalties:** Fines up to RMB 30,000 (~USD 4,600) for non-compliance—considered relatively low [12]
- **Enhanced penalties (post-May 2024):** The new State Council regulations introduced significantly enhanced penalties. Non-compliant entities face fines ranging from **five to ten times** the average monthly transaction price of allowances [14][2]
- Companies face "fines of not less than 5 times and up to 10 times the average transaction price of carbon allowances for non-compliance"
- Additional enforcement measures include potential production suspensions [2][4]
- Auditor firms, verifiers, and testing organizations involved in data fraud may face penalties including business disqualification [6]
- The regulations introduce a comprehensive liability framework combining financial and non-financial enforcement measures
- Mandatory emissions disclosure required for covered entities [6]

**Regional Pilot Compliance:**
Regional pilots demonstrated compliance rates over 96% [5]. Shanghai achieved 100% compliance since 2013, while others like Guangdong and Hubei also performed well [7].

### Comparative Analysis

| Metric | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|

## 5. COVID-19 Economic Disruption Management

### California Cap-and-Trade

CARB's response to the pandemic relied primarily on the program's existing self-ratcheting mechanism rather than emergency interventions.

**Self-Ratcheting Mechanism:**
The program's self-ratcheting mechanism was triggered during the pandemic, removing unsold allowances during low demand periods to preserve carbon price signals [1][8]:

- When an auction is undersubscribed (not all allowances offered for sale are sold), unsold allowances are managed according to specific regulations
- If California state-owned allowances remain unsold for more than 24 months, they are transferred into the Allowance Price Containment Reserve
- Section 95911(f)(3)(D) specifies that the maximum number of unsold allowances that can be returned is 25% of the California allowances offered at that auction
- If previously unsold state-owned allowances were re-designated but still remain unsold, they cannot be re-designated to auction until another two consecutive auctions settle above the Auction Reserve Price [30]

**Auction Performance During Pandemic:**
- Political uncertainty and economic factors, including COVID-19, impacted auction subscription rates and allowance demand [1]
- The fast pace of emission reductions during the pandemic period was identified as the most important factor behind weak demand for allowances [6]
- Because of the modest pace of emission reductions needed through 2020 and continued ramping up of other decarbonization policies, demand during the third compliance period (2018-2020) was very unlikely to significantly boost allowance purchases

**No Suspension of Auctions:**
The program continued quarterly auctions throughout the pandemic period. No evidence was found of suspension or cancellation of scheduled auctions due to COVID-19. The self-ratcheting mechanism was the primary policy tool used to manage the demand shock.

**Post-Pandemic Recovery:**
- Prices increased since February 2023 due to post-pandemic industrial recovery and expectations of stricter future regulations [2]
- Emissions across tracked sectors dropped 8% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels [9]

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Automatic Stabilizer through the MSR:**
The EU ETS relied on its rules-based Market Stability Reserve mechanism to manage pandemic disruption without discretionary intervention [3]:

- The MSR was designed to make the EU ETS more resistant to future events, including economic shocks
- As demand for allowances fell during the pandemic, the MSR automatically absorbed surplus allowances, acting as an automatic stabilizer
- The response was entirely rules-based and automatic, requiring no discretion from the Commission or Member States

**Backloading and Auction Volume Reduction:**
- Initially, auctions of 900 million allowances were delayed (backloaded) between 2019-2020 to reduce surplus, but these were ultimately transferred to the MSR [3]
- The MSR substantially lowered the surplus of emission allowances, with auction volumes in 2021 reduced under the same legal basis
- The MSR cut auction volumes by around 30-40% in 2019 and 2020

**MSR Operation During Recovery:**
- The MSR intake rate was doubled from 12% to 24% for the period 2019-2023 to accelerate surplus reduction
- In 2020, the surplus was 1.58 billion excess permits, down from 2.1 billion in 2014
- By 2023, the surplus decreased to approximately 1,111 million allowances (about 50% less than the 2013 peak)

**Post-COVID Recovery and Ukraine War Impact:**
- The 2021-2022 period saw strong recovery in demand and carbon prices
- The Russian war against Ukraine caused energy market disruptions, with gas market disruptions leading to decreased activity in gas-dependent industries [2]
- Germany's industrial emissions fell 10% in 2023 due to production declines caused by the effects of the war
- The REPowerEU plan was launched in May 2022 as a strategic response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine, aiming to phase out Russian fossil fuel imports [6]
- REPowerEU auction volumes included 93,280,000 allowances in 2026, principally through transfer of allowances from the Innovation Fund

**No Emergency Policy Measures Beyond MSR:**
No specific emergency policy measures beyond the existing MSR mechanism were identified. The system proved resilient due to its rules-based design [3].

### China National Carbon Market

**Delay of National ETS Launch:**
China's national ETS was originally announced in December 2017 and planned for a 2019/2020 launch, but was delayed to July 16, 2021 due to pandemic disruption [5][7][12].

**Economic Impact of COVID-19 on China:**
- Economy contracted by 6.8% in Q1 2020
- Retail sales in January and February fell by 20.5% over the same period in 2019
- Industrial output dropped 13.5% in January and February 2020—the largest contraction on record
- Manufacturing PMI dropped to a historic low of 35.7 in February 2020

**Adjustments to Accommodate COVID Impacts:**
- **Borrowing of allowances** was temporarily permitted in 2021-2022 to provide flexibility for covered entities [2][4]
- The first compliance cycle was relatively generous with allowances to avoid excessive burden on entities recovering from pandemic disruption
- The delay from 2020 to 2021 gave regulators and covered entities additional time to prepare for system launch

**Post-Pandemic Recovery and Emissions Impact:**
- China's economy rebounded quickly, posting 18.3% GDP growth year-on-year in Q1 2021
- Coal consumption reached a new high in 2020, contradicting expectations of coal decoupling from economic growth
- Post-pandemic economic stimulus and infrastructure projects increased industrial emissions from steel and cement
- Emissions are projected to rise by 1.9% annually through 2025, balancing economic growth with environmental commitments

### Comparative Analysis

| Aspect | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Primary Mechanism** | Self-ratcheting/unsold allowance removal | Market Stability Reserve (automatic) | Launch delay + generous first cycle |
| **Discretionary Intervention** | No | No (rules-based MSR) | Yes (temporary borrowing permitted) |
| **Auction Suspension** | No | No (volumes reduced via MSR) | N/A (market not yet launched) |
| **Price Impact** | Temporary undersubscription | Temporary price dip, rapid recovery | N/A (market launched post-COVID) |
| **Post-COVID Recovery** | Prices rising from Feb 2023 | Prices surged to record highs (€106) | Rapid economic rebound, emissions rising |

The EU ETS's pre-existing MSR proved most effective as an automatic stabilizer, while California's self-ratcheting mechanism provided a similar function. China's approach of delaying launch and providing generous initial allowances reflected its status as a newly established system.

## 6. Interaction with Complementary Policies

### California Cap-and-Trade

California's climate policy architecture positions the Cap-and-Trade program as a backstop mechanism alongside sector-specific regulations [11]. Approximately 75% of emission reductions are expected from complementary policies, while the C&T scheme acts as a guarantee to meet overall emissions caps.

**Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) and SB 100:**
SB 100 (2018) establishes California's policy that eligible renewable energy resources and zero-carbon resources supply 100% of retail sales by December 31, 2045 [25]:
- Interim targets: 44% by December 31, 2024; 52% by December 31, 2027; 60% by December 31, 2030
- The mandate requires achieving this policy without increasing carbon emissions elsewhere in the western grid
- The RPS interacts with cap-and-trade by reducing allowance demand from the electricity sector, which can depress allowance prices but does not reduce total emissions because the cap is fixed

**Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS):**
The C&T program and LCFS both cover fuel suppliers but create different incentives [28][29]:
- C&T covers only direct combustion emissions, while LCFS focuses on lifecycle carbon intensity reduction
- Academic analysis (Schatzki & Stavins, 2014) found that overlapping policies targeting the same capped sources generally do not yield additional environmental benefits and may undermine economic efficiency
- Some LCFS fuel substitution costs reach up to $945 per MTCO2e compared to the highest allowance price of $102 per MTCO2e in ARB's 2010 economic analysis
- However, more recent analysis suggests LCFS and cap-and-trade programs together reduce demand for carbon allowances and provide complementary incentives

**2022 Scoping Plan Integration:**
The plan emphasizes [4][36]:
- Aggressive transition to zero-emission transportation and building electrification
- 94% reduction in liquid petroleum use by 2045
- 86% drop in total fossil fuel demand by 2045 relative to 2022
- SB 1020 mandating 90% renewable and zero-carbon electricity by 2035

**Cap-and-Trade as Primary Driver vs. Complementary Mechanism:**
ARB estimated that complementary policies would achieve ~80% of the emissions reductions needed for the 2020 target, with cap-and-trade responsible for ~22.5% (18 MtCO2e) [12]. Underperformance of complementary policies results in higher emissions in covered sectors, requiring greater emission reductions to meet the fixed cap, leading to higher allowance prices. Conversely, overachievement reduces these pressures [27].

**Policy Interaction Challenges:**
The LAO found that the cap-and-trade program covering about 75% of emissions "is currently not stringent enough to close the emissions gap by 2030" due to an oversupply of allowances and the ability to bank unused allowances [34][39].

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Renewable Energy Directive (RED III):**
The revised Renewable Energy Directive targets a 42.5% share of renewables in the EU's overall energy consumption by 2030, with ambition to reach 45% [6]:
- Extends transport emission intensity reduction targets to aviation
- Mandates fuel distributors to reduce greenhouse gas intensity by 14.5% by 2030
- Increased deployment of renewables (wind, hydro, solar, nuclear) was a key driver of the 24% decline in electricity production emissions in 2023

**Energy Efficiency Directive (EED):**
The REPowerEU plan includes updates to the EED to support energy efficiency improvements. The EED targets an 11.7% energy efficiency improvement.

**Effort Sharing Regulation (ESR):**
Covers non-ETS sectors (transport, buildings, agriculture, waste, etc.). The EU ETS has auction volumes reserved for the ESR. Complementary national emissions targets should be extended beyond 2030 to incentivise climate policies needed at local and national levels.

**Coal Phase-out Dates:**
- **Germany:** Coal Commission recommended ending coal use by **2038** at the latest; government aiming for accelerated phase-out by **2030** [1][6]
- **RWE (German energy company):** Brought forward coal phase-out target from 2038 to **2030**
- Germany cancelled over 500,000 emission allowances from the EU ETS (514,000 tonnes CO2) to secure the climate benefits of its coal phase-out, countering the "waterbed effect"

**REPowerEU Plan:**
Launched in May 2022 as the strategic response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine [6]:
- Aims to phase out Russian fossil fuel imports and accelerate clean energy transition
- EU has mobilised close to €300 billion to fund the plan
- REPowerEU auction volumes include 93,280,000 allowances in 2026

**EU 2040 Climate Target:**
In March 2026, the EU amended the European Climate Law to set a legally binding target of reducing net greenhouse gas emissions by **90% by 2040** relative to 1990 levels [6]:
- Includes a domestic reduction of 85% and allows up to 5% international carbon credits
- Council also agreed to a 2035 emissions reduction target between 66.25% and 72.5%
- Massive capital investment of approximately €700 billion annually from 2031-2040 is needed (about 3% of EU GDP)

**Carbon Removal Certification Framework (CRCF):**
The EU is evaluating inclusion of domestic carbon removal credits in the EU ETS. The 2025 report from Carbon Market Watch "strongly warns against integrating international carbon credits (Article 6 offsets) into the ETS, as their poor quality and questionable environmental integrity would undermine the carbon price signal" [6].

### China National Carbon Market

**"Dual Carbon" Goals:**
President Xi Jinping announced the "dual carbon" targets in September 2020: carbon peak before 2030 and carbon neutrality before 2060 [11][12]:
- China's national ETS is intended as the primary tool to meet these targets [1][13]
- In November 2025, China submitted its 2035 NDC, committing to reduce economy-wide GHG emissions by 7-10% from peak levels by 2035—the first absolute reduction commitment

**14th Five-Year Plan (2021-2025):**
Binding targets include [11]:
- 18% reduction in carbon intensity by 2025
- 13.5% reduction in energy intensity by 2025
- 20% non-fossil energy in the energy mix (non-binding)

**"1+N" Climate Policy Framework:**
Coordinates decarbonization efforts across sectors with guiding opinions and detailed action plans, managed by the Leading Small Group and aligned with the National Development and Reform Commission [11].

**Coal Phase-down:**
China continues significant coal plant development despite climate goals. Coal consumption reached a new high in 2020. Xi Jinping has advocated a "build before breaking" approach—expanding low-carbon energy sources before reducing fossil fuel use. China has committed to phasing down coal consumption during the 15th Five-Year Plan period (2026-2030) [12][15].

**Shift from "Dual Control of Energy" to "Dual Control of Carbon":**
In 2024, China's "dual control of carbon" system replaced "dual control of energy," shifting focus to carbon intensity and total emissions—a significant policy evolution aligning with the ETS's role [15].

**Power Market Reforms:**
China's power sector is heavily regulated with government-controlled dispatch and fixed tariffs, limiting the carbon price's impact on dispatch decisions and electricity prices [1]:
- Recent reforms aim to expand spot trading markets and incorporate carbon costs in mid- and long-term contracts
- Goal of establishing a unified national power market by 2030
- Inter-ministerial coordination among MEE, NDRC, NEA, and MIIT is vital to align power sector reform and ETS development

**CCER Voluntary Carbon Market:**
Officially relaunched on January 22, 2024, after six years of suspension [3][14]:
- Over 4,500 new account registrations in 2024
- Allows offsetting of up to 5% of annual emissions under ETS rules
- Methodologies cover forestry carbon sequestration, renewable energy, methane abatement, and industrial energy efficiency

### Comparative Analysis

| Aspect | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Renewable Energy Target** | 100% clean electricity by 2045 | 42.5% renewables by 2030 | 25% non-fossil energy by 2030 |
| **Coal Phase-out** | N/A (limited coal) | Germany 2030-2038 | Phase-down during 2026-2030 |
| **Primary Complementary Policies** | RPS, LCFS, SB 100 | RED III, EED, ESR | 14th FYP, dual carbon goals |
| **Policy Coordination Model** | Cap-and-trade as backstop | Multiple overlapping instruments | "1+N" framework |

## 7. Leakage Concerns and Border Adjustments

### California Cap-and-Trade

**Emissions Leakage Evidence:**
Leakage refers to a reduction in emissions within California that is offset by an increase in GHG emissions outside the state [6]. Ex-ante modeling studies report carbon leakage rates ranging from -8.25% to 90%, averaging 27.27%. The rate tends to be lower when emissions coverage is broader and anti-leakage measures are included [14].

**Academic Findings:**
Research by Fowlie, Reguant & Ryan (2016) found that for the median industry, a 10% increase in domestic energy prices is associated with [41]:
- 4-10% reduction in domestic production
- 3-9% decrease in exports
- 2-4% increase in imports
- Industries with higher energy intensities have systematically larger responses
- For cement, lime, industrial gas, wet corn milling, nitrogen fertilizer, iron and steel: estimated negative impacts on export volumes of 20% or greater

**Free Allowance Allocation Methodology:**

**Three Leakage Classifications:**
Industrial sectors are divided into three leakage classifications with corresponding free allowance allocations [6]:
- **High leakage risk:** Sectors such as oil and gas receive 100% free allocations throughout compliance periods
- **Medium leakage risk:** Receive 75% in the second compliance period and 50% in the third
- **Low leakage risk:** Receive 50% in the second compliance period and 30% in the third

**Two Allocation Methodologies:**
- **Product-based:** Applicable to sectors like oil and gas extraction and refining
- **Energy-based:** For sectors without product benchmarks, like food processing

**Cap Adjustment Factor:**
The California program employs emission efficiency benchmarks and a cap adjustment factor, which declines from 0.981 in 2013 to 0.511 in 2030, meaning industrial facilities receive allocations that decline annually in proportion to the overall cap [7][6].

**Vintage 2026 Allocation Breakdown:**
Total of 133,823,322 allowances allocated:
- Electrical Distribution Utilities (EDUs): 54.4%
- Natural Gas Suppliers: 23.2%
- Industrial sectors: approximately 22%

**Border Adjustment Proposals:**
Economists have shown that full auctioning of emissions allowances, together with a combination of border carbon price adjustment and export rebates, can be effective in mitigating emissions leakage [11]. California leaders have been urged to consider mechanisms similar to the EU's CBAM. The ICAP report "Carbon Leakage and Deep Decarbonization" (2020) explores border carbon adjustments (BCAs) and consumption charges as alternative approaches to free allocation [13][8].

**Reform Recommendations:**
The Climate Center's 2023 policy brief recommends gradually eliminating free allowances to better incentivize decarbonization, noting that "free allowances subsidize polluting firms, diluting incentives for carbon reductions" [24].

### European Union Emissions Trading System

**Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM):**

**Legislative Timeline:**
- **July 2021:** CBAM proposal adopted by European Commission [5]
- **December 2022:** Political agreement reached between EU institutions
- **May 2023:** CBAM published in Official Journal, effective from May 2023
- **October 1, 2023:** Transitional phase begins (reporting only, no financial obligations)
- **January 1, 2026:** Definitive/compliance phase begins
- **2026-2034:** CBAM's charge phases in from 2.5% to 100% of embedded emissions
- **February 1, 2027:** First compliance deadline (retroactive for 2026 imports)
- **2034:** Full coverage of embedded emissions for CBAM goods; free allowances fully phased out

**Sectors Covered by CBAM:**
The initial CBAM scope covers six carbon-intensive sectors [5]:
1. Cement
2. Iron and Steel
3. Aluminium
4. Fertilisers
5. Electricity
6. Hydrogen

When fully phased in, CBAM will capture more than 50% of emissions in ETS-covered sectors. Expansion to organic chemicals, polymers, petroleum products, and industrial gases is planned by 2030.

**How CBAM Works:**
- Importers must apply for authorized declarant status
- Importers exceeding a 50-tonne threshold must declare embedded emissions
- Importers surrender CBAM certificates priced in line with the EU ETS weekly average auction price
- 2026: Price based on quarterly average EUA auction prices
- 2027 onwards: Price based on weekly average auction prices
- Adjustments made if a carbon price has already been paid in the country of production
- CBAM revenues go to the general EU budget

**Free Allowance Phase-Out:**
- From 2026 to 2034, free EU carbon allowances to domestic industry are phased out in parallel with CBAM's phased introduction [5]
- Free allocations decrease by 2.5% annually during 2026 and 2027
- A **Temporary Decarbonization Fund**, funded by 25% of CBAM revenues for 2028-2029, will support decarbonization investments in energy-intensive industries

**Historical Evidence of Carbon Leakage:**
- "No factual evidence for carbon leakage has been found for the past two ETS periods (2005-2007 and 2008-2012)" [6]
- A firm-level analysis from NBER found that current compensation rules based on sector-level carbon and trade intensities result in "substantial overcompensation and poorly target firms with the highest marginal relocation risk"
- Interviews with 761 manufacturing firms across six European countries "reveal low average downsizing risk but substantial variation across sectors and firms"
- The study proposed that "simple firm-level allocation rules based on employment and emissions can substantially improve targeting compared to current EU criteria"

**Free Allocation in Practice:**
- In 2023, 528 million allowances were allocated for free to industry across the EU, covering **84%** of total industry emissions
- In Germany, industrial free allocation coverage is estimated at 96% after adjusting for transfers of waste gases and heat imports

### China National Carbon Market

**EU CBAM Implications for China:**

The EU's CBAM has significant implications for China, which holds the greatest market share for all products in the CBAM scope [9]:

**Exposure and Impact:**
- China has the greatest market share for all products in the CBAM scope and is thus most exposed to the immediate impact of the EU's new carbon tariff [9]
- CBAM-covered products make up only about 1.8% of China's total EU exports, primarily in aluminum and iron/steel [6]
- China's exports of iron, steel, and aluminum products account for more than 90% of all CBAM-related commodities imported by the EU [7]
- Quantitative analyses estimate CBAM-related costs to China at €146-208 million by 2035, representing only 0.04%-0.06% of total exports to the EU in covered sectors [6]
- Among Asian economies, China holds the largest share of exports in CBAM sectors to the EU, followed by India, Japan, and South Korea [9]

**Carbon Price Differential:**
China's ETS carbon price is significantly lower than the EU ETS price: approximately $8-12 USD/ton versus approximately $89 USD/ton in the EU [9]. The CBAM allows deduction of the carbon price already paid in the exporting country, so the lower Chinese price offers minimal protection against CBAM's impact.

**China's Response to CBAM:**
China has responded with a dual approach:
1. Raising concerns at international fora such as the WTO and COP29 about CBAM's potential trade discrimination [1]
2. Simultaneously strengthening its own carbon market to prepare enterprises for global carbon pricing policies

**Specific responses include:**
- The 2024 sectoral expansion specifically targets industries covered by CBAM (steel, cement, aluminum) [1]
- The new carbon market regulations strengthen China's domestic carbon pricing framework to increase international credibility
- China is collaborating with the EU to develop a common green investment taxonomy
- The EU CBAM imposes a 2034 deadline for the CN-ETS to mature fully [6]
- Without accelerated decarbonization, China's steel and aluminum exports to the European market could no longer hope to compete

**Leakage Risk Within China:**
China's national ETS initially covered only the power sector (~40% of China's emissions), which limited domestic leakage concerns but created international risks [12]. With expansion to steel, cement, and aluminum smelting sectors in 2024-2025, coverage increased to over 60% of China's total CO2 emissions [3][7]. China's domestic production focus and large domestic market mean internal leakage risks are different compared to Western systems.

**No Domestic Border Adjustment:**
China's ETS currently has no border adjustment measures of its own. The system remains unlinked internationally but coexists with regional carbon markets and local credit mechanisms [2]. Linking China ETS to the EU ETS is deemed unlikely in the medium term due to fundamental structural differences between the systems [12].

### Comparative Analysis

| Aspect | California Cap-and-Trade | EU ETS | China National ETS |
|--------|-------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Border Adjustment** | None (under consideration) | CBAM (effective 2026) | None |
| **Free Allowance Share** | Declining (100% to 30% by leakage risk) | 84% of industry emissions (2023), phasing out by 2034 | 100% free allocation |
| **Key Leakage Risk Sectors** | Oil & gas, cement, steel, nitrogen fertilizer | Fertilizers, cement, steel, aluminum, refineries | Steel, cement, aluminum (CBAM exposure) |
| **Leakage Evidence** | Mixed (rates averaging 27.27% ex-ante) | Little empirical evidence of leakage | N/A (domestic focus) |
| **Anti-leakage Approach** | Output-based free allocation | Free allocation → CBAM | Expansion of ETS coverage |

## Conclusion

The three carbon pricing systems analyzed represent different stages of maturity and operate within distinct economic and political contexts, yet all demonstrate that carbon pricing can be an effective tool for reducing greenhouse gas emissions when properly designed and enforced.

**California's Cap-and-Trade Program** represents a mature, hybrid system with comprehensive price containment mechanisms (floors, ceilings, and reserves), substantial revenue generation ($34.5 billion) directed toward climate investments and community benefits, and near-perfect compliance rates. The program faces challenges regarding allowance oversupply and the stringency of its 2030 targets, as highlighted by the Legislative Analyst's Office. Its interaction with overlapping complementary policies (RPS, LCFS, SB 100) creates both synergies and inefficiencies that require careful management.

**The European Union Emissions Trading System** stands as the most established carbon market globally, demonstrating the longest track record of emissions reductions (51% since 2005) and the most ambitious recent reforms through the Fit for 55 package. The MSR has proven effective as an automatic stabilizer during both the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent energy crisis. The introduction of CBAM represents a pioneering approach to addressing carbon leakage that is being closely watched by other jurisdictions. The system's high and volatile carbon prices, while driving significant investment, also raise competitiveness concerns that CBAM aims to address.

**China's National Carbon Market** has achieved remarkable progress in a short period, rapidly becoming the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage. Its 99.98% compliance rate demonstrates strong governmental enforcement capacity. However, the intensity-based cap design, 100% free allocation, and relatively low carbon prices mean its near-term emissions impact has been limited. Planned reforms—including the introduction of auctioning, sectoral expansion, and the shift from "dual control of energy" to "dual control of carbon"—promise to strengthen the system significantly. China's response to the EU CBAM, including bringing CBAM-covered sectors into its ETS, demonstrates how international carbon pricing dynamics are driving domestic policy evolution.

**Key Lessons Across Systems:**
1. **Price stability mechanisms matter:** The EU's MSR and California's price floors/ceilings both provide stability, but through different approaches (volume-based vs. price-based)
2. **Revenue allocation shapes political sustainability:** California's community benefit requirements and the EU's Social Climate Fund demonstrate how revenue use can build and maintain political support
3. **Complementary policies are essential but require coordination:** All three systems show that carbon pricing alone is insufficient; sector-specific policies are necessary to drive the technological transformation required for deep decarbonization
4. **Leakage concerns drive policy innovation:** The EU's CBAM is the most ambitious response to date, while California is exploring border adjustments and China is expanding its ETS coverage to mitigate leakage risks
5. **Compliance enforcement is critical:** All three systems achieve near-perfect compliance rates, but through different mechanisms—California's strict liability and financial penalties, the EU's €100/tonne penalty, and China's new enhanced penalties
