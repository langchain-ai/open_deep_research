# Comprehensive Revised Research Report: Management of a 68-Year-Old Man with Recurrent Near-Syncope, ECG Abnormalities, and Insurance Barriers to Monitoring

## Executive Summary

This report provides an updated, comprehensive management plan for a 68-year-old man presenting to the ED with recurrent near-syncope, ECG abnormalities (sinus bradycardia, PR interval 180-240ms, dropped beats), and significant insurance barriers to outpatient monitoring. The revision addresses four critical gaps: (1) comprehensive medication safety recommendations advising avoidance of ALL AV-nodal blocking agents, (2) specifications for urgent cardiology/electrophysiology consultation from the ED, (3) evidence establishing presyncope as carrying a risk profile nearly identical to syncope, and (4) detailed contingency planning for failed admission including expedited outpatient specialist follow-up within 48-72 hours. All prior content from the original report regarding risk stratification, monitoring comparison, admission thresholds, disposition planning, safety netting, and the decision algorithm remains valid and is retained.

## Part 1: Medication Safety — Comprehensive Avoidance of ALL AV-Nodal Blocking Agents

### 1.1 The Clinical Imperative: Why This Patient Should Not Receive AV-Nodal Blocking Agents

This patient presents with sinus bradycardia (HR 52), variable PR interval (180-240 ms), and dropped beats on ECG, with three ED visits in one month for near-syncope and two witnessed episodes of pallor with inability to speak. This clinical picture is highly suggestive of either **intermittent high-grade AV block** (given the variable PR and dropped beats) or **sick sinus syndrome** (given the bradycardia). Current guidelines from the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline and the 2018 ESC Syncope Guidelines classify this patient as high-risk and recommend intensive diagnostic evaluation [1][2].

A critical—and frequently overlooked—point is the distinction between **drug-induced** and **drug-revealed** conduction disease. A 2025 comprehensive review of drug-related AV block found that true drug-induced AV block is rare, accounting for only about 15% of cases [3]. The remaining 85% of patients have underlying conduction system disease that is unmasked by medications. Recurrence rates after drug discontinuation exceed 50% within follow-up periods up to years, meaning that simply stopping the offending medication does not resolve the problem in most patients [3].

**The patient is currently on metoprolol 50 mg BID.** However, the recommendation must extend beyond this single agent to encompass ALL AV-nodal blocking medications, as the patient may have additional medications not yet identified, may be prescribed new agents during the ED visit, and the guidance must apply to future clinical decision-making until specialist evaluation is complete.

### 1.2 Complete List of AV-Nodal Blocking Agents

The main AV node blocking agents are remembered by the mnemonic "ABCD": **A**denosine, **A**miodarone, **B**eta-blockers, **C**alcium channel blockers (non-dihydropyridine), and **D**igoxin [4]. The following comprehensive classification is based on pharmacological mechanisms and guideline recommendations [3][5][6][7].

#### Category 1: Beta-Blockers (β-adrenergic receptor antagonists)

**Specific Agents:**
- **Cardioselective (β1-selective):** Metoprolol (tartrate and succinate), Atenolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol (IV), Acebutolol, Nebivolol
- **Non-selective (β1 and β2):** Propranolol, Nadolol, Timolol, Sotalol* (also class III antiarrhythmic), Pindolol
- **Combined α1/β-blockers:** Carvedilol, Labetalol

**Mechanism:** Beta-blockers competitively antagonize β-adrenergic receptors, reducing sympathetic tone to the AV node, resulting in slowed AV nodal conduction velocity (negative dromotropic effect), prolonged PR interval, decreased heart rate, and suppressed sinus node automaticity. The AV node is highly dependent on sympathetic stimulation; beta-blockade therefore unmasks any underlying conduction system disease.

**Relevance to This Patient:** A study of 596 beta-blocker users found that carvedilol (35.2%), bisoprolol (30.8%), and metoprolol (14.2%) were most commonly used [8]. Beta-blocker use is more likely to be associated with sick sinus syndrome and sinus bradycardia/pauses compared with second- or third-degree AV block. Critically, second- and third-degree AV blocks tend to be **independent of beta-blocker use**, suggesting that when higher-degree block occurs on a beta-blocker, it reflects underlying conduction disease rather than a purely pharmacologic effect [8].

#### Category 2: Non-Dihydropyridine Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

**Specific Agents:** Verapamil, Diltiazem

**Mechanism:** These agents block L-type calcium channels in the myocardium and cardiac conduction tissue. Since phase 0 of the action potential in nodal tissues is calcium-dependent, these agents directly slow AV nodal conduction. They have negative dromotropic effects (prolong PR interval), negative chronotropic effects (reduce heart rate), and negative inotropic effects. Verapamil is the most potent myocardial depressant among CCBs [6]. Both verapamil and diltiazem are **contraindicated in patients with significant AV block without a pacemaker** [6][9].

**Critical Distinction: Dihydropyridine CCBs (Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine)**
These agents are **NOT** AV-nodal blocking agents in clinical practice. They are potent vasodilators with **minimal direct cardiac depression** and reflex sympathetic stimulation counteracts any nodal effects [7][10]. Nifedipine at clinically practical doses has no antiarrhythmic properties and the least effect on AV conduction [10]. **These can generally be safely continued** in this patient, though careful monitoring is warranted.

#### Category 3: Digoxin (Cardiac Glycoside)

**Mechanism:** Digoxin inhibits Na+/K+-ATPase, leading to increased intracellular calcium (positive inotropy) and increased vagal tone, which decreases conduction velocity through the AV node and prolongs the AV nodal refractory period.

**Particular Danger in Sick Sinus Syndrome:** A landmark study by Margolis et al. documented that digoxin, even at low therapeutic serum levels, brought out severe manifestations of sinus node dysfunction including prolonged sinus pauses exceeding six seconds in patients with underlying sick sinus syndrome [11]. Digoxin is contraindicated in patients with significant sinus or AV block unless treated with a permanent pacemaker [12].

#### Category 4: Amiodarone

**Mechanism:** Amiodarone is a complex multichannel blocker (class III antiarrhythmic) that blocks potassium channels (primarily), sodium channels (class I effect), calcium channels (class IV effect), and beta-adrenergic receptors (class II effect) [13][14]. Its calcium channel blocking activity is use-dependent and rate-related, with effects augmented by increasing atrial stimulation frequency [14].

**Critical Pharmacokinetic Concern:** The elimination half-life of amiodarone averages approximately **58 days**, with clinical effects persisting 1 to 3 months after discontinuation [13][15]. Simply holding the medication in the ED does not acutely eliminate its effects. Amiodarone is contraindicated in second- or third-degree heart block without a pacemaker [15].

#### Category 5: Adenosine

**Mechanism:** Activates adenosine A1 receptors in the AV node, causing transient (seconds) complete AV block. Used diagnostically and therapeutically for SVT. **Contraindicated** in sick sinus syndrome or high-grade AV block without a backup pacemaker, as it can cause prolonged asystole.

#### Other Agents with AV-Nodal Blocking Properties

- **Sotalol:** Combines non-selective beta-blockade with class III antiarrhythmic activity; significant AV nodal blocking effects.
- **Class Ic Antiarrhythmics (Flecainide, Propafenone):** Slow atrial conduction and have some AV nodal blocking effects; often co-prescribed with AV node blocking agents.
- **Ivabradine:** Reduces heart rate via If channel inhibition in the sinus node; while not primarily AV nodal, it is often used in combination and can exacerbate bradycardia in SSS.

### 1.3 Risks of Continuing AV-Nodal Blocking Agents in This Patient

The risks are substantial and can be categorized as follows:

**Risk of Progression to Complete Heart Block:** In patients with underlying conduction system disease, AV-nodal blocking agents can convert intermittent high-grade AV block into complete (third-degree) heart block with ventricular escape rhythms. This can occur unpredictably and without warning, potentially leading to syncope, falls, head trauma, or asystole. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS guideline states that acquired second-degree Mobitz type II AV block, high-grade AV block, or third-degree AV block "not caused by reversible or physiologic causes" requires permanent pacing regardless of symptoms [1][16].

**Risk of Prolonged Sinus Pauses:** In patients with sick sinus syndrome, AV-nodal blocking agents can induce prolonged sinus pauses. The median time to first sinus pause detection in ED patients is 4.2 days [17], meaning that even a few days of continued medication exposure carries real risk.

**The "Drug-Revealed" Paradigm:** As noted in Section 1.1, 85% of AV block occurring on AV-nodal blocking agents represents unmasked underlying disease rather than a reversible drug effect [3]. Waiting for drug washout is dangerous in elderly patients because (1) recurrence rates are high (>50%), (2) risks of recurrent syncope (falls, fractures, head trauma) are significant, (3) the morbidity associated with delayed pacing is substantial, and (4) for amiodarone, washout takes weeks to months.

### 1.4 ED Management Protocol for AV-Nodal Blocking Agents

#### Step 1: Complete Medication Reconciliation
- Obtain a complete list of all medications, including over-the-counter, herbal, and supplements
- Specifically identify all agents from the ABCD classification above
- Check for combination therapy (e.g., beta-blocker + diltiazem, amiodarone + beta-blocker), which significantly compounds risk [18]

#### Step 2: Immediate Actions

**HOLD the following agents immediately:**
- **Beta-blockers** (all): Especially if the patient has documented or suspected high-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia, or Mobitz II on ECG. If the patient is hemodynamically stable with only first-degree AV block and no symptoms, the decision may be individualized, but given this patient's symptomatic presentation, holding is recommended [1][19].
- **Non-dihydropyridine CCBs** (verapamil, diltiazem): Hold immediately; these are directly contraindicated in AV block without a pacemaker [6][9][19].
- **Digoxin:** Hold immediately; contraindicated in significant AV block and sick sinus syndrome [11][12][19].
- **Amiodarone:** Hold (though effects persist for weeks due to 58-day half-life); early discussion with cardiology regarding permanent pacing may be necessary [3][13][15].
- **Sotalol:** Hold due to combined beta-blockade and class III effects [19].
- **Combination therapy:** Any combination of AV nodal blocking agents requires immediate discontinuation in this setting.

**Can SAFELY CONTINUE:**
- **Dihydropyridine CCBs** (amlodipine, nifedipine, felodipine): These have minimal to no AV nodal effects [7][10].
- **ACE inhibitors, ARBs, diuretics, statins:** No AV nodal blocking effects.
- **Note:** These can be continued while awaiting specialist consultation.

**Requires Consideration:**
- **Low-dose beta-blockers** in patients with only first-degree AV block and no symptoms/syncope may be continued with monitoring, but this patient has symptomatic presentations and dropped beats, so holding is recommended [1].

#### Step 3: Monitoring During Medication Holding
- **Continuous cardiac telemetry** in a monitored bed
- **Transcutaneous pacing pads applied** (connected to functioning pacer) — recommended for all patients with suspected Mobitz II, high-grade, or complete AV block, including asymptomatic patients, because of propensity to progress [20][21]
- **Serial 12-lead ECGs** to document conduction changes
- **Monitor for worsening bradycardia or AV block** as the underlying conduction disease may become unmasked
- **Atropine available** (0.5 mg IV every 3-5 minutes, max 3 mg) for symptomatic bradycardia; note that atropine may worsen infranodal block [21]
- **Temporary pacing capability** (transcutaneous and transvenous) readily available

#### Step 4: Assessment of Reversibility
- Check serum electrolytes (K+ >4.0, Mg2+ >2.0 mg/dL) and correct abnormalities
- Check renal function (creatinine, BUN) to assess clearance of renally eliminated drugs (digoxin, sotalol)
- Check digoxin level if patient is on digoxin therapy
- Check TSH to rule out thyroid dysfunction as a reversible cause [21]
- Check cardiac troponin to rule out acute coronary syndrome as the cause of new conduction abnormalities
- Assess for sleep apnea (can cause nocturnal heart block)

#### Step 5: Guideline-Based Guidance on When to Hold vs. Continue by Class (Summary Table)

| Drug Class | Hold | Continue (with monitoring) | Can Continue Safely |
|------------|------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Beta-blockers (cardioselective) | High-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia, Mobitz II | First-degree AV block only, asymptomatic | No — hold if any high-grade features |
| Beta-blockers (carvedilol) | High-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia | First-degree AV block only, asymptomatic | No — hold if any high-grade features |
| Non-DHP CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem) | Any degree of AV block with symptoms, suspected SSS | NEVER in AV block | No — contraindicated in AV block |
| DHP CCBs (amlodipine, nifedipine) | No need to hold for AV block reasons | Yes, safe | Yes — can continue safely |
| Digoxin | Any suspected AV block, SSS, symptoms | NEVER in AV block/SSS | No — contraindicated in AV block/SSS |
| Amiodarone | High-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia | May continue if only mild PR prolongation and stable | No — effects persist for weeks; hold and monitor |
| Sotalol | High-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia | Mild PR prolongation, stable | No — hold if any concern |
| Adenosine | Do NOT administer if AV block or SSS suspected | N/A (not a chronic medication) | N/A |

### 1.5 Key Evidence-Based Citations

The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS guideline emphasizes that "AV block attributable to medications, such as beta-blocker overdose or digoxin toxicity, often resolves with supportive care and medication adjustment" but that "most patients with drug-related AV block have persistent or recurrent AV block that ultimately requires pacing" [1]. The 2025 comprehensive review in PMC concludes that "true drug-induced AVB is rare (only 15%), with high recurrence rates (>50%) after drug discontinuation; early permanent pacing is advised, especially in elderly, frail patients" [3]. For elderly patients with drug-related AV block, **early permanent pacing is the safest strategy** due to risks of recurrent syncope, falls, and the morbidity of delayed treatment [3].

## Part 2: Urgent Cardiology/Electrophysiology Consultation from the ED

### 2.1 Recommendation for Same-Day or Within 24 Hours Consultation

This patient requires **urgent cardiology consultation, ideally within 24 hours, and preferably as a same-day consultation** from the ED. The urgency is justified by the following evidence:

- The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline gives a **Class I recommendation** for hospital evaluation and treatment for patients with syncope who have a serious medical condition potentially relevant to the cause of syncope [1][2].
- This patient meets **high-risk criteria** by multiple validated risk stratification tools (San Francisco Syncope Rule, Canadian Syncope Risk Score, ACC/AHA/HRS criteria), and such patients have 30-day serious adverse event rates of 8-19% [22][23].
- The **ER2EP (Emergency Room to Electrophysiology) study**, a prospective multicenter study published by the American College of Cardiology, demonstrated that an organized treatment pathway involving electrophysiology consultation from the emergency room significantly reduced time to EP evaluation from a median of **128 days to 1 day**, antiarrhythmic drug initiation from 25 to 2.7 days, and catheter ablation from 180.6 to 52.8 days, with markedly lower length of stay (2.3 vs. 5.8 days) [24].
- A retrospective cohort study found that early cardiology consultation in the ED was associated with a significant reduction in 30-day unplanned re-hospitalizations (14.8% vs. 22.7%; odds ratio 0.592; P<.01), with effects persisting to 90 days [25].

### 2.2 General Cardiology vs. Electrophysiology Consultation

For this specific presentation, **electrophysiology (EP) consultation is preferred over general cardiology**, though either is acceptable [26]:

- **General cardiology** focuses on the heart's structure, pumping ability, and blood flow. A general cardiologist can manage initial evaluation, medication adjustment, and basic diagnostic testing.
- **Cardiac electrophysiology** is a subspecialty that focuses on the heart's electrical system and treats heart rhythm disorders. An EP specialist is specifically indicated for: **pacemaker implantation decisions**, complex conduction system disease evaluation (including EPS for localization of block), and any CIED-related management [26][27].

The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS guideline states that for patients with acquired Mobitz type II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block, permanent pacing is recommended regardless of symptoms [1]. This decision is best made by an EP specialist who can determine the need for and type of pacing.

If EP consultation is not immediately available, **general cardiology consultation is an acceptable first step** with the understanding that EP referral may follow if pacemaker implantation is indicated.

### 2.3 Tele-Consult Options for Cardiology/EP from the ED

Several telecardiology platforms mirror the hub-and-spoke model used in telestroke programs and can facilitate same-day consultation [28]:

**AmplifyMD TeleCardiology:**
- Offers 24/7 coverage connecting EDs with board-certified cardiologists for real-time and asynchronous cardiac care [28]
- Services include: ED consults, inpatient rounding and cross-cover, asynchronous reads (ECG, echo, Holter, stress testing), peer-to-peer consults, post-discharge appointments
- Over 400 active programs across 50 states with over 220,000 consults annually
- Drives faster access to cardiology care, fewer avoidable interhospital transfers, and improved ED throughput [28]

**Eagle Telemedicine TeleCardiology:**
- Provides remote interpretation of ECG and echocardiogram studies for onsite hospitalists and ED physicians
- Particularly beneficial for smaller and rural hospitals
- One study showed that of 677 patients who could have been transferred, 410 only needed non-invasive treatment, limiting unnecessary transfers [29]
- A rural Kentucky hospital halved its transfers, boosting revenue by $500,000 annually [29]

**Tricog Health Telecardiology:**
- Provides rapid ECG and echocardiogram reporting with turnaround times under 20 minutes for ECGs (10 minutes in critical cases) and within 2 hours for echocardiograms
- AI algorithms detect over 140 heart conditions including arrhythmias and myocardial infarctions [30]

**Hospital-Specific Telemedicine Partnerships:**
- Many hospitals have established tele-cardiology partnerships that can be accessed through the hospital's telemedicine program or medical staff office
- The telestroke model demonstrates that a hub-and-spoke system with two-way videoconferencing can support ED-based specialist consultation with high satisfaction (>95% reported improved care) [31]

### 2.4 Hospitalist-to-Specialist Referral Pathways

The following pathways exist for obtaining urgent cardiology consultation from the ED:

**Direct ED-to-Cardiology Pathway:**
- Most hospitals have an **on-call cardiologist** available 24/7 for consultations
- The ED physician places a **consult order** in the EHR specifying the clinical question (e.g., "evaluate for suspected high-grade AV block, need for pacemaker evaluation")
- The on-call cardiologist is contacted via the hospital's paging or communication system
- For patients with hemodynamic instability, the SMH-Venice guideline specifies: "Patients with Cardiac Arrhythmia with hemodynamic instability should be stabilized in the Emergency Department with Cardiology stat consultation before admission" [32]

**Inpatient Admission with Hospitalist and Cardiology:**
- Cardiology admissions may fall primarily to the hospitalist service except for specific acute cases (e.g., STEMI taken directly to the Cath lab)
- The cardiologist serves as a consultant for pacemaker decisions and management of complex arrhythmias
- Hospitalist manages the overall hospitalization, while cardiology provides specialized expertise [32]

**ED-to-EP Pathway (ER2EP Model):**
- The ER2EP study demonstrated that a structured pathway from the ED directly to electrophysiology can dramatically reduce time to definitive care [24]
- This involves: ED physician identifies arrhythmia concern → places EP consult order → EP team evaluates either in-person or via tele-consult → definitive management plan established
- This model significantly reduced time to EP evaluation from 128 to 1 day and length of stay from 5.8 to 2.3 days [24]

### 2.5 Information and Documents the Specialist Will Need for a Meaningful Consultation

Based on cardiology consultation guidelines and ICD-10 documentation requirements, the following should be prepared [27][33]:

**Essential Documents:**

1. **12-Lead ECG (current and prior):** The 12-lead ECG is the gold standard for initial screening. For AV block, specific documentation of **conduction ratio** (e.g., "3:1 conduction ratio"), **PR interval consistency** (Mobitz II vs. Wenckebach pattern), and **QRS width** is necessary [27][33].

2. **Continuous Telemetry Strips:** Documenting rhythm over time, including any pauses, dropped beats, or conduction changes. "Cardiac monitoring enables clinicians to detect arrhythmias, myocardial ischemia, and QT-interval measurements in real time" [27].

3. **Complete Medication List:** With specific attention to all AV-nodal blocking agents (ABCD list), their doses, and duration of use. "Withdrawal of the offending drugs is the first treatment for heart block" [21].

4. **Laboratory Results:**
   - Cardiac troponin (to rule out myocardial ischemia)
   - Serum electrolytes (K+, Mg2+, Ca2+)
   - Renal function (Cr, BUN)
   - TSH
   - Digoxin level (if applicable)
   - Complete blood count (for anemia)

5. **Vital Sign Trends:** Heart rate trends, blood pressure (including orthostatic measurements), oxygen saturation.

6. **Detailed History:**
   - Description of symptoms (near-syncope, fatigue, witnessed episodes of pallor)
   - Onset, duration, and frequency
   - Provoking factors
   - Prior cardiac history and risk factors
   - Family history of cardiac disease or sudden death

7. **Results of Prior Testing:** Prior Holter monitor results, echocardiograms, stress tests, or any prior cardiology evaluations.

8. **Risk Stratification Scores:** San Francisco Syncope Rule, Canadian Syncope Risk Score, or other validated tools used in the ED evaluation.

**ICD-10 Documentation Requirements for High-Grade AV Block [33]:**
- ECG/Holter showing conduction ratio (≥3:1 for high-grade block)
- PR interval consistency (Mobitz II) versus Wenckebach pattern (Mobitz I)
- Provider attestation of block type
- Documentation of any symptoms at the time of block

### 2.6 Key Phrases for Documentation to Convey Urgency

To ensure the specialist understands the clinical urgency, the following phrases should be used in the consultation request [21][27][32]:

**For AV Block Presentations:**
- "Suspected intermittent high-grade AV block"
- "Symptomatic bradycardia with near-syncope"
- "Risk of progression to complete heart block"
- "New-onset third-degree atrioventricular block is a medical emergency" [21]
- For Mobitz type II: "PR interval constant with intermittent non-conducted beats; always pathologic and pacemaker usually required" [27]
- "A trifascicular block is always pathologic and requires admission for evaluation and likely pacemaker placement" [34]

**For Universal Urgency Designation:**
- "Stat consultation" — defined as "a time-sensitive evaluation performed by a cardiologist to diagnose and treat critical or life-threatening cardiac problems" [35]
- Specify that the request is for a **consult, not a question**
- "Procedures are deemed urgent or emergent if they significantly reduce risks of clinical decompensation, hospitalization, or death" [35]

**For Hemodynamic Instability:**
- "Cardiac arrhythmia with hemodynamic instability requires stabilization in the ED with Cardiology stat consultation before admission" [32]
- "Progressive bradycardia is often a harbinger of death" [36]

### 2.7 Escalation Protocol if Consultation is Delayed

If the initial consultation request is delayed, the following escalation pathway should be followed [37]:

1. **Direct re-communication:** Re-page or re-call the on-call cardiologist/EP stating the urgency explicitly using the key phrases listed above.

2. **Charge nurse/supervisor escalation:** Involve the ED charge nurse to contact the cardiology charge nurse or attending.

3. **Medical chain of command:** Escalate to the ED attending physician who can directly contact the cardiology attending physician or cardiology service chief.

4. **Hospital administration:** If unresolved, escalate to the Chief Medical Officer (CMO), Risk Manager, or Administrator-on-call.

5. **Transfer consideration:** If cardiology/EP consultation cannot be obtained in a timely manner and the patient requires emergent intervention (e.g., transvenous pacing), consider transfer to a facility with available cardiology/EP services.

**Documentation of all notification attempts and escalation steps in the medical record is essential** for medicolegal purposes [37].

## Part 3: Presyncope Risk Equivalence to Syncope — Evidence-Based Support

### 3.1 The Core Finding: Presyncope Carries Nearly Identical Risk

Multiple high-quality studies have definitively established that **presyncope (near-syncope) carries a risk profile nearly identical to syncope** and should be managed with equivalent caution. This is one of the most important paradigm shifts in emergency syncope management over the past decade.

### 3.2 Bastani et al. (2019) — The Landmark Multicenter Study

The most definitive evidence comes from Bastani et al., published in the Annals of Emergency Medicine in March 2019 [38]. This was a **multicenter prospective cohort study** funded by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NIH grant R01 HL 111033), conducted from April 2013 to September 2016 across **11 geographically diverse US emergency departments** (both academic and community).

**Study Population:** 3,581 patients aged ≥60 years (mean age 72.8 years; 51.6% men). Of these:
- 1,380 (39%) presented with near-syncope
- 2,201 (61%) presented with syncope

**Primary Outcome:** 30-day incidence of death or serious clinical events (cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, cardiac interventions, stroke, pulmonary embolism, and other conditions per 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS guidelines)

**Key Quantitative Result:**
- Near-syncope: **18.7%** 30-day serious event rate
- Syncope: **18.2%** 30-day serious event rate
- **No significant difference** (Odds Ratio [OR] 0.94; 95% Confidence Interval [CI] 0.78–1.14)

**Additional Critical Findings:**
- Near-syncope patients actually had *higher* rates of congestive heart failure, coronary artery disease, previous arrhythmias, nonwhite race, and presenting dyspnea compared to the syncope group
- Emergency physicians had difficulty predicting whether near-syncope patients would experience a serious outcome (area under ROC: 0.58)

**Conclusion from the Authors:** "Near-syncope confers risk to patients similar to that of syncope for the composite outcome of 30-day death or serious clinical event." "These two entities should be considered as one when decisions are made in terms of evaluation in the ED" [38].

### 3.3 Suh et al. (2025) — The Most Recent Confirmation

Published online September 23, 2025, in the Annals of Emergency Medicine, Suh et al. conducted a secondary analysis of the PACES multicenter cohort study [39].

**Study Population:** 1,263 adults aged ≥40 years (43% presyncope, 57% syncope)

**Primary Outcome:** Serious cardiac outcomes at 30 days
- Syncope: **4.7%**
- Presyncope: **5.2%**
- **No significant difference** (adjusted odds ratio 1.13; 95% CI 0.66 to 1.79)

**Critical Finding on Physician Perception vs. Reality:**
- Mean physician-estimated risk of serious cardiac outcomes: **7.6%** for syncope vs. only **5.3%** for presyncope — a significant disparity
- Admission rate: **49.5%** for syncope vs. **38.2%** for presyncope

The study demonstrates that despite equivalent risk (4.7% vs. 5.2%), physicians perceive presyncope as lower risk and admit fewer presyncope patients. The conclusion is clear: **"Patients with unexplained presyncope and syncope had similar rates of serious cardiac outcomes at 30 days after their ED visit."**

### 3.4 Grossman et al. (2012) — Earlier Landmark Comparison

Published in the American Journal of Emergency Medicine, this prospective observational study at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center compared 244 near-syncope patients with 293 syncope patients [40].

- ED hospitalization or 30-day adverse outcomes: **20%** near-syncope vs. **23%** syncope (P = 0.40 — no significant difference)
- Near-syncope patients were significantly less likely to be admitted: **49%** vs. **69%** (P = 0.001)
- Critical interventions included: hemorrhage, cardiac ischemia, arrhythmias, sepsis, stroke, and death

**Conclusion:** "Patients with near syncope are as likely as those with syncope to experience critical interventions or adverse outcomes; however, near-syncope patients are less likely to be admitted."

### 3.5 García Reverte et al. (2004) — Structural Heart Disease Study

This study specifically examined patients with structural heart disease [41]. Among 449 patients admitted to a cardiology department (272 with syncope, 177 with presyncope):
- Arrhythmic mechanism in syncope patients: **25.7%**
- Arrhythmic mechanism in presyncope patients: **22.0%**
- **No significant difference** (P = 0.37)
- Long-term survival curves were similar over mean follow-up of 57.4 months

**Conclusion:** "The clinical characteristics and the long-term prognosis in patients with structural heart disease admitted for presyncope are similar to those of patients admitted for syncope."

### 3.6 Systematic Reviews

**Mirfazaelian et al. (2024) — Academic Emergency Medicine Systematic Review [42]:**
- 5 studies, 2,741 presyncope patients
- 30-day serious outcome rate: **4.4% to 26.8%** (wide range depending on study population and definition)
- Most common serious outcome: **cardiac events, particularly arrhythmias** (SVT, sinus node dysfunction, new/uncontrolled atrial fibrillation)
- "The type of short-term serious outcomes are similar for ED patients with syncope and presyncope"

**JournalFeed (2024) Analysis:**
- Commentary from Keith Wrenn: **"Presyncope IS Syncope. He was right."**
- "It is reasonable to treat presyncope with as much respect as syncope in the emergency department"

### 3.7 Guideline Statements on Presyncope

**2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guideline [2]:**
The guideline defines presyncope as "the symptoms before syncope" and establishes the framework for risk stratification. The serious clinical events defined in this guideline (cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, new structural heart disease, stroke, pulmonary embolism) are the outcomes used in the Bastani and Suh studies [2][38][39].

**2018 ESC Syncope Guidelines [43]:**
The ESC guidelines define syncope as "transient loss of consciousness due to cerebral hypoperfusion" and note that "the adjective presyncope is used to indicate symptoms and signs that occur before unconsciousness in syncope." The guidelines recommend risk stratification that applies to both syncope and presyncope [43].

**2024 EUSEM Syncope Group Consensus [44]:**
This consensus emphasizes systematic, protocol-driven management for patients with suspected syncope and presyncope to exclude life-threatening causes and optimize outcomes.

### 3.8 Diagnostic Yield of Monitoring in Presyncope Patients

**Letsas et al. (2024) — Hellenic ILR Registry [45]:**
- 871 patients receiving implantable loop recorders
- For presyncope/syncope indication (61.9% of patients): **31% diagnostic yield**
- Arrhythmias detected: pause (70.4%), bradycardia (18.4%), SVT (5.2%), VT (3.1%), AF (1.0%)
- ILR findings led to therapeutic strategy changes in **26.1%** of presyncope/syncope patients

**Nierop et al. (2000) — Heart Rhythm During Syncope and Presyncope [46]:**
- 35 patients with ILRs, mean follow-up 11 months
- Symptom-rhythm correlation achieved in 83% of patients
- Arrhythmic causes in both syncope and presyncope patients were documented

**Korean ILR Registry (2024) [47]:**
- 394 patients with unexplained syncope receiving ILRs
- Serious arrhythmias identified in **52.0%** of patients
- Median time to diagnosis: 62 days
- Sick sinus syndrome: 43.7%, AV block: 6.1%

### 3.9 Implications for This Patient

For the 68-year-old man in this case:
- His **presyncope (near-syncope) should be managed with the same level of caution as frank syncope**
- The 30-day serious adverse event risk is approximately **18-19%** based on his age and presentation
- Admission and urgent cardiology consultation are warranted on the basis of presyncope alone, independent of whether the patient lost consciousness
- The decision to admit or pursue aggressive monitoring should be made as if the patient had suffered true syncope, because the risk profile is identical

## Part 4: Contingency for Failed Admission — Expedited Outpatient Follow-Up Within 48-72 Hours

### 4.1 The Preferred Pathway: Inpatient Admission

The preferred and guideline-recommended pathway for this patient is **inpatient admission to telemetry**, as detailed in the original report. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline gives a **Class I recommendation** for hospital evaluation and treatment for patients with syncope who have a serious medical condition potentially relevant to the cause [1][2]. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline further specifies that acquired Mobitz type II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block requires permanent pacing regardless of symptoms, and these decisions are best made in the inpatient setting [1][16].

However, the reality of clinical practice is that insurance denials and hospital capacity constraints may prevent admission. The following contingency plan is designed for scenarios where admission is truly not feasible, with the understanding that this represents a deviation from the standard of care that must be documented and risk-mitigated.

### 4.2 Evidence for the 48-72 Hour Follow-Up Window

The evidence supports a **strict 48-72 hour follow-up window** for patients with suspected intermittent high-grade AV block who cannot be admitted [48][49]:

- **Schreiber et al. (West J Emerg Med, 2014):** In ED patients discharged with ambulatory monitors, the median time to first arrhythmia was **1.0 days (IQR 0.2-2.8 days)**, and median time to first symptomatic arrhythmia was **1.5 days (IQR 0.4-6.7 days)** [17]. This means that 50% of arrhythmias occur within the first 24 hours of discharge and 75% within 3 days.

- **REMOSYNC Trial:** The current standard for ambulatory monitoring in high-risk syncope patients (CSRS ≥3) is **48-hour Holter monitoring** after ED discharge, implying that 48 hours is the accepted window for initial surveillance [50].

- **OCPP (Outpatient Chest Pain Pathway) Study:** The 72-hour follow-up window used for intermediate-risk patients demonstrated safety, with a 0.4% 30-day AMI/death rate, significantly lower than the 2.2% rate in the historical cohort [51].

- **Marshall Health Rapid Access Chest Pain Clinic:** Requires patients to be seen within **48 hours** of their ER visit [52].

- **Holy Cross Health MD ASAP Clinic:** Offers appointments within **24 hours** Monday through Friday [53].

**The evidence supports that 48-72 hours is the maximum safe window for follow-up; 7 days is too long** given the median time to first arrhythmia of 1 day and the risk of progression to complete heart block.

### 4.3 Hospital-Based Cardiology Rapid Access Clinics

Several models of rapid access cardiology clinics exist that can accommodate same-day or next-day follow-up from the ED:

**Marshall Health Rapid Access Chest Pain Clinic (Huntington, WV) [52]:**
- Provides urgent cardiology assessments for patients evaluated in the ED where MI has been ruled out
- Patients must be seen within **48 hours** of their ER visit
- Operates Monday through Thursday by appointment only
- Offers same-day diagnostic tests: blood tests, ECG stress tests, CT scans, echocardiograms, myocardial perfusion scans
- Patients must bring insurance information, ER discharge papers, and a current medication list

**Holy Cross Health MD ASAP Cardiac Rapid Response Clinic (Fort Lauderdale, FL) [53]:**
- Launched February 1, 2024
- Patients can be seen within **24 hours**, Monday through Friday, 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.
- Led by Dr. Charles Russo, supported by nurse practitioners and heart failure specialists
- Accepts referrals from physicians, urgent care, emergency medicine providers, or self-referral
- Contact: 954-542-2650; appointments via MyChart portal

**Beacon Hospital Rapid Access Cardiology Clinic (RACC) (Dublin, Ireland) [54]:**
- Consultant-led one-stop diagnostic service for new or recurring cardiac symptoms

- Suitable for clinically and hemodynamically stable patients
- Operates Monday to Friday, 8am-8pm

**Charing Cross Hospital Rapid Access Cardiology Service (London, UK) [55]:**
- Prospective cohort study of 1,223 patients seen without appointment over one year
- Demonstrated **76% positive predictive value** and **98% negative predictive value** for diagnosing cardiac disease

- Patients with initial non-cardiac diagnoses had very low subsequent cardiac mortality (0.3%)

### 4.4 Arranging the Follow-Up from the ED: Step-by-Step Protocol

#### Step 1: Identify Available Rapid Access Resources

Before discharge, the ED physician or case manager should identify:

1. Does the hospital have an internal **Rapid Access Cardiology Clinic** or **Same-Day Emergency Care (SDEC)** cardiology pathway?
2. Is there a **hospital-based cardiology group** that accepts ED referrals for urgent outpatient evaluation?
3. Does the health system have a **tele-cardiology service** that can arrange virtual follow-up within 48-72 hours?
4. Are there **community cardiology practices** that accept urgent referrals with documented clinical urgency?

The NHS England SDEC model, which requires every acute hospital with a Type 1 ED to operate a comprehensive SDEC for a minimum of 12 hours a day, 7 days a week, provides a template for rapid access care [56].

#### Step 2: Directly Schedule the Appointment

The ED should **directly schedule the cardiology appointment** before discharge, not simply give the patient a phone number to call. Studies show that direct scheduling from the ED significantly improves follow-up completion rates [25][51].

**Required information for the scheduling request [27][33]:**
- Patient demographics and contact information
- Clinical indication: "Suspected intermittent high-grade AV block / sick sinus syndrome"
- Clinical urgency: "High-risk presentation per ACC/AHA/HRS guidelines — requires evaluation within 48-72 hours"
- Key clinical details: ECG findings (sinus bradycardia, variable PR 180-240ms, dropped beats), symptoms (near-syncope, fatigue), current medications (metoprolol — held)
- Any prior testing results (Holter denied by insurance, appeal pending)

#### Step 3: Use Clinical Documentation to Expedite Scheduling

The following documentation language should be used to convey urgency and facilitate expedited scheduling [27][33][57]:

**Key Phrases:**
- "This patient meets **high-risk criteria** per the 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guideline and the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline"
- "Suspected intermittent high-grade AV block or sick sinus syndrome with symptomatic bradycardia"
- "30-day risk of serious adverse events: **18-19%** based on validated risk stratification tools"
- "Three ED visits in one month for near-syncope with ECG evidence of conduction disease"
- "Expedited evaluation within 48-72 hours is medically necessary to prevent adverse outcomes"
- "Admission was not feasible due to [insurance denial/capacity constraints]"

#### Step 4: Virtual Visit/Telehealth Options

While general telehealth platforms (Atrium Health, Ballad Health Quick Care) explicitly exclude management of potential cardiac emergencies and are **not appropriate** for this patient [58][59], dedicated cardiology telehealth services may be an option:

**Core Cardiology Telehealth:**
- Offers telehealth appointments for arrhythmia evaluation
- Provides two-way high-definition video, digital patient registration
- HIPAA-compliant
- Insurance coverage varies; Medicare coverage for telehealth was expanded due to COVID-19 [60]

**Limitation:** No published studies were found specifically evaluating virtual cardiology consultation within 48-72 hours for ED patients with suspected bradyarrhythmias. **In-person evaluation with ECG capabilities is strongly preferred** for this presentation.

### 4.5 Bridge Strategy: Wearable Cardiac Monitoring at Discharge

If admission is not feasible, a **14-day ambulatory cardiac monitor (Zio XT or CAM patch) should be applied at ED discharge** to provide continuous monitoring during the follow-up window [17][61]:

**Zio XT Patch (iRhythm Technologies) [17][62]:**
- Single-use continuous recording patch worn for up to 14 days
- Patients mail the device back for interpretation (98.6% analyzable time)
- Study by Schreiber et al.: 47.7% of ED patients had at least one significant arrhythmia
- Median wear time: 6.9 days
- Overall diagnostic yield: **63.2%** — considerably higher than traditional 48-hour Holter
- Serious arrhythmias (VT >120 for 30 sec, complete heart block, symptomatic second-degree type II, pause >6 sec, symptomatic bradycardia <40 for >30 sec) trigger immediate physician notification
- iRhythm Direct Bill program: bills insurance directly, offers discounted self-pay rates, payment plans up to 12 months, and financial assistance. Call 1.888.693.2401

**Carnation Ambulatory Monitor (CAM) — Bardy Diagnostics [63]:**
- P-wave centric, sternal placement, wire-free, up to 14 days wear
- Identified 2.8x more patients with arrhythmias vs. Holter
- Published ED data (Overlake Medical Center, 412 patients): Reduced time to diagnosis from 21 to 7 days; reduced 30-day ED readmissions from 40% to 7% [63]

**Practical Implementation:**
1. Apply the patch in the ED before discharge
2. Provide the patient with the device's return mailer
3. Instruct the patient to keep a symptom diary
4. The monitoring company will provide results to the ordering physician (who should be the cardiologist seeing the patient for follow-up)

### 4.6 Navigating Insurance Barriers to Rapid Follow-Up

Insurance barriers are a significant challenge. The following strategies can help [57][64]:

**Prior Authorization Denial Rates and Strategies:**
- Prior authorization denials for cardiology procedures range from **18% to 25%** [57]
- **Leading cause of denial:** Insufficient clinical documentation that fails to demonstrate medical necessity
- **Citing current clinical guidelines (ACC/AHA/HRS, ESC) significantly increases the likelihood of authorization** [57]

**UnitedHealthcare Cardiology Prior Authorization Program [64]:**
- Prior authorization required for: diagnostic catheterization, electrophysiology implants, stress echocardiograms in outpatient/office settings
- **Urgent requests:** Call 866-889-8054 (option 1, then option 3)
- "The ordering health care professional must state that the case is 'clinically urgent' and explain the clinical urgency"
- **Response time: within 3 hours** of receipt of all required information
- Retrospective review available for urgent procedures outside normal business hours
- Board-certified cardiologists review requests; ordering providers may request physician-to-physician discussion
- Prior authorization number valid for **45 calendar days**
- **Services in emergency rooms, urgent care centers, hospital observation units, and inpatient settings are NOT subject to prior authorization** [64]

**Clinical Documentation to Support Authorization:**
- "This patient meets ACC/AHA/HRS high-risk criteria for cardiac syncope"
- "30-day risk of serious adverse events: 18-19%"
- "Suspected intermittent high-grade AV block requiring urgent electrophysiology evaluation"
- "Three ED visits in one month with no prior outpatient monitoring due to insurance denial"
- "Expedited evaluation within 48-72 hours is medically necessary per evidence-based guidelines"

### 4.7 Safety Net Planning for Outpatient Management

If the patient is discharged with planned outpatient follow-up, the following safety net measures must be implemented and documented [65][66]:

**Patient and Family Education (Using Teach-Back Method):**
1. **Return precautions:** Return to the ED immediately for any of the following:
   - Fainting or loss of consciousness
   - Severe dizziness or near-syncope
   - Falls or head trauma
   - Chest pain or palpitations
   - Shortness of breath
   - Confusion or mental status changes

2. **Warning signs of worsening AV block [67]:**
   - Lightheadedness
   - Dizziness
   - Fatigue
   - Palpitations
   - Syncope
   - Chest pain

3. **Driving restrictions:** The patient should **not drive** until the diagnosis is established and treatment is initiated. Discuss state-specific reporting requirements.

4. **Fall precautions:** Review home safety measures (remove throw rugs, ensure adequate lighting, use handrails, avoid stairs when alone).

5. **Family supervision:** Advise family members to stay with the patient when possible, especially during the first 48-72 hours.

**Contingency Plan Documentation [65][68]:**

The discharge summary and ED record should document:
- The specific follow-up appointment (date, time, location, provider name)
- The ambulatory monitor applied and its purpose
- The return precautions provided
- The driving restriction discussed
- Shared decision-making regarding discharge vs. admission, including discussion of risks and benefits
- The reasons admission was not feasible (insurance denial, capacity constraints)
- The risk-mitigation strategies implemented (monitor, expedited follow-up, safety netting)
- Patient capacity assessment and understanding of instructions

**Medicolegal Documentation [65][68]:**

The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS guideline is clear: acquired Mobitz type II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block requires pacing "regardless of symptoms." Discharging a patient with these findings without admission deviates from the standard of care unless the block is proven to be reversible [1][16]. Therefore:

- Document risk stratification tools used (SFSR, CSRS)
- Document specific ECG findings and why the block is not considered high-grade, Mobitz II, or third-degree at the time of discharge (if that is the case)
- Document that reversible causes (medications, metabolic disturbances, thyroid disorders, sleep apnea) have been evaluated and addressed
- Document that shared decision-making occurred
- Document that the patient received and understood return precautions
- The standard is admission; if discharge is chosen, the burden of proof for the safety of that decision rests on the discharging provider

### 4.8 Evidence-Based Timeframe Summary for Outpatient Follow-Up

| Clinical Scenario | Recommended Follow-Up Window | Evidence |
|-------------------|------------------------------|----------|
| Confirmed Mobitz II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block | **Admission required; discharge not evidence-based** | 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline (Class I recommendation for pacing) [1][16] |
| Suspected intermittent high-grade AV block (normal ED ECG but concerning history/symptoms) | **48-72 hours maximum** | Median time to first arrhythmia = 1 day [17]; REMOSYNC 48-hour standard [50] |
| Symptomatic first-degree AV block with PR >300ms | **72 hours maximum** | ACC/AHA/HRS Class IIa for pacing consideration [1] |
| Asymptomatic first-degree AV block | **1-2 weeks** | Standard outpatient follow-up acceptable [1] |
| Asymptomatic Mobitz I (Wenckebach) | **1-2 weeks** | Standard outpatient follow-up acceptable [1] |
| Sick sinus syndrome (suspected) | **48-72 hours** | Condition not immediately life-threatening but can cause symptomatic pauses [69] |

### 4.9 If 48-72 Hour Follow-Up Cannot Be Arranged

If rapid access cardiology cannot be arranged within 48-72 hours, the following options should be considered [3][27][57]:

1. **Transfer to a facility with available cardiology/EP services** — This may be necessary if the patient's risk profile warrants in-person evaluation that cannot be provided at the current facility.

2. **Re-address admission with the hospitalist and medical director** — If insurance denial was the barrier, a physician-to-physician appeal citing ACC/AHA/HRS guidelines may overturn the denial. The 2018 guidelines provide strong justification for admission [1][2].

3. **Consider implantable loop recorder (ILR) referral** — If external monitoring is contraindicated or non-diagnostic, the ILR has a diagnostic yield of 78% within 1 year for unexplained syncope and is recommended as a Class IIa intervention by ACC/AHA/HRS [2][45].

4. **Document the inability to arrange follow-up and its implications** — If expedited follow-up cannot be arranged, this represents a system failure that increases the patient's risk. This should be documented in the medical record and escalated through the hospital's risk management or quality improvement channels.
