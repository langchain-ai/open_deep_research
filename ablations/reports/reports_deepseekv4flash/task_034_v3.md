# Vertical Integration Strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) in Cocoa Processing (2018–2026): A Comprehensively Revised and Deepened Analysis

**Date of Analysis: May 28, 2026**

---

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensively revised analysis of the vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam Food Ingredients (ofi) in cocoa processing from 2018 to 2026, incorporating rigorous source vetting, systematic quantitative tracking, explicit methodological scrutiny, and detailed regulatory timeline mapping.

The three companies collectively process approximately 60% of the world's cocoa. The analysis reveals a sector undergoing transformative convergence driven by the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), evolving sustainability disclosure obligations, and unprecedented cocoa price volatility (prices surged to ~$12,906/tonne in December 2024 before collapsing to ~$2,886/tonne in February 2026, then recovering to ~$3,889/tonne by May 2026).

**Key findings include:**

- **Olam/ofi leads in explicit direct-sourcing disclosure** (67% direct, disclosed in 2020), while Barry Callebaut's ~40% direct figure and Cargill's ~60% direct figure come from third-party or historical sources. All face significant traceability challenges in indirect supply chains.
- **Cargill has invested the most in West African processing capacity** during the analysis window ($113 million plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan).
- **Farmer income evidence remains dominated by company-commissioned research.** The gap between poverty line metrics (~$2.15/day) and living income benchmarks ($6,372–$8,039/year) remains vast. Independent studies show only 4–13% of farmers achieve living income.
- **All three companies are EUDR-ready in their direct supply chains,** but none have publicly disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions. Independent NGO investigations have documented deforestation linked to all three processors' supply chains.
- **Ferrero's published tier-1 supplier list confirms Olam/ofi's absence** from its direct supply chain while Barry Callebaut and Cargill are included – a significant competitive finding.
- **The EUDR deadline is now December 30, 2026** (large operators), with a mandated simplification review resulting in a May 4, 2026 simplification package.
- **Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate division split** remains under active consideration but unconfirmed. Cargill's "flexibility" strategy and Olam's AtSource platform differentiation represent key competitive vectors.

## 1. Direct-Sourced vs. Broker-Purchased Cocoa Percentages: Historical Trajectory and Current Disclosure Status

### 1.1 Barry Callebaut

**Current Disclosure Status (as of May 2026):** Barry Callebaut does **not formally disclose** a standalone percentage for direct-sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa in its 2024/25 Annual Social and Environmental Impact Report or GRI Report.

**Historical Data Points:**

| Year | Direct Sourcing % | Source Type | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| 2016 (baseline) | Not disclosed | Company report | Forever Chocolate target set |
| 2018/19 | Not specified | Company report | 176,984 farms in Katchilè database |
| 2021 | ~40% direct, ~60% indirect | Third-party study (IDH/GISCO) | IDH/GISCO Traceability Case Study explicitly states: "The traceability system focuses on the direct supply chain, accounting for 40% of sourced cocoa" [1] |
| 2022 (Sept) | "Around half of world bean supply is sourced indirectly" | Company executive quote | Steven Retzlaff, President Global Cocoa, quoted in press article [2] |
| 2024/25 | Not updated | Latest company report | No updated direct vs. indirect percentage |

**Data Quality Flag:** The 40% direct figure comes from a 2021 IDH-commissioned case study, not from Barry Callebaut's own annual disclosure. The company has not updated this percentage in any official filing since 2020-2021.

**What Barry Callebaut Actually Reports:** The 2024/25 Impact Report distinguishes between direct and indirect supply chains operationally: "We have achieved full traceability to farm level for all cocoa volumes sourced directly for the European market. For indirectly sourced volumes, we rely on our suppliers and are actively working with them to meet the same rigorous traceability standards we apply to our own direct sourcing" [3].

**Intermediate Target:** The company set a 2025 target (since achieved) of full traceability for its direct supply chain to the EU, but did not set a specific target for reducing the indirect proportion.

### 1.2 Cargill

**Current Disclosure Status (as of May 2026):** Cargill **does not publicly disclose** the specific percentage or volume of cocoa sourced directly from farmer cooperatives versus through third-party brokers/intermediaries in its 2024 or 2025 Cocoa Impact Reports.

**Historical Data Points:**

| Year | Direct Sourcing % | Source Type | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| ~2012 | ~60% direct, ~40% indirect | Third-party estimate (IDH/C-Lever.org) | The IDH Case Study indicating 60/40 split is approximately a decade old [4] |
| 2021 | Not updated | Company report | Cargill sources directly from 6 countries, indirectly from 19 others |
| 2023/24 | Not disclosed | Company report | 54% of cocoa volumes sold were "sustainable" |
| 2024/25 | Not disclosed | Company report | Over 180,000 certified farmers in Cocoa Promise program |

**Proxy Indicators:** The U.S. Department of Labor's April 2026 report corroborates that "around 40–60% of cocoa moves through indirect supply chains with limited traceability" [5]. Cargill's sustainability program coverage "accounts for about one third of the cocoa we source" according to KPMG-assured data [6].

**Data Quality Flag:** Cargill has never explicitly disclosed a direct vs. indirect sourcing ratio in its own reports. The 60/40 figure is a decade-old estimate from a third-party study.

### 1.3 Olam (ofi)

**Current Disclosure Status (as of May 2026):** Olam remains the **only one of the three companies to have explicitly disclosed a direct-sourcing percentage.** On September 22, 2020, Olam Cocoa announced that its directly sourced cocoa constitutes **approximately two-thirds (66.7%) of its global cocoa purchases** across nine countries, while the remaining one-third (approximately 33.3%) is sourced indirectly through traders and intermediaries [7][8].

**Historical Data Points:**

| Year | Direct Sourcing % | Source Type | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| 2020 (Sept) | ~67% direct, ~33% indirect | Official company press release | "Directly sourced cocoa constitutes two-thirds of its global cocoa supply" [7] |
| 2021 | Confirmed ~67% direct | Annual report statement | "Tracking 12% of the world's cocoa beans back to an individual farm or community" |
| 2024 | Not updated | Company report | 100% traceability in direct supply chain reaffirmed but ratio not updated |

**Data Quality Flag:** The 67/33 direct/indirect split has **not been publicly updated since 2020.** Olam Group's subsequent annual reports do not contain a line-item disclosure of this ratio.

**Critical Caveat (All Three Processors):** The U.S. Department of Labor (April 2026) notes that "co-mingling of cocoa beans begins at or near the first point of aggregation and continues at every subsequent stage of the supply chain, making farm-level traceability structurally difficult to maintain even where it is attempted" [5].

**Independent Verification:** All data collected through the Katchilè system is **independently verified by PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC)** [1][3].

## 5. Farmer Income Metrics Under Sustainability Programs: Methodological Comparison with Rigorous Caveats

### 5.1 Evidence Quality Overview

**Critical finding:** No truly independent, peer-reviewed academic study or standalone NGO impact assessment was found that specifically quantifies farmer income changes under any of the three companies' programs using rigorous counterfactual methods (randomized controlled trials, difference-in-difference, or propensity score matching). The available evidence comes from company-commissioned reports, company-partnered research, or industry-wide studies that do not isolate individual company impacts.

**Evidence Hierarchy Applied in This Analysis:**
1. **Level 1 (Highest):** Independent peer-reviewed academic studies with counterfactual methods
2. **Level 2:** Independent NGO or multilateral investigations with documented methodology
3. **Level 3:** Company-commissioned research with independent assurance (PwC, KPMG, FLOCERT)
4. **Level 4 (Lowest):** Company self-reported data without independent assurance

**No Level 1 evidence** was found for any of the three companies' cocoa sustainability programs.

### 5.2 Barry Callebaut

**Company-Reported Data (PwC-audited):**
- **557,739 cocoa farmers lifted above the poverty line** in 2024/25, a 30% increase from 428,997 in 2023/24
- Uses the **World Bank International Poverty Line threshold of USD 1.90/day** (later updated to ~$2.15/day) [26]

**Year-by-Year "Farmers Out of Poverty" Progression:**

| Reporting Year | Number Above Poverty Line | Benchmark | % Change | Source |
|---------------|--------------------------|-----------|----------|--------|
| 2016/17 | Baseline established | $1.90/day | — | Forever Chocolate target set |
| 2018/19 | 184,623 | $1.90/day | — | Progress Report |
| 2019/20 | 143,233 | $1.90/day | -22.4% (methodology change likely) | GRI Report [27] |
| 2020/21 | 214,584 | $1.90/day | +49.8% | Progress Report |
| 2021/22 | 214,124 | $1.90/day | -0.2% (nearly flat) | GRI Report |
| 2022/23 | Not reported as single number | Yield/income focus shift | — | Report shifted methodology |
| 2023/24 | Not reported as single number | — | — | |
| 2024/25 | **557,739** | $1.90/day (World Bank) | Major jump | Annual Impact Report [3] |

**Methodological Caveats:**
- **Measurement Period:** Fiscal year (September 1 to August 31) [3]
- **Calculation Methodology:** Model-based projection using survey data from the Katchilè database. The term "estimate" is used consistently, indicating a model-based rather than direct measurement approach [27][26].
- **Sample Size:** Varied by year, from ~130,000 farms in 2016/17 to 1.5 million farms by 2024/25
- **Benchmark Used:** World Bank International Poverty Line (~$2.15/day) — significantly lower than the Living Income Benchmark (~$6,372–$8,039/year)
- **Attribution:** The company's own collaborative research (Agri-Logic White Paper 2026, co-funded by Barry Callebaut, IDH, and Rainforest Alliance) shows that only **4% of Côte d'Ivoire farmers** and **11% of Ghana farmers** achieve a living income benchmark [28].
- **Living Income Findings from Agri-Logic Paper (2023/24 season):** Vietnam: 40-45%; Cameroon: 40-45%; Ghana: 11%; Côte d'Ivoire: 4%. "Farmers in liberalized markets (Vietnam and Cameroon) are 2.2 times more likely to reach Living Income than those in regulated markets (Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire)" [28].
- **Independent Verification:** PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) provides assurance on Forever Chocolate metrics [3].

**Key Anomaly:** The 2019/20 number dropped from 184,623 to 143,233. The report states "143,233 cocoa farmers (+37%) in our supply chain are out of poverty" — likely indicating the percentage increase from a lower baseline (approximately 104,000), suggesting the measurement methodology or coverage was refined, or COVID-19 impacted projections [27].

**Exogenous Factor Caveat:** The jump to 557,739 in 2024/25 coincides with the cocoa price surge to ~$12,000/tonne. The company does not quantify how much of this increase is attributable to program interventions versus market conditions.

### 5.3 Cargill

**Company-Reported Data (KPMG Limited Assurance):**
- In Brazil: **32% average productivity increase** and **160% income growth** from technical assistance programs [12]
- Through Nestlé's Income Accelerator Program: **32% increase in cocoa production** and **38% increase in net income** among participating households [12]
- Over 10,300 households enrolled in the Nestlé Income Accelerator Program as of 2025 [12]

**Independent/Third-Party Assessments:**

| Study | Year | Finding | Methodology |
|-------|------|---------|-------------|
| IDH SDM Analysis of Cargill in Côte d'Ivoire | 2021 | Average cocoa farmer incomes ~$2,700 vs. $5,676 living income benchmark (48% of benchmark) | Survey-based, 102 households |
| Impact Institute Living Income Analysis (San Pedro) | 2021-2022 | Average annual household income $3,132 (45% of $6,904 benchmark); 94% below LIB | 102 cocoa-farming households |
| Rainforest Alliance / Oxfam America | 2024 | "Only 13% of cocoa farming households in CIV earn a living income"; Ghana: 10% | Sector-wide data |

**Benchmarks Used:**
- Cargill states: "Until we have defined clear regional benchmarks for living income for cocoa smallholder farmers we can refer to the World Bank's poverty line as a US$2 a day reliable equivalent" [29]
- Living Income Application developed with IDH and Deloitte to "model farmer incomes and measure intervention impacts" [12]

**Methodological Caveats:**
- Cargill does **not** publish absolute farmer income figures (e.g., "average farmer earns USD X per year")
- The Living Income Application methodology is internal and not independently audited
- The IDH and Impact Institute studies cited are independent but do not isolate Cargill-specific program impacts
- No sample sizes for Cargill-specific farmer income surveys are disclosed in public reports
- KPMG assurance covers "selected sustainability indicators" but farmer income figures may not be within the assured scope [6]

### 5.4 Olam (ofi)

**Company-Reported Data (Cocoa Compass Metrics):**
- **155,000 farmers (45% of sustainability program participants) earning a living income** – surpassing the 2030 target of 150,000 six years early [30]
- Increase "largely driven by cocoa market price increases and livelihood support" [30]
- 320,000 cocoa farmers receiving livelihood support annually [30]
- 257,000 farmers trained annually in Good Agricultural Practices [30]

**Living Income Benchmarks Used:**

| Country | Source | Annual LIB | Date |
|---------|--------|------------|------|
| Côte d'Ivoire | Anker Research Institute | CFA 322,225/month (~USD 6,792/year) | June 2025 update [31] |
| Côte d'Ivoire (original) | Anker Research Institute | CFA 262,056/month (~USD 4,680/year) | August 2018 |
| Ghana | SWISSCO/COCOBOD | GHS 52,970 (~USD 4,315/year) | Not specified |

**Methodology:**
- ofi uses a **Cocoa Farmer Income Tool** that "offers detailed income estimates to help address earnings gaps" [32]
- The **Living Income Gap Heuristic Tool (LIGHT)** is "helping to gain insights and develop ways to improve farmer household incomes" [32]
- Living income defined by **Living Income Community of Practice (LICoP)**: "The net annual income required for a household in a particular place to afford a decent standard of living for all members of that household"

**Independent Verification Status:**
- **No independent assurance provider is explicitly named** for the "155,000 farmers earning a living income" metric in the publicly available sources
- Olam ended its **Fair Labor Association (FLA) membership in January 2026** [33]
- FLA's May 2022 assessment found 72% of producers believed child labor decreasing, but **44% were still at risk** of using children under 13 in farm work [33]

**Exogenous Factor Caveat (Critical):** The living income achievement was "largely driven by cocoa market price increases" (prices peaked at ~$12,000/tonne in late 2024). The 2025/26 price plunge to ~$4,000/tonne (and further to ~$2,886/tonne in February 2026) may materially reduce the number of farmers earning a living income absent program-driven improvements. The company acknowledged this: "Farmer incomes are influenced by multiple factors such as weather and markets prices, which has seen high volatility in the last three years" [34].

- **AtSource+:** Granular metrics, advanced environmental footprints, annual third-party verification; **FSA Gold equivalence**
- **AtSource∞:** Aspirational level focusing on landscape regeneration

**Competitive Differentiation:**
- Over **100 environmental and social metrics** on carbon, social impact, water use
- **Carbon Scenario Planner** – helps customers assess carbon reduction pathways
- **Cocoa Farmer Income Tool** – assesses living income and guides targeted support
- **SAI Platform FSA equivalence** – reduces audit burden for farmers

**Key Risk for Olam:** The living income achievement (155,000 farmers) was "largely driven by cocoa market price increases." The 2025/26 price plunge to ~$4,000/tonne (and to ~$2,886 in February 2026) may materially reduce this number absent program-driven improvements. **Ferrero's exclusion from tier-1 supplier list** remains a significant competitive gap.

### 9.8 Price Volatility Scenarios and Farmer Income Implications

**Price History (2024-2026):**
- **December 2024 peak:** ~$12,906/tonne (ICE New York)
- **February 2026 low:** ~$2,886/tonne (three-year low)
- **May 28, 2026:** ~$3,889/tonne (recovering but still -70% from peak)
- **Market balance:** Projected surplus of 287,000 tonnes for 2025/26 (StoneX); another 267,000-tonne surplus expected for 2026/27

**Scenario 1: Sustained Low Prices (~$3,500–$4,000/tonne)**
- Ghana farmgate price: ~$3,580/tonne (GH₵2,587/bag as of February 2026)
- Farmer income: Significantly below living income benchmarks (LIB is $6,372-$8,039/year)
- Company implications:
  - **Barry Callebaut:** Recurring net profit declined 35.9% in FY 2024/25; Gourmet segment suffered from long position in declining market
  - **Cargill:** Not publicly traded; private ownership buffers short-term pressure but sustainability program budgets may face constraints
  - **Olam/ofi:** ofi's living income metric (155,000 farmers) would face significant downward revision as the price-driven component evaporates

**Scenario 2: Recovery to ~$6,000/tonne (J.P. Morgan medium-term expectation)**
- Farmer income: Improved but still below LIB for most households
- Company implications: More sustainable operating environment; certification premiums more viable
- Risk: Structural supply-side issues in CIV and GH "will persist, and prices are unlikely to return to historical levels within the next two years" (Rabobank analyst Oran van Dort)

**Scenario 3: Sustained High Prices ($8,000+/tonne)**
- Farmer income: Improved but regulatory pricing systems (Ghana/CIV fixed prices) limit pass-through
- Company implications: Volume demand destruction accelerates; product reformulation and cocoa alternatives gain traction

## 13. Conclusions and Competitive Outlook

### 13.1 Convergence and Differentiation

The vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have converged significantly over the 2018–2026 period, driven by three primary forces: EUDR compliance requirements, premium chocolate manufacturers' demands for verifiably sustainable cocoa, and unprecedented price volatility. However, important differentiation remains.

**On direct sourcing:** Olam leads with the only explicit disclosure of a 67% direct sourcing ratio (2020). Barry Callebaut's ~40% direct sourcing figure and Cargill's ~60% estimate suggest significant room for growth in direct supply chain control. None of the three companies has updated their direct-sourcing disclosure since 2020–2021.

**On traceability and mapping:** All three companies have achieved 100% polygon mapping or traceability in their direct supply chains. Barry Callebaut's 1.5 million farms mapped, Cargill's 100% of Promise supply chain in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana, and Olam's 100% of sustainability programs mapped represent significant investments. However, indirect supply chains remain a critical vulnerability for all three.

**On capital investments:** Cargill invested the most in West African processing capacity during the 2018–2024 period ($113 million plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan). Olam's major investments predated the analysis window, though Olam Agri's $200 million expansion signals renewed commitment.

**On farmer income:** All three companies report positive income impacts, but the evidence base relies entirely on company-commissioned or co-authored research. The gap between "poverty line" metrics (~$2.15–$3.00/day) and "living income" benchmarks ($6,372–$8,039/year) remains vast. Independent studies consistently show that only 4–13% of farmers achieve living income levels.

**On EUDR compliance:** All three companies have invested heavily in traceability systems, satellite monitoring, and polygon mapping. Barry Callebaut and Cargill appear most advanced in farm mapping, while Olam benefits from its earlier 2020 traceability milestone. None have disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions, which remains a critical transparency gap. Independent audits have documented deforestation linkages for all three processors.

**On competitive positioning:** Ferrero's public supplier list confirms Barry Callebaut and Cargill as tier-1 suppliers while Olam is absent – a significant competitive blow. Mondelēz works with all three processors but has a notably deep partnership with Barry Callebaut through Cocoa Life. Lindt's confidentiality on processor relationships prevents direct comparison.

### 13.2 Forward-Looking Competitive Dynamics

**Regulatory landscape:** The EUDR delay to December 30, 2026, with a simplification package published May 4, 2026, creates regulatory certainty after years of uncertainty. The CSDDD's narrowed scope (5,000+ employees) still captures all three processors. CSRD/ESRS E4 will require biodiversity disclosures that reinforce traceability investments.

**West African reforms:** Ghana's 29% farmgate price cut (February 12, 2026) and Côte d'Ivoire's 57% mid-crop cut (March 4-5, 2026) create income uncertainty for farmers, potentially undermining sustainability program impacts. The LID's status is unclear. COCOBOD's reform package (50% local processing target, new financing model, forensic audit) could reshape the competitive landscape.

**Corporate restructuring:** Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate split (unconfirmed but actively explored) could reshape competitive dynamics. If implemented, it may benefit Cargill and Olam as customers diversify suppliers. Cargill's "flexibility" strategy (portfolio approach, cocoa-free NextCoa, cell-based cacao) positions it to capture market share from disrupted competitors. Olam's AtSource platform and Cocoa Compass living income narrative provide differentiation for sustainability-focused clients, but the metric's vulnerability to market prices is a significant risk.

**The next phase of competition** will likely center on:
1. **Innovation capabilities** (Barry Callebaut's Chocolate Academies and proprietary products; Cargill's NextCoa and cell-based cacao)
2. **Platform-based services** (Olam's AtSource with 100+ sustainability metrics)
3. **Flexibility in formulation and supply chain management** (Cargill's portfolio approach)
4. **Capacity to absorb price volatility** (Barry Callebaut's potential split; Cargill's private ownership; Olam's ofi IPO exploration)

**The strategic differentiation among these three processors is narrowing** as sustainability and traceability become baseline requirements rather than competitive advantages. Companies that can demonstrate independent verification of farmer income impacts, transparent disclosure of non-compliant volumes, and innovative solutions for cocoa price volatility will have the strongest competitive position in premium chocolate manufacturing contracts.
