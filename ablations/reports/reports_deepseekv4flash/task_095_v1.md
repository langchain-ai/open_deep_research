# Comparative Feminist Legal Theory Across Four Legal Traditions

## Introduction

Feminist legal theory has evolved across diverse legal traditions, each responding to distinct historical, political, and cultural contexts while grappling with common questions about women's subordination and emancipation. This comprehensive analysis traces the evolution of feminist legal scholarship across four distinct traditions: Anglo-American critical legal feminism, Islamic feminism's reinterpretation of Sharia, African feminism's critique of customary law and universal human rights, and Chinese feminist legal scholarship navigating state socialism and marketization. Across each tradition, the analysis examines how scholars address three specific legal dimensions: women's property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation.

## Part I: Anglo-American Critical Legal Feminism

Anglo-American critical legal feminism encompasses two influential theoretical frameworks: Catharine MacKinnon's dominance approach and Martha Fineman's vulnerability theory. Both scholars fundamentally critique liberal legalism's formal equality model, yet they offer distinct alternative frameworks for understanding and achieving gender justice.

### 1.1 Catharine MacKinnon's Dominance Approach

Catharine A. MacKinnon, a prominent American feminist legal scholar, developed her dominance approach across several seminal works spanning four decades. Her foundational contributions include *Toward a Feminist Theory of the State* (Harvard University Press, 1989), *Sexual Harassment of Working Women* (Yale University Press, 1979), *Feminism Unmodified: Discourses on Life and Law* (Harvard University Press, 1987), and *Women's Lives, Men's Laws* (Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2005).

#### Core Theoretical Framework

MacKinnon's dominance approach offers a radical critique of liberal legalism's formal equality model. In her seminal article **"Difference and Dominance: On Sex Discrimination"** (*Harvard Law Review*, Vol. 97, No. 6, 1984), she argues that the traditional equality framework—which treats equality as a matter of treating likes alike and unlikes unlike—is fundamentally inadequate because it takes the male standard as the implicit norm. She writes: "Concealed is the substantive way in which man has become the measure of all things."

MacKinnon identifies two paths within the dominant approach:
1. **The sameness/gender-neutrality/single standard**: Women must prove they are like men to gain access to what men have
2. **The difference/special benefit/double standard**: Women are "protected" or compensated for their differences from men, but this too is measured against the male norm

She argues that both paths are inadequate: "Gender-neutrality is thus simply the male standard, and the special protection rule is simply the female standard, but do not be deceived: manhood is the referent for both."

MacKinnon proposes the dominance approach as an alternative: "An equality question is a question of the distribution of power. A gender question is also a question of power, specifically of male supremacy and female subordination." The dominance approach centers on "the most sex-differential abuses of women as a gender, abuses which sex equality law in its difference mode could not confront."

MacKinnon theorizes sexuality as the primary mechanism through which male dominance is constituted and enforced: "Sexuality is to feminism what work is to marxism: that which is most one's own, yet most taken away." She argues that "the social relation between the sexes is organized so that men may dominate and women must submit and this relation is sexual—in fact, is sex."

#### Critique of Liberal Legalism

MacKinnon's critique of liberal legalism is foundational to her dominance approach. She argues that liberal legalism's formal equality model masks the realities of power and subordination. The state is "male jurisprudentially, meaning it adopts the standpoint of male power on law and society" (*Toward a Feminist Theory of the State*). The state "participates in the sexual politics of male dominance by enforcing its epistemology through law."

In *Feminism Unmodified*, MacKinnon argues that "Feminism has not changed the status of women" because liberal legal reforms fail to address the systemic nature of male dominance. She contends that the legal mandate of equal treatment is "a matter of treating likes alike and unlikes unlike," but the problem is that "the sexes are defined as such by their mutual unlikeness."

#### Dimension A: Women's Property Rights and Economic Subordination

MacKinnon's analysis of women's economic subordination is deeply intertwined with her critique of both Marxism and liberal legalism. She argues that women's material desperation is produced through sex segregation into poverty and the relegation to categories of work that pay little to nothing. In *Difference and Dominance*, the dominance approach combines "women's material desperation through the relegation to categories of work that pay little to nothing, with the massive amount of rape and attempted rape."

In *Sexual Harassment of Working Women*, MacKinnon details how women work "as women" in a workplace and workforce divided according to gender: "Women tend to be employed in occupations that are considered 'for women,' to be men's subordinates on the job, and to be paid less." She argues that "sexual harassment at work undercuts woman's potential for social equality in two interpenetrated ways: by using her employment position to coerce her sexually, while using her sexual position to coerce her economically."

MacKinnon critiques the public/private distinction that shields economic subordination within the family from legal scrutiny. She argues that liberal legalism's distinction between public and private spheres has historically insulated the family—and the economic exploitation of women within it—from state intervention. She argues that "noninterference in the 'private' realm has the effect of reinforcing power and powerlessness."

In her analysis of Marxism, MacKinnon argues that sex equality "appears to require a separate effort—an effort with necessary economic dimensions, potentially supported by a revolutionary regime and shaped by transformed relations to production—but a separate effort nonetheless." She critiques Marx and Engels for treating women's status as defined "by nature, not by society," thereby failing to account for the specific dynamics of gender-based economic subordination.

In *Women's Lives, Men's Laws*, MacKinnon documents how the legal system, historically shaped by male dominance, has marginalized women economically. She notes that the law of divorce and custody, under gender-neutrality, has transformed to give men an equal chance at custody and alimony, but this has often disadvantaged women because "men make more money and (as they say) initiate the building of family units." She cites data showing that women's standard of living declines by 73 percent and men's increases by 42 percent within a year after divorce.

#### Dimension B: Bodily Autonomy

MacKinnon's analysis of bodily autonomy spans reproductive rights, freedom from gender-based violence, and her controversial work on pornography.

**Reproductive Rights and the Privacy Critique**: MacKinnon critiques *Roe v. Wade*'s privacy doctrine as fundamentally inadequate for women's equality. She argues that "the accomplishment of *Roe v. Wade* lies not only in the securing of a legal right to abortion, but also the much more nefarious relegation of this choice (and the attending sexual arrangements) to the private sphere." In **"Privacy v. Equality: Beyond Roe v. Wade"** (1983), she develops the argument that the right to abortion must be grounded in equality rather than privacy, because the privacy doctrine "perpetuates the public/private divide" that shields male dominance in the private sphere from legal scrutiny.

MacKinnon contends that "sexuality and reproduction are inextricable from gender inequality, as women often do not truly control sexual encounters or contraceptive use due to social norms of male dominance." The privacy framing fails because "framing abortion as a privacy issue fails to address the context of forced sex and inequality that often lead to unwanted pregnancies."

**Freedom from Gender-Based Violence**: MacKinnon's work on sexual harassment fundamentally transformed the legal understanding of gender-based violence. In *Sexual Harassment of Working Women*, she argued that sexual harassment is sex discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

Her work directly influenced **Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986)**, the landmark Supreme Court case that recognized hostile environment sexual harassment as a violation of Title VII. The Court held: "A claim of 'hostile environment' sexual harassment is a form of sex discrimination that is actionable under Title VII." Following this ruling, reported sexual harassment cases rose from 10 per year to over 4,600 by 1995.

The **Violence Against Women Act (VAWA, 1994)** established the Office on Violence Against Women, and from its enactment through fiscal year 2018, awarded some $8 billion in grants. A Bureau of Justice Studies report found that intimate partner violence was reduced by 67% in the first 10 years of VAWA. The most recent reauthorization was signed into law on March 15, 2022.

**Pornography as a Civil Rights Violation**: In 1983, MacKinnon and Andrea Dworkin drafted an anti-pornography civil rights ordinance, first brought to Minneapolis and then signed into law in Indianapolis. The ordinance defined pornography as a form of sex discrimination. MacKinnon argued that pornography "makes inequality into sex, which makes it enjoyable, and into gender, which makes it seem natural." The Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals, in *American Booksellers Association v. Hudnut* (1985), struck down the ordinance, ruling it unconstitutional for discriminating based on speech content and viewpoint.

#### Dimension C: Political Participation

MacKinnon's analysis of political participation is less developed than her analysis of sexuality and violence, but she offers important critiques of liberal citizenship and representation. She argues that the state "adopts the standpoint of male power on law and society," meaning that women's political participation occurs within structures that are fundamentally male-defined.

In *Difference and Dominance*, she critiques the ways in which male experience defines citizenship: "men's military service defines citizenship, their presence defines family, their inability to get along with each other—their wars and rulerships—defines history, their image defines god, and their genitals define sex." She argues that women are held to a male standard even in political participation, where "gender-neutrality is simply the male standard."

MacKinnon critiques the Equal Rights Amendment effort for "overlooking the foundational issue of male dominance and for adopting limited liberalism-based approaches to sex equality." She argues that formal legal equality in political participation—such as the right to vote, hold office, or serve on juries—does not address the substantive inequality of power that shapes women's political lives.

### 1.2 Martha Fineman's Vulnerability Theory

Martha Albertson Fineman, Robert W. Woodruff Professor of Law at Emory University School of Law and a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, developed vulnerability theory as an alternative to both liberal legalism and identity-based anti-discrimination approaches. She is the Founding Director of the **Feminism and Legal Theory Project** and the **Vulnerability and the Human Condition Initiative**.

#### Core Theoretical Framework

Fineman first articulated vulnerability theory in **"The Vulnerable Subject: Anchoring Equality in the Human Condition"** (*Yale Journal of Law and Feminism*, Vol. 20, No. 1, 2008). She argues that the "vulnerable subject" should replace the "autonomous liberal subject" as the basis for legal and policy analysis. Vulnerability is "a universal, inevitable, enduring aspect of the human condition that must be at the heart of our concept of social and state responsibility."

**The Vulnerable Subject**: Fineman argues that the liberal legal subject—the autonomous, rational, self-sufficient individual—is a myth. Vulnerability theory understands human beings as embodied creatures who are "inexorably embedded in social relationships and institutions." Vulnerability is "not a mark of weakness or victimhood; it is a universal, constant, complex ontological condition."

**Resilience as Socially Produced**: "No one is born resilient. Rather, resilience is produced within and through institutions and relationships that confer privilege and power." Resilience is accrued through access to various resources: physical, human, social, ecological, and existential.

**The Responsive State**: "Vulnerability theory calls for a state that is responsive to universal human needs and for the reorganization of many existing structures." The responsive state recognizes the universality and constancy of vulnerability, as well as the need to provide mechanisms for building resilience.

**Inevitable and Derivative Dependency**: Fineman distinguishes between inevitable dependency (the dependency of childhood, illness, disability, and aging) and derivative dependency (the dependency of caregivers who care for inevitably dependent persons). This concept highlights society's duty to support caretakers through collective means.

#### Critique of Anti-Discrimination Approaches

In **"Beyond Identities: The Limits of an Antidiscrimination Approach to Equality"** (*Boston University Law Review*, Vol. 92, No. 6, 2012), Fineman argues that anti-discrimination law is fundamentally limited because it focuses on individual identities and group-based harms rather than universal vulnerability. She argues: "The discrimination model fractures the theoretical subject... allowing those who maintain the status quo to turn us against each other."

She critiques the Supreme Court's focus on nondiscrimination, advocating for a broader, structural understanding of equality. She presents economic inequality data: between 1979 and 2007, the top 1% of households experienced an income growth of 275% while households in the lowest income quintile experienced an income growth of only 18%.

In **"Equality and Difference: The Restrained State"** (*Alabama Law Review*, Vol. 66, 2015), Fineman argues that "Equality in conjunction with a strong emphasis on personal liberty operates as a mandate for curtailing state action, rather than an aspirational measure of the comparative wellbeing of individuals." She argues for "the development of an approach to social justice issues that puts equality aside and brings differences into consideration."

#### Dimension A: Women's Property Rights and Economic Security

Fineman's vulnerability theory fundamentally reimagines property and economic security through the lens of dependency and caretaking.

**Economic Devaluation of Caretaking Labor**: A foundational critique within Fineman's work is that "the problem was not that caretakers or women as caretakers, were discriminated against, but rather that caretaking itself was devalued by the governing system." In **"The Autonomy Myth: A Theory of Dependency"** (The New Press, 2004), she argues that market institutions are "free-riders," appropriating the labor of the caretaker for their own purposes. The autonomous individual is "anchored in a world of paid labor either directly, as a worker, or indirectly, through marriage to a wage earner," leaving caretaking work without economic recognition.

**Critique of the Marital Family's Privileging**: Fineman critiques the privileging of the marital family in public policy and legal structures, calling for a shift towards recognizing diverse family forms and caretaking roles irrespective of marital status. She argues that "the sexually affiliated couple is not the appropriate building block for contemporary families" and that "society should be organized around 'caretaking relationships,' particularly those involving children or elderly dependents."

**The State's Role in Redistributing Resilience**: "Money is a mechanism... part of an institutional cluster of things... we should not focus only on monetary aspects but also on structural provisions like universal healthcare or daycare." Vulnerability theory calls for a responsive state that provides the resources needed to achieve resilience through social institutions, including administering resilience and addressing inequitable power and privilege.

#### Dimension B: Bodily Autonomy

Fineman's vulnerability theory offers a distinctive framework for understanding reproductive rights and bodily autonomy.

**Vulnerability Theory and *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization***: Deborah Dinner's analysis of Fineman's theories in **"Fineman Speaks to Dobbs"** (*Texas A&M Law Review*) provides the most detailed application of vulnerability theory to reproductive rights. Key findings include:
- Fineman has long warned that equal treatment under law has the capacity to undermine gender justice. The *Dobbs* majority deploys the logic of formal equality to vitiate women's reproductive rights.
- *The Neutered Mother* explains how liberalism reinforces patriarchy by abstracting motherhood and depriving caretaking of socio-political power. *Dobbs* "dis-embodies" the pregnant woman and "neuters" the mother even as it imposes the condition of motherhood.
- Fineman argued in *The Autonomy Myth* that law should respond to universal human vulnerability and dependency rather than liberal autonomy.
- Vulnerability theory may aid reinvigorated struggles for reproductive justice by emphasizing the state's obligation to support pregnant persons across the life course and social embeddedness.

**Critique of the Autonomous Subject Applied to Reproductive Justice**: The *Dobbs* decision relies on formal equality, citing laws like the Pregnancy Discrimination Act and Family and Medical Leave Act, portraying these as a "legal fiction" that obscures the burdens of pregnancy and motherhood. Fineman's scholarship offers "a formidable normative argument for affirmative entitlements for caregivers and caretaking."

**State Responsibility for Violence Prevention**: Vulnerability theory addresses bodily autonomy and freedom from violence by analyzing state responsibility. Rather than focus on the vulnerability of victims, Fineman analyzes the body of rape jurisprudence as "an institutional system expressly designed to mitigate certain forms of human vulnerability," reframing the issue from individual victimhood to institutional state responsibility.

#### Dimension C: Political Participation

**Reconceptualizing Citizenship**: Vulnerability theory reconceptualizes citizenship through the "vulnerable subject" model. Fineman's vulnerable subject citizenship model advocates for a responsive democratic framework addressing vulnerabilities through equitable resource distribution and institutional support.

**The Responsive State and Democratic Representation**: The state "is the ultimate legitimate repository of coercive power... manifest through branches of government and complex institutional arrangements." The responsive state concept reshapes the relationship between individual and state by rejecting the traditional liberal "night-watchman" state model and arguing that the state must actively provide resilience through social institutions.

**Beyond Anti-Discrimination in Political Representation**: Vulnerability theory provides a framework beyond identity-based politics and antidiscrimination law by focusing on structural social relationships—such as employer/employee or parent/child—through which privilege and disadvantage are constructed and maintained. Fineman argues that "the discrimination model fractures the theoretical subject," and that achieving genuine political equality requires addressing the structural inequalities that undermine democratic representation.

## Part II: Islamic Feminism's Reinterpretation of Sharia

Islamic feminism represents a distinctive approach to feminist legal theory that works within Islamic frameworks to advance women's rights. Two scholars—Ziba Mir-Hosseini and Amina Wadud—have been particularly influential in developing hermeneutical approaches that distinguish between immutable divine principles and human interpretations that have institutionalized patriarchy.

### 2.1 Ziba Mir-Hosseini: Distinguishing Sharia from Fiqh

Ziba Mir-Hosseini (born 1952 in Sanandaj, Iran) is a legal anthropologist and Senior Research Associate in Anthropology at the School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS), University of London. Her scholarship focuses on Islamic law, gender equality, and the dynamics between feminism and Islamic discourses. Her Google Scholar profile shows 5,927 total citations, an h-index of 33, and an i10-index of 63.

#### Key Published Works

Mir-Hosseini's key works include **"Islam and Gender: The Religious Debate in Contemporary Iran"** (Princeton University Press, 1999), her most cited work with 979 citations; **"Justice and Equality in Muslim Family Laws"** (I.B. Tauris, 2013); **"Muslim Women's Quest for Equality: Between Islamic Law and Feminism"** (2006); **"Marriage on Trial: A Study of Islamic Family Law: Iran"** (2000); and **"The Feminist Encounter with Muslim Legal Tradition"** (*Samyukta: A Journal of Gender and Culture*, Vol. 2, No. 1, Jan 2017).

#### The Sharia/Fiqh Distinction

The cornerstone of Mir-Hosseini's analytical framework is the critical distinction between Sharia and fiqh. In **"Towards Gender Equality: Muslim Family Laws and the Shari'ah"**, she articulates: "Shari'ah, which literally means 'the path or the road leading to the water', in Muslim belief is the totality of God's will as revealed to the Prophet Muhammad... Fiqh... denotes the process of human endeavour to discern and extract legal rules from the sacred sources of Islam... While the Shari'ah is sacred, eternal and universal, fiqh... is human, mundane, temporal and local."

This distinction is "crucial to Islamic Feminism's epistemological intervention." She emphasizes that many oppressive family law rulings derive from fiqh traditions, not immutable Shari'ah principles, and that patriarchal laws are juristic constructs rather than Qur'anic mandates and thus open to reform.

Mir-Hosseini argues that "Islamic legal thought became the prisoner of its own theories and assumptions... which came to overshadow the ethical and egalitarian voice of Islam and its call for justice and reform, thus negating the spirit of the Shari'ah." She notes that classical jurists' "notions of justice... were constrained by a set of gender assumptions and legal theories that reflected the state of knowledge of the time... essentially time-bound phenomena... were turned into juridical principles of permanent validity."

#### The Musawah Global Movement

Mir-Hosseini is a founding member of **Musawah** ("equality" in Arabic), a global movement for equality and justice in the Muslim family launched in 2009 in Kuala Lumpur. Musawah brings together Islamic scholars, academics, activists, and legal practitioners from about 30 countries. The Musawah Framework for Action integrates Islamic teachings, universal human rights, and national constitutional guarantees.

Musawah's research identifies 12 principal concerns affecting Muslim women, including equality in marriage, consent, capacity and child marriage, polygamy, violence, nationality, inheritance, divorce rights, financial rights, and child guardianship. The organization argues that discriminatory family laws "undermine women's autonomy and economic empowerment" and that "reform of discriminatory family laws is a prerequisite for achieving gender equality."

Mir-Hosseini concludes that "a paradigm shift is underway as feminist voices within Islam challenge the old patriarchal rationale, promoting democratisation of religious knowledge and the legitimacy of gender equality in Muslim contexts."

### 2.2 Amina Wadud: Tawhidic Hermeneutics

Amina Wadud is an American Muslim scholar of African descent whose work focuses on Qur'anic exegesis and gender justice. She is best known for her pioneering interpretive reading of the Qur'an from a woman's perspective.

#### Key Published Works

**"Qur'an and Woman: Rereading the Sacred Text from a Woman's Perspective"** (Oxford University Press, 1999) is the first interpretive reading of the Qur'an by a woman. Wadud argues "The Qur'an does not prescribe one timeless and unchanging social structure for men and women" and affirms that "the Qur'an holds greater possibilities for guiding human society to a more fulfilling and productive mutual collaboration between men and women." Her reading "confirms women's equality and constitutes legitimate grounds for contesting the unequal treatment that women have experienced historically and continue to experience legally in Muslim communities."

**"Inside the Gender Jihad: Women's Reform in Islam"** (Oneworld Publications, 2006) emerges from Wadud's extensive personal and scholarly engagement with Islamic reforms. She emphasizes that "Islam is not a monolith. It has a plethora of meanings and experiences." She argues "Patriarchy is despotism and... feeds on the eradication of women's moral agency; it erases and marginalizes women; and, most significantly, it negates the possibility of true surrender to God."

#### The Tawhidic Paradigm

Wadud proposes a feminist hermeneutical method to interpret the Qur'an which she calls the **tawhidic paradigm**. Three principles guide this approach:
1. The context in which the Qur'an was revealed (historical context)
2. The grammatical composition of the text
3. The whole text, that is its weltanschauung or world-view

This approach demonstrates that "the Qur'an is compatible with modernity and supports gender equality." Wadud's method challenges conservative, patriarchal interpretations by introducing a feminist viewpoint to Qur'anic exegesis. As Rivera de la Fuente explains: "If humans are equal before God, then Women and Others are, by divine right, equally able to read, decipher, interpret and convey their perspective on religious matters."

The concept of "Gender Jihad" is described as "the building of an episteme, understanding the right of Muslim women to enunciate and interpret a reality that challenges them, for and by themselves since, as Amina Wadud says 'defining religion is to have power over it.'"

### 2.3 Dimension A: Women's Property Rights

#### Inheritance Rights (Qur'an 4:11-12)

Classical Islamic law prescribes that daughters typically receive half the share of sons under Qur'an 4:11. Musawah research identifies inheritance as one of 12 principal concerns affecting Muslim women.

The Musawah framework argues that "Muslim family laws around the world are in a constant state of evolution and have been changing according to the shifting realities of time and place," implying that inheritance rules based on classical fiqh can be reformed.

**Morocco's Mudawanna (2004)** : The 2004 reforms granted inheritance rights more aligned with Islamic feminism. While abolition of the male-preferential "ta'asib" law was rejected, provisions like lifetime gifts (hiba) to daughters and the exclusion of marital homes from inheritance aim to improve women's security. As Dr. Mounira Charrad notes: "There are reasons why inheritance has remained the most difficult part of family law to change. You have to think of it both sociologically and culturally."

**Tunisia**: Tunisia ranks highest with its score of 3.6 out of 5 in the categories of nondiscrimination and access to justice (Freedom House, 2005).

#### Right to Own and Manage Property Independently

Classical fiqh recognizes women's rights to own and manage property independently—this was a revolutionary provision in 7th century Arabia when "Islam's inheritance laws guaranteed women property rights in the seventh century when much of the known world considered women barely human."

#### Mahr (Dower)

Classical jurists defined marriage as a contract granting men rights to obedience (tamkin) and unilateral divorce (talaq), while linking women's rights to maintenance (nafaqah) contingent on obedience. The concept of mahr (dower) is part of this contractual framework that Mir-Hosseini and other scholars critically examine as reinforcing patriarchal structures within marriage.

The Morocco 2004 Mudawwana redefined marriage in Article 4 as "a legal contract by which a man and a woman mutually consent to unite in a common and enduring conjugal life... under the supervision of both spouses"—shifting from a model of male authority to mutual supervision.

### 2.4 Dimension B: Bodily Autonomy

#### Qur'an 4:34—The "Husband's Authority" Verse

This is a central focus of both scholars' work. Qur'an 4:34 states: "Men are the protectors and maintainers of women, because God has given the one more (strength) than the other... As to those women on whose part ye fear disloyalty and ill-conduct, admonish them (first), (Next), refuse to share their beds, (And last) strike them."

**Musawah's Decoding of Patriarchy**: The entire edifice of family law in Muslim legal tradition is built on the ways in which classical jurists understood Qur'an verse 4:34. The concept of qiwamah as sanction for male authority over women is "not Qur'anic but a juristic construct." The article **"Decoding the 'DNA of Patriarchy' in Muslim Family Laws"** examines the foundational role of this verse and the related legal concepts of qiwamah and wilayah in shaping patriarchal Muslim family laws.

**Amina Wadud's Analysis**: In "Inside the Gender Jihad," Wadud moves away from reformist interpretation toward outright rejection: "There is no getting around the fact that the Qur'an says daraba to women who are guilty of nushuz"—but she argues this must be read in the context of the Prophet's practice: "[Muhammad] never struck a servant or a woman" (Hadith transmitted by 'A'isha).

Wadud's interpretation of qiwamah emphasizes men's responsibilities toward women rather than inherent superiority, thus not prohibiting female leadership. She interprets: "Men must use their inheritance and earnings to tend to the needs of women as females play an indispensable and arduous role in assuring the continuation of the human species."

#### Marriage, Divorce, and Personal Status Laws

**Iran's Family Laws**: Mir-Hosseini's "Marriage on Trial" analyzes the complexities of divorce within Iranian legal and ideological contexts. Post-1979 Islamic Republic politics, internal elite factional struggles, and external hostility have favored conservative clerical interpretations, restricting women's gains. "The clerics of the unelected Guardian Council... rejected reformist arguments as 'opposed to the Shari'a'" (Mir-Hosseini, **"How the Door of Ijtihad Was Opened and Closed"**, *Washington and Lee Law Review*, Vol. 64, Issue 4, 2007).

**Morocco's Mudawwana (2004)** : These reforms are "radical in that they admit the principle of equality in marriage and cast classical Maliki School of Sunni law in a new light... these reforms were finally achieved by the intervention of the King who claimed the right of ijtihad as the Commander of the Faithful." Key reforms include: raising marriage age to 18, prohibiting forced marriage, abolishing mandatory marriage guardian, granting women the right to choose their partners, enhancing women's divorce rights, making verbal repudiation by husbands invalid, requiring property acquired during marriage to be shared, and imposing stricter controls on polygamy.

**Tunisia's Code of Personal Status (1956)** : Tunisia has taken "the most secularized approach to women's rights in majority-Muslim countries" (A.K. Wing and H. Kassim, 2007). The CPS abolished polygamy, introduced judicial oversight in divorce, and expanded women's marital rights. A subsequent wave in 1993 granted women the ability to pass nationality to their children.

#### Reproductive Rights and Gender-Based Violence

Morocco enacted Law 103.13 in 2018 to combat violence against women, leading to a 2019 court ruling criminalizing marital rape.

### 2.5 Dimension C: Political Participation

#### Queen Bilqis (Sheba) as Model of Female Leadership

Amina Wadud points to the Qur'anic story of Bilqis, the Queen of Sheba (Surah al-Naml 27:23-44), as a positive example of female rulership. She argues: "The Quran uses no terms that imply that the position of ruler is inappropriate for a woman." The Quran positively portrays the Queen of Sheba as a sovereign ruler, praised for her wisdom, consultative governance, and diplomatic skill.

#### Women's Testimony (Qur'an 2:282)

Qur'an 2:282 states that two men should serve as witnesses, or if two men aren't available, one man and two women "so if one forgets the other may remind her." Contemporary Islamic feminist approaches argue that this verse is context-specific to financial transactions in 7th century Arabia and does not establish a general principle of women's inferior testimony.

#### Women's Legal Capacity and Political Leadership

Conservative scholars often interpret verses like 4:34 and 2:228 as assigning men leadership roles over women, while modernists like Amina Wadud argue that these verses emphasize men's responsibilities toward women rather than inherent superiority. A more recent analysis (2025) affirms: "No Qur'anic verse explicitly forbids women from holding public office" and "The Qur'an affirms only a vertical relationship between God and human beings. The relationship among human beings—women and men—is horizontal."

**The hadith "Never will a people prosper who entrust their affairs to a woman"** is critiqued as epistemologically fragile and historically contingent. The article **"Myth Exploded: Muslim Women Can Play Leadership Roles"** shows that "through rigorous scholarly critique, the hadith is shown to be epistemologically weak, contextually tied to political events, and contradicted by Quranic teachings and historical precedents."

Wadud's own act of leading mixed-gender Friday prayers in 2005 was an unprecedented act that sparked global debate on female religious leadership in Islam.

#### International Human Rights Law and Islamic Jurisprudence

**CEDAW and Muslim Family Laws**: Many Muslim-majority states have entered reservations to CEDAW based on compatibility with Sharia law. Musawah's research argues "full implementation of CEDAW is compatible with Islam" and that claims of incompatibility are often based on "the excuse that laws based on Shari'ah and traditions are immutable."

The article **"Beyond International Human Rights: Islamic Feminism as Alternative Rights Praxis"** (*Feminist Legal Studies*, Springer Nature Link, 2025) examines Islamic Feminism as a distinct rights framework that challenges the universality and liberal foundations of international human rights law. Using the example of Saudi women's activism against the former driving ban, the article explores how IF grounds women's rights claims in Islamic sources and epistemologies rather than secular international law.

## Part III: African Feminism's Critique of Customary Law and Universal Human Rights

African feminist legal scholars have developed distinctive critiques of both customary law and universal human rights frameworks, arguing that neither adequately addresses the realities of African women's lives. The tension between customary legal systems, postcolonial state law, and international human rights norms has been a central preoccupation.

### 3.1 Oyeronke Oyewumi: The Invention of Gender

Oyeronke Oyewumi (born November 10, 1957, in Ogbomoso, Nigeria) is a Nigerian academic and professor specializing in sociology and Africana Studies. She was awarded the African Studies Association Distinguished Africanist Award (2021) and the American Sociological Association Distinguished Book Award (1998).

#### Major Works and Core Arguments

**"The Invention of Women: Making an African Sense of Western Gender Discourses"** (University of Minnesota Press, 1997) is a foundational text that won the American Sociological Association Distinguished Book Award (1998). Oyewumi argues that gender as a social category was **not an organizing principle in pre-colonial Yoruba society**. She contends that the imposition of Western gender binaries through colonialism fundamentally restructured Yoruba social relations.

Her central thesis: "I was shocked by the grand and grandiose claims being made about women of all societies and from all times: claimed that women are powerless... because Western societies looked a certain way, then all other societies had to be like that."

In pre-colonial Yorubaland, Oyewumi demonstrates that social organization was structured primarily by **seniority (age) and lineage**, not by gender. The Yoruba language itself has no gender pronouns—pronouns indicate seniority rather than sex. She writes: "My work is the ultimate evidence about the fact that gender is indeed socially constructed. It didn't come from heaven, it didn't come from nature, there are these categories that are created, historically and culturally."

Oyewumi introduces the concept of **"world-sense"** (as opposed to "worldview") to describe cultures that do not privilege the visual/biological in structuring social relations. She develops the concept of **"body-reasoning"**—the Western tendency to read social hierarchy and difference from physical bodies.

**"African Gender Studies: A Reader"** (Palgrave Macmillan, 2005, 433 pp.) brings together 22 chapters across seven sections. The volume aims to "correct the long-standing problem of Western dominance in the interpretation of African realities." Oyewumi emphasizes two critical divergences from mainstream Western women's studies: (1) "the category women cannot be used as a synonym for gender... given the fact that in many African societies social roles are not necessarily biological roles"; and (2) "because some social roles are truly socially constructed in various African societies, discussions of gender in studies of Africa do not immediately generate or link to discussions of sex and sexuality."

#### Implications for Legal Analysis

Oyewumi's framework has profound implications for legal analysis. If gender categories are Western impositions, then customary legal systems cannot be understood through the lens of Western gender analysis. She argues that colonial legal codification of "customary law" itself constructed gender hierarchies that did not previously exist in the same form. Her approach demands that legal scholars: (1) interrogate foundational assumptions about gender; (2) recover pre-colonial social logics, including seniority-based and lineage-based systems of rights and obligations; and (3) recognize that colonial legal codification ossified and transformed customary law, creating "official customary law" that was more patriarchal than "living customary law."

### 3.2 Ifi Amadiume: Male Daughters, Female Husbands

Ifi Amadiume's **"Male Daughters, Female Husbands: Gender and Sex in an African Society"** (Zed Books, 1987; republished Bloomsbury, 2022) is a landmark ethnographic study of the Igbo people of Nigeria. Amadiume documents institutions such as "male daughters" (women who could occupy male social roles, including inheriting property and heading households) and "female husbands" (women who could marry other women in socially recognized unions). These institutions challenge Western assumptions about the biological basis of gender roles, marriage, and kinship.

Amadiume demonstrates that in pre-colonial Igbo society, social roles and categories of "husband" and "wife" were not biologically determined but were social positions that could be occupied regardless of biological sex. Colonialism, through legal codification, missionary education, and new economic structures, imposed rigid gender binaries that displaced these flexible systems.

### 3.3 Nkiru Nzegwu: Family Matters

Nkiru Nzegwu's **"Family Matters: Feminist Concepts in African Philosophy of Culture"** (SUNY Press, 2006) critically examines gender dynamics in Igbo society, arguing that prior to European colonialism, Igboland was "a nonpatriarchal, nongendered society governed by separate but interdependent political systems for men and women." Nzegwu demonstrates how colonial laws, edicts, and judicial institutions facilitated the creation of gender inequality in Igbo society.

### 3.4 Sylvia Tamale: Gender, Law, and Sexuality

Sylvia Tamale is an academic, lawyer, and feminist activist currently serving as the Dean of Law at Makerere University—the first woman to hold this position in Uganda. She holds a Bachelor of Laws from Makerere University, a Master's in Law from Harvard University, and a PhD in Sociology and Feminist Studies from the University of Minnesota.

#### Major Works and Core Arguments

**"When Hens Begin to Crow: Gender and Parliamentary Politics in Uganda"** (Westview Press, 1999) examines gender dynamics within Uganda's parliamentary politics. The book moves "beyond the body count approach to studying women in politics, into the arena of gender and gendered practices." It explores the sociopolitical history of Uganda and analyzes the persistent impact of patriarchal structures.

**"Gender Trauma in Africa: Enhancing Women's Links to Resources"** (*Journal of African Law*, Vol. 48, No. 1, 2004) examines how the public/private divide shapes African women's lives. Tamale argues that the "'protection' of the private from legal and other scrutiny helps to conceal the burden of caring for family which is borne almost exclusively by women." Women's labor in family enterprises is devalued, and their inequitable access to property such as land restricts their ability to participate fully in political and economic spheres. Consequently, "women's access to resources is, like their access to political fora, severely curtailed, thus calling into question the notion that women are full citizens."

**"African Sexualities: A Reader"** (Pambazuka Press/Fahamu Books, 2011) features over 60 contributions from African writers, thinkers, and activists across 16 African countries. The reader challenges reductive narratives about African sexualities dominated by HIV/AIDS research and colonial stereotypes. Tamale was controversially labeled the "Worst Woman of the Year" in 2003 for advocating the inclusion of sexual minorities in Uganda's Equal Opportunities Commission.

**"Exploring the Contours of African Sexualities: Religion, Law and Power"** (*African Human Rights Law Journal*, Vol. 14, No. 1, 2014) explores how organized religion, culture, and law intersect to shape, regulate, and often control African sexualities. Tamale argues: "The current homophobic upsurge and legislative re-criminalisation of same-sex relations in Africa are connected to evangelical movements in the United States using African churches as proxies for US culture war battles."

#### Critiques of Customary Law and Colonial-Era Statutes

Tamale's legal scholarship focuses on how both customary law and its colonial codification have disadvantaged women. She and her colleagues "successfully challenged oppressive and discriminatory laws in the Ugandan Constitutional Court regarding women's rights in divorce, adultery and inheritance legislations." She emphasizes the dual role of law—law as both "shield and sword"—drawing on Albie Sachs: "While one should always be sceptical about the law's pretensions, one should never be cynical about the law's possibilities."

### 3.5 Celestine Nyamu-Musembi: Local Norms and Property Rights

Celestine Nyamu-Musembi's **"Are Local Norms and Practices Fences or Pathways? The Example of Women's Property Rights"** (in *Cultural Transformation and Human Rights in Africa*, edited by Abdullahi A. An-Na'im, Zed Books, 2002) argues that proponents of gender equality "ought to abandon approaches that simply call for eradication of cultural practices contravening international human rights principles." Instead, Nyamu-Musembi advocates engaging with "administrative and informal social institutions" at the local level.

In **"Local Norms, Institutions, and Women's Property Rights in Rural Kenya"** (*East African Journal of Peace and Human Rights*, Vol. 9, No. 2, 2003), based on 1998-1999 fieldwork in Makueni District, Kenya, she examines clans, informal dispute resolution forums, and Land Adjudication Committees. She identifies elements that both promote and hinder gender equality in property access, emphasizing that due to poor access to formal judicial institutions, communities rely on semi-formal dispute resolution forums and clan-based practices.

### 3.6 Dimension A: Women's Property Rights

#### The Tension Between Customary and Statutory Law

Customary land tenure systems across Africa are predominantly patrilineal, with women's rights to land being "secondary" or "usufructuary"—contingent on relationships to male relatives. Only about 1% of land in Kenya is titled solely in women's names and 5-6% jointly with men. Customary law typically governs the personal lives of 65-75% of the population in most African countries.

Muna Ndulo's **"African Customary Law, Customs, and Women's Rights"** (*Indiana Journal of Global Legal Studies*, Vol. 18, No. 1, Winter 2011) summarizes: "Customary law has great impact in the area of personal law in regard to matters such as marriage, inheritance and traditional authority... Some of its norms conflict with human rights norms guaranteeing equality between men and women." Ndulo argues that "the courts have an important role to play in ensuring that customary law is reformed and developed to ensure that it conforms to human rights norms."

#### Kenya's Constitutional Framework

Kenya's **Constitution of 2010** establishes robust equality provisions:
- **Article 27**: "Every person is equal before the law and has the right to equal protection and equal benefit of the law." The State "shall not discriminate directly or indirectly against any person on any ground, including race, sex, pregnancy, marital status..."
- **Article 60(1)(f)**: Mandates the "elimination of gender discrimination in law, customs and practices related to land and property in land."

Despite these provisions, entrenched customary laws governing approximately 65-70% of land through patrilineal systems continue to limit women's land ownership. The shift from communal customary ownership to individualized ownership under the Registered Land Act (1963) "has pushed women into more subordinate positions regarding access to land rights."

#### Uganda's Legal Framework

- **Uganda's Land Act, Cap. 227**: Section 27 declares null and void any decision on customary land that denies women and girls access to ownership or use of any land.
- **Constitution of Uganda, Article 33**: Guarantees women equal rights with men and prohibits any customary laws that discriminate against women.
- The Land Act requires spousal consent for transactions involving family land.

#### Tanzania's Land Act, 1999 and Village Land Act, 1999

Both Acts have been "hailed as a triumph for the women's rights movement in Tanzania." The Land Act provides that "The right of every adult woman to acquire, hold, use deal in land shall to the same extent and subject to the same restrictions be treated as a right of any adult man." The Village Land Act prohibits customary rules that deny women lawful access to customary land. Despite these protections, "customary law generally applies whether or not it violates statutory norms, leading to discrimination against women especially in inheritance."

#### South Africa: Reform of Customary Law of Succession

The **Reform of Customary Law of Succession and Regulation of Related Matters Act 11 of 2009** was a direct legislative response to the Constitutional Court's decision in *Bhe v Khayelitsha Magistrate*. The Act abolished the principle of male primogeniture as incompatible with constitutional rights to equality and human dignity.

#### Landmark Cases

**Bhe and Others v Khayelitsha Magistrate and Others (2005)** — South African Constitutional Court: This landmark case challenged the customary law rule of male primogeniture under African customary law of succession. The Constitutional Court upheld the challenges, struck down the impugned statutory provisions, and put in place a new interim regime. The Commission for Gender Equality argued for a distinction between "official customary law" (which enforces male primogeniture) and "living customary law" (which can evolve to prevent discrimination). Justice Ngcobo emphasized: "We must look at the social context in which customary rules originated and to examine the possibility of developing them to meet the changing needs and circumstances of society."

**Magaya v Magaya (1999)** — Zimbabwe Supreme Court: A deeply controversial case illustrating the tension between customary law and constitutional equality. The deceased died intestate, leaving two wives married under African customary law. Venia Magaya, the eldest daughter by his first wife, was initially appointed heir by a community court, but the Supreme Court upheld the customary preference for male heirs. The case sparked widespread outrage among women's rights groups and highlighted the difficulties of maintaining dual legal systems.

**Ukeje v Ukeje (2014)** — Supreme Court of Nigeria: A unanimous decision declaring that Igbo customary law excluding female children from inheriting their deceased fathers' property was unconstitutional as it violated the non-discrimination clause. The Court held that "no matter the circumstances of the birth of a female child... she is entitled to an inheritance from her father's estate."

### 3.7 Dimension B: Bodily Autonomy

#### The Maputo Protocol

The **Protocol to the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights on the Rights of Women in Africa (Maputo Protocol, 2003)** is a landmark regional human rights instrument. It explicitly addresses women's reproductive rights, including the right to control fertility, the right to decide whether to have children, and the right to medical abortion in cases of sexual assault, rape, incest, or danger to the mother's health. The Protocol also prohibits female genital mutilation and other harmful practices, requires states to enact laws prohibiting all forms of violence against women, including unwanted or forced sex, and mandates the elimination of discrimination in marriage and family matters.

#### Gender-Based Violence Legislation

Several African countries have enacted legislation addressing gender-based violence:
- **Uganda's Prohibition of FGM Act (2010)**
- **Kenya's Prohibition of FGM Act (2011)**
- **South Africa's Domestic Violence Act (1998)**

These laws reflect the influence of both international human rights norms and domestic feminist advocacy, but enforcement remains uneven.

### 3.8 Dimension C: Political Participation

#### Affirmative Action and Quotas

Several African countries have implemented constitutional and legislative measures to enhance women's political participation:

**Rwanda**: Following the 1994 genocide, Rwanda adopted a new constitution in 2003 that reserved 30% of seats in decision-making organs for women. Rwanda now has the highest proportion of women in parliament globally, with women holding over 60% of parliamentary seats.

**Uganda**: Women's representation in parliament has increased to about 35% through a system of reserved seats for women representatives at district level, combined with increased open seat competition. Tamale's *When Hens Begin to Crow* examines the gendered dynamics of parliamentary politics, finding that while increased representation matters, women parliamentarians continue to face patriarchal structures and gendered expectations.

**South Africa**: The post-apartheid constitutional framework, with its strong equality provisions and commitment to non-sexism, has facilitated women's political participation. South Africa's women's parliamentary representation has consistently been among the highest in Africa.

#### Critiques of Representation

Tamale emphasizes that "beyond the body count approach to studying women in politics," genuine political participation requires transformation of patriarchal structures. She highlights the persistent impact of patriarchal structures in Ugandan parliamentary politics and evaluates media portrayals of women politicians that reinforce gender stereotypes.

Oyewumi's critique of Western gender categories calls into question whether increased women's representation within state structures modeled on Western institutions can achieve genuine transformation. She argues that the very categories of "women" and "men" as political subjects are colonial impositions that may not align with pre-colonial political logics.

## Part IV: Chinese Feminist Legal Scholarship Navigating State Socialism and Marketization

Chinese feminist legal scholarship operates within the distinctive constraints of a single-party state, the legacy of socialist-era gender policies, and the profound impacts of market reform. Unlike the other traditions examined, Chinese feminist legal scholars must navigate a political environment where autonomous feminist organizing is restricted and the state itself claims to have already achieved women's liberation through socialist revolution.

### 4.1 Key Scholars and Their Theoretical Frameworks

#### Wang Zheng: Socialist State Feminists

Wang Zheng is a professor of Women's Studies and History at the University of Michigan. Her work focuses on feminism in China, particularly its historical development and contemporary activism.

In **"Finding Women in the State: A Socialist Feminist Revolution in the People's Republic of China, 1949-1964"** (University of California Press, 2017), Wang Zheng investigates the internal workings of the Chinese Communist Party from a gender perspective. She uses the term "state feminists" to refer to "feminists in the CCP who took on various official posts after the CCP gained control of the state in 1949." These women "worked to advance gender and class equality in the early People's Republic and fought to transform sexist norms and practices, all while facing fierce opposition from a male-dominated Chinese Communist Party leadership."

Wang Zheng argues that the socialist state appeared as "a paradoxically 'woman-friendly' patriarchal party-state." The book highlights key female figures such as Chen Bo'er and Shen Zijiu, their involvement in CCP institutions like the All-China Democratic Women's Federation, and their roles as socialist state feminists navigating gender and class in a male-dominated party-state.

Wang Zheng has highlighted that the increasing interest in feminism among young women in contemporary China is mainly due to three factors: the single-child policy, expansion of higher education, and backlash against socialist gender equality practices. She notes that since 2012, over 50% of college enrollees are female, yet young women often experience gender discrimination post-graduation despite educational achievements.

#### Li Xiaojiang: Founding Women's Studies in China

Li Xiaojiang (1951–February 12, 2025) was a pioneering Chinese feminist scholar credited with introducing Women's Studies into post-Mao China. She served as a professor and director of the Gender Research Center at Dalian University.

Li Xiaojiang is "arguably the first to bring Women's Studies to importance in post-Mao China." She founded the first Women's Studies Research Centre in China and a museum of women's cultural anthropology. She established Women's Studies programs at Zhengzhou University and Henan University.

Li "took up an independent feminist viewpoint based on her Marxist background that did not align with the state's position on women's liberation." Her idea of women's liberation involved "personal fulfilment which couldn't be given to anybody from outside." She criticized the All-China Women's Federation for "lacking Gender Identity Theory" and was "unwelcomed by the organization."

Her work **"Chinese People: Family, Nation, and the World"** (Global Century Press, London, 2024) critiques Western-centric feminism, arguing that its theories are not universally applicable to non-Western societies.

#### Tamara Jacka: Rural Women and Migration

Tamara Jacka is a scholar specializing in gender, migration, and social change with a focus on rural and urban China. Her total citation count is 3,772.

**"Women's Work in Rural China: Change and Continuity in an Era of Reform"** (Cambridge University Press, 1997) examines the profound impact of reform policies introduced in rural China since 1978 on women's work patterns and gender divisions of labor.

**"Rural Women in Urban China: Gender, Migration, and Social Change"** (M.E. Sharpe, 2006) explores the experiences of rural migrant women who have moved to urban centers amid China's rapid economic reforms. Jacka examines the significant growth of China's "floating population"—rural migrants without urban household registration (hukou)—which includes between 80 and 120 million people. She argues that "similar to foreign immigrants in other countries, these migrants have only limited citizenship rights in the cities."

#### Dai Jinhua: Feminist Marxist Cultural Critique

Dai Jinhua, born in 1959, is a Chinese feminist cultural critic and professor at Peking University's Institute of Comparative Literature and Culture. Her work **"Cinema and Desire: Feminist Marxism and Cultural Politics in the Work of Dai Jinhua"** (Verso, 2002) presents her feminist Marxist cultural critique.

Dai criticizes Chinese Fifth Generation filmmakers such as Zhang Yimou and Chen Kaige for depicting "oriental landscapes" tailored for Western audiences. She argues that "as the eternal Other in a patriarchal society, women are made to act out a Chinese historical drama that replaces the Father's history with a new history of the Other."

#### Dorothy J. Solinger: Labor and Citizenship

Dorothy J. Solinger, Professor Emerita of Political Science at the University of California, Irvine, examines how China's market reforms in the 1990s led to mass layoffs of state-employed workers. Her work on the floating population and the erosion of socialist-era labor protections is directly relevant to understanding how marketization has impacted women workers, who have been disproportionately affected by the dismantling of state-owned enterprises.

### 4.2 Dimension A: Women's Property Rights

#### Historical Evolution from Socialist Era Through Market Reforms

**The Socialist Era Foundation (1949-1978)** : The 1950 Marriage Law legalized equal rights in marriage. During this period, the CCP mobilized "women's liberation" (funü jiefang) as part of state-building through land reform (1949-1952) and collectivization. Wang Zheng's "Finding Women in the State" documents how socialist state feminists worked to advance gender and class equality in land and property rights.

**The Household Responsibility System (1978 onward)** : The post-Mao reforms introduced the Household Responsibility System, which dismantled collectives and allocated land-use rights to households, typically registered under the male household head. This shift from collective to household-based land tenure had profound gendered consequences.

**The Problem of Rural Women Losing Land Rights Upon Marriage**: Despite constitutional guarantees of gender equality, "ambiguity in the property and marriage laws have allowed village leaders to reassert traditional social norms and deny constitutional equal rights guarantees for women."

#### Key Legal Frameworks

**The Land Administration Law (1986, revised 1998)** : The 1998 revision greatly limited the scope of "readjustments" of land holdings when household size changed. This "cut short the land allocation cycle for women at marriage, divorce, or widowhood—the point when they left behind their land share and moved to a new village."

**The Rural Land Contract Law (2003)** : Article 7 states: "Men and women is to enjoy equal rights with respect to the contracting of rural land. The legal rights of women is to be protected." However, "Article 30 preserves women's land share from loss at marriage, divorce, or widowhood, yet lacks practical mechanisms such as partition rights and fails to require women's consent in land transfers." The law uses a single-representative contract system where only the household head (usually male) signs the contract.

**The Property Law (2007)** : "The Property Law's ambiguous language and registration requirements may further jeopardize women's land tenure by excluding them from titles and limiting their ability to partition land."

**The Civil Code (2020), Book V on Marriage and Family**: The Civil Code came into force on January 1, 2021. Key provisions include:
- Article 1041: "Implement a marriage system featuring freedom of marriage, monogamy and equality between men and women."
- Article 1055: "Husband and wife shall have equal status in the marriage and family."
- Article 1062: Property acquired during marriage is jointly owned.
- Article 1076: Divorce by mutual consent requires written agreement and registration; either party may withdraw application within 30 days (the controversial "cooling off" period).

**Critiques from a Feminist Perspective**: The two most controversial aspects of the Civil Code's marriage provisions are that only men and women can marry, and the 30-day "cooling off" period. "Opponents argue that the cooling off period will threaten lives of victims of domestic abuse, largely women."

**The Law on Protection of Women's Rights and Interests (2022 revision)** : The revised law (effective January 1, 2023) includes specific property rights protections, ensuring women equal property and inheritance rights and establishing legal responsibilities for violations.

#### Empirical Evidence of Land Rights Violations

During the first half of 1999 alone, over 2,000 rural women in twenty-two provinces reported loss of land to the All-China Women's Federation. A 2014 ACWF survey found a 21% increase in cases of infringed women's land rights from 2000 to 2010, with 70% involving married women.

Legal advocacy groups like Beijing's Qianqian Law Firm have taken part in over 3,000 land disputes involving more than 100,000 married women and their children. Nearly 90% of Qianqian Law Firm's related lawsuits in 2018 were dismissed or lost.

The traditional Chinese proverb "marrying out a daughter is like tossing out a basin of water" reflects societal attitudes toward married women's land rights.

A 2024 study analyzing 374 civil judicial judgment documents related to rural women's land rights from 2000-2022 found that judges use the term "human rights" in 86.4% of cases in judicial reasoning, reflecting the role of the 2004 constitutional amendment incorporating human rights in interpreting ambiguous legal provisions.

### 4.3 Dimension B: Bodily Autonomy

#### Abortion and Family Planning Policies

**The One-Child Policy (1979-2015)** : The One-Child Policy was a population control measure implemented nationwide in 1980 and ended in 2016. Enforcement included financial incentives, contraceptives, forced sterilizations, and abortions. The policy resulted in an estimated 400 million births prevented. The policy caused a skewed sex ratio toward males, a rise in female fetus abortions, and increased female infanticide or abandonment.

Feminist critique: "The one-child policy's implementation severely infringed upon Chinese women's reproductive rights, subjecting them to institutionalized coercion."

**The Two-Child Policy (2016-2021)** and **Three-Child Policy (2021-present)** : The Three-Child Policy explicitly identifies its target population as married women who are pregnant or planning to become pregnant. Despite incentives such as tax cuts and subsidies, China's birth rate has continued to decline, with the total fertility rate dropping from 2.6 in the late 1980s to 1.15 in 2021.

Human Rights Watch argues: "Birth limits, no matter the number, are fundamentally an infringement on women's reproductive rights and bodily autonomy."

**Reproductive Rights and Workplace Discrimination**: Women are reluctant to have more children primarily due to the unequal burden of childcare and its negative impact on their careers. Surveys show that 61% of female job applicants reported being asked about childbearing status versus 32% of men. A 2020 study showed 45% of women reported negative employment effects due to pregnancy or childrearing. Chinese women spend almost three times more time on unpaid domestic work than men on average.

#### The Anti-Domestic Violence Law (2016)

China's **Anti-Domestic Violence Law** was enacted on December 27, 2015, and came into force on March 1, 2016, after two decades of advocacy. The law defines domestic violence as "the act of a family member inflicting physical, psychological or other harm on another, including but not limited to beatings, bindings, inflicting bodily injuries, restricting personal liberty, or recurrent verbal abuse and intimidation."

Key provisions include:
- "The state prohibits any form of domestic violence."
- Introduces personal safety protection orders issued by courts with specified protections and durations.
- Establishes responsibilities for state authorities, social organizations, and families.
- The law also applies to violence between cohabitants who are not family members.

**Critiques and Weaknesses**: The DV Law sends mixed messages by elevating family harmony and social stability alongside victim protection, "undermining the effectiveness of the DV Law in protecting the safety of millions of victims." Traditional cultural values and state interests favoring family stability and social harmony can work against victim protection. The law's dual focus on victim safety and maintaining family harmony creates conflicting priorities that often undermine victim protection.

### 4.4 Dimension C: Political Participation

#### The All-China Women's Federation (ACWF)

The All-China Women's Federation (ACWF) is a mass organization established in 1949 to represent women's interests within the state structure. The ACWF has been a central institution through which the state has pursued gender policy, but its role as both a vehicle for women's advancement and an instrument of state control has been a subject of feminist critique.

Wang Zheng's "Finding Women in the State" documents the history of "socialist state feminists" within the CCP who "maneuvered behind the scenes" to advance gender equality. However, as Li Xiaojiang's critique demonstrates, independent feminist voices have often been unwelcome within the ACWF, which has been seen as insufficiently critical of state patriarchy.

#### Constitutional Framework

China's **Constitution (1982, as amended)** provides the legal foundation for gender equality:
- **Article 48**: "Women in the People's Republic of China enjoy equal rights with men in all spheres of life, political, economic, cultural and social, and family life."
- **Article 33 (2004 amendment)** : "The state respects and protects human rights"—the first constitutional inclusion of "human rights" in China.

#### The Law on Protection of Women's Rights and Interests

First enacted in 1992 and revised in 2005, 2018, and 2022, this law establishes comprehensive protections for women's rights. The 2022 revision (effective January 1, 2023) includes enhanced protections against sexual harassment, strengthened property rights, and expanded anti-discrimination provisions.

#### Electoral Representation

Women's political participation in China has been characterized by state-mandated quotas and reserved seats. The National People's Congress (NPC) has maintained a quota for women deputies, with women's representation fluctuating around 20-25% in recent decades. At local levels, representation varies significantly.

However, scholars like Wang Zheng emphasize that the Chinese government is not monolithic; various branches have contradictory stances on feminist activism. After 2015, online feminist activism faces severe censorship, requiring activists to develop new, discreet methods of organizing.

#### Theoretical Critiques of State Feminism

Li Xiaojiang's critique of the ACWF and her emphasis on "personal fulfilment" in women's liberation reflects a fundamental tension in Chinese feminist legal theory: how to navigate between the state's claim to have already achieved women's liberation and the reality of persistent gender inequalities exacerbated by marketization.

Dai Jinhua's feminist Marxist cultural critique provides another lens, analyzing how consumerism in 1990s urban China has reshaped gender relations and political memory. She analyzes the rise of shopping plazas as "a montage of Tiananmen Square's political memories and global capitalist marketplace logic."

## Part V: Comparative Analysis Across Legal Traditions

This section synthesizes the findings across the four legal traditions, comparing how different legal systems structure women's property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation, and how feminist legal scholars within each tradition respond to and theorize these structures.

### 5.1 Comparative Table: Key Dimensions

| Dimension | Anglo-American | Islamic | African | Chinese |
|-----------|---------------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Legal System** | Common law, constitutional | Religious law (Sharia), civil law pluralism | Customary law, statutory law, international human rights | Socialist/state law, civil law |
| **Property Rights** | Formal equality critiqued; MacKinnon: male standard embedded; Fineman: caretaking devalued | Inheritance: daughters half of sons (Qur'an 4:11); reform through ijtihad | Customary law predominantly patrilineal; constitutional equality provisions; landmark cases challenging primogeniture | Socialist-era collective ownership; market reforms created household-based land rights; marriage out = land loss |
| **Bodily Autonomy** | Privacy v. equality debate (MacKinnon); vulnerability-based state responsibility (Fineman) | Qur'an 4:34 contested; family law reforms in Morocco, Tunisia, Iran | Maputo Protocol; FGM legislation; marital rape criminalization in some countries | One-child to three-child policies; Anti-DV Law 2016; workplace reproductive discrimination |
| **Political Participation** | Liberal citizenship critiqued as male-defined; dominance approach | Qur'anic models of female leadership (Bilqis); testimony and legal capacity debated | Affirmative action (Rwanda 60%+; Uganda 35%); representation critiqued as insufficient | State-mandated quotas (NPC 20-25%); ACWF as state organ; independent organizing restricted |

### 5.2 Property Rights: Common Themes and Divergent Approaches

**Common Theme: The Public/Private Divide**: Across all four traditions, feminist scholars critique the public/private distinction that relegates women's property rights to the "private" sphere. MacKinnon argues that noninterference in the private realm reinforces male power. Tamale argues that the public/private divide conceals women's unpaid labor. Chinese scholars document how the household-based land allocation system subordinates women's land rights to male household heads.

**Divergent Approaches to Reform**: Anglo-American feminism seeks transformation through state intervention and reimagining equality as substantive rather than formal. Islamic feminism works within religious frameworks, distinguishing between immutable divine principles and reformable human interpretations. African feminism navigates legal pluralism, seeking to reform both customary and statutory systems while recognizing the colonial origins of current legal structures. Chinese feminism must work within a single-party state that claims to have already achieved gender equality through socialist revolution.

### 5.3 Bodily Autonomy: Privacy, Equality, and State Power

**The Privacy/Equality Debate**: MacKinnon's critique of privacy doctrine as inadequate for reproductive justice resonates across traditions. In Islamic contexts, the question is not privacy but the nature of divine command and human interpretation. In African contexts, the Maputo Protocol represents a regional human rights framework that explicitly links reproductive rights to women's equality. In China, the state's population control policies have fundamentally shaped reproductive autonomy, raising different questions about the relationship between individual rights and state interests.

**Gender-Based Violence**: Each tradition has developed distinctive legal responses to gender-based violence. MacKinnon's conceptualization of sexual harassment as sex discrimination under Title VII transformed U.S. law. The Indianapolic anti-pornography ordinance (though struck down) represented a civil rights approach to gender-based violence. African countries have enacted FGM-specific legislation and domestic violence laws. China's 2016 Anti-Domestic Violence Law represents a significant legislative achievement, though its implementation is limited by cultural values favoring family harmony.

### 5.4 Political Participation: Representation, Citizenship, and Legal Capacity

**Representation and Quotas**: African countries, particularly Rwanda, have achieved the highest levels of women's parliamentary representation through constitutional quotas and affirmative action. China has maintained state-mandated quotas for women in the NPC. Islamic feminist scholars point to Qur'anic models of female leadership while contesting hadith that appear to prohibit women's political authority. Anglo-American feminism has moved beyond the "body count" approach to critique the gendered structures of political institutions.

**Citizenship and Legal Capacity**: Tamale's work in Uganda critiques the very notion of full citizenship for women, arguing that women's access to resources "calls into question the notion that women are full citizens." Fineman's vulnerability theory reconceptualizes citizenship around the universal human condition of vulnerability rather than the autonomous liberal subject. Islamic feminism contests restrictions on women's legal capacity, such as the requirement for two women witnesses in financial transactions (Qur'an 2:282), arguing for contextual interpretation.

### 5.5 The Role of International Human Rights Law

The relationship between international human rights law and each tradition reveals fundamental tensions.

**Anglo-American**: Fineman's vulnerability theory is described as "a stealthily disguised human rights discourse, fashioned for an American audience," reflecting the tension between U.S. constitutional law and international human rights frameworks.

**Islamic**: The relationship between Islamic law and international human rights law, particularly CEDAW, is fraught. Many Muslim-majority states have entered reservations to CEDAW based on compatibility with Sharia. Musawah argues that "full implementation of CEDAW is compatible with Islam."

**African**: The Maputo Protocol represents an African regional human rights framework that explicitly addresses women's rights, including reproductive rights and FGM. However, the tension between customary law, postcolonial state law, and international human rights norms remains unresolved.

**Chinese**: The 2004 constitutional amendment incorporating "human rights" has been used by judges in interpreting ambiguous legal provisions in women's land rights cases. However, international human rights discourse operates within the constraints of a single-party state that resists external scrutiny.

---

## Part VI: Conclusion

This comparative analysis reveals both the diversity of feminist legal theory across legal traditions and the common challenges that feminist scholars address. Several key conclusions emerge:

**First, the critique of formal equality is a unifying theme across all four traditions.** MacKinnon's critique of the male standard embedded in ostensibly neutral law, Fineman's critique of anti-discrimination approaches, Mir-Hosseini's distinction between immutable divine principles and patriarchal human interpretations, Oyewumi's critique of Western gender categories, and Chinese feminists' critique of state feminism all challenge the adequacy of formal legal equality to achieve substantive gender justice.

**Second, the tension between universalism and cultural specificity remains unresolved.** African feminist scholars like Oyewumi and Amadiume argue that Western gender categories are colonial impositions that distort African social realities. Islamic feminists argue that universal human rights frameworks must be grounded in Islamic sources and epistemologies to be effective in Muslim contexts. Chinese feminists argue that Western feminist theories are not universally applicable to non-Western societies.

**Third, legal pluralism—the coexistence of multiple legal systems—presents both opportunities and challenges.** African feminist scholars navigate the tensions between customary law, statutory law, and international human rights norms. Islamic feminists work within the distinction between Sharia and fiqh. Chinese feminists must navigate the gap between constitutional guarantees of gender equality and the persistence of discriminatory customary practices.

**Fourth, the state remains a central but contested actor.** MacKinnon argues that the state is "male jurisprudentially." Fineman calls for a "responsive state" that actively builds resilience. Islamic feminists seek to reform state-enforced family laws. African feminists navigate postcolonial states that inherited colonial legal structures. Chinese feminists must work within and against a state that claims to have already achieved women's liberation.

**Finally, the three legal dimensions analyzed—property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation—are deeply interconnected.** Economic dependence undermines bodily autonomy, which in turn limits political participation. Each tradition's approach to these dimensions reflects its broader theoretical framework and the specific legal and political context in which it operates.
