## 1. RRSP Contribution Room Calculation: Two Scenarios

### Clarification of "Earned Income" for RRSP Purposes

For self-employed individuals, "earned income" for RRSP purposes is defined under **subsection 146(1) of the Income Tax Act** and is calculated as **net business income** (gross revenue minus deductible business expenses), not gross revenue [1][2][3]. This is confirmed by the Canada Revenue Agency's T4040 guide and CRA's definitions for RRSPs [4][5]. The self-employment income reported on **Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities)** — specifically the net income figure — is what counts toward RRSP room [6][7].

The 2026 RRSP dollar limit published by the CRA is **$33,810** [8][9][10].

### Scenario A: $95,000 Gross Revenue with ~$20,000 Deductible Business Expenses (Net ~$75,000)

**Step 1 — Determine earned income (net self-employment income):**
- Gross revenue: $95,000
- Deductible business expenses: $20,000
- Net business income (earned income): **$75,000**

**Step 2 — Calculate 18% of earned income:**
18% × $75,000 = **$13,500**

**Step 3 — Apply the lesser of 18% of earned income and the 2026 annual limit:**
- 18% of earned income: $13,500
- 2026 RRSP dollar limit: $33,810
- Lesser amount = **$13,500** (this is the new RRSP room generated for 2026)

**Step 4 — Add carry-forward room (assumed $0 for base calculation):**
Total RRSP deduction limit for 2026 = $13,500

**Step 5 — Tax refund dollar value at actual marginal rate:**

At $75,000 taxable income, the combined federal + Ontario marginal tax rate is **29.65%** (20.5% federal + 9.15% Ontario) [11][12][13]. The Ontario surtax threshold of $5,818 is not triggered at this income level since Ontario tax before surtax is approximately $4,653 [14][15].

$13,500 × 29.65% = **$4,002.75 refund**

**Step 2 — Calculate 18% of earned income:**

### Per $1,000 Contributed: Exact After-Tax Benefit

**Core Principle:** A spousal RRSP uses the **contributor's RRSP deduction room**, provides the **same upfront tax deduction** as an individual RRSP, but shifts the future withdrawal taxation to the annuitant spouse after the 3-year attribution period expires [20][21][22].

**Assumption:** Contributor (you) is at 31.48% effective marginal rate on the contribution.

**Upfront Tax Savings (identical for both options):**
- Contribution: $1,000
- Tax refund at 31.48%: **$314.80**
- Net after-tax cost: $1,000 − $314.80 = **$685.20**

#### Scenario (a): Spouse at 19.05% Bracket in Retirement

**Individual RRSP (you withdraw):**
- Withdrawal of $1,000 taxed at your retirement rate of 19.05%: $190.50 tax
- After-tax withdrawal value: $1,000 − $190.50 = **$809.50**
- Net benefit: $809.50 − $685.20 = **+$124.30**

**Spousal RRSP (spouse withdraws):**
- Withdrawal of $1,000 taxed at spouse's rate of 19.05%: $190.50 tax
- After-tax withdrawal value: $1,000 − $190.50 = **$809.50**
- Net benefit: $809.50 − $685.20 = **+$124.30**

**Result with same retirement bracket:** **$0 difference** between individual and spousal RRSP when both you and your spouse are in the 19.05% bracket.

**BUT — the real comparison:** If you (contributor) will be in a **higher bracket** (e.g., 29.65%) in retirement while spouse remains at 19.05%:
- Individual RRSP: $1,000 × (1 − 0.2965) = **$703.50** after-tax → net benefit of $18.30
- Spousal RRSP: $1,000 × (1 − 0.1905) = **$809.50** after-tax → net benefit of $124.30
- **Spousal RRSP advantage: +$106.00 per $1,000 contributed**

#### Scenario (b): Spouse at 29.65% Bracket (Same as Your Base Rate)

**Individual RRSP:**
- Withdrawal at 29.65%: $296.50 tax → after-tax **$703.50**
- Net benefit: $703.50 − $685.20 = **+$18.30**

**Spousal RRSP:**
- Withdrawal at 29.65%: $296.50 tax → after-tax **$703.50**
- Net benefit: $703.50 − $685.20 = **+$18.30**

**Result:** **$0 difference** — no advantage to spousal RRSP when both spouses are in identical tax brackets in retirement.

#### Scenario (c): Spouse at 43.41% Bracket (Higher Than Contributor)

**Individual RRSP:**
- Withdrawal at 43.41%: $434.10 tax → after-tax **$565.90**
- Net benefit: $565.90 − $685.20 = **−$119.30** (a loss — worse than not contributing)

**Spousal RRSP:**
- Withdrawal at 43.41%: $434.10 tax → after-tax **$565.90**
- Net benefit: $565.90 − $685.20 = **−$119.30**

**Result:** **$0 difference** — both options produce a net loss because the withdrawal tax rate exceeds the deduction rate. **Neither RRSP option is advisable** if spouse will be in a higher bracket in retirement.

### The 3-Year Attribution Rule: Specific Example

The attribution rule under **subsection 146(8.3) of the Income Tax Act** states that if an annuitant (spouse) withdraws from a spousal RRSP in a calendar year, and the contributor made contributions in that withdrawal year or in either of the two preceding calendar years, the withdrawal is attributed back to the contributor (taxed in their hands) up to the amount of contributions made in that period [20][23][24].

**Your Example: $5,000 Contribution in 2026, Withdrawal in 2028**

**Step 1 — Identify the lookback period:**
When the spouse withdraws in **2028**, the lookback period covers:
- 2028 (withdrawal year)
- 2027 (1st preceding year)
- 2026 (2nd preceding year)

Since the $5,000 contribution was made in **2026**, and 2026 is within the 3-year lookback period, **the attribution rule applies**.

**Step 2 — Attribution calculation:**

**Scenario 2a: Withdrawal = $5,000 (exact contribution amount)**
- Total contributions in lookback period (2026–2028): $5,000
- Amount withdrawn: $5,000
- Attribution = lesser of $5,000 and $5,000 = **$5,000 attributed to contributor**
- Tax payable: $5,000 × 31.48% (contributor's rate) = **$1,574.00**
- The contributor reports $5,000 as income on their 2028 tax return, and the spouse reports $0 from this withdrawal [24][25]

**Scenario 2b: Withdrawal = $7,000 ($5,000 contribution + $2,000 growth)**
- Total contributions in lookback period: $5,000
- Amount withdrawn: $7,000
- Attribution = lesser of $7,000 and $5,000 = **$5,000 attributed to contributor**
- The remaining **$2,000** (investment growth) is **taxed to the spouse** at their rate, not the contributor's [24][26]
- Contributor pays: $5,000 × 31.48% = $1,574.00
- Spouse pays: $2,000 × (spouse's marginal rate)
- This is a critical distinction: only the contribution amount is attributed, not the growth

**Scenario 2c: Withdrawal = $3,000 (less than contribution amount)**
- Total contributions in lookback period: $5,000
- Amount withdrawn: $3,000
- Attribution = lesser of $3,000 and $5,000 = **$3,000 attributed to contributor**
- The remaining $2,000 in contributions stays in the RRSP (no current tax impact)
- Tax payable: $3,000 × 31.48% = **$944.40**

**Attribution Expiry:** If the spouse waits until **2029** (or later) to withdraw, and no additional spousal contributions are made in 2027–2029, the 3-year lookback period would cover 2029, 2028, and 2027. Since the contribution was in 2026, it falls outside the lookback window, and **no attribution applies** — the entire withdrawal is taxed to the spouse at their rate [23][25].

### Practical Recommendation

A spousal RRSP provides a **meaningful advantage only when the spouse will be in a lower tax bracket in retirement than you would be**. Based on the calculations above:

- If your spouse expects retirement income placing them in the **19.05% bracket** while you'd be in the **29.65–31.48% bracket**: Use spousal RRSP — approximately **$106 per $1,000** in tax savings.
- If both spouses will be in **similar brackets** in retirement: No advantage — focus on individual RRSP for simplicity and control.
- If spouse will be in a **higher bracket** in retirement: Avoid both RRSP options and prioritize TFSA/RESP instead [20][22].

The Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) provides **20% matching on the first $2,500 contributed annually per child**, maximum **$500 per year**, lifetime maximum **$7,200 per child** [27][28][29].

**What happens if you miss a year?** If you miss a year entirely, that year's CESG room is **permanently lost**. The catch-up mechanism can only recover **one prior year per year**, so missing multiple years means only the most recent missing year can be recaptured. With only 6 years left, every year is critical — missing even one year reduces the maximum achievable CESG to $5,250 [27][32].

### TFSA Withdrawal/Re-Contribution Timing Trap

**Your example: Withdraw $5,000 from TFSA in July 2026 for an emergency.**

The critical rule, confirmed by the CRA and all major financial institutions: **When you withdraw funds from a TFSA, the withdrawn amount is added back to your contribution room on January 1 of the following year — NOT immediately** [40][41][42][43].

**Timeline:**
- **July 2026:** Withdraw $5,000. Your contribution room decreases by $5,000 (if you had already used your 2026 room). If you had unused room, the withdrawal just doesn't help you — it only matters in future years.
- **2026 (remaining months):** You **cannot** re-contribute that $5,000 in 2026 unless you have other unused contribution room available. Any re-contribution that exceeds your available room is an **over-contribution** subject to **1% per month penalty** on the excess [40][44].
- **January 1, 2027:** The $5,000 in withdrawn room is **added back** to your contribution room. You can now re-contribute the $5,000, plus the new $7,000 annual limit for 2027, minus any contributions already made in 2027.

**Practical strategy for freelancers:** Keep your emergency fund in a **separate HISA** (not in the TFSA) if you anticipate needing to access it mid-year. Alternatively, maintain **unused TFSA contribution room** as a buffer — if you have $10,000 in unused room, you can withdraw $5,000 in July and re-contribute it in September without penalty, as long as you stay within your available room [40][41].

## 6. Sensitivity Analysis at Different Income Levels

### At $70,000 Net Business Income

**Tax rates:**
- Combined marginal rate: **29.65%** (20.5% federal + 9.15% Ontario; surtax does NOT apply) [11][12]
- RRSP room: 18% × $70,000 = **$12,600** [8][9]

**Additional CESG eligibility:** At $70,000 AFNI, you remain in the **$58,523–$117,045** bracket → **10% Additional CESG ($50/year/child)** still applies [30][31].

**Strategy implications:**
- RRSP tax savings per dollar: 29.65% (lower than at $95K)
- CESG value per dollar: Still 30% on first $500 (20% basic + 10% additional)
- **Priority shift:** RESP becomes even more attractive relative to RRSP, since the CESG "return" (30% immediate) now exceeds the RRSP tax savings (29.65%)
- **Recommended strategy:** Education-First approach — maximize RESP catch-up before RRSP
- Annual savings pool reduces to ~$12,000 (20% of ~$60,000 after-tax)
- TFSA contributions would need to be reduced to accommodate RESP priority

### At $120,000 Net Business Income

**Tax rates:**
- Combined marginal rate: **37.16%** (26% federal + 11.16% Ontario) base; with surtax the effective rate is approximately **43.41%** (26% federal + 17.41% effective ON including surtaxes) [11][12][19]
- RRSP room: 18% × $120,000 = **$21,600** [8][9]

**Additional CESG eligibility:** At $120,000 AFNI, you are **ABOVE** the $117,045 threshold → **NO Additional CESG** — only basic $500/year/child [30][31].

**Strategy implications:**
- RRSP tax savings per dollar: 37.16–43.41% (significantly higher — $371.60–$434.10 refund per $1,000)
- CESG value per dollar: 20% on first $2,500 (no additional)
- **Priority shift:** Aggressive RRSP Strategy becomes the clear winner — the immediate RRSP tax savings (up to 43.41%) now far exceed the CESG return (20%)
- **Recommended strategy:** Maximize RRSP first, then RESP (basic $2,500/child/year), then TFSA
- Annual savings pool increases to ~$20,000 (20% of ~$100,000 after-tax)
- Spousal RRSP becomes more attractive if spouse is in lower bracket

### Income Level Comparison Summary

| Factor | $70,000 | $95,000 (Base) | $120,000 |
|--------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Combined marginal rate | 29.65% | 31.48% (with surtax) | 37.16–43.41% |
| RRSP room (annual) | $12,600 | $17,100 | $21,600 |
| Additional CESG? | Yes (10%) | Yes (10%) | No |
| Max annual CESG/child | $550 | $550 | $500 |
| RRSP refund on $1,000 | $296.50 | $314.80 | $371.60–$434.10 |
| **Recommended priority** | **RESP > TFSA > RRSP** | **RESP = TFSA > RRSP** | **RRSP > RESP > TFSA** |

The key inflection point is **$117,045 AFNI** — above this, Additional CESG is lost, making RRSP contributions more attractive. Below this, the guaranteed 30% return on the first $500 of RESP contributions (20% basic + 10% additional) competes favorably with the RRSP deduction [30][31].

## 7. Real-World Tax Treatment of Withdrawals

### RESP Educational Assistance Payments (EAPs)

EAPs consist of government grants (CESG) plus investment earnings inside the RESP. The subscriber's original contributions are withdrawn separately as **non-taxable Post-Secondary Education (PSE) withdrawals** since they were made with after-tax dollars [45][46][47].

**Tax treatment:** EAPs are **taxable as income to the beneficiary (the child/student)**, not to the parent subscriber. The student receives a **T4A slip** each year showing the EAP amount [45][48].

**Concrete example — $20,000 EAP withdrawal for Ontario student (2026) with no other income:**

*Federal tax:*
- Federal Basic Personal Amount (2026): $16,452 [49][50]
- Taxable income after BPA: $20,000 − $16,452 = $3,548
- Federal tax at 14% (lowest bracket): $3,548 × 14% = **$496.72**

*Ontario tax:*
- Ontario Basic Personal Amount: $12,989 [51][52]
- Ontario low-income tax reduction: Eliminates provincial tax entirely for incomes up to $18,930 [51]
- At $20,000, the reduction partially phases out: Ontario tax payable ~**$354**

*Total tax on $20,000 EAP:*
- $496.72 (federal) + ~$354 (Ontario) = **~$850.72**
- **After-tax amount: ~$19,149** (95.7% retention)

**Impact comparison:** If these same funds were withdrawn from a parent's RRSP at 31.48%: $20,000 × (1 − 0.3148) = **$13,704** after-tax. The RESP structure saves **$5,445** in taxes on this withdrawal alone.

**EAP limits:** First 13 weeks of full-time enrollment: max $8,000 EAP ($4,000 for part-time). After 13 weeks: no limit [45][46]. Tax-free PSE (contribution) withdrawals have no limit.

### RRSP Withdrawals

**Tax treatment:** Full withdrawal amount is **taxed as regular income** at the individual's **marginal tax rate** in the year of withdrawal. Contribution room is **permanently lost** for the withdrawn amount [53][54].

**Withholding tax** (prepayment at source, NOT a penalty):

| Withdrawal Amount | Federal Withholding Rate | Quebec Rate |
|------------------|------------------------|-------------|
| Up to $5,000 | 10% | 5% |
| $5,001–$15,000 | 20% | 10% |
| Over $15,000 | 30% | 15% |

[53][54][55]

**Concrete example — $20,000 RRSP withdrawal at 31.48% marginal rate:**

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|------------|--------|
| Withdrawal amount | $20,000 | $20,000 |
| Withholding tax (30%, since over $15K) | $20,000 × 30% | **$6,000** |
| Cash received at withdrawal | $20,000 − $6,000 | **$14,000** |
| Actual tax at filing (31.48%) | $20,000 × 31.48% | **$6,296** |
| Additional tax owed at filing | $6,296 − $6,000 | **$296** |
| **After-tax amount retained** | $20,000 − $6,296 | **$13,704** |
| **Effective retention rate** | 68.5% | |

Note: The withholding tax is just a prepayment — your actual tax liability depends on your full-year income and is settled when you file. If your marginal rate is below the withholding rate, you receive a refund [53].

### TFSA Withdrawals

**Tax treatment:** **Completely tax-free** at any time. Withdrawals have **no impact on taxable income**, no withholding tax, and no reporting on tax returns [40][41][56].

**Concrete example — $20,000 TFSA withdrawal:**

| Step | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Amount withdrawn | $20,000 |
| Tax payable | **$0** |
| Withholding tax | **$0** |
| Impact on taxable income | **None** |
| **After-tax amount retained** | **$20,000** |
| **Effective retention rate** | **100%** |

The $20,000 in withdrawn contribution room is added back on **January 1 of the following year** [40][41][42].

### Impact on After-Tax Wealth: Strategies Compared

For a **hypothetical $100,000 in contributions + growth**, the after-tax value differs dramatically by account type:

| Account Type | Pre-Withdrawal Value | Tax Treatment | After-Tax Value | Retention Rate |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------|----------------|---------------|
| **TFSA** | $100,000 | Tax-free withdrawal | **$100,000** | **100%** |
| **RESP (EAP to student)** | $100,000 | Taxed at student's low rate (~4.25%) | **~$95,750** | **~96%** |
| **RRSP (at 31.48%)** | $100,000 | Taxed at marginal rate (withholding applies) | **~$68,520** | **~69%** |
| **RRSP (at 43.41%)** | $100,000 | Taxed at high marginal rate | **~$56,590** | **~57%** |

**Impact on the recommended strategies (Dec 2032):**

| Strategy | Gross Assets | Tax Adjustment | After-Tax Value |
|----------|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Balanced (A)** | ~$139,000 | RESP EAP tax (~$950); TFSA $0; RRSP $0 | **~$144,295** |
| **Aggressive RRSP (B)** | ~$165,000 | RRSP 30% tax (~$34,500); RESP lower EAP tax; TFSA $0 | **~$130,600** |
| **Education-First (C)** | ~$161,000 | RESP EAP tax (~$1,200); TFSA $0; RRSP 30% tax (~$4,200) | **~$168,800** |

The **30% effective tax rate** on RRSP withdrawals assumes retirement income is in a lower bracket than your current 31.48%. If you remain in a similar bracket, the after-tax values for Strategy B would be even lower. The TFSA's 100% tax-free nature and the RESP's favorable student-level taxation are powerful advantages that compound over time [40][45][53].
