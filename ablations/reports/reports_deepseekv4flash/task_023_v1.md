# Comprehensive Comparison of Data Breach Insurance: Chubb, Coalition, and Cowbell Cyber

## Executive Summary

As a small business owner in Austin, Texas with 15 employees handling both customer payment data (PCI-DSS exposure) and employee health records (HIPAA exposure), selecting the right cyber insurance carrier is critical. This report provides a detailed comparison across six dimensions for Chubb, Coalition, and Cowbell Cyber, based on official carrier documentation, policy forms, and reputable insurance broker analyses.

All three carriers offer robust coverage for businesses with dual PCI/HIPAA exposure, but they differ significantly in policy structure, sublimit treatment, incident response capabilities, and pricing philosophy. Coalition's new Active Cyber Policy (April 2025) provides the most innovative features including Vanishing Retention and unlimited reinstatements, while Chubb offers the most established track record with its Cyber Enterprise Risk Management (Cyber ERM) policy. Cowbell Cyber distinguishes itself through its AI-driven Cowbell Factors risk scoring system and rapid quoting capabilities.

## 1. Regulatory Fine Coverage & Sublimits

### Chubb

**Coverage Structure:** Chubb's Cyber Enterprise Risk Management (Cyber ERM) policy includes a specific **Regulatory Proceedings** insuring agreement as a third-party liability coverage, along with a separate **Payment Card Loss** insuring agreement [1][2]. The policy covers "regulatory fines and payment card losses" as distinct coverage components [3].

**PCI-DSS Fines and Penalties:** Chubb provides affirmative coverage for payment card losses through its Payment Card Loss insuring agreement. However, there is a critical legal precedent that small business owners must understand: In the 2016 case **P.F. Chang's China Bistro, Inc. v. Federal Insurance Company**, an Arizona federal court ruled that Chubb's cyber policy did not cover Mastercard assessments of approximately $1.9 million (including $1.7 million for fraudulent charges and $200,000 for notification fees) passed to P.F. Chang's by its acquiring bank. The court found that **contractual liability exclusions barred coverage** because P.F. Chang's had assumed liability under contract with its acquiring bank [4]. This means coverage depends heavily on how PCI assessments are triggered and whether they arise from contractual obligations versus direct regulatory action.

**HIPAA/HITECH Fines:** Chubb covers regulatory fines and penalties arising from HIPAA/HITECH enforcement actions by HHS/OCR through its Regulatory Proceedings coverage [1][5]. Insura.ai specifically identifies Chubb as offering "premium coverage with regulatory defense and HIPAA penalty sub-limits" [6].

**Sublimit Structure:** Chubb's Cyber ERM uses "multiple sub-limits and oftentimes sub-limits within sub-limits" [3]. Each insuring agreement (Regulatory Proceedings, Payment Card Loss, etc.) has its own specified limit or sublimit shown in the Declarations [7]. The IADC Law article warns that broker liability exposure exists for "failure to recommend an adequate policy limit and to inform the insured of sub-limits" [3].

**Defense Costs Treatment:** Per Chubb's Commercial Cyber Policy Guide for Agents and Brokers, defense costs are generally treated within the applicable limit structure, but specific details on whether defense costs are inside or outside sublimits for regulatory proceedings require reviewing the actual policy form [3].

### Coalition

**Coverage Structure:** Coalition provides affirmative, separate coverage for regulatory fines and penalties through two distinct insuring agreements:
- **Coverage B: Regulatory Defense and Penalties** — Covers defense costs and penalties from regulatory actions including HIPAA/HITECH enforcement
- **Coverage D: PCI Fines and Assessments** — Covers fines and assessments imposed by payment card brands for PCI-DSS non-compliance

These are separate insuring agreements, each with their own sublimits and retentions [8][9].

**Real Policy Example:** In an actual issued Coalition policy for Ventura Country Club (effective 12/15/2024–12/15/2025), the coverage structure showed:
- **Insuring Agreement B. Regulatory Defense and Penalties:** $1,000,000 limit / $2,500 retention
- **Insuring Agreement D. PCI Fines and Assessments:** $1,000,000 limit / $2,500 retention
- All coverages shared a $1,000,000 Aggregate Policy Limit [8]

**HIPAA-Specific Coverage:** Coalition launched a specialized healthcare cyber insurance policy (April 2019) that explicitly includes full HIPAA compliance support, coverage for costs to comply with an OCR-mandated security assessment following HIPAA violations, and in-house breach response [10]. The healthcare industry guide confirms Coalition maintains compliance with HIPAA, HITECH, GDPR, and PCI DSS regulations [11].

**Active Cyber Policy Enhancements:** Coalition's new Active Cyber Policy (effective April 15, 2025) integrates previously endorsement-based coverages into the base policy, providing clearer coverage for regulatory proceedings. The Vanishing Retention feature reduces retention to zero after consecutive claim-free years [12].

**Defense Costs:** Coalition operates on a **duty-to-defend** basis. The sample policy states: "We will have the right and duty to defend any claim against you seeking damages that are payable under the terms of this Policy" [8]. Incident response services (forensics, legal consultation) are provided without retention and without reducing policy limits [13].

### Cowbell Cyber

**Coverage Structure:** Cowbell Prime 100 and Prime 100 Pro policies explicitly include **PCI Fines & Penalties** and **Regulator Defense & Penalties** as separate third-party coverage line items [14][15]. In the Sonora Independent School District quote (2024-2025), both coverages were listed independently within the aggregate limit structure [14].

**PCI Fines Coverage:** Cowbell explicitly covers fines and penalties assessed by payment card brands (Visa, Mastercard, etc.) following a data breach involving cardholder data [14]. PCI DSS non-compliance fines can range from $5,000 to $100,000 per month until compliance is met [5][14][16].

**HIPAA Coverage:** Cowbell's standalone cyber insurance for medical and dental offices specifically addresses that HIPAA violations can result in fines of up to $1.5 million [17]. The Cowbell Factors compliance dimension measures alignment with "HIPAA, PCI, EU GDPR, and CCPA" [18][19].

**Sublimit Structure:** Specific sublimit dollar amounts for PCI fines and regulatory penalties are not publicly disclosed and are set at underwriting. Sublimits are specified in individual policy declarations [14][15]. Both coverages are separate line items within the aggregate limit structure.

**Defense Costs Treatment:** Defense costs for privacy-related regulatory proceedings are covered [20]. The exact treatment (inside vs. outside sublimits) is not explicitly stated in publicly available documentation and would be governed by the individual policy form [15].

## 3. Credit Monitoring & Legal Defense Coverage

### Chubb

**Credit Monitoring Services:** Per Chubb's Commercial Cyber Policy Guide for Agents and Brokers: "Credit monitoring services coverage applies as 'required to comply with Privacy or Cyber Laws,' **without a limitation of time (no cap of 12 or 24 months)**" [3]. This is a significant feature — Chubb does not cap credit monitoring at 12 or 24 months but covers it as required by applicable privacy or cyber laws. Credit monitoring is part of the "Cyber Incident Response Expenses" which includes costs for legal services, forensic investigations, public relations, notifications, and credit monitoring related to a cyber incident [7].

**Legal Defense Structure:** Chubb's Cyber ERM covers "Privacy and Network Security Liability" and "Cyber, Privacy and Network Security Liability" as third-party coverages [2][7]. The policy is structured as a claims-made policy for third-party liabilities, meaning coverage applies to claims first made during the policy period [2].

**Duty to Defend vs. Reimbursement:** The specific legal defense structure for Cyber ERM is that defense costs are generally treated within the applicable limit structure. The policy provides coverage for "regulatory fines" as part of its third-party liability coverage [3].

**Deductible Application:** The Danville policy shows a general retention of $25,000 per incident [7]. The LA LECHE LEAGUE policy shows a $10,000 retention for third-party coverages [2]. Retentions apply per incident.

### Coalition

**Credit Monitoring Services:** Credit monitoring is covered as part of Breach Response costs (Coverage E or First-Party Breach Response). The policy covers customer notification services, credit monitoring for affected individuals, and call center services [40]. Notification and credit monitoring vendors on Coalition's panel include Epiq and Kroll [30]. Post-incident monitoring from Coalition Incident Response provides at least 30 days of post-incident monitoring for the policyholder's systems [26].

**Legal Defense Structure:** Coalition's policy operates on a **duty-to-defend** basis. The sample policy states: "We will have the right and duty to defend any claim against you seeking damages that are payable under the terms of this Policy" [8].

**Key Defense Features:**
- Insurer selects defense counsel, but panel providers include breach counsel firms policyholders can engage [8][30]
- Incident response services are provided **without retention and without reducing policy limits** [13]
- Pre-claim assistance benefit available (e.g., $1,670 in ATU Local 1596 Pension Plan policy) [41]

**Defense Costs Inside vs. Outside Limits:** In Coalition's policy, the Network and Information Security Liability limit (e.g., $1,000,000) is shared between defense and indemnity. However, Breach Response costs (Coverage E) have separate limits that do not erode the liability limits. Incident response services are provided at no cost without retention and without reducing policy limits [42].

**Deductible/Retention Application:**
- Standard retentions: typically $1,000-$2,500 for most coverages [8]
- Funds Transfer Fraud retention: typically higher (e.g., $12,500 in sample policy) [8]
- **Vanishing Retention:** Reduces to zero after three consecutive claim-free years (25% reduction Year 1, 50% Year 2, 100% Year 3) [12]
- Reduced FTF retention if reported within 72 hours of initial fraudulent transfer [12]

## 4. Policy Exclusions for E-Commerce Businesses

### Chubb

**Payment Card Industry Exclusion:** The P.F. Chang's case established a critical precedent: Mastercard assessments of roughly $1.9 million were denied coverage because (1) Privacy Injury coverage did not apply since the acquiring bank had not sustained a privacy injury, and (2) **"Exclusions for liability assumed under any contract barred coverage"** — the contractual liability exclusion was applied [4]. The article warns: "A primary risk to a retailer following a cyber breach is an assessment from Visa or MasterCard passed on to it by an acquiring bank, and this court found those losses are not covered" [4].

**PCI Non-Compliance Impact:** PCI DSS 4.0, which became fully mandatory in March 2025, introduced significant new requirements around web skimming prevention, multi-factor authentication, and script management [45]. Insurers may deny claims due to "inaccurate security attestations during application" [45].

**Neglected Software Exploit Endorsement:** Chubb provides a **45-day grace period** to patch software vulnerabilities published as Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs). After 45 days, coinsurance percentages apply:
- 0% coinsurance if patched within 45 days (full coverage)
- Coinsurance increases with neglected software exploit duration up to over 365 days (up to 50% coinsurance) [25][24][46]

**Other Relevant Exclusions:**
- **Failure to Supply / Government-Mandated Shutdown Exclusions** (Danville policy) [7]
- **OFAC / Trade Sanctions Exclusions** [2]
- **Prior Acts / Knowledge Exclusions** — limits coverage for known prior events or pending proceedings [47]
- **Unencrypted Data** — proposal forms extensively ask about encryption, and status may affect coverage [48][2]
- **Third-Party Vendor Liability** — proposal form asks about vendor assessments, security certifications, and data protection agreements [2]

### Coalition

**Payment Card Industry Treatment:** Crucially, Coalition provides **affirmative coverage for PCI fines and assessments** (Coverage D). This is not an exclusion — it is an affirmative grant of coverage. The policy covers fines and assessments from payment card brands for PCI-DSS non-compliance following a covered security breach [8][9].

**PCI Compliance Requirements:** The policy's PCI Fines coverage may be triggered only if the insured was PCI-compliant at the time of the breach. The application specifically asks about encryption practices, backup procedures, MFA enforcement, and handling of sensitive data including PCI, PII, and PHI [49].

**Common E-Commerce Relevant Exclusions:**
- **Unencrypted Data:** The Mahoney Group notes that "if your business experiences a data breach involving unencrypted data, your insurer may deny the claim based on this exclusion" [50]. Coalition's application asks: "Does Named Insured implement encryption on laptop, desktop, and portable media devices?" [49]
- **Prior Knowledge Exclusion:** One of the most common exclusions, denying coverage for incidents known or foreseeable before policy inception [50]
- **Third-Party Vendor Liability:** Coalition's Network and Information Security Liability (NISL) covers these exposures, but vulnerabilities in third-party software cost businesses $4.33 million annually and cause 14% of breaches [51]
- **Business Class Restrictions:** Coalition's appetite **excludes** specific business classes including "payment processors" (as well as adult entertainment, gambling, cannabis, and data aggregators) [52]. A small business handling payment card data as a merchant (not as a processor) would generally be eligible

**General Exclusions (from sample Ventura Country Club policy):**
- Bodily injury (except emotional distress related to network security claims)
- Contractual liabilities (with certain exceptions)
- Discrimination
- Fraud by senior executives (after final adjudication)
- Intellectual property infringement (with exceptions)
- Natural disasters
- War and terrorism (except cyber terrorism)
- Prior knowledge [8]

### Cowbell Cyber

**Payment Card Industry Treatment:** Cowbell explicitly includes "PCI Fines & Penalties" as a covered third-party item, suggesting no blanket exclusion for PCI-related costs [14][15]. However, PCI compliance is a factor — adhering to PCI compliance requirements "can lead to lower premiums, better coverage limits, and fewer exclusions" [35].

**PCI Compliance Gaps:** PCI compliance is assessed as part of the Cowbell Factors compliance dimension [18][19]. "Cyber insurance providers now expect evidence of operational controls, not just descriptions" [53]. Failure to maintain PCI compliance could result in coverage gaps or denial of claims related to PCI fines [5].

**Encryption/Tokenization Requirements:** "Cyber insurers nowadays require businesses to implement reasonable security measures, including data encryption, to qualify for coverage" [35]. Cowbell recommends "protecting cardholder data requires encryption during transmission and storage" and "tokenization solutions to secure cardholder data effectively" [54].

**Unencrypted Data Exclusion:** Claims arising from breaches involving unencrypted data may be denied [35]. Cowbell Factors assesses network security, endpoint security, and compliance — all evaluating encryption practices [18].

**Other Relevant Exclusions:**
- **Prior Knowledge Exclusion:** Denies coverage for incidents known or foreseeable before policy inception [35]
- **Contractual Liability Exclusions:** May limit or exclude coverage for losses arising from business contractual obligations [35]
- **War and Terrorism Exclusion:** Excludes acts of war or terrorism, including cyberattacks by nation-states or terrorist groups [35]
- **System Failure Exclusion:** Cowbell Prime 100 excludes business interruptions due to system failure or voluntary shutdown not linked to cyber incidents (Prime 100 Pro includes system failure as an enhancement) [15]
- **Higher-Risk Sector Restrictions:** Cowbell restricts "higher-risk sectors such as cryptocurrency and online gambling" [33]

**E-Commerce Specific:** No specific e-commerce industry exclusion was found. Retail is actually listed as a target industry for Cowbell Prime 100 [14][15].

### Key Premium Drivers Across All Three Carriers

**1. Industry Classification:** "Industry is the single biggest driver of cyber premium" [57]. Healthcare and financial services face higher premiums, "potentially exceeding $20,000 due to regulated data exposure" [59][57]. Tech companies pay about 88% above the national average [57].

**2. Volume of Sensitive Records:** All three carriers' proposal forms ask about handling of PII, payment card information with transaction volumes, and HIPAA compliance status. Higher volumes of PHI and cardholder data directly increase premiums [2][49][18].

**3. Security Controls (10-25% Credits):** "Most carriers offer credits of 10-25% for documented security controls including multi-factor authentication, endpoint detection and response (EDR) software, employee phishing training, off-site backups, and a written incident response plan" [57]. "Missing basic controls like MFA or EDR can add 25% to 50% to your quote or disqualify you entirely" [59].

**4. Claims History:** "A single prior cyber claim, even one fully resolved, typically increases premium 25-50% for 3-5 years" [57].

**5. Revenue and Employee Count:** "A 20-to-49-person business has 325% higher cyber insurance costs than a sole proprietor" [59]. Premiums scale with revenue.

**6. State of Operation:** "Locations with strict privacy laws and plaintiff-friendly courts cost more to defend" [59]. Texas is in the middle range. California and New York see 20% higher premiums [59].

## Recommendations for Your Business

For a 15-employee business in Austin, Texas with both PCI-DSS and HIPAA exposure, the following considerations apply:

**Coalition** offers the most innovative policy features with the Active Cyber Policy, including Vanishing Retention that rewards good security hygiene, unlimited reinstatements for first-party incidents, and a 5-minute average claims response time. The Coalition Control platform provides continuous risk monitoring valued at $12,000/year. The affirmative PCI coverage (Coverage D) is explicit and clear, avoiding the contractual liability issues that plagued Chubb in the P.F. Chang's case.

**Chubb** provides the most established policy with a 45-day grace period for patching vulnerabilities (Neglected Software Exploit Endorsement) and uncapped credit monitoring. However, the P.F. Chang's precedent means careful review of how PCI assessments are triggered is essential. Chubb is rated as the "overall best cyber insurer for 2026" by Security.org [1].

**Cowbell Cyber** offers the fastest quoting process (under 5 minutes) and the most accessible entry point for small businesses, with deductibles starting at $1,000. The Cowbell Factors system provides continuous risk improvement feedback, and the curated vendor panel includes specialized vendors like CyberClan with 15-minute response guarantees.

**Key Action Items:**
1. Request specific sublimit dollar amounts for PCI Fines and Regulatory Defense from each carrier
2. Review whether defense costs are inside or outside sublimits for regulatory proceedings
3. Confirm how each carrier handles contractual PCI assessments (particularly relevant given Chubb's P.F. Chang's case)
4. Ask about the specific impact of HIPAA OCR proceedings on policy limits
5. Consider implementing MFA, EDR, encryption, and regular penetration testing to qualify for 10-25% premium credits
6. Request quotes from all three carriers to compare actual pricing for your specific profile
