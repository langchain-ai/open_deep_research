# Comprehensive Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality Rates and Prenatal Care Access: Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh versus Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Executive Summary

This report provides a rigorously revised comparative analysis of maternal health outcomes for two refugee populations: Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh (~963,000) and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand (~34,000–46,000). The analysis tracks five health indicators—ANC 4+ visit completion, skilled birth attendance (SBA), postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, neonatal mortality, and maternal mortality ratios (MMR)—across the 2017–2023 period.

**Key quantitative findings:**

- **Cox's Bazar** shows dramatic improvement in facility-based delivery rates (from 3.9% in November 2017 to 85% by early 2024), yet maternal mortality remains persistently high (estimated 179–447 per 100,000 live births, varying by source and year). Obstetric hemorrhage is the leading cause of maternal death, accounting for 26.7%–44% of deaths across years. Neonatal mortality is estimated at 27 per 1,000 live births (2017–2018).

- **Mae La camp** has no published year-by-year quantitative data (2017–2023) for any of the five requested maternal health indicators specifically for this camp. The nearest proxy data comes from the SMRU combined refugee and migrant cohort (MMR of 176 per 100,000 live births, 1986–2024) and historical pre-2017 studies showing neonatal mortality declined from 21.8 to 10.7 per 1,000 live births (2008–2011).

- **Critical data gap**: Of 70 potential indicator-year cells (5 indicators × 2 settings × 7 years), only approximately 20% have documented, setting-specific quantitative figures for the 2017–2023 period. The most severe gaps exist for Mae La camp (all indicators, all years 2017–2023).

## 1. Population Context and Camp Profiles

### 1.1 Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh — Rohingya Refugee Context

The Rohingya refugee population in Cox's Bazar camps is approximately 963,000–975,350 individuals as of 2023–2024 [1][2]. The population comprises Forcibly Displaced Myanmar Nationals (FDMNs) who fled ethnic cleansing and violence in Myanmar's Rakhine State, with the largest exodus occurring after August 25, 2017. Women and girls represent approximately 51.3% of the refugee population [1].

The camps are located in the Ukhia and Teknaf Upazilas of Cox's Bazar District, with 33 overcrowded camps featuring limited privacy, poor sanitation, and challenging terrain. The Government of Bangladesh restricts refugees from working legally or moving freely outside camps, designating them as "Forcibly Displaced Myanmar Nationals" rather than refugees [3].

**Key contextual factors:**
- Camp maturity: Emergency response established 2017
- Population: ~963,000 (21–28 times larger than Mae La)
- Health system: Emergency response transitioning to protracted care
- Number of actors: 49+ partner organizations, 458 registered health facilities [4]
- Legal status: Bangladesh not signatory to 1951 Refugee Convention

### 1.2 Mae La Camp, Thailand — Karen Refugee Context

Mae La refugee camp (also known as Beh Klaw or Maela) was established in 1984 in Tha Song Yang District, Tak Province, approximately 8 kilometers from the Thai-Myanmar border [5]. As of June 2019, the camp housed 35,373 persons of concern to UNHCR, with 93% of ethnic Karen origin, 51% female [6]. By August 2022, the population had declined to approximately 34,164 due to ongoing third-country resettlement [7]. Some sources estimate the population at over 46,000 [8].

Over 96,000 refugees had been resettled from Mae La to countries including the United States, Australia, Canada, Finland, and Norway since 2004 [5]. Thailand is not a signatory to the 1951 Refugee Convention; refugees live under a policy of temporary shelter overseen by the Ministry of Interior.

**Key contextual factors:**
- Camp maturity: Established 1984 (protracted system, 40+ years)
- Population: ~34,000–46,000
- Health system: Established primary health care (PHC) system with institutional memory
- Key providers: SMRU (Shoklo Malaria Research Unit) for maternal/child health; previously IRC for hospital care
- Legal status: Thailand not signatory to 1951 Convention; refugees as "temporary shelter residents"

**Critical distinction in health service providers:**
- **Mae La camp** → SMRU provides maternal and child health services
- **Mae La Oon camp** (different camp in Mae Hong Son Province) → Malteser International provides health services [9]
- **Mae Ra Ma Luang camp** (different camp) → Malteser International provides health services [9]
- **Do not conflate these camps** — Malteser International's published data (MMR 126.5/100,000, 2000–2018) pertains to Mae Ra Ma Luang and Mae La Oon, NOT Mae La [10][11]

## 4. Operational Analysis of Healthcare Delivery Models

### 4.1 Cox's Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospital Model

#### Organizational Structure

The Health Sector in Cox's Bazar, led by the World Health Organization (WHO), coordinates an integrated health system involving **49 active health partners and 15 appealing partners operating 458 registered facilities** including health posts and primary health centres [4]. The Sexual and Reproductive Health (SRH) Working Group, led by UNFPA, coordinates SRH services across all 33 camps.

**Key coordination bodies:**
- **Health Sector** (Lead: WHO): Strategic Advisory Group includes MOHFW, RRRC, WHO, UNHCR, UNFPA, UNICEF, IOM, BRAC, IRC, MSF, Save the Children, IFRC [4]
- **SRH Working Group** (Lead: UNFPA): Publishes quarterly bulletins, oversees MCH Card operationalization [42]
- **Adolescent SRH Technical Committee**: Reached over 63,000 with SRH services [42]
- **Newborn Technical Sub-Committee**: Focused on neonatal care improvements [42]

#### Staff Headcounts and Cadre Sizes

**Explicit data gap:** Comprehensive, disaggregated headcount data for specific cadres across all camps and partners is not available in a single consolidated source. The following specific workforce numbers were documented:

- **UNFPA midwives**: ~35 midwives deployed initially; approximately 283 midwives supported by UNFPA in 2023, representing over 60% of midwives in the health sector response [43]
- **Midwifery Mentorship Program**: Over 285 midwives trained; 40 midwives trained as trainers [42]
- **IOM medical professionals**: Nearly 200 medical professionals (doctors, paramedics, nurses, midwives) [44]
- **Skilled birth attendants**: Totaled 1,474 as of March 2023, representing **31% below expected staffing level** [45]
- **Community health workers**: 1,437 deployed conducting household visits; over 1.9 million household visits in a quarter in 2021 [46]
- **Traditional Birth Attendants (TBAs)**: 80 TBAs transformed into SRH Referral Assistants; CPI/Green Hill trained TBAs in Kutupalong [47]

#### Midwife-to-Population Ratio

**Explicit data gap:** No specific midwife-to-population ratio is published for the Rohingya camps. The approximate population is ~975,000 refugees. If counting only the ~283 UNFPA-supported midwives (2023), the rough ratio would be approximately 1 midwife per ~3,440 refugees. However, this excludes midwives employed by IOM, BRAC, MSF, and other NGOs.

#### Referral Infrastructure

**Documented referral challenges:**
- "Lack of transport to reach a facility (10 cases)" was the most prominent second delay (MPMSR 2021) [30]
- "Ambulances cannot penetrate much of the camp terrain, forcing women to be carried on stretchers over difficult terrain" [48]
- Anecdotal account: A woman "who was referred to three different facilities before she could access lifesaving care. Her complete referral journey was over six hours" [49]
- **No systematically documented average referral time** found in available sources

**Ambulance fleet:**
- IOM deployed 10 ambulances equipped with critical medical gear (February 2019) [50]
- Emergency Referral Transport Service (Matri Sheba) covered all 33 camps, referring 10,492 emergency obstetric cases in 2023 [43]
- IOM coordinates medical referrals with 24-hour hotline, overseeing transfer of over 200 patients weekly [50]

### 4.2 Mae La Camp: Thai Ministry of Public Health Integration Model

#### Organizational Structure

The healthcare model for Mae La involves a hybrid system:

**Pre-2025 (during the analysis window):**
- **SMRU (Shoklo Malaria Research Unit)**: Provides maternal-child health services, antenatal clinics, skilled birth attendants, manages obstetric emergencies except cesarean sections [20]
- **IRC (International Rescue Committee)**: Operated the main camp hospital funded by US aid [51]
- **Referral hospitals**: Tha Song Yang Hospital and Mae Ramat Hospital in Tak Province [52]

**Post-February 2025 (outside the analysis window, but relevant for context):**
- US-funded hospital forced to close due to funding freeze; Thai MOPH stepped in to provide emergency care [51]

#### Staff Headcounts and Cadre Sizes

**Explicit data gap:** Comprehensive, disaggregated headcount data for specific cadres in Mae La camp is not available in published sources. The following workforce numbers were documented:

- **80 health workers total** across three camps (Mae La, Umpiem, Nupo) receiving 2,500 THB/month from Thai MOPH as of August 2025 [52]
- **SMRU**: 378 employees total across all operations on the Thai-Myanmar border (midwives, obstetric ultrasonographers, health care assistants, public health students, malaria post workers) [53]
- **IRC hospital**: "Minimum one to maximum two full time expatriate medical doctors specifically assigned to clinical supervision" [54]

#### Midwife-to-Population Ratio

**Explicit data gap:** No specific midwife-to-population ratio is published for Mae La camp. SMRU provides midwifery training and services across the border region, but precise numbers of midwives assigned specifically to Mae La are not documented.

#### Referral System

- Emergency cases referred to Thai provincial hospitals (camp lacks surgical specialists) [55]
- Tha Song Yang Hospital is the primary referral hospital [52]
- Previously, "Only severe cases were referred to hospitals along the borders" [54]
- Patients transferred reportedly do not face extra charges: "Once the hospital staff know the patient is from the refugee camps, they usually don't ask for any other fees" [52]

## 5. Cause-of-Death Analysis

### 5.1 Cox's Bazar (Rohingya): Maternal Death Cause Breakdown

The Maternal and Perinatal Mortality Surveillance and Response (MPMSR) system, established in 2019, provides the most comprehensive cause-of-death data. The system uses WHO's EWARS for community-based notifications and facility audits.

**MPMSR 2020 [29]:**
- Obstetric hemorrhage: 26.7% of maternal deaths
- Hypertensive disorders: 20%
- Pregnancy-related infections: 13.3%

**MPMSR 2021 [30]:**
- Obstetric hemorrhage: 29.2% (leading cause)
- Pregnancy-induced hypertensive disorders: 23%
- Other obstetric complication: 9.4%
- Pregnancy-related infections (sepsis): 5.2%
- Unanticipated complications of management: 2.1%
- Indirect causes: 22%

**MPMSR 2022 [13]:**
- Hemorrhage: 44%
- Hypertensive disorders: 31%

**MPMSR 2023 [31]:**
- Obstetric hemorrhage: 35%
- Hypertensive disorders: 33%

**Three Delays Model (cited across all MPMSR reports):**
- **First Delay (deciding to seek care)**: Preference for informal providers (31.6% in 2021), poor knowledge of danger signs (21.5%), negligence (17.7%)
- **Second Delay (reaching care)**: Transportation barriers, poor infrastructure, restricted mobility
- **Third Delay (receiving care)**: Quality of care issues (45.1% of facility death audits revealed suboptimal care in 2021), inadequate blood transfusion services, protocol non-compliance

## 6. Cultural Mediation Practices for Traditional Birth Attendants

### 6.1 Cox's Bazar: Engaging Rohingya Dai Ma

**Cultural role:**
- Traditional birth attendants (dai ma) are older women with extensive childbirth experience but limited formal training
- Nine of 10 pregnant women interviewed planned to use a dai ma for upcoming births; only 1 planned a hospital birth [56]
- Delivery choices made as a family, with husband and parents-in-law as primary decision-makers: "Pregnant women can't make any decisions for themselves. They must receive permission from their husbands, in-laws, and parents" [47]

**Cultural barriers to facility-based care:**
- Concerns about male staff, being undressed during facility-based births, fear of Cesarean section
- Absence of family support during labor — Rohingya birthing culture emphasizes familial presence
- Beliefs about jinn (bad spirits) making women avoid night hospital visits
- Rumors that "newborns are killed or sold at these health facilities" (though less common recently) [57]

**Intervention strategies:**
- **CPI/Green Hill TBA Training (Kutupalong, 2021–2022)**: Trained TBAs to refer women to health facilities rather than conduct home deliveries; facility births rose from 33% (2020) to over 95% (2022) in the project catchment area [47]
- **TBA transformation**: 80 TBAs transformed into SRH Referral Assistants, effectively doubling community referrals [42]
- **UNFPA midwives** work alongside TBAs, described as "a very essential link from the community to the facility" [47]

### 6.2 Mae La: Incorporating TBAs into the Health System

**Cultural context:**
- Approximately 80% of women traditionally delivered at home assisted by TBAs in Karen culture [58]
- Strong Karen cultural preferences for home births reinforced by spiritual beliefs, including the concept of 37 kla (essential spirits) [59]
- Postpartum practices involve extended rest and specific dietary restrictions [59]

**Intervention strategies:**
- **Mother Support Groups (MSGs) led by TBAs (2012)**: Use TBAs' knowledge and community reputation to teach importance of ANC and exclusive breastfeeding [58]
- **KWO TBA Training (2005–ongoing)**: Karen Women's Organization provides hygiene supplies, single-use maternity kits, and annual training; created first Karen language TBA Training Manual with Mae Tao Clinic [58]
- **SMRU Birth Attendant Training (2008–2015)**: Six-month curriculum adapted from WHO guidelines; trained local Karen and Burman women as skilled birth attendants; led to 25% increase in clinic births and 16.4% decrease in episiotomy rates [60]

### 6.3 Comparative Analysis

Both settings demonstrate that TBAs can be effective conduits for improving maternal health outcomes when engaged respectfully. The Rohingya model produced a more dramatic facility birth rate increase over a shorter period (33% to 95% in two years in the CPI catchment), while the Karen model achieved greater integration of TBAs as ongoing members of the health system rather than temporary referral agents.

## 7. Funding Analysis with Causal Qualification

### 7.1 Cox's Bazar: UNFPA and Broader Funding Context

**Pre-2021 funding stress:**
- As early as 2019, funding gaps resulted in scaled-down reproductive health programs: 318,500 women and girls of reproductive age left without adequate family planning; 31,200 pregnant women without adequate prenatal and safe birth care [61]

**2021 and Joint Response Plan:**
- By 2021, stock-outs of essential medicines reported by 45% of facilities [22]
- Only 35% of the required $255 million needed to support Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar was funded [62]

**Post-2021 cascade:**
- **2023**: Seven health posts closed due to funding constraints; SBAs 31% below expected staffing level [45]
- **2024**: Health Sector had a funding gap of 60% against requested $86.8 million [4]
- **2025**: Severe funding cuts forced closure or near-closure of over 1,000 health facilities and mobile teams, along with 250 women's safe spaces [63]
- UNFPA warned that up to 12 million women and girls could lose access to essential health services in 2026 [63]

**Contradictory evidence:**
- Despite funding constraints, maternal health **service utilization** continued improving: facility delivery rates rose from 67% (June 2021) to 85% (early 2024)
- However, maternal **deaths** did not decline correspondingly: 96 (2021) → 83 (2022) → 57 (2023) — the absolute number declined, but MMR estimates vary widely (179–447)
- The contradictory trend of improving utilization alongside persistent high mortality reflects quality-of-care gaps rather than solely funding issues: 51% of women who died in 2023 had attended ≥4 ANC visits; 75% of deaths occurred in facilities [31]

**Causal qualification:** The available evidence does not establish a clear linear causal relationship between the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction and specific maternal health outcomes. Service utilization improved while mortality remained high, suggesting multiple factors at play including surveillance improvements, structural challenges, and quality-of-care gaps.

### 7.2 Mae La Camp: Funding Context

**No specific UNFPA 2021 funding reduction data found for Mae La camp.** The Thai-Myanmar border refugee camps operate under a different funding architecture, with The Border Consortium (TBC) as the primary camp coordination body rather than UNFPA-led joint response plans.

**Documented funding trends:**
- The Border Consortium faced funding cuts affecting approximately 10,000 community workers [64]
- "Inevitably there will be difficulties, but this is the reality we have to face as funding support for camps continues to decline," said Sally Thompson, executive director of The Border Consortium [64]
- 60% of total costs for food and healthcare in refugee camps along the border previously covered by US government [52]
- The most significant documented funding impact for Mae La is the **2025 US aid freeze** (outside the analysis window), which led to closure of the IRC-run hospital [51]

**Key distinction:** For Mae La, the available evidence points to **chronic underfunding** of the entire camp structure (TBC, NGOs) rather than a specific 2021 UNFPA reduction shock. The funding pressures are more diffuse and longer-term.

## 8. Methodological Limitations and Data Quality Assessment

### 8.1 Critical Confounders Preventing Simple Comparisons

| Dimension | Cox's Bazar | Mae La | Implications |
|-----------|-------------|--------|--------------|
| Population size | ~963,000 | ~34,000–46,000 | 21–28 times larger; different service delivery dynamics |
| Camp maturity | Emergency (2017) | Established (1984) | 33+ years of institutional learning in Mae La |
| Health system age | <10 years | 40+ years | Different levels of institutional memory and data systems |
| Surveillance system | MPMSR (established 2019) | No camp-specific published surveillance | Different data availability |
| Data availability | Relatively robust (MPMSR, SRH bulletins) | Extremely limited post-2017 | Direct comparison of outcomes is methodologically unsound |

### 8.2 Data Quality Issues

**Cox's Bazar:**
- MMR estimates vary by 250% (179 to 447 per 100,000) across sources
- SBA data primarily uses facility-based delivery rates as proxies, not direct SBA measurement
- PPH data limited to proportions of maternal deaths, not population-based incidence
- No year-by-year neonatal mortality data after 2017–2018

**Mae La:**
- No year-by-year quantitative data for any of the five requested indicators specifically for Mae La camp (2017–2023)
- The SMRU combined cohort data (which includes Mae La) is aggregated with migrant clinics and other refugee camps
- Malteser International data (MMR 126.5/100,000) pertains to Mae Ra Ma Luang and Mae La Oon camps, NOT Mae La
- Pre-2017 historical data suggests historically good outcomes but cannot be assumed to persist

## 9. Summary of Conclusions

### 9.1 Key Quantitative Findings

1. **Cox's Bazar trajectory**: Facility-based delivery rates improved dramatically from 3.9% (Nov 2017) to 85% (early 2024), yet MMR remains persistently high (179–447/100,000). This represents one of the fastest documented increases in service coverage in a humanitarian setting, but quality-of-care gaps persist.

2. **Mae La data gap**: The most significant finding for Mae La is the absence of published, camp-specific quantitative maternal health data for the 2017–2023 period. The nearest proxy is the SMRU combined cohort MMR of 176/100,000 (1986–2024).

3. **PPH surveillance gap**: Neither setting reports population-based PPH incidence rates. All available data is limited to PPH as a proportion of maternal deaths, which is subject to significant methodological variability.

4. **Neonatal mortality gap**: Only one NMR figure exists for Cox's Bazar (27/1,000, 2017–2018) and none for Mae La (2017–2023). Pre-2017 data shows Mae La's NMR declined to 10.7/1,000 (2011), substantially lower than Cox's Bazar.

5. **Data availability**: Of 70 potential indicator-year cells, only approximately 20% have documented, setting-specific quantitative figures.

### 9.2 Operational Conclusions

1. **Protracted vs. emergency systems**: Mae La's 40-year health system shows higher historical ANC coverage and SBA rates than Cox's Bazar's emergency response, but the absence of post-2017 data prevents confirmation of whether this advantage persisted.

2. **Quality vs. access**: The Cox's Bazar paradox—improving facility delivery rates alongside persistent high mortality—underscores that access alone does not guarantee survival. Quality-of-care gaps (inadequate blood transfusion, protocol non-compliance) remain critical barriers.

3. **TBA engagement**: Both settings demonstrate that TBAs can be effective conduits for improving maternal health outcomes when engaged respectfully and integrated into the health system rather than replaced.

4. **Funding impacts require qualification**: The available evidence does not establish a clear linear causal relationship between funding reductions and specific health outcomes in either setting. Multiple confounding factors (surveillance changes, quality gaps, structural barriers) complicate attribution.

### 9.3 Recommendations for Further Research

1. Access SMRU's internal databases directly for disaggregated Mae La camp-specific data (2017–2023)
2. Establish population-based PPH incidence surveillance in both settings
3. Standardize MMR estimation methodologies across sources to reduce the current 250% variation
4. Publish year-by-year NMR data for Cox's Bazar (currently only one estimate exists for 2017–2018)
5. Conduct comparative implementation research on TBA integration models to identify best practices transferable between settings
