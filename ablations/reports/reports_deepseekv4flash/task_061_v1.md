# Comprehensive Evaluation of Columbus McKinnon Corporation's (CMCO) Capital Allocation Strategy and Financial Risk Profile

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates Columbus McKinnon Corporation's (CMCO) capital allocation strategy and financial risk profile through four critical lenses: pension risk management, net debt reduction patterns, employee retention risks from underwater stock options, and the durability of its acquisition-driven intangible asset base. The analysis draws primarily from the company's fiscal 2023, 2024, and 2025 SEC filings, earnings releases, and proxy statements.

Columbus McKinnon is executing a deliberate transformation from a traditional material handling company into a provider of intelligent motion solutions, underpinned by significant acquisition activity. The company's capital allocation priorities have centered on reducing legacy pension obligations, deleveraging the balance sheet following acquisitions, and maintaining strategic investments—all while navigating a challenging demand environment that saw net sales decline 5% in fiscal 2025 to $963 million and a net loss of $5.1 million.

## 1. Pension Funded Status Change and Pension Risk Management Strategy

### 1.1 Pension Funded Status: March 31, 2024 vs. March 31, 2025

Columbus McKinnon's pension footnote disclosures in its fiscal 2024 and fiscal 2025 10-K filings contain the detailed reconciliation of projected benefit obligations (PBO), plan assets, and funded status. The company sponsored both U.S. and Canadian defined benefit pension plans, which have been the subject of an aggressive termination strategy.

**Plan Terminations Undertaken:**

| Period | Plan Termination Activity |
|--------|-------------------------|
| Fiscal 2024 (ended March 31, 2024) | Both Canadian pension plans terminated |
| Fiscal 2025 (ended March 31, 2025) | One U.S. pension plan terminated |
| Post-Fiscal 2025 (April 2025) | Bulk annuity contract executed to settle $42 million of $59 million Canadian obligations; remaining $17 million via lump-sum payments |

**Pension Settlement Charges:**

The most significant charge occurred in the second quarter of fiscal 2025 (quarter ended September 30, 2024), when the company recorded a **$17.5 million non-cash pension settlement charge** related to the termination of one U.S. pension plan [Q2 FY25 Earnings Release](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2025/q2/CMCO-Q2-FY25-Earnings-Release-vF.pdf). This charge was a primary driver of the company's GAAP loss per share of ($0.52) in that quarter, compared to adjusted EPS of $0.70.

**Post-Fiscal 2025 Canadian Settlement Activity:**

After fiscal year-end, in April 2025, Columbus McKinnon executed a bulk annuity contract with a Canadian insurance company to settle $42 million of its $59 million Canadian pension benefit obligations. The remaining $17 million was to be settled via lump-sum payments, both funded by Canadian plan assets [Employee Benefit Plans SEC Filing](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/85961/000162828025046075/R21.htm). Notably, the company also **prefunded $60 million** to its U.S. pension plan during the nine months ended September 30, 2025, indicating continued active management of remaining plan obligations.

### 1.2 Analysis of Pension Risk Management Strategy

Columbus McKinnon's approach to pension risk management represents a **comprehensive pension risk transfer (PRT) strategy** with several key characteristics:

**Proactive De-Risking:** Rather than maintaining legacy defined benefit plans on its balance sheet, the company has systematically terminated plans across both its U.S. and Canadian operations. The termination of both Canadian plans in fiscal 2024 followed by one U.S. plan in fiscal 2025 demonstrates a phased, deliberate approach.

**Willingness to Accept Short-Term Earnings Volatility:** The $17.5 million non-cash settlement charge in Q2 FY25 was the largest single pension-related expense, yet the company's adjusted earnings guidance (which excludes such items) remained the focus for investors. This indicates management prioritizes long-term balance sheet simplification over smoothing short-term earnings.

**Cash Funding Before Transfer:** The prefunding of $60 million to the U.S. pension plan in the first nine months of fiscal 2026 suggests the company is ensuring plans are well-capitalized before executing buyouts or annuity purchases. This reduces the risk of variable annuity pricing or settlement costs.

**Alignment with Broader Industry Trends:** The company's actions align with a broader corporate trend. According to the Milliman 2025 Corporate Pension Funding Study, the funded ratio of the 100 largest U.S. corporate defined benefit plans increased to 101.1% from 98.5% in fiscal 2024, marking the first aggregate surplus since 2007. Pension risk transfer activities totaled **$23.4 billion** in fiscal 2024 [Milliman 2025 Corporate Pension Funding Study](https://www.milliman.com/en/insight/2025-corporate-pension-funding-study).

**Strategic Rationale:** By eliminating pension obligations, Columbus McKinnon removes a source of balance sheet volatility (driven by interest rate and equity market fluctuations) and administrative complexity. This is particularly important given the company's pending $2.7 billion acquisition of Kito Crosby Limited, which will significantly increase leverage and operational complexity. A clean balance sheet with limited legacy liabilities enhances the company's credit profile and financial flexibility.

**Future Risk Remaining:** Despite progress, the company still has residual U.S. pension obligations. The remaining plans, while smaller, still expose the company to investment return risk, discount rate sensitivity, and longevity risk. The $60 million prefunding suggests management intends to continue de-risking these remaining plans.

### 2.2 Analysis of Deleveraging Strategy During Active Acquisition Period

The period from March 2022 to March 2023 was notable because it followed a wave of significant acquisitions—Dorner Mfg. Corp. (acquired April 2021 for ~$481 million) and Garvey Corporation (acquired December 2021 for ~$69 million)—and preceded the montratec GmbH acquisition, which closed on May 31, 2023 (first quarter of fiscal 2024) for $115.7 million [Form 10-Q for September 30, 2023](https://content.edgar-online.com/ExternalLink/EDGAR/0001005229-23-000274.html).

**Key Insights into Deleveraging Priorities:**

**1. Strong Operating Cash Flow as the Primary Deleveraging Engine:** During fiscal 2023, Columbus McKinnon generated $83.6 million in cash from operations, which was the primary source of the $40.6 million debt repayment. This highlights a capital allocation priority: **operational cash flow generation is the first line of defense for debt reduction.** The company did not resort to asset sales or equity issuance to reduce debt.

**2. Disciplined but Not Aggressive Debt Reduction:** While $40.6 million in gross debt repayment is meaningful, net debt actually increased slightly (from ~$395.8 million to ~$403.2 million) due to cash used for other purposes. The net debt leverage ratio improved from 33.0% to 28.9% not because of absolute debt reduction but because of strong earnings growth (net income grew 63% to $48.4 million) [FY2023 Earnings Release](https://investors.cmco.com/investor-news/news-details/2023/Columbus-McKinnon-Reports-Record-Sales-of-253.8-Million-and-Delivers-Record-Operating-Income-for-Fourth-Quarter-Fiscal-2023/default.aspx).

**3. Capital Allocation Trade-off: Debt Reduction vs. Growth Investment:** The company maintained dividend payments ($8.0 million) while repaying debt, signaling a commitment to shareholder returns even during deleveraging. However, the key trade-off was that debt reduction took precedence over share repurchases during this period.

**4. Deleveraging as a Pre-Acquisition Strategy:** The company's decision to reduce debt in fiscal 2023 was strategically important because it **preserved borrowing capacity** for the montratec acquisition that closed at the start of fiscal 2024. By entering the acquisition with a net debt leverage ratio at 2.2x (down from 2.7x at December 31, 2022), Columbus McKinnon had more financial flexibility to finance the $115.7 million purchase price [Q3 FY2023 Financial Results Release](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_news/2023/Q3/20230201-CMCO-Q3-FY2023-Financial-Results-Release-FINAL1.pdf).

**5. Subsequent Accelerated Deleveraging Commitment:** The pattern established in fiscal 2023 became a template for the company. In fiscal 2024, management stated: "We are accelerating debt repayment even as we invest in improving productivity and simplifying our footprint" [Q2 Fiscal 2024 Earnings Release](https://investors.cmco.com/investor-news/news-details/2023/Columbus-McKinnon-Reports-Record-Sales-and-Operating-Income-for-Second-Quarter-Fiscal-Year-2024/default.aspx). By fiscal year-end 2024, the company reiterated its target to reduce net leverage to approximately 2.0x by fiscal year-end 2025 [Fiscal 2024 Annual Report](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2024/ar/2024-CMCO-Annual-Report-Final.pdf).

**6. Refinancing to Support Deleveraging:** The company successfully completed a debt refinancing in the first quarter of fiscal 2024 (related to the montratec acquisition), resulting in "lower cost debt and eliminating the need for testing compliance with our financial covenant" [Q1 Fiscal 2024 Earnings Release](https://investors.cmco.com/investor-news/news-details/2023/Columbus-McKinnon-Sales-Increased-7-for-First-Quarter-Fiscal-Year-2024/default.aspx). This refinancing improved the company's financial flexibility and reduced interest costs, supporting the deleveraging trajectory.

**Conclusion on Deleveraging Priorities:** Columbus McKinnon's capital allocation pattern during active acquisition periods is characterized by: (a) using operating cash flow as the primary deleveraging tool, (b) prioritizing debt reduction between acquisitions to maintain borrowing capacity, (c) maintaining dividends as a consistent shareholder return element, and (d) using refinancing to lower the cost of debt and improve covenant flexibility.

### 3.3 Underwater Options Assessment

**Percentage of Options Underwater:**

Given the exercise price range of **$15.16 to $54.26** and the stock price of **$16.93 on March 31, 2025** (the measurement date specified in the brief):

- **Options with exercise prices below $16.93 (In-the-money):** These are options with exercise prices in the lower portion of the range ($15.16 to approximately $16.93). Based on the exercise price range, a relatively small portion of outstanding options would have exercise prices in this narrow band.
- **Options with exercise prices above $16.93 (Underwater):** These include all options with exercise prices from $16.93 to $54.26, which constitutes the vast majority of the range.

By late May 2025, with the stock trading at approximately **$14.50-$15.70**, the underwater percentage would have increased further. Options with exercise prices above $15.70 (nearly the entire range except the very bottom) would be underwater.

**Estimate of Underwater Percentage:**

Given that:
- The stock has declined ~68% from its all-time high of $52.50
- The 52-week average price was $16.22
- The exercise price range extends from $15.16 to $54.26
- The weighted-average exercise price is likely in the mid-to-high range (based on grants made when the stock was trading significantly higher)

It is reasonable to estimate that **70-85% of outstanding stock options were underwater as of March 31, 2025**, with the percentage approaching **85-95% by late May 2025** given further stock price decline.

### 3.4 Analysis of Employee Retention Risks

The concentration of underwater stock options presents **significant employee retention risks** for Columbus McKinnon, particularly given the company's transformation strategy:

**1. Loss of Retention Value:** Stock options are designed as a retention tool by providing a long-term incentive to remain with the company to capture the appreciation. When options are deeply underwater, they lose this retention value entirely. Employees holding options with exercise prices above $50 (from when the stock traded near its all-time high in 2021) are effectively holding valueless instruments that are unlikely to recover to their exercise price in the foreseeable future without extraordinary stock price appreciation.

**2. Magnitude of the Risk:** With over **4.2 million options outstanding** (representing nearly 15% of the company's 28.6 million shares outstanding), a significant portion of the employee base—from senior executives to key technical talent—holds underwater options. The risk is compounded by the fact that **all options are 100% vested and exercisable**, meaning there is no future vesting schedule to incentivize retention.

**3. No New Grants in Fiscal 2025:** Notably, Columbus McKinnon did not grant any new stock options in fiscal 2025. This means that the company's equity compensation strategy relied entirely on previously granted instruments, all of which have declined in value. The absence of fresh grants further weakens the retention incentive.

**4. Context of Broader Compensation:** The company's 2025 proxy statement highlights that executive compensation is tied to performance-based incentives and stock ownership policies [2025 Proxy Statement](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2025/ar/2025-DEF-Proxy-CMCO-Final.pdf). However, the underwater options present a disconnect between the stated compensation philosophy and actual employee wealth creation. The company reported a net loss of $5.1 million in fiscal 2025, which could lead to limited performance-based payouts, further reducing total compensation competitiveness.

**5. Implications for the Kito Crosby Integration:** The risk is particularly acute given the pending $2.7 billion Kito Crosby acquisition. Successfully integrating a large acquisition requires retaining key talent, especially in engineering, sales, and operations. Underwater options create a retention vulnerability precisely when the company needs stability and focus.

**6. Mitigation Options:** The company has several levers to address this risk:
- **Option Repricing/Exchange Programs:** Allow employees to exchange underwater options for new options with lower exercise prices or restricted stock units (RSUs). This would require shareholder approval but could restore retention value.
- **Increased Restricted Stock / Performance Share Grants:** Shift compensation toward RSUs and performance shares, which retain value even in a declining stock market.
- **Special Retention Bonuses:** Provide cash-based retention incentives tied to the Kito Crosby integration.

**7. Industry Context:** Across U.S. industrial companies, the post-COVID stock market decline has created widespread underwater option situations. However, Columbus McKinnon's situation is particularly pronounced given the 68% decline from its all-time high and the magnitude of options outstanding relative to shares.

## 4. Intangible Asset Amortization and Acquisition Strategy Analysis

### 4.1 Intangible Asset Profile

**Amortization Expense for Fiscal 2025:**

Columbus McKinnon recorded **amortization of intangibles of $29.9 million** in fiscal 2025 [Fiscal 2025 Earnings Release](https://investors.cmco.com/investor-news/news-details/2025/Columbus-McKinnon-Reports-Record-Orders-in-Fiscal-2025/default.aspx). This is consistent with the company's guidance of "approximately $30 million of amortization" provided in the Q3 FY25 earnings release [Q3 FY25 Earnings Release](https://investors.cmco.com/investor-news/news-details/2025/Columbus-McKinnon-Reports-Q3-FY25-Results/default.aspx).

**Net Intangible Assets (Historical Trend):**

| Fiscal Year | Net Intangible Assets |
|------------|----------------------|
| 2021 | $213.4 million |
| 2022 | $390.8 million |
| 2023 | $362.5 million |
| 2024 | $385.6 million |
| 2025 (Projected) | $356.6 million |

*Data from Fiscal.ai [Net Intangible Assets - CMCO](https://fiscal.ai/company/NasdaqGS-CMCO/metrics/balance-sheet/net-intangible-assets).*

**Weighted-Average Amortization Period:**

Based on the company's disclosures, intangible assets acquired through acquisitions are **"amortized on average over 16 years"** [Q1 FY25 10-Q](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1005229/000100522924000284/cmco-20240930.htm). This provides the basis for calculating a weighted-average amortization rate.

**Calculation of Weighted-Average Amortization Rate:**

Using the fiscal 2025 figures:
- **Annual Amortization Expense:** $29.9 million
- **Net Intangible Assets (estimated at fiscal 2025 year-end):** ~$356.6 million (midpoint of the trend)
- **Estimated Gross Intangible Assets:** Based on historical accumulated amortization rates and the average life of 16 years, the gross carrying amount is approximately $500-550 million
- **Weighted-Average Amortization Rate (based on net carrying value):** ~8.4% ($29.9M / $356.6M)
- **Weighted-Average Amortization Rate (based on estimated gross carrying value):** ~5.5-6.0% ($29.9M / $500-550M)

The difference between these rates reflects that a portion of intangible assets have been fully amortized, leaving only the remaining balance of still-amortizing assets on the books.

### 4.2 Analysis of Acquisition Strategy Durability

The intangible asset amortization profile reveals several important insights about Columbus McKinnon's acquisition strategy:

**1. Heavy Reliance on Amortizable Intangibles:** The company's balance sheet carries a significant intangible asset base relative to its tangible net assets. The $356.6 million in net intangibles represents a substantial portion of the company's $882.1 million in shareholders' equity [Fiscal 2025 Annual Report](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2024/ar/CMCO-2025-Annual-Report-FINAL-with-10-K.pdf). This indicates acquisitions have been a primary driver of growth and value creation.

**2. Customer Relationships as a Key Value Driver:** The montratec acquisition (May 2023 for $115.7 million) included identifiable intangible assets of customer relationships valued at **$33.5 million**, a tradename valued at **$2.9 million**, and technology—along with $66.6 million in goodwill [Q1 FY25 10-Q](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1005229/000100522924000284/cmco-20240930.htm). The heavy weighting toward customer relationships and technology aligns with the company's strategy of expanding its precision conveyance and automation capabilities.

**3. Implications of a 16-Year Average Life:** The 16-year weighted-average amortization period is notably long for industrial technology acquisitions. This suggests several things:
- **Durable Customer Relationships:** The company believes its acquired customer relationships have long-lasting value, likely due to high switching costs, proprietary product integration, and recurring service/maintenance revenue.
- **Technology with Long Commercial Lifespans:** The technology intangible assets (from acquisitions like Dorner, Garvey, and montratec) are expected to remain commercially relevant for extended periods, likely reflecting the mature nature of industrial automation technology.
- **Conservative Amortization Approach:** A 16-year life is conservative compared to shorter periods (5-7 years) used by many technology companies, reducing annual earnings drag from amortization.

**4. Strategic Value of Acquisitions:**

The acquisition strategy has focused on three key areas:
- **Precision Conveyance:** Dorner (acquired April 2021), Garvey (acquired December 2021), and montratec (acquired May 2023) have added ~$5 billion to the company's total addressable market [Fiscal 2025 Annual Report](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2024/ar/CMCO-2025-Annual-Report-FINAL-with-10-K.pdf).
- **Intelligent Motion Solutions:** Moving beyond traditional hoists and rigging into digital power control systems and automation.
- **Geographic Expansion:** The montratec acquisition expanded European presence, while the pending Kito Crosby acquisition ($2.7 billion) will dramatically increase global scale.

**5. The Kito Crosby Acquisition and Intangible Asset Implications:**

The pending acquisition of Kito Crosby (closed February 3, 2026, after fiscal 2025 year-end) will fundamentally transform the company's intangible asset profile. The preliminary purchase price allocation includes:
- Identifiable intangible assets valued at roughly **$1.38 billion** (customer relationships ~$1.09 billion, trade names ~$287 million)
- Goodwill of approximately **$870-927 million**
- Pro forma amortization of intangibles estimated at **$106 million annually** (more than 3x the current $30 million level)

*Data from the Form 8-K/A filed June 17, 2025 [Form 8-K/A - Kito Crosby](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1005229/000114036125022831/ef20050551_8k.htm).*

**6. Assessment of Durability:**

The acquisition strategy demonstrates several characteristics of durability:

**Positive Indicators:**
- The 16-year weighted-average life suggests management believes acquired assets have long-term value
- The focus on customer relationships (high switching costs) and proprietary technology provides recurring revenue streams
- The company's record orders of $1.0 billion in fiscal 2025 (4% constant currency growth) and 15% backlog growth indicate that acquired products are gaining market acceptance
- The company's adjusted EBITDA margin of 15.6% in fiscal 2025 suggests acquired businesses are being integrated profitably, despite integration costs

**Risk Factors:**
- The significant increase in amortization from ~$30 million to ~$106 million post-Kito Crosby will create a substantial non-cash earnings drag, potentially masking underlying operational performance
- The company reported a net loss of $5.1 million in fiscal 2025, and pro forma combined net loss for the twelve months ended March 31, 2025 was $311 million (driven by $106 million amortization and $216 million debt interest costs), raising questions about near-term earnings quality
- Impairment risk exists if anticipated synergies from Kito Crosby do not materialize, though the company expects $70 million in annual run-rate net cost synergies [Fiscal 2025 Annual Report](https://s24.q4cdn.com/875787111/files/doc_financials/2024/ar/CMCO-2025-Annual-Report-FINAL-with-10-K.pdf)
- The company's restructuring activities (including the relocation of North American linear motion operations to Monterrey, Mexico, with expected asset impairments of $4-5 million) indicate ongoing challenges in fully realizing acquisition value [Q1 FY25 10-Q](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1005229/000100522924000284/cmco-20240930.htm)

## 5. Synthesis: Overall Capital Allocation Strategy Assessment

### 5.1 Key Themes

Columbus McKinnon's capital allocation strategy reveals several coherent themes:

**1. Transformative Growth Through Acquisitions:** The company has pursued a deliberate strategy of acquiring complementary businesses in precision conveyance, automation, and intelligent motion. The pending Kito Crosby acquisition represents a step-change in scale, with the combined entity expected to have significantly greater market presence.

**2. Disciplined Balance Sheet Management:** The company has consistently prioritized deleveraging between acquisitions, using operating cash flow as the primary tool. The net debt leverage ratio improved from 33.0% of total capitalization in fiscal 2022 to 28.9% in fiscal 2023, with targets to reach 2.0x by fiscal 2025 (though the Kito Crosby acquisition will reset this metric higher in the short term).

**3. Legacy Liability Reduction:** The comprehensive pension risk transfer strategy demonstrates a commitment to simplifying the balance sheet and eliminating legacy liabilities that create earnings volatility.

**4. Consistent Shareholder Returns:** The company has maintained a regular quarterly dividend ($0.28 per share, ~1.87% yield) throughout its transformation, signaling confidence in cash flow generation even during periods of heavy investment and deleveraging.

### 5.2 Risk Assessment

**Primary Risks:**
- **Integration Risk:** The Kito Crosby acquisition (valued at 2.8x fiscal 2025 revenue of $963 million) will require significant integration efforts, particularly in combining two large organizations with different cultures and systems
- **Leverage Increase:** Post-acquisition leverage will increase substantially, reducing financial flexibility during a period of tariff uncertainty and potential economic softening
- **Employee Retention:** The concentration of underwater stock options creates retention risk precisely when the company needs stability during integration
- **Earnings Quality:** The significant increase in intangible amortization ($106 million pro forma) and interest expense ($216 million pro forma) will create a large gap between GAAP earnings and adjusted earnings, potentially confusing investors

**Mitigating Factors:**
- The company has a proven track record of successful integration from prior acquisitions (Dorner, Garvey, montratec)
- Expected $70 million in annual cost synergies from Kito Crosby within a specified timeframe
- Strong operational cash flow generation ($45.6 million in fiscal 2025 despite a net loss)
- Record orders ($1.0 billion) and 15% backlog growth indicate healthy demand fundamentals

### 5.3 Outlook

Columbus McKinnon enters fiscal 2026 with a transformed business portfolio, a pending transformative acquisition, and a clear strategic direction. The company's capital allocation historically has balanced growth investment with balance sheet discipline. However, the Kito Crosby acquisition represents a step-function increase in scale, leverage, and complexity. The company's ability to successfully integrate the acquisition, realize the projected $70 million in synergies, and navigate tariff-related headwinds will be critical to delivering value for shareholders.
