# UCC Article 2 Legal Analysis: Custom Widgets Color Deviation Dispute

## Timeline-Anchored Legal Analysis with Enhanced Factual Foundation

### Executive Summary

This comprehensive analysis examines a commercial dispute under UCC Article 2 involving 1,000 custom widgets delivered on **March 15, 2024** in navy blue instead of the contractually specified charcoal gray. The widgets meet all functional specifications (load capacity, durability). The buyer installed and operated all widgets for eight weeks (March 15 to May 10), painted 600 of the 1,000 widgets black during that period, sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color, and only notified the vendor of the color issue on **May 10, 2024** — citing aesthetic concerns for showroom display. The contract contains a **10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause**. Industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. The vendor offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time; the buyer demanded a 40% refund while keeping all widgets. The buyer's architect later confirmed the navy created contrast issues.

This analysis anchors each legal conclusion to specific timeline dates and factual events, addressing: (1) whether the 8-week delay constitutes a reasonable time for rejection under UCC § 2-602(1); (2) whether the buyer's conduct constitutes acceptance under UCC § 2-606; (3) whether the color deviation constitutes substantial impairment under UCC § 2-608; (4) whether the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable under UCC § 2-508(2); (5) whether the buyer's conduct raises good faith concerns under UCC § 1-304; (6) what damages are available under UCC § 2-714 for a color-only deviation; and (7) whether the buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping the widgets is supported under UCC damage principles.

## Part I: Timeline and Factual Anchors

The following timeline is central to the legal analysis:

| Date/Period | Event | Legal Significance |
|-------------|-------|-------------------|
| March 15, 2024 | Delivery of 1,000 navy blue widgets | Trigger for reasonable time to reject (§ 2-602); start of reasonable opportunity to inspect (§ 2-606); date buyer "should have discovered" non-conformity (§ 2-608) |
| March 15–25, 2024 | **10-day contractual inspection window** | Contractual period for inspection and rejection; presumptively reasonable for visually apparent defects |
| March 15–May 10, 2024 | **8-week period** | Buyer installed and operated all widgets; painted 600 black; sent praise emails |

| May 10, 2024+ | Buyer demands 40% refund while keeping widgets | Unilateral remedy demand; rejection of cure |

The **10-day contractual inspection clause** (March 15–25) is critical: it represents the parties' agreed-upon reasonable time for inspection of a visually apparent defect. The buyer exceeded this period by nearly six times before raising any objection.

## Part II: Whether 8 Weeks is a Reasonable Time to Reject Under UCC § 2-602(1)

### A. The Statutory Standard

UCC § 2-602(1) provides: "Rejection of goods must be within a reasonable time after their delivery or tender. It is ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller." [1][2][3] The buyer's failure to comply with this requirement results in acceptance by default. As the Hillhouse article explains, if the buyer fails to comply with § 2-602 procedures, the buyer "will be deemed to have accepted the goods." [4]

### B. The 10-Day Contractual Inspection Clause: A Presumptive Benchmark

UCC § 1-204(1) provides that "any time which is not manifestly unreasonable may be fixed by agreement." The 10-day inspection clause is presumptively enforceable and constitutes the parties' agreed-upon standard for what constitutes a reasonable inspection period. The IRMI article specifically notes: "Timing of rejection must be reasonable and timely notification to the seller is required for rejection to be effective, and contract terms may further define those timeframes." [5]

**Application to the timeline:** The 10-day window (March 15–25) was the contractual period for inspection. The buyer did not notify the seller of any issue until May 10 — **56 days later**. This is nearly six times the contractual period. For a visually apparent defect like color, which requires no testing or complex inspection, a 10-day period is eminently reasonable, and the 56-day delay is manifestly unreasonable.

### C. The Visually Apparent Nature of the Defect Accelerates the Timeline

Courts consistently hold that patent defects — those immediately discoverable upon visual inspection — require prompt rejection. As the Fordham Law Review article explains: "Since a buyer will necessarily require a substantially greater amount of time to discover defects which are latent, he should accordingly be given a respectively longer amount of time in which to revoke." [6] By necessary implication, patent defects require a **shorter** time for rejection.

**Application to the timeline:** The color deviation (navy blue vs. charcoal gray) was visually apparent the moment the widgets were delivered on March 15. No testing, measuring, or complex inspection was required. The buyer's later claim that the architect "confirmed the navy created contrast issues" is irrelevant to the timeliness inquiry because the color difference was apparent from delivery — the architect's confirmation merely confirms what was already observable. The UCC does not require architectural validation to discover a color difference.

### D. Case Law Support

**Linscott v. Smith** (Kansas Court of Appeals): The court held that by moving into and continuously using a mobile home despite defects (including leaking issues and malfunctioning components), the plaintiffs accepted the goods under K.S.A. 84-2-606. The court stated: "Acceptance of goods occurs when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, signifies that the goods are conforming or retains them despite nonconformity." [7]

**La Villa Fair v. Lewis Carpet Mills, Inc., 219 Kan. 395, 548 P.2d 825:** The Kansas Comment to § 84-2-606 cites this case for the proposition that a "nine-month delay in inspecting carpet did not constitute acceptance under facts and circumstances." [8] This case is important because it demonstrates that delay alone is not dispositive — the facts and circumstances control. However, the key distinguishing fact here is that the buyer did more than merely delay: the buyer **used the goods for 8 weeks, painted 600 of them, and sent praise emails**.

**Michael M. Berlin & Co. v. T. Whiting Mfg., Inc.:** A six-week delay in inspecting steel that could have been measured with a simple micrometer was held unreasonable. [9] The 8-week delay here, for a defect visible to the naked eye, is even more clearly unreasonable.

**Max Bauer Meat Packer, Inc. v. United States:** A three-hour delay was found unreasonable for defects that should have been immediately apparent. [10] While the facts differ, the principle is consistent: patent defects demand prompt rejection.

### E. Conclusion on § 2-602(1)

The 8-week delay from March 15 to May 10 is manifestly unreasonable for a visually apparent color defect. The 10-day contractual inspection clause reinforces this conclusion. The buyer's rejection on May 10 is ineffective under § 2-602(1). The buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods through failure to make an effective rejection.

## Part III: Whether the Buyer's Conduct Constitutes Acceptance Under UCC § 2-606

### A. The Three Independent Grounds for Acceptance

UCC § 2-606 defines acceptance of goods by a buyer as occurring in three scenarios [11][12][13]:

**(a)** When the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods, signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming or that he will take or retain them in spite of their non-conformity;

**(b)** When the buyer fails to make an effective rejection (but such acceptance does not occur until the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect them); or

**(c)** When the buyer does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership; but if such act is wrongful as against the seller, it is an acceptance only if ratified by him.

The Yassi Law guide explains: "Acceptance often happens without a dramatic moment. Common real world examples include installing equipment, reselling inventory, or paying the invoice without reservation." [14] "Once you accept, rejection is usually off the table, deadlines tighten, and you may be forced into a narrower set of remedies."

### B. Prong (a): Emails Praising Performance as Signification of Conformity

**Key facts:** During the 8-week period (March 15–May 10), the buyer sent emails praising widget performance with **no mention of color**. The Yassi Law guide notes: "A strong rejection notice usually includes: Date of delivery and delivery location; Purchase order or invoice number; What is nonconforming; Your demand; A statement that you are preserving rights." [14] The buyer's emails did none of this. Instead, they affirmatively communicated satisfaction with the goods' performance.

**Application to timeline:** The emails were sent after delivery (March 15) and before any notice of defect (May 10). During this period, the buyer had a reasonable opportunity to inspect (the 10-day contractual window had long passed by the time of the emails). The emails signify that the goods were conforming under § 2-606(1)(a). This constitutes an express acceptance and waiver of any objections.

### C. Prong (b): Failure to Make Effective Rejection

As established in Part II, the buyer failed to make an effective rejection within a reasonable time under § 2-602(1). Under § 2-606(1)(b), this failure constitutes acceptance. The Kansas Comment to § 84-2-606 explains: "The buyer has a reasonable time in which to reject the goods before it will be deemed to have accepted them." [8] The Nebraska Supreme Court in **Fabricators, Inc. v. Farmers Elevator, Inc.** held: "Acceptance occurs when the buyer fails to make an effective rejection after reasonable inspection." [15]

**Application to timeline:** The 8-week delay (March 15 to May 10) far exceeds the 10-day contractual inspection period and any reasonable time for rejection of a patent defect. The buyer's failure to reject during this period constitutes acceptance under § 2-606(1)(b).

### D. Prong (c): Painting 600 Widgets as an Act Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership

**Key facts:** During the 8-week period, the buyer painted **600 of the 1,000 widgets black**. This is the most powerful evidence of acceptance by conduct.

**Johnson v. Holdrege Coop. Equity Exchange, 206 Neb. 568, 293 N.W.2d 863 (1980):** The Nebraska Supreme Court held that "the commingling by a buyer of one farmer's grain with that of other farmers is an act inconsistent with the buyer's ownership and may constitute acceptance" under § 2-606(1)(c). [15][16] Painting goods is a more deliberate and permanent act of modification than commingling grain. The Supply Chain Survival Series (Quarles & Brady) explains: "A buyer's acceptance of goods generally occurs after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods if the buyer signifies conformity or keeps the goods despite defects." [17]

**Application to timeline:** By painting 600 widgets black during the period **before** any communication about the color issue, the buyer:
1. Exercised dominion and control over the goods inconsistent with the seller's ownership
2. Materially altered the goods without the seller's consent
3. Cured the alleged defect themselves without notifying the seller
4. Acted in a manner that precludes returning the goods to the seller in their original condition

This conduct alone satisfies § 2-606(1)(c) and constitutes acceptance of all 1,000 widgets (acceptance of part of a commercial unit is acceptance of that entire unit under § 2-606(2)).

### E. Conclusion on § 2-606

The buyer accepted the goods under **all three independent grounds** in § 2-606:
1. **§ 2-606(1)(a):** Signified conformity through praise emails
2. **§ 2-606(1)(b):** Failed to make effective rejection within reasonable time
3. **§ 2-606(1)(c):** Painted 600 widgets, an act inconsistent with seller's ownership

Once goods are accepted, the buyer's remedies are limited to damages for breach of warranty (§ 2-714) and possible revocation under § 2-608 — rejection is no longer available.

## Part IV: Whether the Color Deviation Constitutes Substantial Impairment Under UCC § 2-608

### A. The Substantial Impairment Standard

UCC § 2-608(1) permits revocation of acceptance only where the non-conformity "substantially impairs its value to him." [11][18][19][20] This is a significantly higher standard than the perfect tender rule's "any respect" test under § 2-601. Official Comment 2 explains that "the test is not what the seller had reason to know at the time of contracting; the question is whether the nonconformity is such as will in fact cause a substantial impairment of value to the buyer." [11]

### B. The Subjective-Objective Test

**Jorgensen v. Pressnall, 274 Or. 285, 545 P.2d 1382 (1976)** — The Oregon Supreme Court established the leading framework: "the buyer is permitted to revoke acceptance if the nonconformity substantially impairs the value to the buyer and promised cures or assurances fail." [19][20][21] The court adopted a **subjective-objective test**:

- **Subjective prong:** The buyer's particular needs and circumstances
- **Objective prong:** Whether the nonconformity in fact substantially impaired the value to that buyer (not merely the buyer's assertion)

The Kansas Comment to § 84-2-608 echoes this standard: "A buyer must show the nonconformity of the goods, the needs and circumstances of the buyer (subjectively), and that the nonconformity in fact (objectively) impaired the value of the goods to the buyer." [22]

### C. Application to Color Deviation: Weighing Factors Against Substantial Impairment

**1. Functional Performance is Unaffected**

**Durfee v. Rod Baxter Imports, Inc., 262 N.W.2d 349 (Minn. 1977):** The Minnesota Supreme Court "characterized minor defects as those without significant effect on product operation; only defects materially interfering with operation constitute substantial impairment." [23][24] The widgets meet all functional specifications — load capacity and durability. The color deviation has no effect on product operation. Under Durfee, this is a "minor" defect.

**2. Industry Practice Treats Minor Color Variations as Acceptable**

UCC § 1-303(c) provides that a "usage of trade" is "any practice or method of dealing having such regularity of observance in a place, vocation, or trade as to justify an expectation that it will be observed with respect to the transaction." [25] If industry practice accepts minor color variations for functional components, this is admissible to show that the deviation does not substantially impair value. The goods that "pass without objection in the trade" under their contract description are likely merchantable under UCC § 2-314, and a color variation that is standard in the trade cannot constitute substantial impairment.

**3. The Buyer's Own Conduct Belies the Claim of Substantial Impairment**

The buyer's conduct during the 8-week period is powerful evidence against substantial impairment:

- **Painted 600 widgets black:** The buyer cured the alleged defect themselves at relatively low cost. This demonstrates that the color issue was remediable, not a fundamental impairment. If the color had substantially impaired the value, the buyer would not have been able to cure it so simply.
- **Praised performance via email:** The buyer affirmatively communicated satisfaction with the widgets' performance. This is inconsistent with a claim that the color rendered the widgets substantially less valuable.
- **Used all widgets for 8 weeks:** The buyer derived full operational value from all 1,000 widgets for 8 weeks. This is inconsistent with a claim of substantial impairment.

**4. The Aesthetic Nature of the Defect**

**Freeman Oldsmobile Mazda Co. v. Pinson, 580 S.W.2d 112 (Tex. Civ. App. 1979):** The court refused to allow revocation where the cost of repairing small dents and imperfect paint on a new car would cost less than $100. [26] Minor aesthetic defects generally do not meet the substantial impairment standard.

**5. The "Shaken Faith" Doctrine**

**Zabriskie Chevrolet, Inc. v. Smith, 99 N.J. Super. 441, 240 A.2d 195 (1968):** The "shaken faith" doctrine allows revocation where a defect destroys the buyer's confidence in the goods. [27][28][29] However, this doctrine typically applies to safety-related defects or cases where the goods are so defective that the buyer cannot trust them. Here, the goods perform exactly as expected functionally, and there is no basis for "shaken faith."

### D. Arguments the Buyer Might Make Under the Subjective Prong

**Jorgensen v. Pressnall** adopted a **subjective** component: "the relatively small amount of money needed to repair the defect is not necessarily relevant because the impairment of the value to the purchaser may be substantial even though the cost of curing the defect may be relatively small." [19][20] Under this approach:

- **The buyer's showroom display needs:** The buyer cited "aesthetic concerns for showroom display." The architect later "confirmed the navy created contrast issues." Under the subjective prong, the buyer's particular needs (aesthetic matching for showroom display) are relevant.
- **The color reference number in the purchase order:** The contract specified a color reference number, indicating that color was a term of the contract.

However, even under Jorgensen, the **objective prong** requires that the nonconformity **in fact** substantially impaired the value. The buyer's own conduct (painting 600 widgets, praising performance, using all widgets for 8 weeks) is evidence that the value was **not** substantially impaired. The buyer cured the issue at relatively low cost, which is a strong indicator that the objective impairment was minimal.

### E. Procedural Bars to Revocation

**1. Timeliness Under § 2-608(2)**

Section 2-608(2) requires that "Revocation of acceptance must occur within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it." [11][18] The Fordham Law Review article explains: "The reasonable period commences from the point when the buyer discovers, or should have discovered, the non-conformity." [6]

**Application to timeline:** The color deviation was **visually apparent** upon delivery on March 15. The buyer "discovered or should have discovered" the ground for revocation on March 15. Waiting until **May 10** — 56 days — is a lengthy delay for a patent, visual defect. The Fordham article states: "There is a direct correlation between the difficulty of discovery of the non-conformity in the goods and the determination of a reasonable time in which to revoke acceptance." [6] For patent defects, the reasonable time is short.

**Factors that could extend the time:** The Fordham article notes that courts will extend the reasonable time when the seller gave assurances or the parties were engaged in repair negotiations. [6] Here, there were no such assurances or negotiations. The buyer simply used the goods for 8 weeks without any communication about the color issue.

**2. Substantial Change in Condition Under § 2-608(2)**

Section 2-608(2) also requires revocation to occur "before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects." [11][18] **Painting 600 widgets black** constitutes a substantial, deliberate alteration of the goods by the buyer — not caused by any defect. This alone bars revocation.

The Hruby article on postrevocation use explains: "Section 2-608(3) affords rejecting and revoking buyers similar treatment" and "the Code drafters apparently intended that postrevocation use would invalidate revocation of acceptance." [30] If use alone can invalidate revocation, then painting — a far more intrusive act — certainly does.

**3. Neither § 2-608(1)(a) nor (b) Is Satisfied**

For revocation under § 2-608(1), the buyer must have accepted either:
- **(a)** "on the reasonable assumption that its non-conformity would be cured" — **Not applicable.** The buyer never asked for cure and never received assurances of cure.
- **(b)** "without discovery of such non-conformity if his acceptance was reasonably induced either by the difficulty of discovery before acceptance or by the seller's assurances" — **Not applicable.** The color was immediately apparent, and there were no seller assurances.

### F. Conclusion on § 2-608

The buyer cannot satisfy the substantial impairment requirement for a color-only deviation on functional components. Moreover, even if substantial impairment could be shown, two procedural bars apply: the 56-day delay is unreasonable for a patent defect, and painting 600 widgets constitutes a substantial change in condition. Revocation is not available.

## Part V: Whether the Seller Had Reasonable Grounds to Believe the Tender Would Be Acceptable Under UCC § 2-508(2)

### A. The Statutory Standard

UCC § 2-508(2) provides: "Where the buyer rejects a non-conforming tender which the seller had reasonable grounds to believe would be acceptable with or without money allowance the seller may if he seasonably notifies the buyer have a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender." [31][32][33]

### B. Wilson v. Scampoli: The Leading Case

**Wilson v. Scampoli, 228 A.2d 848 (D.C. Ct. App. 1967)** — This is the seminal case on § 2-508(2) and is directly analogous to the color deviation hypothetical. [33][34][35][36]

**Facts:** Nick Scampoli purchased a color television set. Upon delivery, the television "showed a reddish picture tint." The buyer refused to allow the seller to repair or replace it, demanding a new set or refund. The seller offered to remove the chassis for inspection and potential repair.

**Holding:** The D.C. Court of Appeals reversed the trial court's rescission order, holding that under § 2-508, "the seller must be afforded a reasonable opportunity to cure defects by repair or replacement before the buyer can rescind the contract." [33]

**Critical language:** "A retail dealer would certainly expect and have reasonable grounds to believe that merchandise like color television sets, new and delivered as crated at the factory, would be acceptable as delivered and that, if defective in some way, he would have the right to substitute a conforming tender." [33][35][36]

**Application to the hypothetical:** The "reddish picture tint" in Wilson is analogous to the navy blue vs. charcoal gray color deviation. Both are visual/color defects that do not affect the core functionality of the goods. The court held that the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the goods would be acceptable as delivered. Here, the seller delivered custom widgets as received from the manufacturer, meeting all functional specifications, with only a color deviation. Under Wilson, the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the goods would be acceptable.

### C. Factors Supporting the Seller's Reasonable Belief

**1. Emphasis on Functional Specifications**

The purchase order "emphasized functional specifications (load capacity, durability) with a color reference number but no rejection clause for shade variance." [37] This objective signals to a reasonable seller that the buyer's primary concern is functionality, not exact color matching. The color reference number is a specification, but the emphasis on function over form supports the seller's belief that goods meeting all functional specs would be acceptable.

**2. Industry Practice**

UCC § 1-303(c) provides that a "usage of trade" gives meaning to contract terms. [25] Industry practice treating minor color variations as acceptable for functional components establishes an objective basis for the seller's belief. The Schmitt & Frisch article explains: "The seller is not protected unless he had 'reasonable grounds to believe' that the tender would be acceptable. Such reasonable grounds can lie in prior course of dealing, course of performance or usage of trade as well as in the particular circumstances surrounding the making of the contract." [38]

**3. No Rejection Clause for Shade Variance**

The contract's absence of a specific clause rejecting shade variance — especially given the contract's emphasis on performance criteria — supports the seller's reasonable belief that minor color variations were within acceptable parameters.

**4. Reasonable Cure Offer**

The seller's offer of "immediate replacement with a two-week lead time" is commercially reasonable. In **Wilson v. Scampoli**, the court held that the buyer's refusal to allow cure was wrongful. [33][35] The Michigan Law Review analysis of § 2-508(2) notes that courts consider "buyer inconvenience, position changes, and the time needed to make repairs" in assessing cure reasonableness. [39]

### D. The T.W. Oil v. Con Edison Framework

**T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison Co. of New York, 57 N.Y.2d 574 (1982)** — The New York Court of Appeals held that "if seasonable notice be given, such a seller may offer to cure the defect within a reasonable period beyond the time when the contract was to be performed so long as it has acted in good faith and with a reasonable expectation that the original goods would be acceptable to the buyer." [40][41][42]

**Key language:** The court found that what turned out to be a 0.92% sulfur content (exceeding the 0.5% contractual specification) was "within the range of contemplation of reasonable acceptability" to Con Ed. [41][42] The court stressed that parties must act in good faith and fairly under the UCC and that the UCC lets sellers fix small delivery problems so buyers cannot unjustly escape contracts.

**Application to the hypothetical:** The navy blue vs. charcoal gray color deviation, like the sulfur content deviation in T.W. Oil, is "within the range of contemplation of reasonable acceptability" — especially given industry practice and the contract's emphasis on functional specs. The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is a seasonable and reasonable offer to cure.

### E. The Schmitt & Frisch Critique

The Schmitt & Frisch article, "The Perfect Tender Rule — An 'Acceptable' Interpretation," argues that Wilson v. Scampoli "broadened the right to cure beyond situations where the seller knows the goods are nonconforming, leading to inconsistent judicial applications contrary to the UCC's purpose." [38] They propose a narrower interpretation: "If section 2-508(2) were read to be limited to situations where the seller knew beforehand that the goods were not conforming, and had reasonable grounds to believe that the buyer would retain them in spite of their nonconformity, the 'perfect tender rule' as embodied in section 2-601 would remain strong and its exceptions would be few." [38]

**Application under this critique:** Even under this narrower interpretation, the seller could argue it knew the goods might have minor color variations consistent with industry practice and reasonably believed the buyer (whose purchase order emphasized function) would retain them. The buyer's subsequent conduct (using, praising, and painting the goods) confirms this reasonable belief.

### F. Interaction: Cure vs. Acceptance by Conduct

A critical analytical point: § 2-508 cure rights are triggered by **rejection** of goods. If the buyer has already **accepted** the goods under § 2-606, the cure provisions of § 2-508 are not directly triggered. However, as a practical matter:

1. If the buyer attempts to reject (which is precluded by acceptance), the seller would have cure rights under § 2-508(2).
2. If the buyer revokes acceptance (which is unlikely to succeed), most courts hold the seller has no right to cure. The Kansas Comment to 84-2-508 states: "A seller has no right to cure following revocation of acceptance." [32]
3. Since the buyer has accepted by conduct and cannot effectively reject or revoke, the cure question becomes moot as to the buyer's rejection rights, but relevant to the overall dispute resolution.

### G. Conclusion on § 2-508(2)

The seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable under § 2-508(2) based on: (1) the contract's emphasis on functional specifications; (2) industry practice accepting minor color variations; (3) the absence of a rejection clause for shade variance; and (4) the Wilson v. Scampoli precedent on visual/color deviations. The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is a seasonable and reasonable cure offer.

## Part VI: Whether the Buyer's Conduct Raises Good Faith Concerns Under UCC § 1-304

### A. The Statutory Standard

UCC § 1-304 provides: "Every contract or duty within the Uniform Commercial Code imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement." [43][44] UCC § 1-201(20) defines "good faith" as "honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing." For merchants, this means "honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing in the trade." [44][45] Parties cannot contract out of this duty.

### B. The "Excluder Model" — What Constitutes Bad Faith

The Connecticut Law Review Note, "Good Faith Rejection of Goods in a Falling Market," explains: "All aspects of the performance of a contract must be performed in 'good faith.' Since the rejection of goods is an aspect of performance, rejection — even if otherwise rightful — must be performed in good faith." [46]

**Neumiller Farms, Inc. v. Cornett, 368 So. 2d 272 (Ala. 1979)** — The Alabama Supreme Court held that a merchant buyer's "satisfaction" rejection of potatoes lacked good faith. [47][48][49]

**Key facts:** Neumiller Farms contracted with Cornett to supply chipping potatoes at $4.25 per hundredweight. When the market price fell to $2.00 per hundredweight, Cornett rejected nine loads claiming they were unsuitable for chipping, even though expert testing confirmed their suitability.

**Holding:** The court held that "the rejection appeared to be influenced by the decline in market price rather than the quality of the potatoes" and constituted a breach of contract for lack of good faith. [47][48] The court stated: "A buyer's rejection must be exercised in good faith and in a commercially reasonable manner." "Even if the contract contemplated that the processor had to be 'satisfied,' that kind of satisfaction term invokes an objective, reasonable-merchant standard, not a purely subjective whim." [49]

**Application to the hypothetical:** While the facts here do not involve a falling market, the principle applies: a buyer's conduct that is motivated by opportunism — seeking to obtain a windfall rather than genuine relief from a non-conformity — violates the obligation of good faith.

### C. Specific Indicia of Bad Faith in the Buyer's Conduct

**1. Delay in Raising the Defect**

The buyer waited **8 weeks** (56 days) after delivery to raise a **visually apparent** defect. During this entire period, the buyer said nothing about the color issue. **Arch Ins. Co. v. Centerplan Constr. Co.** held that "acting in self-interest under contractual rights is not, by itself, a breach of good faith; rather, a violation arises only when improper motives drive the conduct." [50] Here, the pattern of silence followed by a demand for a 40% refund suggests the buyer was not genuinely dissatisfied but is seeking a windfall.

**2. Contradictory Conduct**

The buyer's conduct is inherently contradictory:
- **Praised performance via email** (suggesting satisfaction)
- **Painted 600 widgets black** (curing the color issue themselves)
- **Used all widgets for 8 weeks** (deriving full operational value)
- **Then demanded a 40% refund** while keeping all widgets

This pattern of conduct — praising, using, modifying, and then demanding a massive refund — "unfairly frustrates the agreed common purpose and disappoints the reasonable expectations of the other party" under the standard articulated in **Shibata v. Lim.** [51]

**3. Rejection of Reasonable Cure Offer**

The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time was commercially reasonable. The buyer's refusal of this offer while demanding a 40% refund to keep goods that the buyer praised and used for 8 weeks is strong evidence of bad faith. Under **Neumiller Farms**, a buyer cannot reject a reasonable cure offer and then seek a windfall.

**4. Disproportionate Demand**

The buyer's demand for a **40% refund** for a purely cosmetic color deviation on goods that meet all functional specifications is grossly disproportionate to any actual injury. As discussed in Part VII, the proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation is likely minimal. A demand for 40% appears opportunistic.

### D. Tongish v. Thomas and Market Stability

**Tongish v. Thomas, 251 Kan. 728, 840 P.2d 471 (1992)** — The Kansas Supreme Court held that when a breach of contract governed by the UCC is made in bad faith, damages should be structured to promote market efficiency and discourage breaches that exploit contractual purity for unfair advantage. [52][53][54]

The court reasoned: "A core tenet of contract law is that a breaching party must not be punished for a breach, but rather, the nonbreaching party must simply be made whole. However, where the breach is in bad faith, the Uniform Commercial Code provides for damages that increase market stability." [53]

**Application:** While Tongish involved a seller's bad faith, the principle applies symmetrically. A buyer who exploits a trivial non-conformity to extract a disproportionate refund undermines contractual stability and the reasonable expectations of commercial parties.

### E. Conclusion on § 1-304

The buyer's conduct — using and praising the goods for 8 weeks, painting 600 of them, failing to communicate any dissatisfaction, and then demanding a 40% refund while rejecting a reasonable cure offer — raises serious good faith concerns. The pattern strongly suggests opportunistic behavior rather than genuine dissatisfaction with the goods. This conduct violates the obligation of good faith under § 1-304.

## Part VII: Damages Available Under UCC § 2-714 for a Color-Only Deviation

### A. The Statutory Standard

UCC § 2-714 provides the measure of damages when the buyer has accepted non-conforming goods [55][56][57][58][59]:

**(1)** Where the buyer has accepted goods and given notification (subsection (3) of Section 2-607) he may recover as damages for any non-conformity of tender the loss resulting in the ordinary course of events from the seller's breach as determined in any manner which is reasonable.

**(2)** The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted, unless special circumstances show proximate damages of a different amount.

**(3)** In a proper case any incidental and consequential damages under the next section may also be recovered.

### B. Chatlos Systems: The Leading Case on § 2-714(2)

**Chatlos Systems, Inc. v. National Cash Register Corp., 670 F.2d 1304 (3d Cir. 1982)** — The Third Circuit held that "UCC warranty damages equal the value difference between goods as warranted and goods as delivered." [60][61][62]

**Key holdings:**
- "The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted."
- "The contract price may be relevant but is not controlling for determining fair market value under UCC damage provisions."
- The court upheld the "benefit of the bargain" rule: the buyer is entitled to the value the goods would have had if conforming, not the contract price.

**Application to the hypothetical:** For a color-only deviation where functionality is unaffected, the § 2-714(2) measure requires determining:
- Value of goods **as warranted**: charcoal gray widgets meeting all functional specs
- Value of goods **as accepted**: navy blue widgets meeting all functional specs
- Damages = the difference between these two values at the time and place of acceptance (March 15, 2024)

### C. Measuring Damages for a Color-Only Defect

**1. The Difference in Value Is Likely Minimal**

For functional components where industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable, the difference in market value between navy blue widgets and charcoal gray widgets is likely minimal or zero. White and Summers explain in their treatise that § 2-714(2) applies the "benefit of the bargain" rule, measuring the difference between the fair market value of the goods as warranted and the fair market value of the goods as accepted. [63] For custom widgets made to a specific buyer's specifications, where the color does not affect utility, the market value difference may be negligible.

**2. Cost of Repair as a Measure**

The Kansas Comment to § 84-2-714 (1996) explains: "When repair of the goods is possible, the best measure of damages for breach of warranty is the cost of repair. If the cost of repair exceeds the price of the goods, however, the purchase price should serve as a cap on damages." [59]

**Application:** The buyer already demonstrated that the color issue can be cured by painting. The buyer painted **600 widgets black** during the 8-week period. The cost of painting the remaining 400 widgets (or the cost the buyer incurred for painting 600) would be a reasonable measure of damages under the "cost of repair" approach.

**3. No Damages for the 600 Painted Widgets**

Since the buyer already painted 600 widgets black, those widgets now **match the contractual color**. The buyer has already cured the defect for those 600 widgets. The buyer cannot recover damages for a defect that has already been cured by the buyer's own action, unless the buyer can prove the cost of painting as incidental damages.

**4. The Buyer's Own Conduct as Evidence of Value**

**McCoolidge v. Oyvetsky, 292 Neb. 955 (2016):** The Nebraska Supreme Court affirmed the principle that damages under § 2-714 are limited to the proven difference in value. [56] The buyer's use of all widgets for 8 weeks, the praise emails, and the buyer's own painting of 600 widgets are all evidence that the value of the goods as accepted was very close to the value as warranted.

**5. Mitigation and Offset**

As held in Chatlos Systems (both opinions), "Buyers must act in good faith and mitigate damages reasonably after a breach." [60][61] The buyer's own conduct in painting 600 widgets demonstrates that mitigation was possible at relatively low cost. The buyer cannot claim substantial damages when the cure was simple and the buyer already achieved it.

**Additionally, the benefit obtained from use must be offset.** The buyer used all 1,000 widgets for 8 weeks and derived operational value from them. As the Sales Compendium explains: "Gains made by the injured party on other transactions after the breach are never to be deducted from the damages that are otherwise recoverable, unless such gains could not have been made, had there been no breach." [63] Here, the buyer's use of the goods conferred a benefit that must be offset against any damages.

### D. Section 2-717: Deduction of Damages from the Price

UCC § 2-717 provides: "The buyer on notifying the seller of his intention to do so may deduct all or any part of the damages resulting from any breach of the contract from any part of the price still due under the same contract." [64][65]

**Key limitations:**
- The deduction is for "damages resulting from any breach" — i.e., actual proven damages under § 2-714(2), not an arbitrary percentage
- The buyer must give notice of intention to deduct
- The deduction is limited to the price still due

**Application:** The buyer may deduct from remaining payments only the actual proven damages under § 2-714(2) — the difference in value (likely minimal or the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets). The buyer cannot unilaterally deduct 40%.

### E. Incidental and Consequential Damages Under § 2-714(3)

In a proper case, the buyer may recover incidental and consequential damages (e.g., costs of repainting, if not already recovered under § 2-714(2)). However:
- Consequential damages may be limited or excluded by the contract under § 2-719
- The buyer must have given proper notice under § 2-607(3)

### F. The Henning & Lawrence Article on § 2-607(3) Notice

Henning & Lawrence explain: "An aggrieved buyer that fails to give its seller timely notification of breach, or that gives a timely but insufficient notification, suffers serious and sometimes catastrophic consequences under Article 2 of the Uniform Commercial Code." [66] The timeliness requirement "creates an incentive for a buyer to exercise its inspection rights quickly." [66]

**Application:** The buyer's notice on May 10, 2024 — 56 days after delivery and 46 days after the 10-day inspection window expired — likely fails the timeliness requirement under § 2-607(3). However, Comment 4 to § 2-607(3) states that the purpose of the notification requirement is "to defeat commercial bad faith, not to deprive a good faith consumer of his remedy." [66] Given the buyer's conduct, a court might find the notice sufficient for a minimal damages claim but insufficient to support substantial damages.

### G. Conclusion on § 2-714 Damages

The proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation on functional components is:
- **§ 2-714(2) difference in value:** Likely minimal — perhaps the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets (the buyer already painted 600)
- **Offset for benefit of use:** The buyer used all widgets for 8 weeks, which must be credited to the seller
- **No windfall:** The buyer cannot recover the full purchase price or a 40% refund while keeping the goods
- **§ 2-717 deduction:** Limited to actual proven damages, not an arbitrary percentage

## Part VIII: Whether the Buyer's Demand for a 40% Refund While Keeping the Widgets Is Supported Under UCC Damage Principles

### A. The Fundamental Principle: Pay the Contract Price for Accepted Goods

**Midwest Hatchery & Poultry Farms, Inc. v. Doorenbos Poultry, Inc., 783 N.W.2d 56 (Iowa Ct. App. 2010):** The court affirmed that "A fundamental principle of contract law is that a party that accepts the delivery of nonconforming goods must pay the full contract price." [67] "If a buyer accepts goods, the buyer takes them 'in spite of their nonconformity' and must pay the contract rate." [67]

The court further held that acceptance of nonconforming goods creates an enforceable contract requiring payment at contract price regardless of nonconformity, but the buyer is entitled to compensation for losses — the buyer must pay the full price but can recover damages for the nonconformity. [67]

### B. The Buyer's Demand Is Disproportionate to Any Actual Injury

As established in Part VII, the proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation is the difference in value between navy blue widgets and charcoal gray widgets — likely minimal for functional components. The buyer's demand for **40% of the contract price** while keeping **all 1,000 widgets** is grossly disproportionate to any actual injury.

**Application of UCC § 1-305(a):** "The remedies provided by this code shall be liberally administered to the end that the aggrieved party may be put in as good a position as if the other party had fully performed." [68] This means the buyer is entitled to the **benefit of the bargain** — not a windfall. The buyer who keeps accepted goods can only recover the difference in value (plus incidental and consequential damages where appropriate). A 40% refund while keeping all widgets would put the buyer in a **better** position than if the contract had been performed — which is not the purpose of UCC remedies.

### C. The Buyer Cannot Reject or Revoke

Since the buyer has accepted the goods under § 2-606 and cannot effectively reject or revoke (Parts II, III, IV), the buyer's remedies are limited to:
- **§ 2-714 damages** (difference in value)
- **§ 2-717 deduction** (limited to actual proven damages)

The buyer cannot obtain a 40% refund via rejection or revocation.

### D. The Seller's Cure Offer Is More Reasonable

The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is commercially reasonable. Under **Zabriskie Chevrolet v. Smith** (discussed in Part IV), a cure must restore the goods to the contracted condition. [27][28] The seller's offer to replace all 1,000 widgets with charcoal gray widgets within two weeks would do exactly that. The buyer's refusal of this offer while demanding a 40% refund supports a finding of bad faith.

### E. The Buyer's Potential Remedies

If the buyer wishes to keep the widgets (as the buyer's demand for a 40% refund indicates), the buyer's proper remedies are:

1. **§ 2-714 damages:** The difference in value between navy and charcoal gray widgets (likely minimal, possibly the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets)

2. **§ 2-717 deduction:** Offset of actual proven damages from any remaining price due, with proper notice

3. **Incidental damages:** Costs of inspection, handling, and storage directly related to the breach

4. **Consequential damages:** Only if not excluded by the contract and if properly proven

### F. The Buyer's Proper Course of Action

The buyer should either:
1. **Accept the seller's cure offer** of replacement widgets with a two-week lead time; or
2. **Retain the widgets** and seek damages under § 2-714 for the actual difference in value (likely the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets), subject to providing proper notice under § 2-607(3) and § 2-717.

The buyer is not entitled to reject the goods, revoke acceptance, or unilaterally demand a 40% refund.

### G. Conclusion on the 40% Refund Demand

The buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping all widgets is not supported under UCC damage principles. It is disproportionate to any actual injury, inconsistent with the fundamental principle that accepted goods must be paid for at the contract rate, and appears opportunistic given the minimal nature of the color deviation.
