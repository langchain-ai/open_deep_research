# Comprehensive UCC Article 2 Legal Analysis: Custom Widgets Color Deviation Dispute

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines a commercial dispute under UCC Article 2 involving 1,000 custom widgets delivered on **March 15, 2024** in navy blue instead of the contractually specified charcoal gray. The purchase order emphasized functional performance criteria (load capacity, durability) with a color reference number but no rejection clause for shade variance. The buyer installed and operated all widgets for eight weeks (March 15 to May 10, 2024), painted 600 of them black during that period, sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color, and only notified the vendor of the color issue on **May 10, 2024** — citing aesthetic concerns for showroom display. The contract includes a standard **10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause**. Industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. The vendor offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time; the buyer demanded a 40% refund instead while keeping all widgets in use. The buyer's architect later confirmed the navy created contrast issues in the showroom design.

This analysis applies the element-by-element methodology explicitly required by UCC §2-606 (Acceptance), §2-608 (Revocation of Acceptance), §2-508 (Seller's Right to Cure), §2-714 (Damages for Accepted Goods), §2-602 (Rejection), and §1-304 (Good Faith). Each multi-pronged standard is broken down independently with factual application to specific dates and events from the timeline. Authoritative citations to the UCC, official commentaries, relevant case law, and secondary sources are provided throughout.

## Part II: UCC §2-602 — Rejection of Goods

### Statutory Framework

UCC §2-602(1) provides: "Rejection of goods must be within a reasonable time after their delivery or tender. It is ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller." [1][2][3][4] The buyer's failure to comply with this requirement results in acceptance by default under §2-606(1)(b). [5]

### Element (a): Reasonable Time — Was the May 10, 2024 Notification (8 Weeks After Delivery) Within a Reasonable Time?

**Legal Standard:** The determination of "reasonable time" depends on the nature of the goods, the parties' conduct, trade usage, and any contractual provisions specifying inspection periods. UCC §1-204(1) provides that "any time which is not manifestly unreasonable may be fixed by agreement." [6] Courts consistently hold that patent defects — those immediately discoverable upon visual inspection — require prompt rejection. [7]

**The 10-Day Contractual Inspection Clause:** The 10-day inspection clause (March 15–25) is presumptively enforceable and constitutes the parties' agreed-upon standard for what constitutes a reasonable inspection period. The IRMI article on UCC rights notes: "Timing of rejection must be reasonable and timely notification to the seller is required for rejection to be effective, and contract terms may further define those timeframes." [8] The buyer exceeded this period by nearly six times before raising any objection.

**Visually Apparent Nature of the Defect:** The color deviation (navy blue vs. charcoal gray) was visually apparent the moment the widgets were delivered on March 15. No testing, measuring, or complex inspection was required. The Fordham Law Review article on §2-608 explains: "Since a buyer will necessarily require a substantially greater amount of time to discover defects which are latent, he should accordingly be given a respectively longer amount of time in which to revoke." [7] By necessary implication, patent defects require a **shorter** time for rejection. The buyer's later claim that the architect "confirmed the navy created contrast issues" is irrelevant to the timeliness inquiry because the color difference was apparent from delivery.

**Case Law Supporting Unreasonableness:**

**Michael M. Berlin & Co. v. T. Whiting Mfg., Inc.:** A six-week delay in inspecting steel that could have been measured with a simple micrometer was held unreasonable. [9] The 8-week delay here, for a defect visible to the naked eye, is even more clearly unreasonable.

**Max Bauer Meat Packer, Inc. v. United States:** A three-hour delay was found unreasonable for defects that should have been immediately apparent. [10] While the facts differ, the principle is consistent: patent defects demand prompt rejection.

**La Villa Fair v. Lewis Carpet Mills, Inc., 219 Kan. 395, 548 P.2d 825 (1976):** The court held that a nine-month delay in inspecting carpet did **not** constitute acceptance under the facts and circumstances. [11][12] **Critical Distinction:** In La Villa Fair, the goods were **stored, not used**, due to a construction strike and financial issues beyond the buyer's control. The court found that "the carpet was not in conformity with the specifications of plaintiff's order, of which the defendant was seasonably notified by the plaintiff as provided by K.S.A. 84-2-602." [11][12] Here, the buyer **installed and operated all widgets for eight weeks**, **used them continuously**, and **painted 600 of them**. The goods were not stored — they were actively used, modified, and praised.

**Buyer's Conduct Weighing Against Reasonableness:**

1. **Operation of All Widgets for 8 Weeks:** The Yassi Law guide states: "Acceptance often happens without a dramatic moment: installing equipment and putting it into production, reselling inventory, paying invoice without reserving rights can be 'silent acceptance.'" [13] The buyer's installation and 8-week operation is precisely the type of conduct that constitutes "silent acceptance."

2. **Sending Praise Emails With No Mention of Color:** The buyer's own **contemporaneous communications praising performance while remaining silent on color** strongly indicate satisfaction and acceptance. The Yassi Law guide emphasizes that "documentation is not busywork; it is your proof. Keep contracts, delivery documents, inspection checklists, photos, communications, and repair logs." [13] The buyer's emails provide powerful evidence against the claim of a material defect.

3. **Painting 600 Widgets Black:** This is a **fundamental act of ownership**. The Nebraska Legislature §2-606 annotations include **Johnson v. Holdrege Coop. Equity Exchange, 206 Neb. 568, 293 N.W.2d 863 (1980)** which held that "the commingling by a buyer of one farmer's grain with that of other farmers is an act inconsistent with the buyer's ownership and may constitute acceptance under this section." [14] By analogy, painting 600 widgets alters their identity and commingles them with the buyer's own modifications.

**Conclusion on Element (a):** The 8-week delay (March 15 to May 10) is manifestly unreasonable for a visually apparent color defect. The 10-day contractual inspection clause reinforces this conclusion. The buyer's conduct (operation, painting, praise emails) is inconsistent with a claim of rejection.

### Element (b): Seasonable Notification — Did the Buyer Give Seasonable Notification?

**Legal Standard:** UCC §2-602(1) requires that rejection be "ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller." [1][2][3][4] The requirement of seasonable notification is separate from the reasonable time requirement, though they are closely related.

**Application to Facts:**

1. **Failure to Notify Within 10-Day Inspection Period:** The contract's inspection clause put the buyer on notice of the need for timely inspection and rejection. The buyer had a contractual right to inspect and reject within 10 days but failed to do so.

2. **No Communication About Color During 8-Week Period:** The buyer sent emails praising performance but never mentioned the color issue. The Yassi Law guide emphasizes that "a strong rejection notice usually includes: Date of delivery and delivery location; Purchase order or invoice number; What is nonconforming; Your demand; A statement that you are preserving rights." [13] The buyer's communications did none of this.

3. **Silence Constitutes Acceptance:** The UCC does not require the buyer to use specific words to accept goods — silence and inaction can constitute acceptance when the buyer fails to object within a reasonable time. [15]

**Conclusion on Element (b):** The buyer did not give seasonable notification. The failure to notify the seller of any color issue for 56 days, despite having the contractual right to inspect within 10 days, strongly suggests the buyer either did not consider the color variation material or had accepted the goods.

### Overall Conclusion on §2-602

The buyer's attempted rejection on May 10, 2024 is **ineffective** because: (a) the 8-week delay exceeded a reasonable time, especially given the 10-day inspection clause and the obvious nature of the color variation; (b) the buyer did not give seasonable notification; and (c) the buyer's conduct (operation, painting, praise emails) demonstrates acceptance rather than rejection. The buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods through failure to make an effective rejection.

## Part III: UCC §2-606 — Acceptance of Goods

### Statutory Framework

UCC §2-606 defines acceptance of goods by a buyer as occurring in three independent scenarios: [16][17][18]

**(a)** When the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods, signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming or that he will take or retain them in spite of their non-conformity;

**(b)** When the buyer fails to make an effective rejection (but such acceptance does not occur until the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect them); or

**(c)** When the buyer does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership; but if such act is wrongful as against the seller, it is an acceptance only if ratified by him.

Acceptance of a part of any commercial unit is acceptance of that entire unit under §2-606(2). [16][17][18]

### Prong (a): §2-606(1)(a) — Signifying Conformity

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-606(1)(a), acceptance occurs when the buyer, "after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming or that he will take or retain them in spite of their nonconformity." [16][17][18]

**Application to Facts:**

1. **Reasonable Opportunity to Inspect:** The 10-day inspection clause gave the buyer a contractual right to inspect and reject within 10 days. The buyer had a reasonable opportunity to inspect by March 25, 2024. The color variation was visually apparent.

2. **Emails Praising Performance:** During the 8-week period (March 15–May 10), the buyer sent emails praising widget performance with **no mention of color**. While the emails praise "performance" (functionality), they do not directly state "the goods are conforming" in terms of color. However, the **silence on color** — despite the obvious visual difference between navy blue and charcoal gray — is significant.

3. **The Kansas 1996 Comment on §2-606** explains: "Under paragraph (1)(b), acceptance occurs when the buyer fails to make an effective rejection of the goods... The buyer's silence is not a rejection and thus may constitute acceptance." [19] The comment further states: "Payment is only one circumstance indicating acceptance and it is not conclusive." [19]

4. **Course of Performance (§1-303(a)):** Under UCC §1-303(a), the buyer's eight-week period of operation and praise emails constitute a "course of performance" — a sequence of conduct where the buyer accepted the performance or acquiesced in it without objection. [20] "A course of performance is relevant in ascertaining meaning of the parties' agreement and may supplement or qualify the terms of the agreement." [20] Importantly, "A course of performance is relevant to show a waiver or modification of any term inconsistent with the course of performance." [20]

**Conclusion on Prong (a):** While the emails do not contain an explicit statement of "the goods are conforming," the combination of: (1) operation of all widgets for 8 weeks; (2) sending praise emails; (3) silence on the color issue despite the obvious nature of the defect; and (4) painting 600 widgets, likely constitutes a **communication of acceptance** by conduct under §2-606(1)(a). At minimum, it constitutes a **waiver of the right to reject** based on the color variation.

### Prong (b): §2-606(1)(b) — Failure to Make Effective Rejection

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-606(1)(b), acceptance occurs when the buyer "fails to make an effective rejection (subsection (1) of Section 2-602)" but such acceptance does not occur until the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect them. [16][17][18]

**Interaction Between §2-602 and §2-606(1)(b):** The New York UCC §2-606(1)(b) explicitly cross-references §2-602(1): acceptance occurs under §2-606(1)(b) when the buyer "fails to make an effective rejection (subsection (1) of Section 2-602)." [21] This means that if the buyer's rejection fails under §2-602 (because it was not within a reasonable time or not seasonably notified), the buyer has accepted the goods under §2-606(1)(b).

**The Kansas 1996 Comment** explains: "Under paragraph (1)(b), acceptance occurs when the buyer fails to make an effective rejection of the goods... The buyer has a reasonable time in which to reject the goods before it will be deemed to have accepted them." [19]

**Case Law Support:**

**Alliance Tractor & Implement Co. v. Lukens Tool & Die Co., 199 Neb. 489, 260 N.W.2d 193 (1977):** "Buyer of defective machine failed to make effective rejection, and by his actions accepted nonconforming goods." [14]

**Linscott v. Smith, 3 Kan. App. 2d 1, 587 P.2d 1271 (1978):** The Kansas Court of Appeals held that buyers who "moved into and resided in the mobile home with knowledge of defects" had **accepted** the goods through their conduct. [22][23] The court held: "Plaintiffs' conduct indicated their willingness to accept the mobile home as delivered; they failed to make an effective rejection; and their acts were inconsistent with defendant's ownership of the mobile home." [22][23] The court analyzed all three prongs of §2-606: "Acceptance of goods tendered in performance of a sale agreement occurs under any of the three circumstances described in K.S.A. 84-2-606." [22][23]

**Application to Facts:** As established under §2-602 above, the buyer's rejection on May 10, 2024 was ineffective because: (a) the 8-week delay exceeded a reasonable time; (b) the buyer did not give seasonable notification; and (c) the buyer's conduct is inconsistent with rejection. Because the rejection was ineffective under §2-602, **acceptance occurred by default** under §2-606(1)(b). The 10-day inspection clause further supports this conclusion — the buyer had a contractual right to inspect and reject within 10 days but failed to do so.

**Conclusion on Prong (b):** The buyer **failed to make an effective rejection** and therefore **accepted the goods by default** under §2-606(1)(b).

### Prong (c): §2-606(1)(c) — Acts Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-606(1)(c), acceptance occurs when the buyer "does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership; but if such act is wrongful as against the seller it is an acceptance only if ratified by him." [16][17][18] The Kansas 1996 Comment states: "Acceptance occurs when the buyer takes any action inconsistent with the seller's ownership of the goods." [19]

**Key Facts:** During the 8-week period, the buyer painted **600 of the 1,000 widgets black**. This is the most powerful evidence of acceptance by conduct.

**Case Law on Acts Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership:**

**Johnson v. Holdrege Coop. Equity Exchange, 206 Neb. 568, 293 N.W.2d 863 (1980):** The Nebraska Supreme Court held that "the commingling by a buyer of one farmer's grain with that of other farmers is an act inconsistent with the buyer's ownership and may constitute acceptance under this section." [14] Painting goods is a more deliberate and permanent act of modification than commingling grain.

**Linscott v. Smith, 3 Kan. App. 2d 1, 587 P.2d 1271 (1978):** The court held that moving into a mobile home despite defects constituted an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership. [22][23] The court further held that "The remedy of rejection is not available to a buyer after he has accepted goods tendered in performance of a sale agreement." [22][23] And crucially: "Because defendant had no right to cure nonconformance after plaintiffs' acceptance, the trial court erred in concluding defendant was entitled to judgment because defendant's compliance with the sale agreement was prevented by plaintiffs." [22][23]

**Ford v. Starr Fireworks, Inc., Wyoming Supreme Court (1994):** This case cited Linscott v. Smith for the proposition: "A buyer's rejection can be converted into an acceptance by acts which are inconsistent with the seller's ownership rights." [24]

**Application to Facts:** The buyer engaged in **multiple acts inconsistent with the seller's ownership**:

1. **Painting 600 Widgets Black:** This is the most significant act. Painting goods is a **fundamental alteration** that changes the nature, identity, and appearance of the goods. It is analogous to "commingling" grain (Johnson v. Holdrege) or moving into a home with defects (Linscott v. Smith). Once the buyer painted 600 widgets, the goods could not be returned to the seller in their original condition. This constitutes acceptance under §2-606(1)(c).

2. **Operating All Widgets for 8 Weeks:** The Quarles Law Firm article notes: "A buyer's acceptance of goods generally occurs when the buyer takes any actions inconsistent with the seller's ownership of the goods." [25] Using goods in the buyer's business for 8 weeks is an act of ownership.

3. **Installing the Goods:** Installation constitutes an act inconsistent with seller's ownership. The Yassi Law guide specifically identifies "installing equipment and putting it into production" as "silent acceptance." [13]

**Conclusion on Prong (c):** The buyer's acts — particularly **painting 600 widgets black** and **operating all widgets for 8 weeks** — are **acts inconsistent with the seller's ownership** that constitute acceptance under §2-606(1)(c). The painting alone is a definitive act of ownership that fundamentally alters the goods.

### Overall Conclusion on §2-606

The buyer **accepted** the goods under **all three independent grounds** in §2-606:

1. **§2-606(1)(a):** Signified conformity through praise emails and silence on color
2. **§2-606(1)(b):** Failed to make effective rejection within reasonable time
3. **§2-606(1)(c):** Painted 600 widgets — an act inconsistent with seller's ownership

Once goods are accepted, the buyer's remedies are limited to damages for breach of warranty (§2-714) and possible revocation under §2-608 — rejection is no longer available.

## Part IV: UCC §2-608 — Revocation of Acceptance

### Statutory Framework

UCC §2-608(1) permits revocation of acceptance only where the non-conformity "substantially impairs its value to him." [26][27][28] This is a significantly higher standard than the perfect tender rule's "any respect" test under §2-601. Official Comment 2 explains that "the test is not what the seller had reason to know at the time of contracting; the question is whether the nonconformity is such as will in fact cause a substantial impairment of value to the buyer." [26]

UCC §2-608 provides that a buyer may revoke acceptance of a lot or commercial unit whose non-conformity substantially impairs its value to him if he has accepted it: [26][27][28]
- **(a)** on the reasonable assumption that its non-conformity would be cured and it has not been seasonably cured; or
- **(b)** without discovery of such non-conformity if his acceptance was reasonably induced either by the difficulty of discovery before acceptance or by the seller's assurances.

UCC §2-608(2) requires: "Revocation of acceptance must occur within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it and before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects." [26][27][28]

### Element (a): Substantial Impairment — The Subjective-Objective Test

**Legal Standard:** The test for substantial impairment has both subjective and objective components. The Kansas commentary on §2-608 explains: "A buyer must show the nonconformity of the goods, the needs and circumstances of the buyer (subjectively), and that the nonconformity in fact (objectively) impaired the value of the goods to the buyer." [27]

**Jorgensen v. Pressnall, 274 Or. 285, 545 P.2d 1382 (Or. 1976)** — The Oregon Supreme Court established the leading framework. [29][30][31] The court adopted a **subjective-objective test**:

- **Subjective prong:** Whether the nonconformity substantially impairs the value **to the particular buyer**, considering the buyer's particular needs and circumstances
- **Objective prong:** Whether the nonconformity **in fact** substantially impaired the value to that buyer (not merely the buyer's assertion)

The court stated: "Because of the defects and defendant's failure to cure them, the value of the mobile home to plaintiffs as a residence was substantially impaired." [29] The court also noted that "the relatively small amount of money needed to repair the defect is not necessarily relevant because the impairment of the value to the purchaser may be substantial even though the cost of curing the defect may be relatively small." [29]

**Durfee v. Rod Baxter Imports, Inc., 262 N.W.2d 349 (Minn. 1977)** — The Minnesota Supreme Court drew an analogy between the concept of substantial impairment under section 2-608 and the principle of material breach under traditional contract law, adopting a common sense approach. [32][33][34] The court characterized minor defects as those without significant effect on product operation; only defects materially interfering with operation constitute substantial impairment. [32][33][34]

**Subjective Prong Analysis — Buyer's Particular Needs:**

The buyer claims the color variance substantially impaired value given their showroom display needs. The buyer's architect later confirmed the navy created contrast issues in the showroom design. Under Jorgensen v. Pressnall, the court focuses on the buyer's specific use and needs. Subjectively, the buyer's showroom display aesthetic needs may have been genuinely impaired.

**However, the buyer's own conduct weighs heavily against a finding of subjective substantial impairment:**

1. **Operated all widgets for eight weeks:** If the color was so substantially impairing that it justified revocation, the buyer would not have waited 8 weeks to raise the issue.

2. **Sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color:** This is directly inconsistent with a claim of substantial impairment.

3. **Painted 600 widgets black during that period:** This is particularly damaging. If the color was substantially impairing, the buyer would not have invested resources in modifying 60% of the widgets (painting them black) rather than raising the issue immediately. Painting the widgets black also demonstrates that the color was remediable and that the buyer considered a different color (black) acceptable for functional use.

**Objective Prong Analysis — Whether Nonconformity In Fact Impairs Value:**

1. **Functional Performance is Unaffected:** The widgets meet all functional specifications — load capacity and durability. The color deviation has no effect on product operation. Under Durfee v. Rod Baxter Imports, this is a "minor" defect. [32][33][34]

2. **Industry Practice Treats Minor Color Variations as Acceptable:** UCC §1-303(c) provides that a "usage of trade" is "any practice or method of dealing having such regularity of observance in a place, vocation, or trade as to justify an expectation that it will be observed with respect to the transaction." [20] If industry practice accepts minor color variations for functional components, this is admissible to show that the deviation does not substantially impair value.

3. **The Buyer's Own Conduct Belies the Claim of Substantial Impairment:** The buyer's conduct during the 8-week period is powerful evidence against substantial impairment:
   - Painting 600 widgets black: The buyer cured the alleged defect themselves at relatively low cost. This demonstrates that the color issue was remediable, not a fundamental impairment.
   - Praised performance via email: The buyer affirmatively communicated satisfaction with the widgets' performance.
   - Used all widgets for 8 weeks: The buyer derived full operational value from all 1,000 widgets for 8 weeks.

4. **The Aesthetic Nature of the Defect:** **Freeman Oldsmobile Mazda Co. v. Pinson, 580 S.W.2d 112 (Tex. Civ. App. 1979):** The court refused to allow revocation where the cost of repairing small dents and imperfect paint on a new car would cost less than $100. [35] Minor aesthetic defects generally do not meet the substantial impairment standard.

5. **The "Shaken Faith" Doctrine:** **Zabriskie Chevrolet, Inc. v. Smith, 99 N.J. Super. 441, 240 A.2d 195 (1968):** The "shaken faith" doctrine allows revocation where a defect destroys the buyer's confidence in the goods. [36][37][38] However, this doctrine typically applies to safety-related defects or cases where the goods are so defective that the buyer cannot trust them. Here, the goods perform exactly as expected functionally, and there is no basis for "shaken faith."

**Conclusion on Element (a):** The buyer is unlikely to satisfy either the subjective or objective prong of the substantial impairment test. The buyer's own conduct (8 weeks of use, praising performance, painting 600 widgets black, no mention of color) directly contradicts the claim that the color variance substantially impaired value. While the architect's later confirmation of contrast issues provides some support for the subjective prong, it was raised only after the buyer sought a 40% refund. Objectively, industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components.

### Element (b): Excuse for Acceptance — Was Acceptance Excused Under §2-608(1)(a) or (b)?

**Path (b)(i): Difficulty of Discovery — §2-608(1)(b)**

**Legal Standard:** The buyer may revoke if acceptance was "without discovery of such nonconformity if his acceptance was reasonably induced either by the difficulty of discovery before acceptance or by the seller's assurances." [26][27][28]

**Application:** The color was immediately visible upon delivery. March 15 delivery of widgets in navy blue instead of charcoal gray is a patently obvious, visually apparent nonconformity. The Fordham Law Review article notes: "The reasonable period [for revocation] commences from the point when the buyer discovers, or should have discovered, the non-conformity. Since a buyer will require substantially greater amount of time to discover defects which are latent, he should be given a correspondingly longer amount of time in which to revoke." [7] Conversely, a patent defect — one immediately visible — is discoverable upon delivery. The color difference between navy blue and charcoal gray is a patent, not latent, defect.

**Conclusion:** This path is almost certainly unavailable. The color difference between charcoal gray and navy blue is immediately observable upon visual inspection. There is no "difficulty of discovery" when the defect is a visible color variance on goods delivered in plain sight.

**Path (b)(ii): Seller's Assurances — §2-608(1)(b)**

**Application:** There is no evidence in the facts that the seller gave any specific assurances regarding color conformity that induced the buyer to accept. The buyer did not claim the seller promised the color was correct or provided any post-delivery assurances.

**Conclusion:** The absence of any specific assurances makes this path unavailable.

**Path (b)(iii): Reasonable Assumption of Cure — §2-608(1)(a)**

**Legal Standard:** The buyer may revoke if he accepted the goods "on the reasonable assumption that its nonconformity would be cured and it has not been seasonably cured." [26][27][28]

**Application:** The 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause is critical here. The buyer had a contractual right to inspect and reject within 10 days. Instead of raising the color issue within that period, the buyer installed, operated, and praised the widgets for 8 weeks. The buyer never communicated any assumption that the color would be cured. In fact, the buyer's conduct (operating, praising, painting 600 black) suggests the buyer had no expectation that cure was needed.

**Wilson v. Scampoli, 228 A.2d 848 (D.C. Ct. App. 1967):** The court held that a seller is entitled to a reasonable opportunity to cure. [39][40][41] The court found that "as he was denied access and a reasonable opportunity to repair, appellee has not shown a breach of warranty entitling him either to a brand new set or to rescission." [39][40][41] In this case, the vendor offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time. The buyer rejected this and instead demanded a 40% refund while keeping all widgets in use. Under Wilson, this refusal of cure weighs heavily against the buyer.

**Conclusion on Element (b):** None of the three alternative paths are likely satisfied. Path (b)(i) fails because the color was immediately visible and discoverable. Path (b)(ii) fails because there were no seller assurances. Path (b)(iii) fails because the buyer never assumed cure was needed (the buyer used and praised the widgets for 8 weeks without complaint) and refused the vendor's offered cure.

### Element (c): Timeliness — Was the May 10 Notification Within a Reasonable Time After Discovery?

**Legal Standard:** UCC §2-608(2) requires revocation to occur "within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it." [26][27][28] The Fordham Law Review article notes that the reasonable time period should extend in most cases beyond the time in which notification of breach must be given, but the period commences from the point when the buyer discovers, or should have discovered, the non-conformity. [7]

**Application:** The color difference between charcoal gray and navy blue was immediately observable upon delivery on March 15, 2024. The buyer discovered or should have discovered the color variance immediately upon delivery. It is a patent, not latent, defect. The buyer did not notify the vendor of the color issue until May 10, 2024 — 56 days after delivery and 8 weeks after the buyer knew or should have known of the color variance.

**Factors That Could Extend the Time:** The Fordham Law Review article notes that courts will extend the reasonable time when the seller gave assurances or the parties were engaged in repair negotiations. [7] In Durfee v. Rod Baxter Imports, the buyer continued to work with the seller on repairs over an extended period, and the court allowed revocation to be timely because the buyer was acting in reliance on the seller's repair promises. [32][33][34] Here, there were no repair promises or assurances. The buyer simply used the widgets for 8 weeks, praised their performance, and painted 600 of them black — all without ever mentioning the color issue.

**Conclusion on Element (c):** The 56-day delay in notifying the vendor of an immediately visible color defect is likely outside a "reasonable time" under §2-608(2). The buyer discovered or should have discovered the nonconformity upon delivery (March 15) but waited until May 10 to notify. No seller assurances or cure attempts justify the delay. The 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause in the contract underscores the parties' expectation that inspection and any complaints would occur promptly.

### Element (d): Substantial Change in Condition — Did Painting 600 Widgets Bar Revocation?

**Legal Standard:** UCC §2-608(2) requires revocation to occur "before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects." [26][27][28]

**Application:** The buyer painted **600 of the 1,000 widgets black** (60% of the total). This is an affirmative, voluntary alteration of the goods by the buyer. The color change (from navy blue to black) is a permanent modification. This constitutes a substantial change in condition. The defect alleged is the wrong color (navy blue instead of charcoal gray). Painting 600 widgets black changes their condition in a manner unrelated to any inherent defect — it is an act by the buyer that fundamentally alters the goods.

The 8 weeks of operation also constitutes a change in condition — the widgets have been used in commerce for 8 weeks, likely showing wear and tear, installation marks, and operational degradation.

**The Indiana Law Journal article** on rights and obligations of buyers notes that actions inconsistent with rejection, such as use or substantial alteration, may constitute acceptance. The buyer's painting of 600 widgets is a clear act inconsistent with the seller's ownership, indicating ownership and control over the goods. [42]

**Conclusion on Element (d):** Painting 600 widgets black (60% of the total) is a substantial change in condition not caused by the goods' own defects. This change alone likely bars revocation under §2-608(2). The 8 weeks of operation also constitutes a change in condition. The buyer cannot return the widgets in substantially the same condition as received because 600 have been painted and all 1,000 have been used.

### Element (e): Effect of Continued Use After Demanding Refund

**Legal Standard:** The Case Western Reserve Law Review article "Postrevocation Use: An Impermissible Interpretation of the UCC" (1984) by Patricia J. Hruby argues that postrevocation use invalidates revocation. [43] Key excerpts: "Permitting revocation after use violates the Uniform Commercial Code and gives buyers a gratuitous remedy." "Section 2-608(3) affords rejecting and revoking buyers similar treatment... Any exercise of ownership by one who does not own the goods... must be inconsistent with the ownership of the individual who does own the goods, that is, the seller." "The code drafters apparently intended that postrevocation use would invalidate revocation of acceptance." [43]

UCC §2-606 defines acceptance of goods as occurring when the buyer "does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership." Continued use of the goods after demanding revocation is an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership.

**Application:** The buyer continued using all 1,000 widgets after the May 10 notification. This continued use — especially after the buyer refused the vendor's offer of replacement — is strongly inconsistent with revocation. The buyer is effectively retaining the benefit of the goods while seeking a 40% price reduction. This looks more like a demand for a price adjustment (which would be governed by UCC §2-714 for accepted goods and breach of warranty) rather than a genuine revocation.

**Conclusion on Element (e):** The buyer's continued use of all 1,000 widgets after the May 10 notification and after demanding a refund is fundamentally inconsistent with revocation of acceptance. This conduct likely constitutes either a waiver of the right to revoke or evidence that the buyer has accepted the goods and is merely seeking a price reduction under UCC §2-714.

### Overall Conclusion on §2-608

The buyer cannot satisfy the requirements for revocation of acceptance:

1. **Substantial impairment (Element a):** Not met — the color deviation does not substantially impair value given the functional nature of the widgets, industry practice, and the buyer's own conduct.

2. **Excuse for acceptance (Element b):** Not met — the color was immediately discoverable, no seller assurances induced acceptance, and the buyer never assumed cure was needed.

3. **Timeliness (Element c):** Not met — the 56-day delay is unreasonable for a patent defect.

4. **Substantial change (Element d):** Bars revocation — painting 600 widgets is a substantial change in condition not caused by the defect.

5. **Continued use (Element e):** Inconsistent with revocation — continued use after demanding refund undermines any claim of revocation.

Revocation of acceptance is not available to the buyer.

## Part V: UCC §2-508 — Seller's Right to Cure

### Statutory Framework

UCC §2-508 provides the seller's rights to cure improper tenders or deliveries:

**§2-508(1):** "Where any tender or delivery by the seller is rejected because non-conforming and the time for performance has not yet expired, the seller may seasonably notify the buyer of his intention to cure and may then within the contract time make a conforming delivery." [44][45][46]

**§2-508(2):** "Where the buyer rejects a non-conforming tender which the seller had reasonable grounds to believe would be acceptable with or without money allowance the seller may if he seasonably notifies the buyer have a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender." [44][45][46]

The purpose of §2-508 is to allow sellers to correct delivery issues involving non-conforming goods in order to avoid immediate breach of contract claims. The provision promotes business relationships, reduces legal disputes, and facilitates contract completion. [47]

### Element (a): Reasonable Grounds to Believe Tender Would Be Acceptable

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-508(2), the seller must have had "reasonable grounds to believe" that the non-conforming tender would be acceptable to the buyer, with or without a money allowance. [44][45][46] A scholarly article by Michael A. Schmitt and David Frisch explains that this determination should focus on "whether the seller had some reason such as prior course of dealing or trade usage which reasonably led him to believe that the goods would be acceptable." [48]

**Wilson v. Scampoli, 228 A.2d 848 (D.C. Ct. App. 1967):** This is the seminal case on §2-508(2) and is directly analogous to the color deviation at issue. [39][40][41]

**Facts:** Nick Scampoli purchased a color television set. Upon delivery, the television "showed a reddish picture tint." The buyer refused to allow the seller to repair or replace it, demanding a new set or refund. The seller offered to remove the chassis for inspection and potential repair.

**Holding:** The D.C. Court of Appeals reversed the trial court's rescission order, holding that under §2-508, "the seller must be afforded a reasonable opportunity to cure defects by repair or replacement before the buyer can rescind the contract." [39][40][41]

**Critical language:** "A retail dealer would certainly expect and have reasonable grounds to believe that merchandise like color television sets, new and delivered as crated at the factory, would be acceptable as delivered and that, if defective in some way, he would have the right to substitute a conforming tender." [39][40][41]

**Application:** The "reddish picture tint" in Wilson is analogous to the navy blue vs. charcoal gray color deviation. Both are visual/color defects that do not affect the core functionality of the goods. The court held that the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the goods would be acceptable. Here, the seller delivered custom widgets as received from the manufacturer, meeting all functional specifications, with only a color deviation. Under Wilson, the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the goods would be acceptable.

**T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison Co. of New York, 57 N.Y.2d 574 (1982):** The New York Court of Appeals held that "if seasonable notice be given, such a seller may offer to cure the defect within a reasonable period beyond the time when the contract was to be performed so long as it has acted in good faith and with a reasonable expectation that the original goods would be acceptable to the buyer." [49][50][51]

**Key language:** The court found that what turned out to be a 0.92% sulfur content (exceeding the 0.5% contractual specification) was "within the range of contemplation of reasonable acceptability" to Con Ed. [49][50][51] The court stressed that parties must act in good faith and fairly under the UCC and that the UCC lets sellers fix small delivery problems so buyers cannot unjustly escape contracts.

**Application:** The navy blue vs. charcoal gray color deviation, like the sulfur content deviation in T.W. Oil, is "within the range of contemplation of reasonable acceptability" — especially given industry practice and the contract's emphasis on functional specs. The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is a seasonable and reasonable offer to cure.

**Factors Supporting the Seller's Reasonable Belief:**

1. **Emphasis on Functional Specifications:** The purchase order "emphasized functional specifications (load capacity, durability) with a color reference number but no rejection clause for shade variance." This objective signals to a reasonable seller that the buyer's primary concern is functionality, not exact color matching.

2. **Industry Practice:** UCC §1-303(c) provides that a "usage of trade" gives meaning to contract terms. [20] Industry practice treating minor color variations as acceptable for functional components establishes an objective basis for the seller's belief. The Schmitt & Frisch article explains: "The seller is not protected unless he had 'reasonable grounds to believe' that the tender would be acceptable. Such reasonable grounds can lie in prior course of dealing, course of performance or usage of trade as well as in the particular circumstances surrounding the making of the contract." [48]

3. **No Rejection Clause for Shade Variance:** The contract's absence of a specific clause rejecting shade variance — especially given the contract's emphasis on performance criteria — supports the seller's reasonable belief that minor color variations were within acceptable parameters.

4. **Buyer's Course of Performance:** The buyer's conduct during the 8-week period (operation, praise emails, painting 600 black) confirmed the seller's reasonable belief that the goods would be acceptable.

**Conclusion on Element (a):** The seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable under §2-508(2) based on: (1) the contract's emphasis on functional specifications; (2) industry practice accepting minor color variations; (3) the absence of a rejection clause for shade variance; and (4) the Wilson v. Scampoli precedent on visual/color deviations.

### Element (b): Seasonable Notice of Intent to Cure

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-508(2), the seller must "seasonably notify the buyer" of the intention to cure. [44][45][46]

**Application:** In T.W. Oil, the seller promptly notified the buyer of its intention to cure the defect through a substitute shipment, and the court found this constituted seasonable notice. [49][50][51]

In the present dispute, the vendor offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time upon receiving the buyer's notification of the color issue on May 10, 2024. This offer was made without delay and constituted seasonable notice of intent to cure. This is consistent with Wilson v. Scampoli, where the seller's offer to repair or replace the television was deemed an appropriate exercise of the right to cure. [39][40][41]

**Conclusion on Element (b):** The vendor's immediate offer of replacement with a two-week lead time constitutes seasonable notice under §2-508(2).

### Element (c): Further Reasonable Time to Substitute Conforming Tender

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-508(2), the seller is entitled to "a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender." [44][45][46]

**Application:** A two-week lead time for replacement widgets is commercially reasonable in the context of custom-manufactured goods. In T.W. Oil, the seller offered a substitute shipment of conforming oil, and the court found this to be a reasonable exercise of the cure right. [49][50][51] The buyer's painting of 600 widgets and continued use of all 1,000 widgets for eight weeks undermines any claim of urgency or substantial impairment.

The buyer's refusal of the cure offer and demand for a 40% refund instead parallels the situation in T.W. Oil, where the buyer's rejection of a reasonable cure offer was deemed improper. Similarly, in Wilson v. Scampoli, the buyer's refusal to allow the seller to cure through repair or replacement was found unreasonable and precluded rescission. [39][40][41]

**Conclusion on Element (c):** The two-week lead time for replacement widgets is a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender under §2-508(2).

### Interaction: Cure vs. Acceptance by Conduct

A critical analytical point: §2-508 cure rights are triggered by **rejection** of goods. If the buyer has already **accepted** the goods under §2-606, the cure provisions of §2-508 are not directly triggered. However, as a practical matter:

1. If the buyer attempts to reject (which is precluded by acceptance), the seller would have cure rights under §2-508(2).
2. If the buyer revokes acceptance (which is unlikely to succeed), most courts hold the seller has no right to cure. The Kansas Comment to 84-2-508 states: "A seller has no right to cure following revocation of acceptance." [46]

Since the buyer has accepted by conduct and cannot effectively reject or revoke, the cure question becomes moot as to the buyer's rejection rights, but relevant to the overall dispute resolution.

### Overall Conclusion on §2-508

The seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable, gave seasonable notice of intent to cure, and offered a further reasonable time to substitute conforming tender. The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time was commercially reasonable, and the buyer's refusal of this offer undermines any claim of substantial impairment or good faith.

## Part VI: UCC §2-714 — Damages for Accepted Goods

### Statutory Framework

UCC §2-714 provides the measure of damages when the buyer has accepted non-conforming goods: [52][53][54][55][56]

**§2-714(1):** "Where the buyer has accepted goods and given notification (subsection (3) of Section 2-607) he may recover as damages for any non-conformity of tender the loss resulting in the ordinary course of events from the seller's breach as determined in any manner which is reasonable." [52][53][54][55][56]

**§2-714(2):** "The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted, unless special circumstances show proximate damages of a different amount." [52][53][54][55][56]

**§2-714(3):** "In a proper case any incidental and consequential damages under the next section may also be recovered." [52][53][54][55][56]

### Critical Preliminary Issue: The Buyer Accepted the Goods

Under the analysis in Part III (§2-606), the buyer **accepted** the goods. Under UCC §2-606, acceptance occurs when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods: (a) signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming or that he will take or retain them in spite of their nonconformity; (b) fails to make an effective rejection after having a reasonable opportunity to inspect; or (c) does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership. [16][17][18] The buyer accepted by all three measures.

### Prong (a): §2-714(1) — General Damages for Accepted Goods

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-714(1), if the buyer accepted the goods and gave notification, the buyer may recover damages for "the loss resulting in the ordinary course of events from the seller's breach as determined in any manner which is reasonable." [52][53][54][55][56]

**Application:** The "loss resulting" from a color nonconformity in functional widgets is minimal, especially where: (a) industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable; (b) the buyer used and benefited from the widgets for eight weeks; (c) the buyer painted 600 of them black, curing the alleged defect for those units; and (d) the buyer refused a prompt cure offer.

**The Notice Requirement Under §2-607(3)(a):** A critical threshold issue is whether the buyer properly notified the seller of the breach. UCC §2-607(3)(a) requires that "the buyer must within a reasonable time after he discovers or should have discovered any breach notify the seller of breach or be barred from any remedy." [57][58] Case law interpreting this requirement is strict in commercial settings:

**Peavey Electronics Corp. v. Baan U.S.A., Inc.** (Mississippi Court of Appeals, 2009): "Strict compliance with the notice requirement was required in a claim between merchants." Despite years of expressed dissatisfaction and negotiation, Peavey was found to have failed to provide sufficient notice that it considered Baan to be in breach. [59]

**Bunn v. Navistar, Inc.** (6th Circuit, 2020): The court held that in commercial cases, general complaints of defects are insufficient; the buyer must notify the seller that it is considered to be in breach. "Plaintiffs must demonstrate that they provided notice of a breach (i.e., that the defendant will be asked to meet a claim for damages)." [60]

**Application:** The buyer had a 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause, operated widgets for eight weeks without complaint, sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color, and only notified the vendor of the color issue on May 10, 2024 — eight weeks after delivery. This eight-week delay in notifying the seller of the alleged breach, combined with emails praising performance, likely constitutes a failure to give timely notice under §2-607(3)(a). The buyer may be **barred from any remedy** for this reason.

**Conclusion on Prong (a):** Even if the buyer could overcome the notice bar, the "loss resulting in the ordinary course of events" from a color-only deviation on functional widgets is minimal.

### Prong (b): §2-714(2) — Breach of Warranty Measure

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-714(2), the measure is "the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted, unless special circumstances show proximate damages of a different amount." [52][53][54][55][56]

**Chatlos Systems, Inc. v. National Cash Register Corp., 670 F.2d 1304 (3d Cir. 1982):** The Third Circuit applied the "benefit of the bargain" rule. [61][62][63] Key holdings:
- "The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted."
- "Evidence of the contract price may be relevant to the issue of fair market value, but it is not controlling."
- "The buyer may not receive more than it bargained for; it may not obtain the value of a superior system which it did not purchase." [61][62][63]

**Application:** For a color-only deviation where functionality is unaffected, the §2-714(2) measure requires determining:
- Value of goods **as warranted**: charcoal gray widgets meeting all functional specs
- Value of goods **as accepted**: navy blue widgets meeting all functional specs
- Damages = the difference between these two values at the time and place of acceptance (March 15, 2024)

**The Difference in Value Is Likely Minimal:** For functional components where industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable, the difference in market value between navy blue widgets and charcoal gray widgets is likely minimal or zero. White and Summers explain in their treatise that §2-714(2) applies the "benefit of the bargain" rule, measuring the difference between the fair market value of the goods as warranted and the fair market value of the goods as accepted. [64] For custom widgets made to a specific buyer's specifications, where the color does not affect utility, the market value difference may be negligible.

**Cost of Repair as a Measure:** The Kansas Comment to §84-2-714 (1996) explains: "When repair of the goods is possible, the best measure of damages for breach of warranty is the cost of repair. If the cost of repair exceeds the price of the goods, however, the purchase price should serve as a cap on damages." [56]

**Application:** The buyer already demonstrated that the color issue can be cured by painting. The buyer painted **600 widgets black** during the 8-week period. The cost of painting the remaining 400 widgets (or the cost the buyer incurred for painting 600) would be a reasonable measure of damages under the "cost of repair" approach.

**No Damages for the 600 Painted Widgets:** Since the buyer already painted 600 widgets black, those widgets now **match the contractual color** (assuming black was the desired charcoal gray or an acceptable substitute). The buyer has already cured the defect for those 600 widgets. The buyer cannot recover damages for a defect that has already been cured by the buyer's own action, unless the buyer can prove the cost of painting as incidental damages.

**The Buyer's Own Conduct as Evidence of Value:** The buyer's use of all widgets for 8 weeks, the praise emails, and the buyer's own painting of 600 widgets are all evidence that the value of the goods as accepted was very close to the value as warranted.

**Mitigation and Offset:** As held in Chatlos Systems, "Buyers must act in good faith and mitigate damages reasonably after a breach." [61][62][63] The buyer's own conduct in painting 600 widgets demonstrates that mitigation was possible at relatively low cost. The buyer cannot claim substantial damages when the cure was simple and the buyer already achieved it.

Additionally, the benefit obtained from use must be offset. The buyer used all 1,000 widgets for 8 weeks and derived operational value from them. As the Sales Compendium explains: "Gains made by the injured party on other transactions after the breach are never to be deducted from the damages that are otherwise recoverable, unless such gains could not have been made, had there been no breach." [64] Here, the buyer's use of the goods conferred a benefit that must be offset against any damages.

**Section 2-717: Deduction of Damages from the Price:** UCC §2-717 provides: "The buyer on notifying the seller of his intention to do so may deduct all or any part of the damages resulting from any breach of the contract from any part of the price still due under the same contract." [65][66] Key limitations: (a) the deduction is for "damages resulting from any breach" — i.e., actual proven damages under §2-714(2), not an arbitrary percentage; (b) the buyer must give notice of intention to deduct; and (c) the deduction is limited to the price still due.

**Conclusion on Prong (b):** The proper measure of damages under §2-714(2) for a color-only deviation is the difference in value (likely minimal) or the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets (the buyer already painted 600). The buyer cannot recover the full purchase price or a 40% refund while keeping the goods.

### Prong (c): §2-714(3) — Incidental and Consequential Damages

**Legal Standard:** Under §2-714(3), the buyer may recover incidental and consequential damages under §2-715 "in a proper case." [52][53][54][55][56]

**UCC §2-715 provides:** [67][68]
- **Incidental damages:** Expenses reasonably incurred in inspection, receipt, transportation, and care and custody of goods rightfully rejected, charges in connection with effecting cover, and any other reasonable expense incident to the delay or other breach.
- **Consequential damages:** "Any loss resulting from general or particular requirements and needs of which the seller at the time of contracting had reason to know and which could not reasonably be prevented by cover or otherwise," and injury to person or property proximately resulting from any breach of warranty.

**Midwest Hatchery & Poultry Farms, Inc. v. Doorenbos Poultry, Inc., 783 N.W.2d 56 (Iowa App. 2010):** The court held that "because Doorenbos accepted and kept the pullets, Midwest is entitled to the unpaid balance of the contract price." [69][70] However, because the contract's limitation of damages clause failed in its essential purpose, the court awarded Doorenbos lost profits of $31,732.79, representing the losses caused by not being able to replace its existing flock with eighteen-week-old birds. [69][70]

**Application to the Widget Dispute:**

1. **Incidental Damages:** The buyer may recover reasonable expenses of inspection, handling, and storage directly related to the breach. However, the buyer painted 600 widgets and used all widgets for 8 weeks — these acts may convert what would be incidental expenses into costs of the buyer's own business operations.

2. **Consequential Damages for Aesthetic Concerns:** Even if consequential damages were not excluded by the contract, the buyer faces significant barriers:

   a. **Lack of Seller's Knowledge:** The buyer's architect's later confirmation that navy blue created contrast issues in the showroom design does not establish that the seller had reason to know of this specific aesthetic requirement at the time of contracting. The purchase order emphasized functional performance criteria (load capacity, durability), not aesthetic showroom requirements. Absent disclosure of a particular aesthetic need, the seller cannot be charged with knowledge of such requirements.

   b. **Failure to Mitigate:** The buyer could have prevented this loss by: (i) notifying the seller promptly; (ii) accepting the cure offer; (iii) simply not using the goods in a showroom where color was critical; or (iv) painting the remaining 400 widgets as the buyer already did for 600 units.

   c. **Notice Bar:** As discussed under Prong (a), the buyer's failure to give timely notice under §2-607(3)(a) may bar any remedy, including consequential damages.

**Conclusion on Prong (c):** Consequential damages are not available given the failure of timely notice, the lack of seller's knowledge of aesthetic requirements, and the failure to mitigate. Incidental damages, if any, would be minimal.

### Overall Conclusion on §2-714

The proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation on functional components is:

- **§2-714(2) difference in value:** Likely minimal — perhaps the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets (the buyer already painted 600)
- **Offset for benefit of use:** The buyer used all widgets for 8 weeks, which must be credited to the seller
- **No windfall:** The buyer cannot recover the full purchase price or a 40% refund while keeping the goods
- **§2-717 deduction:** Limited to actual proven damages, not an arbitrary percentage

## Part VII: Whether the Buyer's Demand for a 40% Refund While Keeping the Widgets Is Supported Under UCC Damage Principles

### The Fundamental Principle: Pay the Contract Price for Accepted Goods

**Midwest Hatchery & Poultry Farms, Inc. v. Doorenbos Poultry, Inc., 783 N.W.2d 56 (Iowa Ct. App. 2010):** The court affirmed that "A fundamental principle of contract law is that a party that accepts the delivery of nonconforming goods must pay the full contract price." [69][70] "If a buyer accepts goods, the buyer takes them 'in spite of their nonconformity' and must pay the contract rate." [69][70]

The court further held that acceptance of nonconforming goods creates an enforceable contract requiring payment at contract price regardless of nonconformity, but the buyer is entitled to compensation for losses — the buyer must pay the full price but can recover damages for the nonconformity. [69][70]

### The Buyer's Demand Is Disproportionate to Any Actual Injury

As established in Part VI, the proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation is the difference in value between navy blue widgets and charcoal gray widgets — likely minimal for functional components. The buyer's demand for **40% of the contract price** while keeping **all 1,000 widgets** is grossly disproportionate to any actual injury.

**Application of UCC §1-305(a):** "The remedies provided by this code shall be liberally administered to the end that the aggrieved party may be put in as good a position as if the other party had fully performed." [71] This means the buyer is entitled to the **benefit of the bargain** — not a windfall. The buyer who keeps accepted goods can only recover the difference in value (plus incidental and consequential damages where appropriate). A 40% refund while keeping all widgets would put the buyer in a **better** position than if the contract had been performed — which is not the purpose of UCC remedies.

### The Buyer Cannot Reject or Revoke

Since the buyer has accepted the goods under §2-606 and cannot effectively reject or revoke (Parts II, III, IV), the buyer's remedies are limited to:
- **§2-714 damages** (difference in value)
- **§2-717 deduction** (limited to actual proven damages)

The buyer cannot obtain a 40% refund via rejection or revocation.

### The Seller's Cure Offer Is More Reasonable

The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is commercially reasonable. Under **Zabriskie Chevrolet v. Smith**, a cure must restore the goods to the contracted condition. [36][37][38] The seller's offer to replace all 1,000 widgets with charcoal gray widgets within two weeks would do exactly that. The buyer's refusal of this offer while demanding a 40% refund supports a finding of bad faith.

### The Buyer's Demand Constitutes a Request for a Windfall

Under **Chatlos Systems v. NCR**: "The buyer may not receive more than it bargained for." [61][62][63] The buyer is demanding a 40% refund while retaining full use and possession of all 1,000 widgets. This would place the buyer in a **better** position than if the contract had been fully performed — the buyer would have functionally identical widgets plus 40% of the purchase price back. This directly contradicts the UCC's fundamental principle that remedies should put the aggrieved party "in as good a position as if the other party had fully performed," not in a **superior** position. [71]

### The Buyer's Potential Proper Remedies

If the buyer wishes to keep the widgets (as the buyer's demand for a 40% refund indicates), the buyer's proper remedies are:

1. **§2-714 damages:** The difference in value between navy and charcoal gray widgets (likely minimal, possibly the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets)

2. **§2-717 deduction:** Offset of actual proven damages from any remaining price due, with proper notice

3. **Incidental damages:** Costs of inspection, handling, and storage directly related to the breach

4. **Consequential damages:** Only if not excluded by the contract and if properly proven (unlikely here)

### Conclusion on the 40% Refund Demand

The buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping all widgets is **not supported** under UCC damage principles. It is disproportionate to any actual injury, inconsistent with the fundamental principle that accepted goods must be paid for at the contract rate, and appears opportunistic given the minimal nature of the color deviation. The buyer's proper course is to either (a) accept the seller's cure offer of replacement widgets, or (b) retain the widgets and seek damages under §2-714 for the actual difference in value (likely the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets), subject to providing proper notice under §2-607(3) and §2-717.

## Part VIII: UCC §1-304 — Good Faith

### Statutory Framework

UCC §1-304 provides: "Every contract or duty within the Uniform Commercial Code imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement." [72][73] UCC §1-201(b)(20) defines "good faith" as "honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing." [74] For merchants, UCC §2-103(1)(b) additionally requires "observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing in the trade." [75]

The Restatement (Second) of Contracts §205 parallels this: "Every contract imposes upon each party a duty of good faith and fair dealing in its performance and its enforcement." [76]

### The "Excluder Model" — What Constitutes Bad Faith

The Connecticut Law Review Note, "Good Faith Rejection of Goods in a Falling Market," explains: "All aspects of the performance of a contract must be performed in 'good faith.' Since the rejection of goods is an aspect of performance, rejection — even if otherwise rightful — must be performed in good faith." [77]

**Critical interpretive principles:**

- This section does not support an independent cause of action for failure to perform or enforce in good faith. Rather, the failure to act in good faith constitutes a breach of the existing contract or may affect the availability of remedies. [72]
- "Acting in self-interest under contractual rights is not, by itself, a breach of good faith; rather, a violation arises only when improper motives drive the conduct." — *Arch Ins. Co. v. Centerplan Constr. Co.*, 368 F. Supp. 3d 350, 372-73 (D. Conn. 2019). [78]
- A breach arises "if conduct 'unfairly frustrates the agreed common purpose and disappoints the reasonable expectations of the other party.'" — *Shibata v. Lim*, 133 F. Supp. 2d 1311 (M.D. Fla. 2000). [79]

### Neumiller Farms, Inc. v. Cornett — The Leading Case on Bad Faith Rejection

**Neumiller Farms, Inc. v. Cornett, 368 So. 2d 272 (Ala. 1979)** — The Alabama Supreme Court held that a merchant buyer's "satisfaction" rejection of potatoes lacked good faith. [80][81][82]

**Key facts:** Neumiller Farms contracted with Cornett to supply chipping potatoes at $4.25 per hundredweight. When the market price fell to $2.00 per hundredweight, Cornett rejected nine loads claiming they were unsuitable for chipping, even though expert testing confirmed their suitability.

**Holding:** The court held that "the rejection appeared to be influenced by the decline in market price rather than the quality of the potatoes" and constituted a breach of contract for lack of good faith. [80][81][82] The court stated:
- "A rejection of goods based on a claim of dissatisfaction, which is not made in good faith, is ineffectual and constitutes a breach of contract for which damages are recoverable." [80]
- "For merchant buyers, dissatisfaction claims must be made in good faith measured by objective commercial standards, not subjective honesty alone." [80][81]
- "The buyer's purported dissatisfaction with goods cannot be used as a pretext to escape a contract when the true motivation is a falling market price." [81]

### Application: Specific Indicia of Bad Faith in the Buyer's Conduct

**1. Delay in Raising the Defect**

The buyer waited **8 weeks** (56 days) after delivery to raise a **visually apparent** defect. During this entire period, the buyer said nothing about the color issue. While "acting in self-interest under contractual rights is not, by itself, a breach of good faith" (Arch Ins. Co.), [78] the pattern of silence followed by a demand for a 40% refund suggests the buyer was not genuinely dissatisfied but is seeking a windfall.

**2. Contradictory Conduct**

The buyer's conduct is inherently contradictory:
- **Praised performance via email** (suggesting satisfaction)
- **Painted 600 widgets black** (curing the color issue themselves)
- **Used all widgets for 8 weeks** (deriving full operational value)
- **Then demanded a 40% refund** while keeping all widgets

This pattern of conduct — praising, using, modifying, and then demanding a massive refund — "unfairly frustrates the agreed common purpose and disappoints the reasonable expectations of the other party" under the standard articulated in *Shibata v. Lim*. [79]

**3. Rejection of Reasonable Cure Offer**

The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time was commercially reasonable. The buyer's refusal of this offer while demanding a 40% refund to keep goods that the buyer praised and used for 8 weeks is strong evidence of bad faith. Under *Neumiller Farms*, a buyer cannot reject a reasonable cure offer and then seek a windfall. [80][81][82]

**4. Disproportionate Demand**

The buyer's demand for a **40% refund** for a purely cosmetic color deviation on goods that meet all functional specifications is grossly disproportionate to any actual injury. As established in Part VII, the proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation is likely minimal. A demand for 40% appears opportunistic.

**5. Timing Raises Suspicion**

The buyer's notice on May 10, 2024 — 58 days after the 10-day inspection clause expired, and only after the buyer had used the widgets for 8 weeks and painted 600 — suggests the buyer was not genuinely dissatisfied but rather is seeking a price concession. Under *Neumiller Farms*, the court looks to whether the rejection is "influenced by" factors other than genuine dissatisfaction with the goods. [80][81][82]

### Tongish v. Thomas and Market Stability

**Tongish v. Thomas, 251 Kan. 728, 840 P.2d 471 (1992)** — The Kansas Supreme Court held that when a breach of contract governed by the UCC is made in bad faith, damages should be structured to promote market efficiency and discourage breaches that exploit contractual purity for unfair advantage. [83][84][85]

The court reasoned: "A core tenet of contract law is that a breaching party must not be punished for a breach, but rather, the nonbreaching party must simply be made whole. However, where the breach is in bad faith, the Uniform Commercial Code provides for damages that increase market stability." [84]

**Application:** While Tongish involved a seller's bad faith, the principle applies symmetrically. A buyer who exploits a trivial non-conformity to extract a disproportionate refund undermines contractual stability and the reasonable expectations of commercial parties.

### Course of Performance and Usage of Trade as Good Faith Standards

**UCC §1-303(a)** provides that a course of performance is relevant in ascertaining the meaning of the parties' agreement and may supplement or qualify the terms of the agreement. [20] The buyer's eight-week period of operation, praise emails, and painting of 600 widgets constitute a course of performance that is inconsistent with the buyer's later claim of substantial impairment.

**Industry Practice (§1-303(c)):** The industry practice of treating minor color variations as acceptable for functional components establishes the "reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing in the trade" that the buyer must observe under the merchant standard of good faith (§2-103(1)(b)). [75] A buyer who knows that color variations are standard in the industry cannot later claim that such a variation constitutes a material breach justifying a 40% discount.

### Conclusion on §1-304

The buyer's conduct — using and praising the goods for 8 weeks, painting 600 of them, failing to communicate any dissatisfaction, and then demanding a 40% refund while rejecting a reasonable cure offer — raises serious good faith concerns. The pattern strongly suggests opportunistic behavior rather than genuine dissatisfaction with the goods. This conduct violates the obligation of good faith under §1-304 and, under *Neumiller Farms*, the buyer's rejection or dissatisfaction claim may be held ineffectual. [80][81][82]

### Overall Conclusion

The buyer's legal position is weak on all major issues:

1. **The buyer accepted the goods** under all three prongs of §2-606 (signifying conformity through praise emails, failure to reject timely, and painting 600 widgets — an act inconsistent with seller's ownership).

2. **The buyer cannot revoke acceptance** because: (a) the color deviation does not substantially impair value subjectively or objectively; (b) none of the three excuses for acceptance are satisfied; (c) the 56-day delay is unreasonable for a patent defect; (d) painting 600 widgets constitutes a substantial change in condition; and (e) continued use after demanding refund is inconsistent with revocation.

3. **The seller had a right to cure** under §2-508(2) based on reasonable grounds to believe the goods would be acceptable, and the vendor's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time was seasonable and commercially reasonable.

4. **The buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping all widgets is not supported** under any provision of the UCC. The proper measure of damages for a color-only deviation on functional components is the difference in value (likely minimal) or the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets, offset by the buyer's 8 weeks of beneficial use.

5. **The buyer's conduct raises serious good faith concerns** under §1-304, including the 8-week delay, the contradictory conduct (praising, using, painting, then complaining), the rejection of a reasonable cure offer, and the disproportionate demand for a 40% refund.

### Recommended Course of Action

The buyer should either:

1. **Accept the seller's cure offer** of replacement widgets with a two-week lead time; or

2. **Retain the widgets** and seek damages under §2-714 for the actual difference in value (likely the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets), subject to providing proper notice under §2-607(3) and §2-717.

The buyer is not entitled to reject the goods, revoke acceptance, or unilaterally demand a 40% refund while keeping all widgets in use.
