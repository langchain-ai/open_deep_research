# Comprehensive Legal Analysis: Texas Promissory Estoppel and Unreasonable Reliance — The Hayes Scenario

## Executive Summary

This report addresses whether software engineer Jordan Hayes's reliance on a verbal promise of full remote work is unreasonable as a matter of law under Texas promissory estoppel doctrine, justifying summary judgment for the employer. The analysis focuses on six specific issues: (1) when a contradictory written contract with a merger clause renders reliance unreasonable; (2) the timing of detrimental acts occurring after receiving the contradictory writing; (3) the effect of professional sophistication; (4) the impact of a formal exception process for remote work approvals; (5) the limitation of remedies to reliance damages; and (6) venue sensitivity given the split among Texas Courts of Appeals.

Based on the research conducted, the conclusion is that Hayes's reliance is likely unreasonable as a matter of law, justifying summary judgment for the employer in most Texas venues. The critical facts driving this conclusion are: (a) Hayes received the May 1, 2024 offer letter stating "hybrid schedule per company policy" with a merger clause BEFORE selling the home and signing the lease on May 15; (b) the manager's June 2024 email came AFTER the detrimental acts had already occurred; (c) other engineers received written approvals through a formal exception process that Hayes never used; and (d) as a software engineer, Hayes is a sophisticated professional held to a higher standard of understanding contractual documents.

## Issue 1: When Reliance on a Prior Oral Promise Is Rendered Unreasonable as a Matter of Law — The "Contract Bar" Doctrine vs. "Reasonable Reliance"

### The Distinction Between Two Separate Doctrines

Texas law recognizes two distinct but related barriers to promissory estoppel claims when a written contract exists. These barriers operate independently, and a plaintiff can be defeated by either one.

**The Contract Bar Doctrine:** This is the rule that promissory estoppel does not apply when a valid, enforceable written contract exists between the parties covering the same subject matter. As stated in *Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco, Inc.*, 52 S.W.3d 671, 684 (Tex. 2000): "Promissory estoppel is an equitable claim that is unavailable when an express contract covers the dispute's subject matter" [1]. The Fifth Circuit in *Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp.* (Case No. 12-10063, 5th Cir., Jan 31, 2013) held that "promissory estoppel applies only when the elements of a contract cannot be shown to exist" [2]. The district court in *Clinton Growers* concluded "that promissory estoppel is not a viable theory... because it is barred by reason of the existence of a contract... and because the merger language in the contracts prohibits reliance on the extra-contractual statements" [2].

**The Reasonable Reliance Element:** This is a separate factual inquiry into whether the plaintiff's reliance on the promise was objectively reasonable under the circumstances. Even when the contract bar does not apply (for example, because the written document is not a binding contract), the plaintiff's reliance can still be deemed unreasonable as a matter of law if they had notice of contradictory terms. As stated in *Gilmartin v. KVTV–Channel 13*, 985 S.W.2d 553, 558 (Tex. App.—San Antonio 1998): "Promissory estoppel requires 'detrimental reliance which is reasonable and justified'" [3].

### The Critical Analysis: Does the At-Will Offer Letter Trigger the Contract Bar?

The threshold question is whether the May 1, 2024 offer letter constitutes a "valid contract" for purposes of the contract bar doctrine. Texas at-will employment is terminable at any time by either party, raising the question of whether such an arrangement constitutes a "valid contract" that bars a promissory estoppel claim. The Texas courts are divided on this issue.

*Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services, Inc.*, 757 S.W.2d 48 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1988) held that an employer's "undisputed oral promise clearly imposed a duty on Geosource to employ Roberts—but not for a fixed duration—and that duty was breached by Geosource" [4]. The court allowed the promissory estoppel claim to proceed despite the at-will nature of the employment.

However, *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc.*, 871 S.W.2d 929 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1994) rejected this approach, holding that "promissory estoppel may not be applied to recover reliance damages when there is a valid contract terminable at will" and that "[a] promise to provide at-will employment does not provide a reasonable basis for reliance, as a matter of law" [5].

### The Independent Reasonable Reliance Analysis: Why the Merger Clause Defeats the Claim

Crucially, even in jurisdictions that follow *Roberts* and allow promissory estoppel in the at-will context, the existence of a contradictory writing with a merger clause independently defeats the reasonable reliance element. This is because the reasonable reliance inquiry is separate from the contract bar inquiry.

The leading Texas case on this point is *Sonny Ellen and Lynn C. Ellen v. F.H. Partners, LLC*, No. 03-09-00310-CV (Tex. App.—Austin 2010). The Ellens had signed a loan agreement that contained a "Notice of Invalidity of Oral Agreements" expressly prohibiting oral modifications. They later claimed that a loan officer orally promised to delay foreclosure. The court held: "it was not reasonable or justifiable for the Ellens to rely on the oral 'promise' because the Loan Agreement addressed the substance of the oral statement... and specifically prohibited oral modification of its terms" [6]. The court affirmed summary judgment for the defendant.

The court's reasoning is directly applicable to the Hayes scenario:

> "The terms of the Loan Agreement itself prohibit enforcement of the 'promise' here even if section 26.02 does not."
> "Parties to a written contract that is within the provisions of the statute of frauds... 'may not by mere oral agreement alter one or more of the terms'" of that contract (quoting *Dracopoulas v. Rachal*, 411 S.W.2d 719, 721 (Tex. 1967)).

Applying this to Hayes: The May 1 offer letter states "hybrid schedule per company policy." This directly addresses the subject matter of work schedule—the same subject matter as the alleged verbal promise of "full remote work." The bolded merger clause states: "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations." Even if the at-will nature means the offer letter is not a "valid contract" for contract bar purposes, the existence of this writing with these specific terms makes any subsequent reliance on a contradictory verbal promise unreasonable as a matter of law.

### The Merger Clause Analysis Under Italian Cowboy Partners

The Texas Supreme Court in *Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential Insurance Co. of America*, 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011) held that a standard merger clause does not bar fraudulent inducement claims unless it contains a clear and unequivocal disclaimer of reliance [7]. The court distinguished between:

- **Standard merger clause** (disclaiming existence of other representations): Does not bar reliance-based claims
- **No-reliance clause** (explicitly disclaiming reliance on extra-contractual representations): May bar such claims

However, this distinction applies to **fraudulent inducement** claims, not to the **reasonable reliance** element of promissory estoppel. For promissory estoppel purposes, the merger clause is relevant as evidence affecting the reasonableness of the plaintiff's reliance—not as an absolute bar. The question is: Given this merger clause, was it objectively reasonable for Hayes to rely on the verbal promise?

The answer under Texas law is likely no. A reasonable software engineer reading a bolded clause that "this agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations" would be on notice that prior promises may not be enforceable. This is particularly true where, as here, the written document explicitly states a contradictory term ("hybrid schedule per company policy").

### Summary Judgment Standard for Unreasonable Reliance

Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a, a defendant moving for traditional summary judgment must show there is no genuine issue of material fact and that the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. For a no-evidence motion under Rule 166a(i), the movant may challenge one or more elements on which the non-movant bears the burden of proof [8].

Several Texas cases hold that reliance on an oral promise contradicted by a signed writing is unreasonable as a matter of law, justifying summary judgment:

- *Ellen v. F.H. Partners* (Tex. App.—Austin 2010): Summary judgment affirmed; reliance on oral promise unreasonable where written agreement prohibited oral modifications [6]
- *Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp.* (5th Cir. 2013): Summary judgment affirmed; merger language prohibits reliance on extra-contractual statements [2]
- *Jefferson v. Mike Bloomberg 2020, Inc.* (N.D. Tex. 2021): Summary judgment granted; signed offer letter establishing at-will status precluded claims that oral promises modified that status [9]
- *Biosilk Spa, L.P. v. HG Shopping Centers, L.P.* (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 2008): The court held that sophisticated parties cannot reasonably rely on oral promises contradicted by written contracts [10]

## Issue 2: Timing of Reliance — The Significance of Detrimental Acts Occurring After Receiving the Contradictory Writing

### The Critical Timeline Fact

One of the most damaging facts for Hayes is the timing sequence:

- **April 2024**: Verbal promise of "full remote work" during negotiations
- **May 1, 2024**: Written offer letter received stating "hybrid schedule per company policy" with bolded merger clause
- **May 15, 2024**: Hayes sells Seattle home at $40,000 loss and signs Texas lease—**AFTER** receiving the contradictory writing
- **June 2024**: Manager sends confirmation email—**AFTER** the detrimental acts

This sequence means that Hayes undertook the most significant acts of detrimental reliance (selling a home, signing a lease) with actual knowledge that the written offer letter contradicted the verbal promise. The June 2024 confirmation email came too late to influence the May 15 decisions.

### The Ellen Analogy: Timing Makes Reliance Unreasonable

The timing in *Ellen v. F.H. Partners* is directly analogous. The Ellens signed their loan agreement (containing the prohibition on oral modifications) **BEFORE** they relied on the oral promise to delay foreclosure. The court held this timing made their reliance unreasonable as a matter of law [6].

The court reasoned that the earlier writing put the promisee on notice that the later oral assurance was not authorized and could not be relied upon. Critically, the detrimental acts in *Ellen* (not paying the loan, not seeking alternative financing) occurred **after** the oral promise was made—yet the court still held the reliance was unreasonable because the written contract had already provided notice that oral modifications were prohibited.

Applying this to Hayes: The May 1 offer letter put Hayes on notice that the schedule was "hybrid per company policy" and that prior verbal representations were superseded. Hayes then undertook the May 15 detrimental acts with this knowledge. The timing problem is actually **worse** for Hayes than for the Ellens, because:

1. In *Ellen*, the oral promise came **after** the written contract, and the detrimental acts came **after** the oral promise
2. In Hayes's case, the written document (May 1) came **before** the detrimental acts (May 15), and the confirming email (June 2024) came **after** the detrimental acts

This means Hayes acted with knowing disregard of the contradictory written terms—not in reasonable reliance on a later assurance that might have revived a reasonable belief.

### The "Post-Contract Reliance" Rule

Several Texas cases establish the principle that post-contract reliance on oral promises is unreasonable when the written contract addresses the same subject matter:

- *Jefferson v. Mike Bloomberg 2020, Inc.* (N.D. Tex. 2021): The plaintiff signed an offer letter and employee handbook (both received before she began employment) that "explicitly stated her at-will employment status with no binding oral modifications." She then relied on public statements promising employment through November 2020. The court held that the signed documents precluded her promissory estoppel claim, emphasizing that the written documents were received **before** any detrimental reliance [9].

- *Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp.* (5th Cir. 2013): The growers signed written contracts with merger clauses before any alleged oral promises about future business continuation. The contracts explicitly stated they "supersede all oral statements and other communications." The court held that "the merger language in the contracts prohibits reliance on the extra-contractual statements" [2].

### The June 2024 Email Cannot Retroactively Justify the May 15 Acts

A critical question is whether the manager's June 2024 email confirming the remote promise can retroactively make the May 15 detrimental acts reasonable. Under Texas law, the answer is no. The reasonableness of reliance is assessed at the time the detrimental acts occur—not based on later events.

The manager's June email came after Hayes had already:
- Sold the Seattle home at a $40,000 loss
- Signed the Texas lease
- Incurred relocation and moving expenses

These acts cannot be undone. Even if the June email gave Hayes a reasonable belief going forward, it cannot retroactively justify decisions made before receiving it. Moreover, the manager lacked authority to make such promises (as discussed below), so even the June email could not create reasonable reliance for future acts, let alone past ones.

## Issue 3: Professional Sophistication and Its Effect on Reasonable Reliance

### The Legal Standard: Sophistication Is Relevant to Reasonableness

Under Texas law, the standard for assessing reasonable reliance in a promissory estoppel claim has both objective and subjective components. The plaintiff's professional background, experience, and sophistication are relevant considerations.

The leading Texas Supreme Court case on reasonable reliance, *Fretz Construction Co. v. Southern National Bank of Houston*, 626 S.W.2d 478 (Tex. 1981), involved a sophisticated commercial party (a general contractor) [11]. The court allowed the promissory estoppel claim to proceed because the bank's specific promise (the letter confirming funds were set aside) was clear and definite enough that even a sophisticated contractor could reasonably rely on it. However, the court emphasized the specificity of the promise and the existence of a written document supporting it.

### The Principle: Sophistication Cuts Against Reasonable Reliance

Several Texas Courts of Appeals decisions suggest that sophistication can cut the other way—the more sophisticated the plaintiff, the greater the expectation that they will understand the legal significance of a writing contradicting an oral promise:

- *Biosilk Spa, L.P. v. HG Shopping Centers, L.P.* (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 2008): The court held that "no reasonable reliance" was found in a fraud and promissory estoppel case involving sophisticated commercial parties [10].

- *IBM v. Lufkin Industries, LLC*, 573 S.W.3d 224 (Tex. 2019): "When sophisticated parties represented by counsel disclaim reliance on representations about a specific matter in dispute, such a disclaimer may be binding, conclusively negating the element of reliance in a suit for fraudulent inducement" [12].

- *Allied Vista, Inc. v. Holt*, 987 S.W.2d 138, 141-42 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1999): "While a specific, detailed promise might support a promissory estoppel claim, relying on a vague, indefinite promise of future business is unreasonable as a matter of law" [13]. This implies that a sophisticated commercial party should recognize when a promise is too vague to be enforceable.

### Application to Hayes: Software Engineer as Sophisticated Professional

Hayes is a software engineer—a professional with technical expertise and, presumably, a college degree. Under Texas law, this professional status makes Hayes's reliance on the verbal promise **less** reasonable than it would be for an unsophisticated consumer. The reasoning is:

1. **Understanding of Written Documents:** A software engineer is expected to read and understand contractual documents, including merger clauses. When the offer letter states "hybrid schedule per company policy" and contains a bolded merger clause stating "this agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations," a reasonable software engineer would understand that the written terms control.

2. **Knowledge of Company Policies:** As a professional, Hayes is expected to understand that company policies exist and that a statement "per company policy" means the policy governs, not an informal verbal assurance.

3. **Understanding of Authority:** A software engineer in a corporate environment should understand that hiring managers may not have authority to make promises that deviate from standard company policies, especially when there is a formal process for exceptions.

### Contrast with Cases Where Unsophisticated Parties Were Allowed to Proceed

Texas courts have allowed unsophisticated parties to proceed with promissory estoppel claims in some circumstances:

- *Frost Crushed Stone Company, Inc. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Inc.* (Tex. 1995): Even though no formal written contract was signed, a written price quote supported the oral promise, and the court found "factually sufficient" evidence of detrimental reliance [14].

- *Wheeler v. White*, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965): The Texas Supreme Court allowed Wheeler's claim to proceed even though the underlying written contract was too indefinite to be enforced. The court found a "promise designedly made to influence the conduct of the promisee" [15].

However, these cases are distinguishable because they involved either (a) a written document supporting the promise, (b) special circumstances where the promisor actively encouraged reliance, or (c) the absence of a clear merger clause or contradictory term. None of these favorable circumstances exist in Hayes's case.

### Summary: Sophistication Cuts Strongly Against Hayes

The research indicates the following framework:

- A sophisticated party like Hayes is **held to a higher standard**—expected to know that oral promises are unenforceable when contradicted by a written contract, especially when the written contract contains an integration or merger clause
- The existence of a signed writing prohibiting reliance on oral representations makes reliance **unreasonable as a matter of law** for any party, regardless of sophistication
- There is **no Texas case** holding that a software engineer or similar professional's status alone makes reliance unreasonable, but the cases consistently treat sophistication as a factor weighing against reasonableness
- The key is not sophistication alone, but the combination of: (a) a contradictory writing, (b) a merger clause, and (c) facts showing the plaintiff had actual notice of the contradiction

## Issue 4: The Formal Exception Process — Effect of Other Engineers Receiving Written Approvals

### The Factual Significance

The fact that two other engineers hired simultaneously received written remote approvals is highly significant for two reasons:

1. **It proves the existence of a formal exception process:** The company had a mechanism for granting remote work exceptions, and it was followed for other employees
2. **It shows Hayes failed to use that process:** Hayes never obtained written approval through the formal process

### Texas Law on Formal Processes and Unreasonable Reliance

Under Texas agency law, the existence of a formal process for obtaining approvals, combined with the employee's failure to use it, makes reliance on an unapproved verbal promise unreasonable.

**Guerra v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc.** (Tex. App.—Corpus Christi): This case is directly relevant. Wal-Mart's agents lacked actual authority to enter into a lifetime employment contract with Guerra. The court found no sufficient evidence of apparent authority based on Wal-Mart's actions or prior ratifications. The court stated: "We conclude the record does not contain any acts by Wal-Mart from which a reasonable person could believe that Wal-Mart authorized Tippens, Moore, and Williams to enter into any contract on its behalf, much less an extraordinary lifetime employment contract" [16].

The court emphasized:
- Prior limited authority to make recommendations does not imply authority to bind a principal contractually
- The evidence was legally insufficient to support a binding contract or apparent authority
- The fact that an agent had authority to do some things does not create apparent authority to do other things

**Application to Hayes:** The manager who made the verbal promise lacked actual authority to modify standard employment terms (per HR records). The existence of a formal exception process (proven by the written approvals given to other engineers) means that:
- Hayes was or should have been aware that written approvals were required
- The manager's verbal promise was made outside the formal process
- A reasonable employee in Hayes's position would understand that the verbal promise was not binding without written approval

### The Manager's Lack of Authority and the June 2024 Email

The manager's June 2024 email—sent after the May 15 detrimental acts and lacking any approval through the formal process—cannot create reasonable reliance. Under Texas agency law as established in *Gaines v. Kelly*, 235 S.W.3d 179, 182 (Tex. 2007):

> "Apparent authority is based on estoppel, arising either from a principal knowingly permitting an agent to hold [himself] out as having authority or by a principal's actions which lack such ordinary care as to clothe an agent with the indicia of authority" [17].

For apparent authority to exist, the principal (the company) must have communicated—directly or indirectly—that the manager had authority to modify remote work terms. Factors that negate apparent authority here include:
- The written offer letter itself (stating "hybrid schedule per company policy") contradicts the idea that the manager had authority to promise full remote work
- The formal exception process requiring written approvals shows the company's actual procedures
- The HR records confirming the manager lacked such authority
- The manager's retirement in June 2024, indicating no continuing representation

### The "Power of Position" Doctrine and Its Limitations

Some courts recognize that a manager, by virtue of their position, may have apparent authority to make certain promises. However, this doctrine has limitations:

- **The manager's authority presumptively extends only to matters within the ordinary scope of their position.** Hiring and managing employees are within a hiring manager's scope, but modifying standard company policies (like a remote work policy) is presumptively outside that scope
- **The merger clause puts the employee on notice that deviations require written authorization.** When the written offer letter states that it "supersedes all prior verbal representations," this informs the employee that the manager's verbal promises are not binding unless reduced to writing
- **The formal exception process is further evidence.** The company's practice of granting written approvals to other engineers demonstrates that written authorization was required for deviations from standard policy

### The Disparate Treatment Argument

The fact that two other engineers received written approvals could be used by Hayes to argue disparate treatment or unfairness. However, in the context of summary judgment on reasonable reliance, this fact cuts **against** Hayes. It proves that:
1. A mechanism existed to obtain the promised benefit (full remote work)
2. Hayes failed to use that mechanism
3. Other employees who used the mechanism received the benefit

A reasonable employee who wanted the security of a remote work arrangement would have followed the same process as the other engineers. Choosing to rely on an unapproved verbal promise instead is unreasonable as a matter of law.

## Issue 5: Remedy Limitation — Only Reliance Damages Are Available

### The Controlling Rule

Under Texas law, a claim for promissory estoppel is strictly limited to **reliance damages** (also called detrimental reliance damages or out-of-pocket losses). Texas does not allow enforcement of the promise itself (specific performance) or expectation damages (including lost future wages).

The controlling Texas Supreme Court authority is *Fretz Construction Co. v. Southern National Bank of Houston*, 626 S.W.2d 478, 483 (Tex. 1981): **"In a claim of promissory estoppel, only reliance damages are allowed"** [11].

### The Historical Foundation

*Wheeler v. White*, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965), the seminal Texas case adopting promissory estoppel, established this limitation:

> "The promisee is allowed to recover no more than reliance damages measured by the detriment sustained" [15].

The court further stated: "Where a promisee acts to his detriment in reasonable reliance upon an otherwise unenforceable promise, the promissory estoppel theory may be invoked, thereby supplying a remedy which will enable the injured party to be compensated for his foreseeable, definite and substantial reliance."

### Application to Hayes: What Is and Is Not Recoverable

**Recoverable Reliance Damages (if the claim survives summary judgment):**

| Category | Description | Likely Recoverable? |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| $40,000 home sale loss | Loss incurred from selling Seattle home at a discount | Yes—classic out-of-pocket reliance damage |
| Relocation expenses | Moving costs from Seattle to Austin | Yes—direct expenditure made in reliance |
| Lease-signing costs | Security deposits, broker fees, lease fees | Yes—out-of-pocket costs incurred in reliance |
| Moving/storage expenses | During transition period | Yes—if documented |
| Lost wages from prior job | Income given up by leaving Seattle employment | Potentially—as actual out-of-pocket loss, not expectation |

**NOT Recoverable:**

| Category | Description | Why Not Recoverable |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Specific performance of remote work | Court order requiring company to provide full remote work | Promissory estoppel does not create a contract; only monetary damages |
| Lost future wages from new job | Anticipated income from the promised arrangement | Promissory estoppel does not allow damages for anticipated profits |
| Benefit of the bargain | Value of the remote work arrangement itself | Only reliance damages, not expectation damages |
| Emotional distress | Damages for stress or inconvenience | Not available under promissory estoppel |
| Punitive damages | Damages to punish the employer | Not available under promissory estoppel |

### Key Precedent: Frost Crushed Stone

In *Frost Crushed Stone Company, Inc. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Inc.* (Tex. 1995), the Texas Supreme Court "upheld damages to Geer amounting to about $40,000, representing the cost to restore him to his original financial position, as promissory estoppel allows for recovery of such reliance damages but not anticipated profits" [14].

### Cannot Obtain Specific Performance

A Texas court **cannot** order specific performance of a remote work arrangement under a promissory estoppel theory. The reasons are:

1. **Promissory estoppel does not create a contract.** As stated in *Fretz Construction Co. v. Southern National Bank of Houston*, 600 S.W.2d 878, 881 (Tex. Civ. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1980): "promissory estoppel and a contract cannot exist simultaneously" and "promissory estoppel does not create a contract where none previously existed" [18].

2. **The remedy is monetary, not equitable.** Texas limits the remedy for promissory estoppel to monetary damages measured by reliance costs.

3. **Employment is at-will.** Even if a court could order specific performance (which it cannot), the at-will nature of Texas employment means the employer could terminate the arrangement at any time, making specific performance meaningless.

### Practical Implications for Hayes

Even if Hayes could establish the elements of promissory estoppel, the maximum recovery would be the out-of-pocket losses incurred in reliance on the promise. This would include:
- The $40,000 loss on the home sale
- Moving and relocation expenses
- Lease-signing costs
- Potentially lost wages from the prior job (as reliance damages, not expectation damages)

The remedy would **not** include:
- The right to continue working remotely
- Lost future wages from the new job
- Damages for emotional distress or frustration
- Attorney's fees (unless otherwise provided by contract or statute)

## Issue 6: Venue Sensitivity — The Split Among Texas Courts of Appeals

### The Three-Way Split

Texas appellate courts are divided on whether promissory estoppel applies in the employment context, particularly for pre-employment promises of at-will employment. The outcome of Hayes's case depends significantly on where it is filed.

| Case | Court | Holding | Key Language |
|------|-------|---------|-------------|
| *Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services*, 757 S.W.2d 48 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1988) | 1st Court (Houston) | **ALLOWS** promissory estoppel; employee quit prior job in reliance | "It is no answer that the parties' written contract was for an employment-at-will, where the employer foreseeably and intentionally induces the prospective employee to materially change his position" [4] |
| *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management*, 871 S.W.2d 929 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1994) | 14th Court (Houston) | **REJECTS** promissory estoppel; at-will promise cannot support reliance | "A promise to provide employment which is subject to termination at any time... does not provide a basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law" [5] |
| *Patterson v. Leal*, 942 S.W.2d 692 (Tex. App.—Austin [3rd Dist.] 1997) | 3rd Court (Austin) | **REJECTS** promissory estoppel; purely oral indefinite employment | "No claim exists under Texas law for promissory estoppel when the underlying evidence consists of purely oral representations as to employment for an indefinite period" [19] |
| *Lotito v. Knife River Corporation–South* (Tex. App.—Corpus Christi 2012) | 13th Court (Corpus Christi) | **REJECTS** promissory estoppel; reliance on oral promise of extended employment unreasonable as matter of law | "Promissory estoppel is an equitable exception used to avoid a statute of frauds defense... In the employment context, promissory estoppel is a shield, not a sword" [20] |

### Analysis by Venue

#### If Filed in Travis County (Austin) — 3rd Court of Appeals

Under *Patterson v. Leal*, Hayes's claim would almost certainly be dismissed on summary judgment. The Patterson court held that "no claim exists under Texas law for promissory estoppel when the underlying evidence consists of purely oral representations as to employment for an indefinite period" [19].

The court explicitly rejected the idea that it should create such a cause of action, stating: "We therefore cannot create a cause of action for promissory estoppel when the underlying evidence consists of purely oral representations as to employment for an indefinite period."

The court distinguished *Roberts* on the basis that "*Roberts* involved an employment agreement memorialized by a written contract, rendering it inapplicable here." In Hayes's case, there is a written contract (the offer letter), but the promise of full remote work is not memorialized in it—it is purely oral. This distinction could cut either way, but under the reasoning of *Patterson*, the absence of a written document confirming the specific promise (full remote) would likely doom the claim.

**Conclusion for Travis County:** Summary judgment for the employer is highly likely.

#### If Filed in Harris County (Houston) — 1st vs. 14th Court of Appeals

Harris County presents a unique problem because it is home to two intermediate appellate courts: the First Court of Appeals (Houston [1st Dist.]) and the Fourteenth Court of Appeals (Houston [14th Dist.]). These two courts have issued conflicting decisions:

- The **First Court** (in *Roberts*) allowed promissory estoppel to proceed where an employee quit a prior job in reliance on a promise of at-will employment, repudiated before the employee started work
- The **Fourteenth Court** (in *Collins*) held that a promise of at-will employment cannot support promissory estoppel as a matter of law

**How trial courts handle the conflict:** When there is a direct conflict between two Houston courts of appeals, the general rule in Texas is that a trial court in Harris County is not bound by either court's decision. The trial court may choose which line of authority to follow. Whichever party loses can appeal, and the case will be randomly assigned to either the First or Fourteenth Court.

- If assigned to the **First Court**: Hayes may prevail (under *Roberts*)
- If assigned to the **Fourteenth Court**: The employer likely prevails (under *Collins*)

This creates a "lottery" effect for litigants in Harris County. Neither *Roberts* nor *Collins* has been overruled by its respective court.

**Conclusion for Harris County:** Outcome depends on appellate assignment; if assigned to the Fourteenth Court, summary judgment for employer is likely; if assigned to the First Court, the claim may survive summary judgment on the contract bar issue (though the independent reasonable reliance analysis with the merger clause still presents challenges).

#### If Filed in Federal Diversity Court

A federal court sitting in diversity jurisdiction applies the substantive law of the forum state, including the state's common law as declared by its highest court (*Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins*, 304 U.S. 64 (1938)). When the state's highest court has not spoken on an issue, the federal court must predict how that court would rule. This is called an "Erie guess" or "Erie prediction" [21].

**The Erie Guess Methodology:** The Fifth Circuit has stated that when making an Erie guess, it must predict how the Texas Supreme Court would rule. The court considers "relevant state precedent, analogous decisions, considered dicta, ... and any other reliable data" (*Lindsay Mfg. Co. v. Hartford Accident & Indem. Co.*, 118 F.3d 1263 (8th Cir. 1997), cited with approval in the Fifth Circuit) [22].

**How a Federal Court Would Likely Rule:** A federal court conducting an Erie guess would likely predict that the Texas Supreme Court would follow the *Collins/Patterson* line of cases and reject promissory estoppel for pre-employment promises of at-will employment. The reasoning would be:

1. The Texas Supreme Court has strongly protected the at-will doctrine for over 135 years (*East Line & Red River Ry. v. Scott*, 10 S.W. 99 (Tex. 1888))
2. The Court has rejected implied duties in employment (*City of Midland v. O'Bryant*, 18 S.W.3d 209 (Tex. 2000))
3. In *Sawyer v. E.I. Du Pont De Nemours & Co.*, 430 S.W.3d 396, 400 n.16 (Tex. 2014), the Court listed several employment promissory estoppel cases in a footnote—including both *Roberts* and *Collins*—but declined to resolve the split. The Court held that at-will employees cannot bring fraud claims based on promises of continued employment. The reasoning logically extends to promissory estoppel [23]
4. *Roberts* is the outlier case; the majority of Texas appellate courts that have addressed the issue have rejected promissory estoppel in this context

**Conclusion for Federal Court:** Summary judgment for the employer is likely on the Erie guess.

### The Texas Supreme Court's Position

The Texas Supreme Court **has not definitively resolved the split** among the Courts of Appeals. In *Sawyer v. E.I. Du Pont De Nemours & Co.*, 430 S.W.3d 396, 400 n.16 (Tex. 2014), the Court listed several employment promissory estoppel cases in a footnote but did not endorse or reject any of them. The Court was aware of the split but declined to resolve it.

The Court's statement in *Sawyer* that "a representation dependent on continued at-will employment cannot be material because employment can terminate at any time" (430 S.W.3d at 399) was made in the context of a fraud claim, not a promissory estoppel claim. However, this reasoning logically extends to promissory estoppel, since the reasonable reliance element similarly requires that the promise have some substance.

As of the research date (May 27, 2026), no pending case before the Texas Supreme Court specifically involves pre-employment promissory estoppel and remote work promises.

### Statute of Frauds Considerations

Texas Business & Commerce Code § 26.01(b)(6) requires certain agreements to be in writing. This includes agreements "not to be performed within one year from the date of making the agreement" [24].

**Application to Hayes:** The promise of full remote work is not for a fixed duration. It is an arrangement that could theoretically be performed within one year (the company could change its policy, the employee could quit, etc.). Therefore, the Statute of Frauds likely does not bar this claim. However, if the promise is interpreted as an indefinite promise of remote work, the company could argue it falls within the statute.

**The Statute of Frauds and Promissory Estoppel:** Texas law on whether promissory estoppel can overcome the Statute of Frauds is nuanced:
- *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management* held: "Promissory estoppel operates to preclude a statute of frauds defense only where the promise is to sign a written agreement complying with the statute, or there must be substantial reliance upon a misrepresentation that the statute has been satisfied" [5].
- *Leach v. Conoco* (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1995) held: "promissory estoppel could not circumvent the Statute of Frauds absent a promise to sign a written agreement or substantial reliance that the statute was met" [25].

Since the Statute of Frauds likely does not apply to the indefinite remote work promise, this issue is secondary.

### Summary Judgment Likely for the Employer in Most Venues

Based on the research, Hayes's promissory estoppel claim faces substantial hurdles:

1. **Reasonable reliance is likely unreasonable as a matter of law.** The combination of the contradictory writing ("hybrid schedule per company policy"), the merger clause ("This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations"), and the timing (receipt of the writing before the detrimental acts) makes reliance on the verbal promise unreasonable under *Ellen v. F.H. Partners* and related cases.

2. **The contract bar doctrine may apply.** If the court treats the at-will offer letter as a "valid contract" for these purposes, the claim is barred because an express contract covers the same subject matter (*Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco*, *Clinton Growers*). Even if not, the independent reasonable reliance analysis defeats the claim.

3. **The timing is fatal.** Hayes received the contradictory writing before undertaking detrimental acts. The June confirmation email came too late to influence the May 15 decisions.

4. **Professional sophistication cuts against Hayes.** As a software engineer, Hayes is expected to understand that a written contract with a merger clause controls over verbal promises.

5. **The formal exception process shows Hayes should have known better.** Other engineers received written approvals through a formal process; Hayes's failure to use that process makes his reliance on an unapproved verbal promise unreasonable.

6. **Venue matters, but the trend is against Hayes.** Most Texas appellate courts that have addressed the issue (*Collins*, *Patterson*, *Lotito*) have rejected promissory estoppel in the employment context. Only the First Court of Appeals (*Roberts*) allows it. In federal diversity court, the Erie guess likely predicts the Texas Supreme Court would follow the majority view.

### The Most Likely Outcome

The most likely outcome is that the employer obtains summary judgment, either because:

1. **No claim exists under *Patterson v. Leal*** (if filed in Travis County or similar venue)
2. **Reliance was unreasonable as a matter of law** under *Ellen v. F.H. Partners* and related cases (applicable in any venue)
3. **The contract bar doctrine applies** under *Clinton Growers* and *Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco* (if the court treats the offer letter as a valid contract)
4. **The manager lacked authority to bind the company** under *Guerra v. Wal-Mart* (applicable in any venue)

The only venue where Hayes has a realistic chance of surviving summary judgment is the First Court of Appeals (Houston 1st Dist.) under *Roberts v. Geosource*, and even then, the contradictory writing and merger clause create independent reasonable reliance problems that *Roberts* does not directly address.

### What Hayes Can Recover If He Prevails

Even if Hayes could somehow establish the elements of promissory estoppel and survive summary judgment, the remedy is strictly limited to reliance damages:

- $40,000 loss on home sale
- Relocation and moving expenses
- Lease-signing costs
- Potentially lost wages from prior job (as reliance damages, not expectation)

The court **cannot** order the company to provide remote work. The maximum recovery is monetary damages to restore Hayes to his pre-reliance financial position.
