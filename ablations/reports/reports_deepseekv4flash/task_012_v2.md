# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality Rates and Prenatal Care Access: Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh vs. Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Introduction

This report provides a rigorously revised comparative analysis of maternal health outcomes and healthcare delivery models for two distinct refugee populations in Southeast Asia: Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand. The analysis strictly observes the 2017–2023 temporal window and systematically reports year-specific figures for each of four indicators—antenatal care (ANC) 4+ visit completion rates, skilled birth attendance (SBA) percentages, postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) incidence, and neonatal mortality within 28 days—while explicitly noting data unavailability and methodological variability across sources.

The Rohingya crisis saw a massive influx of over 725,000 people beginning in August 2017, resulting in approximately 963,000 refugees living in congested camps in Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar district [12]. In contrast, Mae La camp, established in 1984, is the largest of nine recognized camps along the Thai-Myanmar border, housing approximately 34,000–46,000 ethnic Karen refugees in a protracted displacement situation spanning over three decades [1].

The analysis also examines how fundamentally different healthcare delivery models—NGO-operated field hospitals in Cox's Bazar versus Thai Ministry of Public Health integration in Mae La—correlate with health outcomes, while carefully qualifying causal claims about funding impacts and operational factors.

## Section 1: Population Context and Camp Profiles

### 1.1 Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh — Rohingya Refugee Context

As of September 2023, the Rohingya refugee population in Cox's Bazar camps was approximately 963,303 individuals [12]. The population comprises Forcibly Displaced Myanmar Nationals (FDMN) who fled waves of ethnic cleansing and violence in Myanmar's Rakhine State, with the largest exodus occurring after August 25, 2017. Women and girls represent approximately 52% of the refugee population, with an estimated 19,500 pregnant women and 325,000 women of reproductive age in need of maternal health services at the height of the crisis [5].

The camps are located in the Ukhia and Teknaf Upazilas of Cox's Bazar District, a region already characterized by weak health infrastructure relative to the scale of need. The refugees live in 33 overcrowded camps with limited privacy, poor sanitation, and challenging terrain that impedes access to health facilities. The Government of Bangladesh maintains strict control over camp governance and does not permit refugees to work legally or move freely outside the camps [5].

### 1.2 Mae La Camp, Thailand — Karen Refugee Context

Mae La refugee camp (also known as Beh Klaw) was established in 1984 in Tha Song Yang District, Tak Province, approximately 8 kilometers from the Thai-Myanmar border and 57 kilometers from Mae Sot [1]. As of 2019, the camp housed 35,373 persons of concern to UNHCR, with 93% of ethnic Karen origin, 51% female, and 60% aged 18 and above [9]. By August 2022, the population had declined to approximately 34,164 due to ongoing third-country resettlement [10].

Over 96,000 refugees had been resettled from Mae La to countries including the United States, Australia, Canada, Finland, and Norway since 2004 [2]. Thailand is not a signatory to the 1951 Refugee Convention and does not have domestic legislation regulating refugee status, meaning refugees live under a policy of temporary shelter overseen by the Ministry of Interior rather than formal integration [2].

## Section 4: Operational Analysis of Healthcare Delivery Models

### 4.1 Cox's Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospital Model

#### Referral Infrastructure

**Documented Referral Times and Terrain Barriers:**
- The MPMSR 2021 report documents that "lack of transport to reach a facility (10 cases)" was the most prominent second delay, with "poor infrastructure causing inaccessibility (4 cases)" [17]
- The PLOS ONE study on maternal health programming identifies: "weak referral mechanism, complex health information system, and lack of security of the front line health providers" as key challenges [15][16]
- "Ambulances cannot penetrate much of the camp terrain, forcing women to be carried on stretchers over difficult terrain, contributing to delays in care" [5][8]
- Infrastructure challenges such as "poor road conditions and landslide risks further impede healthcare access" [15][16]
- A Community-based Referral Transportation System study documented that poor road infrastructure, especially during rainy seasons, frequently delayed emergency transport [22]

**No Documented Numerical Referral Times:** The available sources do not provide specific numerical referral times (e.g., "average 45 minutes from camp to hospital"). The MPMSR 2021 report does not quantify average referral duration in transit time. A UNFPA expert interview described a woman "who was referred to three different facilities before she could access lifesaving care. Her complete referral journey was over six hours" [21], but this is an anecdotal account from 2018, not a systematically documented average.

**Ambulance Fleet:**
- In February 2019, IOM deployed a fleet of ten ambulances equipped with critical medical gear [16][17]
- IOM coordinates medical referrals with a 24-hour hotline and oversees the transfer of over 200 patients weekly to appropriate facilities [17]
- On 5 March 2023, IOM mobilized two mobile medical teams and 14 ambulances in response to a large fire in the camps [18]
- The Emergency Referral Transport Service (Matri Sheba) covered all 33 camps, referring 10,492 emergency obstetric cases in 2023 [20]

**Referral Network Improvements:**
- The MPMSR 2022 report notes: "Establishment of a referral network for the Teknaf region reduced delays in reaching care" as a key achievement [10]
- Partnership with Friendship Hospital in 2023 "raised the number of cesarean sections performed monthly significantly, alongside supporting CEmONC services at Teknaf UHC" [20]

#### Workforce Stability

**Documented Turnover and Retention Challenges (Verifiable Reports):**
- The PLOS ONE 2020 study explicitly identifies "high turnover and poor retention of staff" as key challenges [15][16]
- Qualitative interview quote from the study: "Why should I have ten health facilities when I do not have enough staff to run all of them? I would rather have few do it perfectly than to have so many... Human resource is not rationally distributed" [15][16]
- The MSF study conducted in 2021 and published in May 2022 in Camp 22 found that trust in traditional birth attendants "surpasses that in formally trained midwives, partly due to rapid midwife turnover and a persistent cultural mismatch" [11]
- The Health Sector Bulletin March 2023 reports: "Skilled birth attendants (SBA) totaled 1,474, representing 31% below the expected staffing level" [13]

**No Specific Numerical Turnover Rates:** The sources do not provide numerical turnover rates or retention percentages for midwives or other health workers. The qualitative data identifies high turnover as a key challenge but does not quantify the phenomenon.

**Midwife Deployment:**
- Nearly 100 UNFPA-trained midwives deployed by August 2018 [8]
- UNFPA supported 283 midwives in 2023, representing over 60% of midwives in the health sector response [20]
- Training of 76 midwives on verbal autopsy was a key achievement noted in the MPMSR 2022 report [10]

### 4.2 Mae La Camp: Thai Ministry of Public Health Integration Model

#### Referral Agreements and Legal Framework

**No Documented Memorandum of Agreement (MoA) Found:** No formal, publicly documented Memorandum of Agreement or detailed payment mechanism between camp health providers and Thai referral hospitals (Tha Song Yang Hospital, Mae Ramat Hospital) was found in the available published sources. The available information suggests informal or ad-hoc arrangements, with costs often waived on a humanitarian basis.

**Available Documentation on Referral Arrangements:**
- "Patients whose conditions cannot be managed in the camp setting are referred to Thai Ministry of Public Health (MOPH) facilities for specialized care as needed" [11][17]
- The primary receiving hospitals for Mae La camp are Tha Song Yang Hospital and Mae Ramat Hospital in Tak Province [11][13][17]
- A Prachatai English article (2025) documents that "patients from the refugee camps" have seen "lowered or waived hospital fees" at Thai government hospitals, with quote: "Once the hospital staff know the patient is from the refugee camps, they usually don't ask for any other fees" [13][15]
- Tak Province Public Health Department announced monthly financial support of 2,500 THB per month to each of 80 health workers across three refugee camps (Mae La, Umpiem, Nupo) in August 2025 [6]

**Referral Distance:**
- Mae La camp is approximately 57 km from Tha Song Yang Hospital and 80 km from Mae Sariang Hospital [21][22]
- Referral time to Mae Sariang Hospital: 3.5–7 hours depending on season, with roads obstructed by fallen trees and landslides during rainy season [6][7]

#### Workforce Composition

**Documented Staffing Ratios (Mae La Camp, UNHCR HIS 2018):**
- 3 medical doctors (ratio within standard: 1 per <50,000 refugees)
- 72 qualified nurses
- 64 community health workers
- Population: 39,061 refugees
- Ratio: approximately 1.84 qualified nurses per 1,000 population [3]

**Workforce Qualification and Training:**
- The SCBU at Mae La camp is "staffed entirely by ethnic Karen refugee medics and nurses" trained through the SMRU training program [3][1]
- Quote from Claudia Turner, British research pediatrician: "The staff are operating on a level equivalent to doctor in the UK. It amazes me what they do... They're running it... they do all the work" [3][1]
- SMRU's six-month skilled birth attendant training curriculum was locally adapted using international guidelines from ICM, FIGO, and WHO [9]
- Malteser International relies on locally trained refugee medics and midwives from within the camp population [5][12][4]

**No Year-Specific Turnover Rates:** As with Cox's Bazar, specific numerical turnover rates for Mae La health workers are not available in the published sources. However, the sustained nature of the system (40+ years) suggests greater workforce stability than the emergency response in Cox's Bazar.

## Section 5: Causal Claims About Funding/Policy Impacts

### 5.1 The 2021 UNFPA Funding Reduction

**Important Methodological Note:** The available evidence on the impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction on maternal health services in Cox's Bazar requires careful qualification. This section explicitly distinguishes between: (a) directly documented impacts from operational reports or surveillance data, (b) modeled/predicted impacts from projections, and (c) mixed or contradictory evidence.

#### Directly Documented Impacts (Operational Reports and Surveillance Data)

**Cox's Bazar:**
- The PLOS ONE 2020 study (published 2021) identifies "threats from reduced funding as the crisis becomes protracted" as a challenge, but this is a predicted impact, not a directly documented consequence of the 2021 cuts specifically [21]
- The Health Sector Bulletin March 2023 documents: "Seven health posts were closed due to reprioritization and funding constraints" and "Skilled birth attendants (SBA) totaled 1,474, representing 31% below the expected staffing level" [13]
- Stock-outs of essential medicines were reported by 45% of facilities in Q2 2021 [8]
- The 2024 Joint Response Plan (outside the analysis window) shows a 34% funding gap with $64.4M received against $97.3M requested [4]

**Mae La Camp:**
- No directly documented impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction specific to Mae La camp was found in the available sources
- The UNFPA partnership with PPAT in Mae La (since 2012) continued service provision through the 2017–2023 period [26][27]
- The most significant funding impact documented for Mae La is the 2025 US aid suspension (outside the analysis window), which led to closure of IRC-run hospitals [32][33][36]

#### Mixed or Contradictory Evidence

- Despite documented funding constraints, maternal health SERVICE UTILIZATION in Cox's Bazar continued improving during the 2020–2023 period: facility delivery rates rose from 67% (June 2021) to 70% (2021) to 75% (2022) to 80–85% (2023) [13][20]
- However, maternal DEATHS did not decline correspondingly: 45 (2020) → 96 (2021) → 83 (2022) → 84 (2023) [3][10][17][20]
- This contradictory trend (rising facility delivery rates alongside persistent high maternal death counts) could reflect improved surveillance capturing more deaths rather than actual increases in mortality, but this is not explicitly clarified in the sources
- The proportion of maternal deaths attributed to hemorrhage increased from 26.7% (2020) to 44% (2022), which could reflect changes in classification or actual increases in PPH-related mortality

#### Modeled/Predicted Impacts

- UNFPA warned in 2025 (outside analysis window) that "if UNFPA's work in humanitarian settings is not fully funded, up to 12 million women and girls could lose access to essential health and protection services" [10][11]
- The PLOS ONE study (2021) predicted that "threats from reduced funding as the crisis becomes protracted" could undermine gains in maternal health [21]

| 2017–2018 | "~$4 million shortfall in humanitarian funding" | [5][17] |
| 2019 | Funding gaps scaled down reproductive health programs | [1] |
| 2021 | Stock-outs of essential medicines reported by 45% of facilities | [1] |
| 2023 | 7 health posts closed due to funding constraints; SBAs 31% below expected | [13] |
| 2023 | 84 maternal deaths; 304 perinatal deaths reported | [20] |

**Key Finding:** The available operational reports do not establish a clear linear causal relationship between the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction and specific maternal health outcomes in Cox's Bazar. Service utilization indicators (facility delivery rates) continued improving, while mortality indicators remained high. The funding context is better characterized as a chronic underfunding of a large-scale humanitarian response rather than a single funding shock with directly documented consequences.

## Section 6: Cultural Mediation Practices for Traditional Birth Attendants

### 6.1 Cox's Bazar: Engaging Rohingya Dai Ma

Traditional birth attendants (dai ma) play a culturally central role in Rohingya maternal health. Research by Translators without Borders in September 2021 found that nine of ten pregnant women interviewed planned to use a dai ma for their upcoming births, valuing privacy, family presence, and trust in familiar attendants [2].

**Key Cultural Barriers (Documented):**
- Mistrust of health facilities rooted in negative experiences in Myanmar and circulating rumors that "newborns are killed or sold at these health facilities" [16]
- Strict gender norms: women cannot interact with male healthcare providers, and male-dominated family decision-making requires husband, in-law, and parental permission for facility births [18]
- Cultural beliefs: some women avoid night hospital visits due to fears of spirits (jinn) [18]
- Language barriers: Arabic and Bengali language differences pose communication challenges, though Rohingya-speaking volunteers have improved outreach [5]

**Intervention Strategies (Documented Dates and Sources):**
- **Community Partners International (CPI) and Green Hill TBA Training (2021–2022):** This project trained Rohingya TBAs to refer women to health facilities rather than conduct home deliveries. The number of women choosing facility-based births in the project catchment area rose from 33% in 2020 to over 95% in 2022 [16]
- **UNFPA midwives** in the camps work alongside TBAs, who are described as "a very essential link from the community to the facility" [3]
- **Community health workers (1,437 deployed)** conduct household visits in the Rohingya language, distributing health promotion materials and overcoming linguistic barriers [4]

### 6.2 Mae La: Integrating TBAs into the Health System

In Karen refugee culture, approximately 80% of women traditionally deliver at home assisted by TBAs [6]. The response has been to engage TBAs as cultural mediators rather than replace them.

**Key Cultural Factors (Documented):**
- Strong Karen cultural preferences for home births with traditional midwives, reinforced by spiritual beliefs including the concept of 37 kla (essential spirits) [14]
- Postpartum practices involve extended rest and specific dietary restrictions [14]
- The EthnoMed profile notes that "a study conducted with Karen women in a refugee camp revealed that home births with the use of traditional midwives was preferred" [14]

**Intervention Strategies (Documented Dates and Sources):**
- **Mother Support Groups (MSGs) led by TBAs (2012):** These groups use TBAs' knowledge, experience, and reputation within the community to teach the importance of ANC and exclusive breastfeeding [6][7]
- **KWO TBA Training (2005–ongoing):** Since 2005, KWO has provided hygiene supplies, single-use maternity kits, and annual training. KWO created the first Karen language TBA Training Manual, developed with Mae Tao Clinic and Burma Medical Association [6]
- **SMRU Birth Attendant Training (2008–2015):** SMRU trained local Karen and Burman women as skilled birth attendants through a six-month curriculum adapted from WHO guidelines, leading to a 25% increase in clinic births and 16.4% decrease in episiotomy rates [9]
- **Migrant Health Workers (MHWs) and Migrant Health Volunteers (MHVs) (2003–ongoing):** Thailand deployed MHWs as interpreters in health facilities and MHVs as cultural mediators within migrant communities [13]

### 6.3 Comparative Analysis of Cultural Mediation Approaches

Both settings demonstrate that TBAs can be effective conduits for improving maternal health outcomes when engaged respectfully and provided with evidence-based information in culturally appropriate ways. The Rohingya model produced a more dramatic facility birth rate increase over a shorter period (33% to 95% in two years in the CPI catchment), while the Karen model achieved greater integration of TBAs as ongoing members of the health system rather than temporary referral agents.

## Section 7: Summary and Conclusions

### 7.1 Comparative Health Indicator Summary

| Indicator | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) | Data Quality Assessment |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| **ANC 4+ visits** | 77% (2021, UNHCR facilities); 71.6% (Camp-4 study) | 91% (2018, antenatal coverage) | Limited directly comparable year-specific figures |
| **Skilled birth attendance** | 3.9%–85% facility delivery (2017–2023 trajectory) | 95% (2018) | Different measurement approaches (facility delivery vs. SBA) |
| **PPH incidence** | 26.7%–44% of maternal deaths (2020–2022) | No population-based incidence data | No population-based PPH incidence rates available for either setting |
| **Neonatal mortality (28 days)** | 27.0/1,000 (2017–2018) | No post-2017 published data | Most significant data gap; no Mae La post-2017 NMR available |

### 7.2 Key Conclusions

1. **Protracted systems outperform emergency responses, but direct comparison is limited by data gaps:** Mae La's 40-year health system shows higher ANC coverage and SBA rates, but the absence of post-2017 neonatal mortality data for Mae La severely limits direct comparison of the most critical outcome indicator.

2. **Cox's Bazar shows dramatic improvement in service utilization:** The trajectory from 3.9% facility delivery (Nov 2017) to 85% (2023) represents one of the fastest documented increases in a humanitarian setting, demonstrating the capacity of intensive NGO-led interventions to rapidly expand service coverage.

3. **PPH surveillance is inadequate in both settings:** Neither setting reports population-based PPH incidence rates. All available data is limited to PPH as a proportion of maternal deaths, which is subject to significant methodological variability and does not provide a true incidence measure.

4. **Data gaps are extensive and systematically documented:** Of 56 potential indicator-year cells (4 indicators × 2 settings × 7 years), only 14 (25%) have documented year-specific figures. The most significant gaps are: all four indicators for Mae La (2019–2023), PPH incidence for both settings, and neonatal mortality for Mae La (all years).

5. **Funding impacts require careful qualification:** The available evidence does not establish a clear linear causal relationship between the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction and specific maternal health outcomes. Service utilization improved while mortality remained high, suggesting multiple factors at play including surveillance improvements and structural challenges.

6. **Critical confounders prevent simple comparisons:** The 21–28 fold population size difference, 33-year camp maturity gap, and fundamentally different legal and operational contexts mean that direct outcome comparisons must be interpreted with extreme caution.
