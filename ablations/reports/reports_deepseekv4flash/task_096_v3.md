# Revised Comparative Analysis of Land Reform Outcomes in Zimbabwe, South Africa, and Namibia

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive report provides a deepened, temporally anchored comparative analysis of land reform outcomes across Zimbabwe (2000–present), South Africa (post-1994), and Namibia (1990–present). Drawing on official statistical agencies, international datasets, parliamentary reports, and peer-reviewed academic research, the analysis examines eight dimensions: quantitative metrics with time-series data, sectoral causal mechanisms, source scrutiny, legal frameworks, wealth distribution, food security, political violence, and cross-cutting comparative assessment.

The three countries represent a spectrum of land reform approaches with markedly different outcomes. Zimbabwe's Fast Track Land Reform Programme (FTLRP, 2000–present) achieved the most dramatic redistribution—approximately 13 million hectares transferred to over 200,000 families—but at enormous human and economic cost, with agricultural GDP declining from approximately 20% of GDP pre-reform to 8.69% by 2024 [1][2][3]. Tobacco has recovered spectacularly (exceeding 300 million kg in 2025, valued at US$1.36 billion) driven by Chinese contract farming, while maize and wheat remain depressed due to the collapse of state marketing boards and lack of equivalent private-sector financing [4][5][6]. South Africa's market-based approach (1994–present) has maintained a productive commercial agricultural sector—with the 2024–25 maize crop of 14.66 million tonnes and record agricultural exports of US$15.1 billion in 2025—but has redistributed only 10–14% of agricultural land against a 30% target, leaving the most unequal land ownership structure in the region [7][8][9]. Namibia's willing-seller-willing-buyer model has been the slowest, transferring approximately 23% of commercial farmland since independence in 1990, constrained by constitutional property protections, the dual communal/commercial tenure system, and the veterinary cordon fence (Red Line) that structurally disadvantages northern communal farmers [10][11][12].

Key findings include: (1) contract farming systems that provide credit, inputs, and guaranteed markets (tobacco in Zimbabwe) can drive post-reform recovery, while state-dependent systems (maize/wheat) falter; (2) post-settlement support is the single most important determinant of beneficiary success across all three countries; (3) constitutional protections for property rights, when coupled with robust judicial oversight, slow reform but prevent the catastrophic economic collapse seen in Zimbabwe; (4) elite capture is a persistent problem across all three countries, though its extent varies significantly; and (5) gender inequality in land access remains deeply entrenched despite legal provisions for equality.

## 1. Temporally Anchored Quantitative Metrics

### 1.1 Zimbabwe: Land Transfer and Beneficiary Numbers

The Fast Track Land Reform Programme (FTLRP), launched in 2000, represents the most radical land redistribution in Southern Africa. By 2010, over 8.3 million hectares of farmland had been redistributed to more than 169,000 black Zimbabwean families [1]. By 2014, the FTLRP had resettled approximately 180,000 smallholder families and 45,000 medium-to-large-scale commercially oriented farmers onto land previously owned by about 6,000 white farmers [2]. The programme allocated over 4,500 farms covering 7.6 million hectares—representing approximately 20% of Zimbabwe's total land area [3].

**A1 and A2 Farm Models:**

- **A1 model (1999–present)**: Targeted landless peasants with small-to-medium-sized plots for subsistence and limited market production. By 2010, approximately 145,000 A1 families had been resettled. A1 permits are offered in perpetuity but are non-transferable without state approval [13][14].
- **A2 model (2000–present)**: Aimed at empowering black commercial farmers with larger plots on a 99-year leasehold basis. Approximately 22,000 A2 farmers had been allocated land by 2010 [1][3]. These leases cannot be sold, mortgaged, or transferred without state approval [15].
- Total beneficiary families settled: over 200,000 across all phases [3]. An interdisciplinary study by IDS/PLAAS tracks 2,500 households over 25 years across four districts: Mazowe, Gutu, Masvingo, and Matobo [16].

**2025/2026 Developments:**

In April 2025, Zimbabwe made its first compensation payments to white farmers displaced during the FTLRP. A 10-year, US$3.5 billion compensation agreement was finalized in 2020 but delayed multiple times, with first installments paid in March 2025 [17]. On March 24, 2025, the government disbursed US$3.1 million in cash to the first batch of 378 farms, representing 1% of the total US$311 million claim value for the first compensation batch. The remainder is paid through US Dollar-denominated Treasury bonds carrying a 2% coupon, maturing between 2 to 10 years [18].

In May 2026, the Zimbabwean government announced plans to return 67 foreign-owned farms seized during the FTLRP, with $146 million in compensation to be paid to foreign owners. Additionally, 840 black-owned farms and 400 white-owned farms are under consideration for return or reallocation as part of a broader land rationalization plan [19].

**The 30% Target:**

The original 1994 target was to transfer 30% (~25 million hectares) of white-owned farmland to non-white farmers. Progress has been contested:

- By 2016, only 11% had been redistributed according to Wikipedia synthesis [36].
- A 2024 Frontiers review found only 10% had been redistributed [35].
- The MDPI Land Journal (2024) reports less than 14% of agricultural land transferred [37].
- However, President Ramaphosa in December 2025 claimed ~25% when including all forms of transfer—government redistribution, restitution, private acquisition, PLAS, and state acquisitions—with a revised target of 30% by 2030 [8].

**Restitution Claims:**

Since 1995 through 2023, over 83,000 land claims have been settled, benefiting approximately 2.3 million people. A total of 94% of "old-order" claims have been successfully resolved, with R25 billion spent on the purchase and transfer of 3.9 million hectares, and an additional R22.5 billion paid in financial compensation to those choosing monetary settlements [38].

As of December 2023, over 83,056 claims have been settled, benefiting 460,952 households. During the 2024/25 financial year, the Commission on Restitution of Land Rights settled 288 land claims against a target of 319 (90% achievement). Outstanding claims will require approximately R65 billion to settle [39].

*Note: These figures cover only land owned by individuals (39% of private land). Trusts hold 31%, companies 25% [40].*

The AgriSA Land Audit (2017) using a transaction-based methodology found that Previously Disadvantaged Individuals (PDIs) and Government increased ownership from 14.9% in 1994 to 26.7% in 2016 by hectares, and 29.1% by value. When considering land potential (fertility), the PDI+Government share reaches 46.5% in provinces like KwaZulu-Natal, Limpopo, and Eastern Cape [41].

### 1.5 South Africa: Agricultural Production

South Africa's agricultural sector has remained productive throughout the reform period:

- **2024–25 maize harvest**: Estimated at 14.66 million tonnes, up 14% year-on-year [42].
- **2025 agricultural GDP growth**: 17.4% year-on-year increase in gross value added, rebounding from an 8.4% contraction in 2024, driven by favorable rainfall conditions [43].
- **2025 agricultural exports**: Record US$15.1 billion, up 10% from 2024, despite significant trade policy shifts. Major products: table grapes, maize, berries, wine, citrus, apples and pears, sugar, nuts, fruit juices, wool [9].
- **NAMC 2024 data**: Agricultural exports reached $13.7 billion (R251 billion), a 3.6% increase from 2023. Major export products: grapes ($818.8 million), maize ($814.7 million), oranges ($774 million). 44% destined for Africa, 29% for Europe, 21% for Asia [44].
- **Employment**: About 956,000 people working in primary agriculture in late 2025/early 2026, adding 14,000 to 30,000 jobs [45].
- **Agriculture sector value**: 2.6–3% of GDP, with the whole value chain making up more than 14% of GDP [46].

### 1.6 South Africa: Land Reform Budget

South Africa has spent approximately R50–R60 billion on land reform since 1994 [47][48][49]. The 2024/25 budget for the Department of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development allocates approximately R16.7 billion, increasing to R18.1 billion by 2026/27, with about 47.7% of expenditure dedicated to transfers and subsidies. Over the medium term, the department plans to settle 1,234 land claims at a projected cost of R11 billion and support 120,000 subsistence and smallholder producers [50].

**2025 Situation (July–September):**
- Approximately 456,000 people (15% of analysed population) experiencing high levels of acute food insecurity (IPC Phase 3 or above), down from 1.15 million (38%) in same period 2024—a dramatic improvement due to above-average rainfall, increased crop production (75% rise in communal areas), and government interventions [69].

**Lean Season Projection (October 2025 – March 2026):**
- Worsening expected: approximately 612,000 people (20%) projected to be in IPC Phase 3 due to end of nationwide drought relief (August 2025), reduced labor opportunities, high national unemployment at 36.9%, and poor livestock conditions in drought-prone areas [69].

### 1.10 Second National Land Conference (2018) – Implementation Status

The Second National Land Conference was held October 1–5, 2018. Key resolutions included abolition of the Willing Seller-Willing Buyer principle, expropriation with just compensation for foreign-owned and underutilized agricultural land, gradual removal of the Red Line, and improved tenure security to allow bankability of allotments [71].

- Only 14% of the 176 resolutions have been fully implemented, according to Namibia's Land Minister Inge Zaamwani [57].
- Only 29 resolutions had been implemented as of February 2022 [72].
- A new comprehensive Land Bill was introduced in 2025 with objectives including banning foreign ownership of land and improving land access for women, youth, and marginalized groups [57].

## 2. Sectoral Causal Mechanism Analysis

### 2.1 Zimbabwe: Why Tobacco Recovered While Maize and Wheat Remain Depressed

The divergent trajectories of Zimbabwe's agricultural sectors under land reform provide crucial insights into the mechanisms driving post-reform outcomes.

**Tobacco Recovery Factors:**

1. **Contract Farming System**: The tobacco sector benefited from a well-developed contract farming system that provided credit, inputs, extension services, and guaranteed markets. By 2016, approximately 80% of Zimbabwe's tobacco was grown under contract; by 2018, this reached 86% [73].
2. **Tian Ze/CNTC Investment**: The Chinese state-owned company Tian Ze (wholly owned by China National Tobacco Company—CNTC) entered Zimbabwe's tobacco sector under the contract farming scheme. Tian Ze provides comprehensive support including seeds, fertilizers, chemicals, labor funds, and capital equipment—recouped through crop sales in the same season. The company invested approximately US$40 million in input support during the 2013/14 season [74].
3. **Market Structure**: Tobacco has a well-organized auction floor system, private grading, and established export channels. China remains the major buyer, purchasing over half of Zimbabwe's tobacco exports by value. In 2025, China purchased 89.1 million kg valued at US$630.7 million [5][25].
4. **Legislative Framework**: The 2004 contract farming legislation obligated merchants to finance at least 8% of farmers from newly resettled areas, creating an institutional framework for private sector-led agricultural finance [74].
5. **Productivity Gains**: Contract farming boosted productivity by 39% among participating farmers [75].
6. **Tobacco Farmer Classification**: The tobacco boom under contract farming has resulted in distinct farmer classes: Accumulators, Aspiring Accumulators, Peasant Producers, and Diversifiers/Strugglers, each with varying degrees of success [76].

**Maize and Wheat Failure Factors:**

1. **Collapse of State Marketing Boards**: The Grain Marketing Board (GMB), which was the sole buyer and distributor of maize and wheat, suffered from mismanagement, lack of financing, and corruption. During the 2007–2008 hyperinflation period, the GMB effectively ceased functioning.
2. **No Equivalent Private-Sector Financing**: Unlike tobacco, maize and wheat lack a well-developed private-sector contract farming system. The state-provided input distribution systems (Command Agriculture, Operation Pfumvudza) have been plagued by delays, corruption, and inefficiencies.
3. **Rain-Fed Dependency**: Maize is predominantly rain-fed, making it highly vulnerable to droughts and climate variability. The 2024 El Niño drought cut maize production to 635,000 MT—the lowest in decades [22][23].
4. **Market Access Issues**: The GMB's role is now limited to maintaining a Strategic Grain Reserve of 500,000 MT. The absence of a guaranteed market at viable prices discourages private investment in the sector.
5. **Credit Availability Differences**: Tobacco farmers access private contract finance (inputs, labor, capital equipment) while maize/wheat farmers rely on state schemes that have been unreliable. A 2015 study found that Tian Ze-contracted farmers received comprehensive input support while non-contracted maize farmers had virtually no access to credit [74].

### 2.2 South Africa: Why Large-Scale Commercial Agriculture Remains Productive While Land Reform Beneficiaries Struggle

South Africa's agricultural sector exhibits a stark dualism between the productive large-scale commercial sector and struggling land reform beneficiaries.

**Commercial Agriculture Success Factors:**

1. **Secure Tenure**: Commercial farmers hold freehold title—the strongest form of tenure security—enabling long-term investment, access to bank financing, and collateral for credit.
2. **Integrated Value Chains**: Large-scale commercial farms are integrated into established agribusiness value chains with access to mechanization, irrigation, crop insurance, and export markets.
3. **Technical Capacity**: Commercial farmers have access to extension services, agricultural research, and skilled labor.
4. **Scale Economies**: Farms average 1,000+ hectares for crop production, enabling mechanization and cost efficiency.
5. **R&D Investment**: South African commercial agriculture benefited significantly from R&D and extension services, achieving yields of 5.6 tonnes per hectare for maize—more than four times Zimbabwe's national average [28][46].

**Land Reform Beneficiary Failures:**

1. **Inadequate Post-Settlement Support**: A PLAAS study covering 62 land reform projects across five provinces found that only 18% of redistributed farms were allocated to farm workers, only 19% to women, with the majority benefiting male elites—well-off, urban-based business people and politically-connected individuals [77].
2. **Collapse of the Land Bank**: The Land Bank's financial difficulties further starved Black farmers of crucial funding [78].
3. **Communal Property Association Failures**: Group-based ownership through Communal Property Associations (CPAs) has been plagued by governance failures, infighting, and mismanagement. By 2024, there were 1,742 registered CPAs nationally, with 75–82% non-compliant with reporting and governance requirements [79].
4. **Tenure Insecurity**: Beneficiaries on redistributed farms often lack freehold title, instead holding leasehold arrangements through PLAS, which limits investment incentives and access to credit.
5. **Under-Capitalization**: A study of 761 smallholder farmers across Eastern Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and Limpopo found that only 12% own tractors, and the majority feel constrained farming on communal lands. 61% expressed willingness to relocate to commercial farms, desiring an average of 95 hectares [80].
6. **High Input Costs**: A study found that high input costs were the biggest cause of farm failures among CPAs and state-owned farms in Mpumalanga [81].
7. **Budget Inefficiency**: Despite spending approximately R50–R60 billion on land reform since 1994, a 2016 Financial and Fiscal Commission report found that crop production on land reform farms decreased by 79% and job losses averaged 84% [47][48].

### 2.3 Namibia: The Red Line and Dual Tenure System

Namibia's agricultural sector is shaped by the Red Line (Veterinary Cordon Fence—VCF) and the dual communal/commercial tenure system, which create structurally different outcomes for livestock versus crop sectors.

**The Red Line: Origins and Impact**

The Red Line was established in 1897 during German colonial rule as a disease control measure to protect livestock from rinderpest, foot-and-mouth disease (FMD), and lung sickness [64]. Under South Africa's apartheid administration, it acted as a physical barrier restricting the movement of black Namibians. The physical fence was constructed in the early 1960s [82].

**Current Impact:**

- **South of the Red Line**: Commercially farmed lands, predominantly white-owned, with access to FMD-free zone certification (recognized since 1997) enabling export to premium markets (EU, US, China, UK, Norway) [66].
- **North of the Red Line**: Communal farmland, mostly black-operated, where livestock cannot be exported overseas and face quarantines even for interregional trade [82].

**Mechanisms of Differential Outcomes:**

1. **Beef Sector vs. Crop Sector**:
   - **Beef (commercial south)**: Benefits from full property rights under freehold title enabling access to credit for fencing, veterinary services, and infrastructure. Cattle can enter the FMD-free zone, accessing premium export markets. Namibia's beef exports to the EU alone were valued at N$3.4 billion in 2017/18 [63][64].
   - **Beef (communal north)**: Communal farmers north of the Red Line cannot access export markets. During FMD outbreaks (e.g., 2015 outbreak which took nearly a year and US$13 million to eradicate), the entire northern sector faces market closures [66]. The Mayor of Windhoek has challenged the fence's existence, arguing it violates Articles 8 and 10 of the Namibian Constitution (dignity and equality) [64].

2. **Tenure Security and Investment**:
   - **Commercial freehold land**: Full bundle of property rights—sale, lease, mortgage, bequest. Enables access to agribusiness value chains, credit markets, and bank financing. The AALS provides subsidised loans for commercial farm acquisition.
   - **Communal land**: State-owned, administered by customary authorities under the Communal Land Reform Act 2002. Customary rights offer limited investment incentives, no freehold title, and limited collateral value. A Bank of Namibia study found that the inability to use communal land as collateral significantly constrains agricultural investment [83].
   - **Leasehold limitations**: Commercial financial institutions do not accept registered leasehold over state land as collateral, meaning such leases cannot be traded. Residential leaseholds have been "widely misunderstood" with few actually registered or used as collateral [84].

3. **Post-Settlement Support**:
   - Resettled farmers on acquired commercial farms face challenges similar to South Africa: the LAC 2007 report found that "resettled people mostly continue subsistence farming" and that "many resettled people were just idling on formerly thriving commercial farms" while others "illegally sub-lease their portion" [85].

**The Red Line and Resettlement Paradox**: The LAC 2007 report identified a stark social cost: "Any 'average' resettlement process will move five 'disadvantaged' families to a farm at the same time that it removes six farmworkers and their families to homelessness and poverty" [85]. This highlights how land reform without adequate support can worsen the situation for the most vulnerable.

## 3. Source Scrutiny and Credibility Assessment

### 3.1 Zimbabwe: Contested Production Figures During Hyperinflation (2007–2008)

During Zimbabwe's hyperinflation period (2007–2008, peaking at an estimated 89.7 sextillion percent in November 2008), data reliability varied significantly across sources [86][87].

**Source Reliability Hierarchy:**

1. **FAO FAOSTAT and GIEWS**: Generally considered most reliable for cross-country comparability, applying standardized methodologies. However, during the hyperinflation period, FAO data relied on approximations and should be treated with caution. FAO GIEWS Country Briefs provide real-time monitoring [20].
2. **USDA FAS**: Foreign Agricultural Service reports are based on attaché reports from the field and satellite data. These are more independent than ZimStat but may use modeling when ground-level data collection is impossible. USDA data shows significant year-to-year volatility [22][23].
3. **ZimStat**: Faces credibility challenges due to political interference during the Mugabe era. Data collection during the hyperinflation period was severely disrupted. Figures from this period are often contested by independent researchers and international organizations.
4. **Commercial/Trade Sources**: IndexMundi, World Grain, and trade publications provide context but do not generate primary data.

**Specific Disputed Figures:**

| Metric | Source A | Source B | Discrepancy |
|--------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Beneficiary numbers | 169,000 (Source 2010) | 240,000 families (Moyo 2013) | Different phases and counting methodologies |
| Elite capture | 5% farms (Scoones, Masvingo) | 78% high-value A2 farms (Zhang, national) | Not contradictory—Zhang focuses on high-value A2, Scoones on all farms |
| White farmers displaced | ~6,000 | ~4,500 | Different definitions of "farmer" and time periods |

**Verification Recommendation**: Cross-reference ZimStat data with FAO FAOSTAT and USDA FAS for any specific year. During 2007–2008 hyperinflation, commercial/trade estimates may be more reliable than official government statistics. Scoones et al.'s long-term panel data (2,500 households across 4 districts) provides a strong empirical base for land reform outcomes [16].

**Conclusion**: The 2017 Government Land Audit (individuals only) gives the highest white ownership percentage. The AgriSA transaction-based audit (all ownership types) gives the most comprehensive picture. Both are official, credible sources with transparent methodologies—they answer different questions.

## 4. Legal Framework-Policy-Outcome Linkages

### 4.1 Zimbabwe: Constitutional Amendments and Judicial Subversion

Zimbabwe's legal framework for land reform involved systematic dismantling of property protections, with direct causal links to on-the-ground outcomes.

**Constitution of Zimbabwe Amendment (No. 16), 2000:**
- Modified Section 16 and inserted Section 16A, which declared that if the former colonial power does not pay compensation, the Government of Zimbabwe has no obligation to do so [93].
- Factors for assessing compensation included: history of ownership, price paid, improvements, current use, state investments, and financial constraints—effectively reducing compensation to near zero.

**Constitutional Amendment 16B (Later Section 16B of 2013 Constitution):**
- After acquisition, previous title and ownership is extinguished by operation of law and vests in the State [94].
- No compensation is payable for land—only for improvements effected before acquisition.
- Land identified for resettlement on or before July 8, 2005, and itemized in Schedule 7, is protected from compensation claims [94].

**Section 72 of the 2013 Constitution:**
- Grants the State authority to compulsorily acquire agricultural land, often without compensation for the land itself [95].
- Section 72(2) allows the Minister to acquire land on behalf of the State for public purposes.

**Outcome Linkages:**
- **Legal subversion → No judicial oversight**: The government systematically undermined judicial independence. The Supreme Court's ruling that land invasions were illegal was ignored. Chief Justice Gubbay was forced into early retirement (2001). Courts were explicitly ousted from jurisdiction over land matters [93].
- **No compensation → No rule of law**: Zimbabwe withdrew from the SADC Tribunal after it ruled in Mike Campbell (Pvt) Ltd v Republic of Zimbabwe (2008) that the expropriations were illegal based on racial discrimination [96].
- **Constitutional entrenchment → International isolation**: The 2013 Constitution's land provisions effectively barred legal challenges, but also deterred foreign investment and triggered Western sanctions.

**Post-Mugabe Shift:**
- Since 2018, compulsory acquisition without compensation was formally discontinued.
- The Global Compensation Deed (2020) valued at US$3.5 billion aims to compensate dispossessed farmers.
- In March–April 2025, first compensation payments were made—a historic shift toward rule-of-law-based resolution [17][18].

### 4.2 South Africa: Constitutional Balance and the Expropriation Act

South Africa's legal framework has evolved from market-based to more interventionist approaches, with judicial oversight shaping outcomes.

**Section 25 of the Constitution (1996):**
- Manages a political tension "to balance the rights and interests of the (overwhelmingly white) haves against the rights and interests of the (overwhelmingly black) have-nots" [97].
- Prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property.
- Authorizes expropriation for public interest—including land reform—subject to just and equitable compensation.
- Courts have interpreted deprivation broadly and expropriation narrowly, allowing transformative regulatory measures without compensation [97].

**Expropriation Act No. 13 of 2024:**
- Signed into law on January 23, 2025 [98].
- Repeals the 1975 Expropriation Act.
- Introduces "just and equitable" compensation rather than strictly market value.
- Allows expropriation without compensation (EWC) specifically when land is unused, abandoned, or held for speculative purposes.
- Requires judicial oversight to prevent arbitrary expropriations.

**SAHRC Position (June 2025):** The Commission clarifies that the Act:
- "Merely delineates the procedures and conditions under which the State may acquire privately owned land"
- Does not serve as a substantive mechanism for land redistribution
- Section 25(5) places a positive duty on the State to "take reasonable legislative and other measures" to enable equitable land access—which has not been fulfilled nearly 30 years after enactment [99].

**Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) 62 of 1997:**
- Regulates eviction of vulnerable occupiers, protecting farm dwellers' rights.
- The Land Claims Court has mandated a two-stage process for evictions: (1) lawful termination of right of residence with proper notice, followed by (2) a separate eviction notice after a two-month period [100].
- The ESTA Amendment Act 2018 (effective 1 April 2024) formally establishes a Land Rights Management Board and land rights management committees tasked with identifying, monitoring and settling land rights disputes [101].

**CPA and Communal Tenure Issues:**
- By 2024, there are 1,742 registered CPAs nationally, with 75–82% non-compliant with reporting and governance requirements [79].
- The Ingonyama Trust, controlling about 2.8 million hectares of land in KwaZulu-Natal, has faced successful legal challenges against its conversion of customary land holdings into common law leaseholds [102].

### 4.3 Namibia: Entrenched Property Rights and Slow Reform

Namibia's legal framework remains the most protective of existing property rights, with direct causal links to the slow pace of reform.

**Article 16 of the Constitution:**
- Entitles all persons to acquire, own, and dispose of property.
- The State may expropriate property in the public interest subject to just compensation.
- Article 16 is an entrenched provision that can only be derogated from under a state of emergency [103].
- The 1982 Constitutional Principles (pre-independence) protected private property rights and limited expropriation without compensation.

**Agricultural (Commercial) Land Reform Act 1995:**
- Establishes the Land Reform Advisory Commission and Land Acquisition and Development Fund [104].
- Confers on the State a preferent right to purchase agricultural land whenever an owner intends to alienate it.
- Regulates compulsory acquisition (expropriation) for under-utilized, excessive, or foreign-owned land.
- Compensation: "The amount of compensation to be paid... shall be determined with due regard to market value, financial losses, and may include an additional amount up to 10 per cent but not more than N$10,000" [104].

**Communal Land Reform Act 2002 (Act No. 5 of 2002):**
- Establishes Communal Land Boards to administer communal land.
- Provides for registration of customary land rights and leasehold rights [10].
- Formally grants women equal rights when applying for communal land—a progressive provision that has not been fully implemented due to customary laws and male-dominated traditional leadership [105].

**Second National Land Conference (2018) Resolutions:**
- Abolish the Willing Seller-Willing Buyer Principle [71].
- Agricultural land (foreign owned) should be expropriated with just compensation.
- Under-utilised commercial farms owned by Namibians should be subjected to expropriation.
- "One Namibian-One Farm" principle agreed upon.
- 70:30 land allocation favoring dispossessed communities and liberation veterans.

**Implementation Gap:**
- As of 2025, only 14% of the 176 resolutions have been fully implemented [57].
- The Land Bill, planned to ban foreign land ownership and enhance land access for women, youth, and corridor farmers, has been delayed [57].

**Foreign Ownership:**
Foreign nationals own approximately 1.2 million hectares (3%) of commercial farms, predominantly Germans (53%), South Africans (29%), and Americans (7%). The proposed Land Bill aims to prohibit foreign nationals from owning land or being allocated customary land rights [53].

## 7. Political Violence with Causal Attribution

### 7.1 Zimbabwe: Systematic State-Orchestrated Violence

Zimbabwe's land reform was accompanied by severe, systematic political violence that was directly caused by state policy and implementation.

**Human Rights Violations (2000–2005):**

A comprehensive survey conducted by the Zimbabwe Human Rights NGO Forum and Justice for Agriculture Trust (2007) documented:
- **53,022 people**—farmers, farm workers, and their families—identified as having experienced at least one human rights violation during the seizures from 2000 to 2005.
- When extrapolated nationwide, this could exceed **1 million victims** [148].
- An estimated **10,000 farm workers and their families died** due to displacement-related hardships, with HIV/AIDS exacerbated by loss of healthcare access, starvation, and violence [148][149].
- Financial losses: US$368 million identified in the survey sample; potentially US$8.4 billion when scaled to the entire sector [148].
- Only 6% of former farmers remain on their farms; 75% identify as Zimbabwean citizens [148].

**Perpetrators:**
- War veterans and ZANU-PF members were the largest number of perpetrators.
- Followed by the police and government officials.
- The evidence is "strongly suggestive of a systematic campaign... A plausible case can be made for crimes against humanity being committed during these displacements" [148].

**Farm Worker Displacement:**
- Out of approximately 320,000 farm worker households, about two-thirds were affected by the reform in terms of job losses and diminished access to shelter and basic social services [149].
- Coping strategies included piece jobs, informal trade, gold panning, and relocation to informal settlements characterized by poor living conditions [149].
- Vulnerable subgroups such as migrant workers, women, elderly, children, and youth faced amplified hardships, compounded by high HIV/AIDS prevalence (estimated around 25% among farm workers), malnutrition, and orphanhood [149].

**Operation Murambatsvina (2005):**
- A government-led crackdown targeting illegal urban structures.
- Resulted in the displacement of approximately 700,000 people across the country [150].
- Characterized by extreme violence and brutality—providing little if any notice of impending evictions [151].
- UN Special Envoy Anna Tibaijuka (2005): "The operation... was carried out in an indiscriminate and brutal manner" [151].

**Causal Attribution:**
- The violence was directly caused by land reform policy design and implementation. The invasions were state-orchestrated, coordinated by ZANU-PF, and carried out with impunity. The government used land reform as a political tool to consolidate power following the 2000 constitutional referendum defeat.
- President Mugabe stated: "The courts can do whatever they want, but no judicial decision will stand in our way" [148].
- The SADC Tribunal ruling in Mike Campbell (Pvt) Ltd v Republic of Zimbabwe (2008) confirmed the illegal nature of the expropriations [96].

South Africa has experienced persistent farm attacks, but evidence suggests predominantly criminal rather than politically motivated violence.

### 7.3 Namibia: Peaceful Mobilization with Limited Violence

Namibia has experienced the least political violence related to land reform among the three countries, with mobilization occurring primarily through legal and political channels.

**Affirmative Repositioning Movement (AR, 2014):**

- SWAPO's gradual approach prioritizes stability to avoid the economic disruptions seen in Zimbabwe [11].
- The 2018 Land Conference provided a peaceful, negotiated forum for addressing land grievances.

## 8. Distributional Outcomes: Who Wins, Who Loses, and Why

### 8.1 Zimbabwe: Beneficiaries and Excluded Groups

**Primary Beneficiaries (Winners):**

1. **ZANU-PF Loyalists and Political Elites**: The FTLRP was fundamentally politically driven. Allocation prioritized political loyalty over productive capacity or equity. A "new, well-connected black elite" of about 2,200 people controls nearly 40% of the 14 million hectares seized from white farmers, with farms ranging from 250 to 4,000 hectares in the most fertile farming regions [121].

2. **War Veterans**: War veterans were the key political constituency driving the 2000 invasions of white-owned farms. They were prioritized in land allocations.

3. **Some Ordinary Smallholder Farmers**: The Scoones et al. study found that the majority of new settlers were ordinary people—nearly half from nearby communal areas, 18% from urban areas [16]. Over half of all 400 sample households were either "stepping up" (accumulating assets and regularly producing crops for sale) or "stepping out" (successfully diversifying off-farm) [16].

**Excluded or Negatively Impacted Groups (Losers):**

1. **White Commercial Farmers**: Approximately 4,000–6,000 white farmers lost their farms during the FTLRP. Nearly all white-owned farms had been expropriated by 2013 [2][86].

2. **Farmworkers (Most Severely Affected)**: 
   - About two-thirds of farm workers lost jobs, housing, and access to social services such as health care and schools [149].
   - An estimated 10,000 farm workers and their families died due to displacement-related hardships [148][149].
   - 53,022+ people experienced at least one human rights violation from 2000–2005 [148].
   - "Zimbabwe's farm workers have witnessed an arbitrary and inexorable decline in their fortunes and status, from being 'poor' to being 'chronically poor'" [149].
   - This outcome was not incidental but a structural result of the reform design: the FTLRP had "no systematic contingency planning for farmworkers displaced in the process" [149].

3. **Women**: Women received only 12–18% of land under the FTLRP despite being 52% of the population [122][128]. This exclusion was structural: "eligibility criteria favoring those with prior assets or farming knowledge" systematically disadvantaged women, and "customary and patriarchal structures continue to limit women's effective access and ownership of land despite policy changes" [128].

4. **Urban Dwellers (Operation Murambatsvina)**: Approximately 700,000 people were displaced by the 2005 government crackdown on informal settlements [150][151].

5. **Youth**: "Formal access to land for young people is highly constrained as new allocations usually rely on local patronage networks, which they are not part of" [76].

### 8.2 South Africa: Beneficiaries and Excluded Groups

**Primary Beneficiaries (Winners):**

1. **Urban Middle-Class Elites**: Research led by Prof. Ben Cousins and Farai Mtero found that 44% of beneficiaries are economically and politically influential urban individuals, mostly men [77]. This is a structural outcome of the market-based approach: "Market-led programs achieved higher productivity but favored better-resourced beneficiaries" [35].

2. **Commercial Farmers (Predominantly White)**: The existing commercial sector has maintained secure freehold title, enabling continued access to credit, markets, and investment. The sector has thrived, with record agricultural exports of US$15.1 billion in 2025 [9].

3. **Restitution Claimants**: Over 83,000 claims settled, benefiting approximately 2.3 million people. A study found a 16% increase in economic power for restitution beneficiaries compared to a control group [38].

**Excluded or Negatively Impacted Groups (Losers):**

1. **Farmworkers**: Only 18% of redistributed farms were allocated to farm workers [77]. Farmworker evictions remain a major challenge despite ESTA protections. The Surplus People Project notes: "Currently, the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) only provides for the procedural aspects of eviction and not necessarily the issue of tenure security" [155].

2. **Farm Dwellers**: ESTA evictions continue. The 2021 General Household Survey found that 22.2% of households (nearly 14 million people) experienced daily hunger [138]. These outcomes are structural: the market-based approach systematically favors those with capital and political connections.

3. **Women**: Women own only 13% of farmland directly (individuals), and only 19% of redistributed farms went to women [40][77]. This is a structural outcome: "There are no tangible programmes that have been put in place to facilitate women's access to land... traditional and cultural structures have effectively barred women from acquiring land in the rural areas" [156].

4. **Communal Area Residents**: Residents of former homelands lack secure tenure. The Ingonyama Trust controversy over converting customary land into leaseholds requiring rent payments illustrates the ongoing tenure insecurity affecting millions [102].

5. **Smallholder Farmers**: The majority of the 2.5 million small-scale farmers produce only for household consumption rather than surplus, lacking access to credit, markets, and extension services [77].

### 8.3 Namibia: Beneficiaries and Excluded Groups

**Primary Beneficiaries (Winners):**

1. **Political and Economic Elites**: "Commercial land reform has been criticised for not targeting the people that are in need, and for being directed to the advantage of the political and economic elite" [125]. Post-independence, almost every cabinet member acquired a private farm with state support [126].

2. **Commercial Farmers (South of the Red Line)**: Farmers with freehold title south of the Red Line have access to premium export markets (EU, Norway, UK, US, China) and full banking services [66].

**Excluded or Negatively Impacted Groups (Losers):**

1. **Farmworkers (Zero-Sum Outcome)**: The LAC 2007 finding is devastating: "Any 'average' resettlement process will move five 'disadvantaged' families to a farm at the same time that it removes six farmworkers and their families to homelessness and poverty" [85]. This is a structural result of the resettlement design: the Agricultural (Commercial) Land Reform Act "does not provide for resettlement of farm workers displaced by expropriation" [157].

2. **Communal Farmers North of the Red Line**: 
   - Structurally excluded from export markets due to the Red Line [64][82].
   - Cannot use land as collateral for credit (commercial financial institutions do not accept registered leasehold over state land as collateral) [84].
   - Livestock cannot be exported overseas without 21-day quarantine, limiting market access [82].
   - This exclusion is not incidental but a structural result of the dual communal/commercial system maintained since the colonial era.

3. **Women**: Women register only 23% of commercial land and 28% of communal land rights [53][105]. Despite the Communal Land Reform Act granting equal rights, "customary practices continue to disadvantage women" [105]. Property grabbing from widows persists [105].

4. **San/Bushmen Communities**: 
   - San groups "have been dispossessed of most of their ancestral lands and face marginalization, poverty, and legal insecurity" [158].
   - The Hai//om San (approximately 15,000 people, Namibia's largest San group) were forcibly removed from Etosha National Park in 1953–1954 [159].
   - By November 2012, only 7 resettlement farms covering roughly 30,359 hectares had been purchased for Hai//om resettlement, housing about 121 households (approximately 621 persons) [159].
   - "Common threads are an inadequate legal basis for San land tenure, an unwillingness on the part of the Namibian Government to defend San interests, and a lack of legal resources to defend San land rights" [158].

5. **Illegal Fencing Victims**: Politically connected local elites have illegally fenced off up to 10,000 hectares of communal land, dispossessing poor subsistence farmers of vital grazing and arable land [127].

## 9. Cross-Cutting Comparative Assessment

### 9.1 Comparative Outcomes Matrix

| Dimension | Zimbabwe | South Africa | Namibia |
|-----------|----------|--------------|---------|
| **Speed of Reform** | Rapid (2000–2005) | Slow (1994–present) | Very slow (1990–present) |
| **Hectares Transferred** | ~13 million ha | ~9.5 million ha (govt); ~19.5 million ha (incl. private) | ~3.4 million ha (govt); ~6.5 million ha (incl. AALS) |
| **% of Commercial Farmland Redistributed** | ~90%+ | 10–14% (govt); ~25% (incl. private) | ~23% |
| **Agricultural Productivity** | Collapsed then partial recovery | Stable/high commercial sector | Stable but low growth |
| **Food Security** | Chronic crisis with recovery | National secure, household insecure | High vulnerability |
| **Income Gini Coefficient** | >0.6 | 0.63 (highest globally) | 0.59 (2nd highest) |
| **Political Violence** | High (state-orchestrated) | Moderate (criminal) | Low (peaceful) |
| **Rule of Law** | Severely undermined | Robust judicial oversight | Intact but slow |
| **Elite Capture** | Significant (politicized) | Significant (diversion of funds) | Moderate (political connections) |
| **Gender Equity** | Very low (12–18% women) | Low (13–19% women) | Moderate (23–28%) |

### 9.2 What Has Worked

**1. Contract Farming Systems (Zimbabwe Tobacco):**
- The recovery of Zimbabwe's tobacco sector demonstrates that private-sector-led contract farming can drive post-reform recovery by providing credit, inputs, extension services, and guaranteed markets.
- Tian Ze/CNTC's comprehensive input support model (seeds, fertilizers, chemicals, labor funds, equipment) recouped through crop sales provides a replicable template.
- Key lesson: When the state cannot provide reliable input support, private-sector contract farming can fill the gap if supported by appropriate legislation and export market demand.

**2. Productive Commercial Agriculture (South Africa):**
- South Africa's large-scale commercial sector has remained productive throughout the reform period due to secure freehold tenure, integrated value chains, and technical capacity.
- The sector demonstrates that maintaining existing productive capacity while pursuing redistribution is possible with adequate legal protections and institutional capacity.

**3. Constitutional Balance with Judicial Oversight (South Africa):**
- South Africa's Constitutional Court has played a robust role in balancing property rights with socio-economic rights to land access.
- The Expropriation Act No. 13 of 2024 provides a clear legal pathway for accelerated reform within constitutional bounds.
- Key lesson: Judicial oversight, while slowing reform, prevents the catastrophic economic collapse and humanitarian crisis seen in Zimbabwe.

**4. Peaceful Negotiated Reform (Namibia):**
- The Second National Land Conference (2018) provided a peaceful, negotiated forum for addressing land grievances without violence.
- The government's response to the AR movement—committing to service 200,000 land plots rather than repressing the movement—demonstrates the value of engagement over confrontation.

**5. Restitution Program Benefits (South Africa):**
- Over 83,000 claims settled benefiting 2.3 million people, with a 16% increase in economic power for beneficiaries compared to control groups [38].
- This demonstrates that well-designed restitution programs can produce measurable positive outcomes.

### 9.3 What Has Failed

**1. Coercive Land Reform Without Legal Safeguards (Zimbabwe):**
- The violent and coercive nature of Zimbabwe's land reform caused enormous human suffering, economic collapse, and food insecurity.
- At least 10,000 farm workers died due to displacement-related hardships; 53,022+ documented human rights violations; approximately 700,000 displaced by Operation Murambatsvina.
- The subversion of the judiciary and rule of law undermined the legitimacy of reform and triggered international isolation.
- Key lesson: Rapid redistribution without adequate legal protections, compensation, and post-settlement support produces catastrophic outcomes.

**2. Willing-Buyer Willing-Seller Approach (South Africa and Namibia):**
- The market-based approach proved extremely slow and ineffective in both countries.
- South Africa redistributed only 10–14% against a 30% target over 30 years despite spending R50–R60 billion.
- Namibia redistributed only ~23% of commercial farmland over 30+ years; at current pace, "the process will take over 100 years to complete" [85].
- Key lesson: Market-based reform alone cannot achieve equitable land distribution within acceptable timeframes without complementary policy interventions.

**3. Inadequate Post-Settlement Support (All Three Countries):**
- Across all three countries, land redistribution without adequate support for new farmers (credit, extension services, infrastructure, market access) has resulted in underutilization and productivity declines.
- In South Africa, only 18% of redistributed farms were allocated to farm workers; crop production decreased by 79% and job losses averaged 84% on land reform farms [47][48].
- In Namibia, the LAC 2007 report found that "resettled people mostly continue subsistence farming" [85].
- Key lesson: Land transfer is necessary but not sufficient—post-settlement support is the critical determinant of beneficiary success.

**4. Elite Capture and Corruption (All Three Countries):**
- Elite capture has been a persistent problem across all three countries, though its extent varies.
- In Zimbabwe, 78% of high-value A2 farms went to ZANU-PF aligned elites; ~2,200 politically-connected individuals control nearly 40% of seized land [86][121].
- In South Africa, 44% of beneficiaries are urban elites; land reform budgets have been "diverted to elites rather than reaching intended beneficiaries" [77][123].
- In Namibia, "almost every cabinet member acquired a private farm with state support under preferential conditions"; illegal fencing by political elites dispossesses poor subsistence farmers [126][127].
- Key lesson: Transparent, accountable land administration systems with clear beneficiary selection criteria and independent oversight are essential.

**5. Gender Inequality in Land Access (All Three Countries):**
- Despite legal provisions for gender equality, women remain significantly disadvantaged in land access across all three countries.
- Zimbabwe: 12–18% women beneficiaries; South Africa: 13–19%; Namibia: 23–28%.
- This is a structural outcome: in Zimbabwe, "eligibility criteria favoring those with prior assets or farming knowledge" systematically disadvantaged women; in Namibia, "customary practices continue to disadvantage women" [105][128].
- Key lesson: Legal provisions alone are insufficient—dedicated quotas, enforcement mechanisms, and reform of customary laws are necessary for gender equity.

**6. Farmworker Exclusion (All Three Countries):**
- In all three countries, farmworkers—among the poorest and most vulnerable groups—have been systematically excluded or negatively impacted.
- Zimbabwe: ~10,000 farmworker deaths; two-thirds lost jobs and housing; no contingency planning [148][149].
- South Africa: Only 18% of redistributed farms to farmworkers; ongoing ESTA evictions [77][155].
- Namibia: Each resettlement "removes six farmworkers and their families to homelessness" [85].
- This exclusion is not incidental but structural: the design of land reform programs has prioritized political constituencies and capital-holders over the poorest.

### 9.4 Actionable Policy Lessons for the Region

**1. Hybrid Models Integrating Market and Supply-Led Approaches:**
- A Frontiers review (2026) found that hybrid models integrating market and supply-led approaches with participatory governance show promising results [35].
- Market-led programs achieve higher productivity but tend to favor well-resourced beneficiaries.
- Supply-led initiatives expand access to marginalized groups but suffer from bureaucratic delays and under-resourced support.
- Recommendation: Combine market-based acquisition with state-funded post-settlement support, targeting both commercial farmers and smallholders.

**2. Phased Reform with Pre-Investment in Institutional Capacity:**
- Zimbabwe demonstrates the dangers of rapid reform without institutional readiness.
- South Africa and Namibia demonstrate the dangers of excessive slowness.
- Recommendation: Implement phased reform that pre-invests in institutional capacity (land administration, dispute resolution, extension services, credit systems) before large-scale land transfers.

**3. Sector-Specific Strategies:**
- Zimbabwe's tobacco recovery shows that contract farming systems can revive specific sectors post-reform.
- Recommendation: For high-value export crops (tobacco, horticulture, beef), develop contract farming legislation that mandates private-sector financing of new farmers. For staple crops (maize, wheat, millet), maintain or rebuild state marketing boards with transparent governance, complemented by private-sector partnerships.

**4. Address Dual Tenure Systems:**
- Namibia's Red Line structurally disadvantages communal farmers, while South Africa's former homeland areas lack secure tenure.
- Recommendation: Invest in veterinary infrastructure to bridge the Red Line; provide pathways for converting communal rights to freehold title where appropriate; register all communal land rights (Namibia has only 49% registered).

**5. Link Land Reform to Broader Agrarian Transformation:**
- Sustainable land reform requires integration with broader rural development—infrastructure, education, health, and market access.
- Recommendation: Land reform should be part of comprehensive rural development strategies, not a standalone program.

**6. Transparent Beneficiary Selection and Anti-Corruption Mechanisms:**
- Elite capture undermines the legitimacy and effectiveness of land reform.
- Recommendation: Establish independent oversight bodies; publish all land allocations publicly; implement digital land registries; require parliamentary reporting on beneficiary demographics (gender, income, political affiliation).

**7. Constitutional Reform with Safeguards:**
- The evidence suggests that constitutional protection for property rights, when coupled with expropriation provisions for public interest (as in South Africa and Namibia), is compatible with land reform—provided judicial oversight is robust.
- Recommendation: Pursue constitutional amendments that explicitly permit expropriation without compensation for specific categories (abandoned, under-utilized, speculative land) while maintaining independent judicial review and just compensation for active farmers.

**8. Gender Quotas with Enforcement:**
- Legal provisions alone have failed to achieve gender equity.
- Recommendation: Implement and enforce 50% quotas for women in all land allocation programs (South Africa's policy target of 50% has not been met); reform customary laws that discriminate against women; provide targeted credit, extension, and training programs for women farmers.

**9. Protect Farmworkers in Reform Design:**
- The systematic exclusion of farmworkers is the most consistent failure across all three countries.
- Recommendation: Mandate that a minimum percentage of redistributed land be allocated to farmworkers (not less than 30%); include farmworker housing and employment guarantees in expropriation compensation assessments; establish transition support programs for displaced farmworkers.

## 10. Tenure Security and Credit: Detailed Linkages

### 10.1 Zimbabwe: Tenure Instruments and Credit Constraints

**Tenure Instruments:**

1. **A1 Settlement Permits**: Post-FTLRP, the Zimbabwean government introduced A1 settlement permits to provide resettled smallholder farmers with security of tenure over their allocated land [13]. A1 permits are offered in perpetuity but are non-transferable without state approval [14].

2. **A2 99-Year Leases**: A2 farmers received Letters of Occupation (LOL) with 99-year leases, while wildlife safari and forestry areas got 25-year leases. Leases and LOL do not provide full ownership [15].

3. **Non-Transferability Restrictions**: Under Zimbabwean law, A1 settlement permits and A2 99-year leases cannot be sold, mortgaged, or transferred without state approval. This prohibition was designed to prevent land consolidation but has effectively locked beneficiaries out of formal credit markets.

**Credit Access Effects:**

| Finding | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **85% of A1 farmers had no formal credit access** | Most smallholder farmers in Masvingo East relied largely on informal networks or remittances | Maireva (2022) |
| Banks deemed A1 permits "not bankable" | Most formal financial institutions shunned A1 settlement permits as security of tenure documents, as they believed that the permits were not bankable due to lack of collateral and verified income | Maireva (2022) |
| Government financing programs | ASPEF and input schemes existed but were marred by limited resources and allegations of political favoritism, limiting equitable access | Maireva (2022) |
| Private contract farming | Contract farming schemes did not benefit Masvingo East due to the absence of relevant cash crops | Maireva (2022) |
| Tobacco contract farming | The tobacco boom has provided a significant group of land reform beneficiaries the opportunity to accumulate, generating employment and changing local economies. Contract farming works as alternative financing mechanism | Nicotine Insider (2025) |

*Sources: [13][14][15]*

**Investment Behavior Consequences:**
- Lack of access to finance by smallholder farmers results in a vicious cycle of poverty, food insecurity and limited development in the resettlement areas [13].
- The only claim to the land title is the A1 offer letter and the settlement permit—these are insufficient for long-term investment [14].
- Several pilot programmes—including the 99-year lease and the A2 offer letters—exposed the gaps. Banks were reluctant to lend against these documents [15].

### 10.2 South Africa: Tenure Instruments and Credit Constraints

**Tenure Instruments:**

1. **PLAS (Proactive Land Acquisition Strategy) Leasehold**: PLAS involves state-owned land leased to beneficiaries, whereas LRAD grants title deeds to beneficiary groups who purchase the land. PLAS beneficiaries often operate individually under lease agreements without title deeds, limiting financial support access [160].

2. **Freehold Title**: Commercial farmers hold freehold title—the strongest form of tenure security—enabling long-term investment, access to bank financing, and collateral for credit.

3. **Communal Property Associations (CPAs)**: Group-based ownership through CPAs has been plagued by governance failures. By 2024, 75–82% of 1,742 registered CPAs were non-compliant with reporting and governance requirements [79].

4. **ESTA Protections**: The Extension of Security of Tenure Act protects lawful occupiers from eviction but only addresses procedural aspects, not substantive tenure security [155].

5. **Ingonyama Trust**: Customary communal land tenure grants collective ownership rights. The Trust unilaterally attempted to convert customary holdings into common law leaseholds requiring rent payments—successfully challenged in court [102].

**Credit Access Effects:**

| Finding | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| PLAS lack of finance | 77.8% of PLAS projects cited lack of finance as a major constraint | Nxumalo (2013) |
| PLAS limited collateral | PLAS beneficiaries lack title deeds, limiting credit access | Masemola (UFS) |
| PLAS long-term leases | Long-term lease contracts help some farmers access financing but hinder others due to lack of full land titles | UP PLAS Mahikeng study |
| CPA non-compliance | 82% of CPAs fail to submit required annual reports, indicating governance crisis | Vumelana (Nov 2025) |
| LRAD group farms | Individual PLAS farms show more robust agricultural production compared to LRAD group farms where production is weaker or absent | Masemola (UFS) |

*Sources: [79][124][160]*

**Quantified Effects:**
- A study of 761 smallholder farmers found that only 12% own tractors, and the majority feel constrained farming on communal lands [80].
- High input costs were the biggest cause of failure among CPAs [81].
- The Land Bank's collapse further starved Black farmers of crucial funding [78].

### 10.3 Namibia: Tenure Instruments and Credit Constraints

**Tenure Instruments:**

1. **Commercial Freehold Title**: Full bundle of property rights—sale, lease, mortgage, bequest. Enables access to agribusiness value chains, credit markets, and bank financing. The AALS provides subsidised loans for commercial farm acquisition.

2. **Communal Land Rights (CLRA 2002)**: Customary land rights registered under the Communal Land Reform Act 2002 offer limited investment incentives, no freehold title, and limited collateral value. The CLRA prohibits payment for land allocation (except N$25 administrative fee) [105].

3. **Leasehold Rights under Resettlement**: The Farm Unit Resettlement Scheme allocates farm units averaging 1,200–2,138 hectares to individuals on leasehold (99-year) terms. By 2018, only about 35% of resettlement beneficiaries had lease agreements issued [53].

**Credit Access Effects:**

| Finding | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Commercial banks do not accept leasehold over state land as collateral | "At present, commercial financial institutions do not accept registered leasehold over state land as collateral, which means that such leases cannot be traded" | Leasehold Policy Brief (NUST, 2017) |
| No active land market | "If foreclosure is impossible, land loses its attractiveness as collateral" | Leasehold Policy Brief |
| Insufficient registration | "The number of leaseholds allocated and registered has been insufficient to provide beneficiaries security of tenure and access to credit" | Leasehold Policy Brief |
| Prohibitive transaction costs | "Direct transaction costs were equivalent to or exceeded the annual potential income of the allotments" | Leasehold Policy Brief |
| Asset poverty | "Asset poverty is a major impediment to obtaining loans" | Leasehold Policy Brief |
| Farmer interviews | "The land belongs to the government and not to us... A government official can always come and say I am not producing and that I should go" | Amadhila & Ikhide (2016) |
| Agribank loan officer | "As people don't have collateral... that becomes a bit difficult for us to grant loans" | Amadhila & Ikhide (2016) |

*Sources: [84][161][162]*

**The 2018 Land Conference Resolution Acknowledging the Problem:**
The Conference explicitly resolved that the Resettlement Policy should "provide Security of tenure to allow bankability of allotment/farming units"—acknowledging that current tenure arrangements are not bankable [71].

**Investment Behavior Consequences:**
- Resettled farms frequently exhibit poor management, overgrazing, and lack of technical skills [11].
- "Resettled people mostly continue subsistence farming, and that does not fulfill the government's aim of commercial production" (Theo Eiseb, Governor of Otjozondjupa) [85].
- "Most collective resettlement projects show major deficiencies in production, with poor management, lack of technical skills, and insecure land tenure for beneficiaries" (FAO) [163].
