# New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market Analysis (2018–2024, with Outlook to 2026)

## Executive Summary

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market, valued at approximately **NZD $1.8 billion** in 2025–2026, has experienced a complex trajectory from 2018 to 2024. The industry, comprising approximately **267 businesses** and employing around **4,100–5,000 workers**, processes roughly **100,000 tonnes of structural steel annually** against a capacity that expanded from **120,000 to 150,000 tonnes** between 2018 and 2023. The sector faces a pivotal moment: near-term headwinds from reduced construction activity and import competition are balanced by a substantial **$275 billion national infrastructure pipeline**, growing demand for green steel solutions, and structural advantages in seismic-resistant construction.

## (c) NZ Import Tariff Rates for Raw Steel (HS 7207–7216)

### (i) Standard MFN Tariff Rates

New Zealand's tariff regime for steel products is characterised by relatively low Most Favoured Nation (MFN) rates, with extensive duty-free access under Free Trade Agreements:

**General Framework:**
- "Most goods imported into New Zealand have no import tariffs. Tariffs of 5 or 10 per cent apply to some imported goods including some textiles, footwear, processed foods, **machinery, steel**, and plastic products." [30]
- The simple average MFN applied tariff rate for non-agricultural products is **2.0%** (2024), with **57.1% of tariff lines already duty-free under MFN** [31]

**Specific HS Code Rates (HS 7207–7216):**

| HS Code Range | Product Description | Standard MFN Rate | Preferential Rate (FTA) |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| HS 7207 | Semi-finished products of iron or non-alloy steel | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7208 | Flat-rolled products, hot-rolled, width ≥600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7209 | Flat-rolled products, cold-rolled, width ≥600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7210 | Flat-rolled products, coated (tin, zinc, Al-Zn etc.) | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7211 | Flat-rolled products, width <600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7212 | Flat-rolled products, coated, width <600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7213 | Bars and rods, hot-rolled, in irregular coils | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7214 | Other bars and rods of iron/non-alloy steel | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7215 | Other bars and rods | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7216 | Angles, shapes, and sections of iron/non-alloy steel | **5%** | **0%** |

*Source: NZ Customs Working Tariff Document; Section XV PDF; tariffnumber.com* [32][33]

### (ii) Preferential Tariff Rates Under Free Trade Agreements

New Zealand has negotiated comprehensive FTAs that provide **0% duty-free access** for steel imports from partner countries:

**Active FTAs with 0% Steel Tariffs:**
- **Australia** (CER) – NZ's closest trading partner
- **China** (NZ-China FTA, 2008) – NZ's largest steel import source
- **Korea** (NZ-Korea FTA, 2015)
- **EU** (NZ-EU FTA, effective 1 May 2024) – 91% of tariffs eliminated immediately
- **UK** (NZ-UK FTA) – immediate elimination of NZ tariffs on UK goods
- **CPTPP** – covers Japan, Canada, Australia, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, Brunei, Chile, Mexico, Peru
- **RCEP** – includes China, Japan, Korea, Australia, ASEAN countries
- **ASEAN-Australia-New Zealand FTA (AANZFTA)**
- **Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia** (bilateral agreements)
- **Less Developed Countries (LDCs)** – duty-free access

**Key Insight:** The **preferential rate of 0% applies to steel imports from virtually all significant steel-exporting nations** that trade with New Zealand. Under the Working Tariff Document, preferential tariff codes include: AU, CN, EU, HK, KR, LDC, LLDC, MY, PPP, RCEP, SG, UK, AAN, CPT [32].

### (iii) Anti-Dumping Duties on Steel Products

New Zealand maintains targeted anti-dumping measures on specific steel products:

**Aluminium–Zinc Coated Steel from the Republic of Korea:**
- Anti-dumping duty effective from **1 January 2023**
- Remains in force until **29 March 2027**
- Classified under specific HS subheadings [34]

**Galvanised Wire from China and Malaysia (Terminated):**
- Original duty rates: **24% to 36%** for specific Chinese exporters (set via MBIE investigation)
- **Terminated effective 1 November 2025** due to cessation of production of like goods in New Zealand
- Classified under tariff items ex 7217.20.10 and ex 7217.20.90 [35][36]

**Goods Subject to Anti-Dumping Duties (as of early 2024):**
- Aluminium–zinc coated steel from Korea
- Galvanised wire from Malaysia and China *(terminated 2025)*
- Canned peaches from Greece, Spain, South Africa (non-steel)

**"There are currently no goods subject to countervailing action."** [34]

### (iv) Effective Tariff Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

**Direct Tariff Cost Impact:**
- For fabricators sourcing steel from **FTA partner countries** (the vast majority of NZ's steel imports): **effective tariff impact is 0%** (duty-free entry)
- For fabricators sourcing from **non-FTA countries**: typical MFN rate of **5% adds to landed cost**, directly reducing profit margins unless passed through to customers
- In practice, the **0% preferential rate applies to China, Australia, Korea, Japan, EU, UK, and CPTPP members**—covering the overwhelming majority of steel imports

**IBISWorld Assessment:**
The Structural Steel Fabricating industry analysis states: **"Import tariffs moderately impact the industry, while export tariffs have minimal effect."** [2]

**Broader Dynamics Affecting Margins:**
- "Import penetration has plummeted in recent years, partly due to weaker local construction trends, the slump in global steel prices and the escalation in freight costs." [2]
- The 5% MFN tariff provides a modest competitive buffer for local fabricators against non-FTA imported steel
- Imported fabricated steel (completed structures) faces additional quality compliance costs: NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing for mechanical properties and Charpy V-notch toughness at 0°C or -15°C—critical for seismic resilience [37]

**Case Study: Challenge Steel Collapse:**
RNZ reported on the collapse of Christchurch-based Challenge Steel, which imported Chinese steel. "Extended delays at a major unidentified building project" and the cost of ensuring Chinese steel met NZ's seismic standards contributed to its failure with debts of $10 million. Unsecured creditors were owed $6.1 million. This illustrates that **quality assurance costs can negate import price advantages** [38].

## (e) Top 3 Strategic Risks and Opportunities

### Top 3 Strategic Risks

#### Risk 1: Competition from Imported Fabricated Steel (Particularly Chinese Imports)

**Nature of the Risk:**
New Zealand structural steel fabricators face sustained competition from cheaper imported fabricated steel, particularly from China. The IBISWorld industry analysis describes the level of competition as **"high and increasing"** [2].

**Evidence:**
- New Zealand imports from China of articles of iron or steel reached **US$367.92 million** in 2025 (UN COMTRADE) [56]
- As reported by Stuff: "New Zealand steel fabricators were losing contracts because customers were buying cheaper fabricated steel imported from China" [57]
- A substantive MBIE subsidy investigation (2018–2019) into hollow steel sections from China found subsidisation below de minimis threshold (0.03%), leading to termination without countervailing duties [58]
- A Beca technical paper on "Imported Structural Steel Issues & Considerations" documents significant challenges: NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing and third-party certification, increasing project costs and duration despite initial cost savings [37]
- The collapse of Challenge Steel (Christchurch, ~2018) illustrates the risks: $10 million in debts; quality assurance costs diminished the cost advantage of Chinese steel; extended project delays compounded financial pressures [38]

**Mitigating Factors:**
- 5% MFN tariff provides a modest competitive buffer (though 0% FTA rates for Chinese-origin steel eliminate this advantage)
- Quality compliance costs for imported steel reduce price differential
- NZ's Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC) programme covering 92.5% of output provides quality assurance advantage
- Seismic performance requirements favour local expertise and certified materials

**Impact on Margins:** High. Import competition compresses pricing power and forces local fabricators to compete on quality, speed, and technical service rather than price alone.

#### Risk 2: Labour and Skills Shortages

**Nature of the Risk:**
New Zealand faces a chronic shortage of skilled welders, fabricators, and engineering professionals, constraining capacity and increasing labour costs.

**Evidence:**
- Engineering New Zealand reports: "New Zealand is experiencing a long-term engineering skills shortage, and this problem is increasing." An estimated **1,500–2,300 additional engineers** are needed annually [59]
- Fraser Wyllie, Managing Director at McConnell Dowell: "The labour market remains one of the most structural risks we face" [60]
- The Hubexo/BCI Central Construction Outlook 2026 identifies labour as a top risk: "We see cost still as a risk, but actually it's more about resource availability and skills shortages are really starting to bite" [60]
- Welding and fabrication roles (Fitter Welder, Welder, Metal Fabricator, Pressure Welder) were added to the **Green List (Tier 1 Straight to Residence pathway)** as of August 2025, reflecting acute shortages [61]
- The average business in the structural steel fabricating industry now employs fewer workers than five years ago, despite revenue per employee increasing [3]
- A scholarly article in the Journal of Infrastructure Preservation and Resilience found "workforce constraints" as one of three consistent vulnerability areas across crises (GFC, earthquakes, COVID-19) for NZ construction businesses [62]
- Despite unemployment rising to 5.2–5.3%, "skilled construction roles remain hard to fill" (RLB Infrastructure Forecast Q3 2025) [63]

**Impact on Fabricators:**
- Increases project costs and reduces competitiveness against imported steel
- Limits ability to scale production when infrastructure project pipeline accelerates
- Creates dependency on immigration policy and international labour markets
- Requires increased investment in automation, robotics, and training to supplement workforce

**Mitigating Actions:**
- Industry apprenticeship programmes (10.6% of workforce in training as of Sep 2022)
- HERA's training courses and internationally recognised welding qualifications
- Technology adoption (robotics, CNC cutting, automated welding)

#### Risk 3: Trade Policy and Supply Chain Concentration

**Nature of the Risk:**
NZ steel fabricators face dual exposure: (1) global trade tensions affecting steel input costs and export competitiveness, and (2) concentration of supply chains through China and ASEAN.

**Evidence:**
- **US Tariff Escalation:** On June 4, 2025, US tariffs on steel and aluminium imports doubled from 25% to **50%** (Section 232). The US applied a baseline 10% reciprocal tariff to NZ on April 2, 2025 ("Liberation Day"), rising to **15%** on August 7, 2025 [64][65]
- NZ exporters are placed at a competitive disadvantage compared to rivals like Australia and the EU (which have their own trade deals or exemptions) [65]
- **Cost to NZ:** The 10% baseline tariff cost NZ nearly **$900 million/year**; at 15%, that rises to approximately **$1.35 billion annually** [65]
- **Supply Chain Concentration:** A Treasury-commissioned report notes NZ's "physical remoteness means that New Zealand's supply chains face unique physical connectivity risks, with long and thin servicing from shipping lines and airlines" [66]
- The New Zealand China Council reports China's manufacturing advantage is deeply embedded in global supply chains; diversification is described as "an expensive policy that may not fully decouple New Zealand" [67]
- **Steel & Tube FY25 results** illustrate market impact: revenue fell 20% to $385.4M, volumes down 12% YoY, net loss of $24.4M. CEO Mark Malpass described "a very challenging bottom of cycle trading environment" [68]

**Impact on Fabricators:**
- Global trade fragmentation creates uncertainty in steel pricing and availability
- Indirect exposure via NZ exporters facing US tariffs reduces overall economic activity
- Supply chain concentration (China accounts for 16% of NZ imports) creates vulnerability to disruptions
- Anti-dumping duties on specific products (aluminium-zinc coated steel from Korea) add complexity

### Top 3 Strategic Opportunities

#### Opportunity 1: Major Infrastructure Project Pipeline

**Nature of the Opportunity:**
New Zealand's National Infrastructure Pipeline represents an unprecedented volume of projects with significant structural steel demand, providing a multi-year demand horizon for local fabricators.

**Evidence:**
- **$275 billion** in projects across nearly 12,000 initiatives (September 2025), up from $237 billion in June 2025
- **$181 billion** pertains to initiatives with committed or identified funding
- **$61 billion** of projects under construction; another **$20 billion** progressing through planning and scheduled to start within 12 months
- Between June and September 2025, **$28 billion** worth of projects progressed to confirmed funding [69]
- **MBIE National Construction Pipeline Report:** Infrastructure activity forecast to reach **$19.6 billion by 2030**, with total construction activity rising to **$65.4 billion** [70]
- **Oxford Economics Construction Outlook:** Activity levels expected to improve, averaging **5.6% p.a. over four years to 2029** [71]
- **New Zealand Upgrade Programme:** $6.8 billion in transport infrastructure creating up to 9,000 direct supply chain opportunities [72]
- **RLB Infrastructure Forecast:** Civil construction costs remain resilient due to significant ongoing pipeline; OCR cut from 5.5% to 3% (Aug 2024–Aug 2025) supports investment [63]
- **Steel & Tube CEO Mark Malpass:** "We are starting to see early signs of recovery, and expect activity to continue to improve through FY26 as the benefits of lower interest rates take effect and stimulate confidence, spending and investment, along with Government-backed construction projects." [68]

**Key Projects Driving Steel Demand:**
- City Rail Link ($5.5B, 4,000t structural steel, H2 2026 opening)
- Ō2NL Expressway ($2.1B, 14 bridges, 2029 completion)
- Dunedin Hospital ($1.47B, ~2,000t+ steel, 2026–2031)
- National Green Steel Plant (Hampton Downs, 200,000t/year capacity)
- Auckland rail electrification, Penlink, Waikato Expressway completions
- Wellington seismic strengthening programme

**Strategic Implication:** Local fabricators with capacity, certification, and project-delivery capability are well-positioned to capture substantial work over the next 5–7 years. Infrastructure-driven demand provides a counter-cyclical buffer against residential construction volatility.

#### Opportunity 2: Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication and Seismic Retrofit Demand

**Nature of the Opportunity:**
Growing demand for low-carbon construction materials, combined with ongoing seismic retrofit requirements, creates premium market segments for NZ fabricators.

**Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication:**
- **National Green Steel Limited** is developing NZ's first fully integrated structural steel manufacturing plant (Hampton Downs, Waikato) using **electric arc furnace (EAF) technology** on recycled steel. Target: **200,000 tonnes of structural steel annually**, creating up to 200 jobs [73]
- HERA advanced low-carbon, resilient steel construction in 2025 with new guidance, tools, and research. Research highlights the need to improve scrap steel collection rates (currently ~85%) [74]
- Researchers at Victoria University Wellington have demonstrated hydrogen ironmaking using NZ ironsand, potentially eliminating CO2 emissions from steel production. Technology is at proof-of-concept stage, seeking scale-up partners [75]
- The **Sustainable Steel Council NZ (SSC)** has over 80 active members, is developing a **Steel RoadMap to Carbon Zero 2050** for the construction sector, and has developed NZ's first **Green Star-recognized sustainable steel certification** programmes [76]
- **HERA's $10.3 million MBIE Endeavour-funded Construction 4.0** programme engages 50+ researchers from nine institutions on low-carbon design, AI-based quality monitoring, and circular economy systems [77]
- **George Grant Engineering** holds ISO14001 and Gold Standard certification for Green Steel supporting NZGBC Green Star framework [78]

**Seismic Retrofit Demand:**
- New Zealand's structural steel market is projected to grow from **US$50.3 million (2024) to US$68.5 million by 2030**, a CAGR of 6% [79]
- Structural steel holds over 50% of NZ's steel market, "known to be earthquake proof, making it preferred for construction in earthquake risk zones" [79]
- Under the **Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Act 2016**, Wellington is classified as high seismic risk. GNS Science estimates a **10-15% probability of a major rupture in 50 years**. Most engineers recommend targeting at least **67% NBS** [80]
- Wellington has "proactive regulation of earthquake prone buildings as well as strong market demand for buildings that meet seismic standards" [81]
- HERA's $9.47 million Endeavour-funded research (Dr Shahab Ramhormozian) aims to develop systems so buildings suffer **no structural damage under severe earthquakes** [77]

**Strategic Implication:** Fabricators that invest in low-carbon production capabilities and SFC certification position themselves for premium pricing and preferred-supplier status on government and institutional projects with sustainability mandates.

#### Opportunity 3: Modular/Prefabricated Construction and Technology Adoption

**Nature of the Opportunity:**
Global trends toward modular construction, combined with advances in digital manufacturing technologies (BIM, robotics, automation), offer NZ fabricators pathways to improve productivity, reduce costs, and capture higher-value work.

**Modular/Prefabricated Construction:**
- Global modular construction market: **US$173.5 billion (2025) projected to US$302.0 billion by 2035**, CAGR 5.7%
- Steel leads material usage globally with **47.8% share**, favoured for strength, durability, recyclability, and design flexibility
- Permanent modular construction holds **61.3% of the market**; multi-family residential dominates with **33.6% share** [82]
- NZ modular construction market: approximately **US$1.1 billion**, driven by housing shortages, sustainability initiatives, and BIM adoption. Key players: PrefabNZ/OffsiteNZ, Modulus Homes, Fletcher Building [83]
- Globally, modular construction can cut building times by **30–50%** and achieve cost savings of around **20%** [84]
- Structural steel offers **10-15% shorter project timelines** compared to alternative materials, via off-site manufacturing and just-in-time delivery [4]

**Technology Adoption:**
- **HERA's Construction 4.0 programme** ($10.3M, MBIE Endeavour-funded) engages 50+ researchers across nine institutions. Includes pioneering dynamic Life Cycle Assessment, AI-based quality monitoring of steelwork, indigenous knowledge integration (Mātauranga Māori) [77]
- HERA plans a national **centre of excellence in building health monitoring (tabs4.0lab)** through a 6-star Green Star Innovation Centre, alongside the existing **Fab4.0Lab** for advanced manufacturing
- HERA's Future Forum Awards recognised: **Farra Engineering** and **MJH Engineering** (Fab 4.0 Awards for fabrication efficiency and welding skills); **Tīra** (automated spiral welding technology expanding export markets); and **Beca** (adaptive reuse of 1,900 tonnes of structural steel frame at Civic Administration Building, achieving 100% reuse and 6,150 tonnes CO2e reduction) [77]
- The NZ structural steel market report notes "industry players are investing substantially in high-tech fabrication technology to boost the market" [79]
- **D&H Steel Construction** embraces Industry 4.0 including robotics and data-driven decision-making [28]
- **Steel & Tube** reports adoption of 3D modelling in its Reinforcing segment and digital innovation investment [68]

**Vertical Integration Opportunities:**
- **Steel & Tube's dual pathway strategy**: strengthening core distribution while expanding into high-value products (Perry Metal Protection acquisition for galvanising, new QBT450 roofing profile, aluminium products expansion)
- Approximately **$7 million in annualized cost savings** through cost reduction initiatives
- Customer satisfaction **Net Promoter Score (NPS) of 42**—above industry average of 32 [68]

**Strategic Implication:** Fabricators that invest in automation, BIM integration, and modular fabrication capabilities can capture margin improvements, reduce labour dependency, and differentiate themselves on speed and quality—particularly important given the skills shortage.

---

## Conclusion and Outlook

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market enters 2026 at an inflection point. The industry has weathered a period of declining construction activity (market size down 6% in 2025), compressed margins, and persistent skills shortages. However, the structural drivers are strongly positive:

- **$275 billion infrastructure pipeline** provides multi-year demand visibility
- **Green steel** and **seismic retrofit** requirements create premium market segments
- **Technology adoption** (robotics, BIM, modular construction) offers pathways to improved productivity
- **Consolidation** among top fabricators may create stronger, more resilient players

The principal risks—import competition from China, labour shortages, and trade policy uncertainty—require active management through investment in technology, quality certification, and workforce development. Fabricators that successfully navigate these challenges will be well-positioned to benefit from New Zealand's substantial infrastructure investment programme over the next decade.
