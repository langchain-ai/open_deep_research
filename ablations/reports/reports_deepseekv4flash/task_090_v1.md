# UCC Article 2 Legal Analysis: Custom Widgets Color Deviation Dispute

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines a commercial dispute under UCC Article 2 involving 1,000 custom widgets delivered in navy instead of the contractually specified charcoal gray. The widgets meet all functional specifications (load capacity, durability). The buyer installed and operated all widgets for eight weeks, painted 600 of them black, sent emails praising performance without mentioning color, and only then notified the seller of the color issue. The contract contains a 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause, and industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. The central questions concern whether the buyer effectively rejected the goods, accepted them by conduct, may revoke acceptance, provided timely notice, acted in good faith, and must accept the seller's cure offer of immediate replacement within two weeks.

---

## (a) Acceptance by Conduct Under UCC § 2-606 vs. Rejection Under the Perfect Tender Rule (§ 2-601)

### The Perfect Tender Rule: A Limited Window

UCC § 2-601 provides that "if the goods or the tender of delivery fail in any respect to conform to the contract, the buyer may (a) reject the whole; or (b) accept the whole; or (c) accept any commercial unit or units and reject the rest." This "perfect tender rule" gives buyers the right to reject goods for any nonconformity, no matter how trivial. As courts have explained, this rule imposes "a very high level of conformity" on sellers, allowing buyers to reject for "any trivial defect" [KCA Electronics, Inc. v. Legacy Electronics, Inc.].

However, this right is not absolute. The perfect tender rule is subject to limitations, and critically, **the right to reject is lost once the buyer accepts the goods**. The Schmitt & Frisch article, "The Perfect Tender Rule — An 'Acceptable' Interpretation," explains that while UCC § 2-601 appears absolute, "there are several limitations and exceptions" that restrict the perfect tender concept.

### Acceptance by Conduct Under § 2-606: Three Independent Grounds

UCC § 2-606 defines acceptance as occurring when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect: (a) signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming; (b) fails to make an effective rejection; or (c) does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership. All three prongs are satisfied here.

**Prong (a): Signifying Conformity.** The buyer sent emails praising widget performance with no mention of color. Under § 2-606(1)(a), acceptance occurs when the buyer "after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming." The emails praising performance constitute a clear signification that the goods are conforming to the contract. Official Comment 3 notes that "payment made after tender is always one circumstance tending to signify acceptance of the goods but in itself it can never be more than one circumstance." Here, the buyer did more than pay—they affirmatively communicated satisfaction with the goods' performance without any reservation about the color.

**Prong (b): Failure to Make Effective Rejection.** Under § 2-606(1)(b), acceptance occurs when the buyer "fails to make an effective rejection." Section 2-602(1) requires that "rejection of goods must be within a reasonable time after their delivery or tender" and that the buyer must "seasonably notify the seller." The contract contains a 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause. The buyer waited 8 weeks (56 days)—nearly six times the contractual period—before notifying the seller of the color issue. The color deviation (navy vs. charcoal gray) was visually apparent immediately upon delivery; no testing or complex inspection was needed. The 8-week delay far exceeds any reasonable time for rejection, particularly given the contractual 10-day period. [Cornell LII, UCC § 2-602]

**Prong (c): Act Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership.** The buyer painted 600 of the 1,000 widgets black during the 8-week period. This is the quintessential "act inconsistent with the seller's ownership" under § 2-606(1)(c). As the Nebraska Supreme Court held in Johnson v. Holdrege Coop. Equity Exchange, "commingling by a buyer of one farmer's grain with that of other farmers is an act inconsistent with the buyer's ownership and may constitute acceptance." Painting goods materially alters them and is a clear exercise of ownership rights. [Quarles & Brady, Supply Chain Survival Series]

### The 10-Day Inspection Clause Reinforces Acceptance

UCC § 1-204 provides that "any time which is not manifestly unreasonable may be fixed by agreement." The 10-day inspection clause is presumptively reasonable for a visually apparent defect like color. As the IRMI article on UCC rights explains, "Purchase orders may set specific inspection and rejection periods, which supersede default UCC timings." [IRMI, UCC Rights and Responsibilities] The buyer's 8-week delay far exceeds this contractual period, strongly supporting a finding of acceptance.

### Industry Practice: Color as a Minor Variation

UCC § 1-205 provides that "usage of trade" gives meaning to contract terms. Industry practice treating minor color variations as acceptable for functional components shapes what constitutes a "conforming" tender. Where the contract emphasizes performance criteria (load capacity, durability) and the industry views color as non-essential for functional components, the "conforming goods" analysis shifts. As White and Summers note, commercial practices "do not change the perfect tender rule, they merely shape the requirements of the contract in a way that makes what at first appeared to be a breach actually turn out to be conforming tender."

## (b) Revocation of Acceptance Under § 2-608

### The Substantial Impairment Standard

UCC § 2-608 allows a buyer to revoke acceptance only where the nonconformity "substantially impairs its value to him." This is a significantly higher standard than the perfect tender rule's "any respect" test. Official Comment 2 explains that "the test is not what the seller had reason to know at the time of contracting; the question is whether the nonconformity is such as will in fact cause a substantial impairment of value to the buyer."

The leading case of Jorgensen v. Pressnall articulated a two-part test: (1) a subjective inquiry into the needs and circumstances of the particular buyer, and (2) an objective inquiry requiring evidence that the nonconformity in fact substantially impaired value to the buyer, not merely the buyer's assertion. [Jorgensen v. Pressnall, 545 P.2d 1382 (Or. 1976)]

### Color Deviation as Substantial Impairment: Unlikely

Several factors weigh against finding that a navy vs. charcoal gray color deviation constitutes substantial impairment:

**Functional Performance is Unaffected.** The widgets meet all functional specifications—load capacity and durability. In Freeman Oldsmobile Mazda Co. v. Pinson, the court refused to allow revocation where the cost of repairing small dents and imperfect paint on a new car would cost less than $100. Minor aesthetic defects generally do not meet the substantial impairment standard. [D.C. Law Library, § 28:2-608]

**Industry Practice.** Industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. If the goods "pass without objection in the trade" under their contract description, they are likely merchantable under UCC § 2-314, and a color variation that is standard in the trade cannot constitute substantial impairment.

**Buyer's Own Conduct Belies Claim.** The buyer used the widgets for 8 weeks, painted 600 of them black, and sent emails praising performance. The buyer's act of painting 600 widgets black is strong evidence that the color difference did not substantially impair value, as the buyer was able to remedy the color issue at relatively low cost. The buyer's praise of widget performance further undercuts any claim of substantial impairment.

**The "Shaken Faith" Doctrine.** The Zabriskie Chevrolet v. Smith "shaken faith" doctrine, which allows revocation where a defect destroys the buyer's confidence in the goods, typically applies to safety-related defects, not aesthetic deviations. Here, the goods perform exactly as expected, and there is no basis for "shaken faith."

### Procedural Bars to Revocation

Even if substantial impairment could be shown, two procedural bars apply:

**Timeliness.** Section 2-608(2) requires revocation "within a reasonable time after the buyer discovers or should have discovered the ground for it." The color deviation was immediately discoverable upon visual inspection. The 8-week delay exceeds any reasonable time. [Kansas Revisor of Statutes, 84-2-608]

**Substantial Change in Condition.** The buyer painted 600 of the 1,000 widgets black. Section 2-608(2) requires revocation to occur "before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects." Painting 600 widgets constitutes a substantial, deliberate alteration of the goods by the buyer—not caused by any defect. This alone bars revocation.

### Neither Subsection (a) nor (b) of § 2-608(1) Is Satisfied

For revocation, the buyer must have accepted either (a) "on the reasonable assumption that its non-conformity would be cured" or (b) "without discovery of such non-conformity if his acceptance was reasonably induced either by the difficulty of discovery before acceptance or by the seller's assurances." Neither applies: the color was immediately apparent (no difficulty of discovery), there were no seller assurances inducing acceptance, and the buyer did not accept with any expectation of cure (indeed, the buyer painted 600 widgets themselves without ever raising the issue).

## (c) Timeliness and Effectiveness of Notice of Rejection

### Section 2-602: Reasonable Time Requirement

UCC § 2-602(1) requires that "rejection of goods must be within a reasonable time after their delivery or tender" and is "ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller." Official Comment explains that "a tender or delivery of goods made pursuant to a contract of sale, even though wholly non-conforming, requires affirmative action by the buyer to avoid acceptance." [Cornell LII, UCC § 2-602]

### 8-Week Delay: Clearly Unreasonable

The 8-week delay is unreasonable for several reasons:

**Contractual 10-Day Clause.** The contract's 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause is presumptively enforceable under UCC § 1-204(1) ("any time which is not manifestly unreasonable may be fixed by agreement"). A 10-day period for discovering a visually apparent color defect is eminently reasonable. The buyer exceeded this period by nearly six times.

**Nature of the Defect.** In Michael M. Berlin & Co. v. T. Whiting Mfg., Inc., a six-week delay in inspecting steel that could have been measured with a simple micrometer was held unreasonable. Here, the color deviation was immediately visually apparent upon delivery. No testing or complex inspection was required. [Kansas Revisor of Statutes, 84-2-602]

**Case Law Comparisons.** In Don's Marine, Inc. v. Haldeman, a 30-day rejection of a boat was effective because multiple test runs in varying weather were required. In contrast, in Max Bauer Meat Packer, Inc. v. United States, a three-hour delay was found unreasonable for defects that should have been immediately apparent. The 8-week delay here falls firmly on the unreasonable side.

### Emails Praising Performance: Waiver Under § 2-606(1)(a)

The buyer's emails praising widget performance with no mention of color constitute a signification that the goods are conforming under § 2-606(1)(a). This operates as an express acceptance and waiver of any objections. The Yassi Law article on rejection vs. revocation explains: "A strong rejection notice usually includes: Date of delivery and delivery location; Purchase order or invoice number; What is nonconforming; Your demand; A statement that you are preserving rights." The emails did none of this. Instead, they affirmatively praised the goods.

UCC § 2-605 further provides that failure to state a particular defect "which is ascertainable by reasonable inspection precludes him from relying on the unstated defect to justify rejection or to establish breach." The color deviation was ascertainable upon reasonable inspection. The buyer failed to identify this defect until 8 weeks later, after praising performance.

### Section 2-607(3): Notice of Breach Requirement

Even apart from rejection, UCC § 2-607(3) requires that "the buyer must within a reasonable time after he discovers or should have discovered any breach notify the seller of breach or be barred from any remedy." This notice requirement serves three purposes: to give the seller an opportunity to correct the defect, to prepare for negotiation and litigation, and to safeguard against stale claims. The Butler Snow article on pre-suit notice notes that in commercial cases, courts often apply a strict standard requiring notice that the seller is considered in breach, beyond mere dissatisfaction. The 8-week delay likely fails this reasonableness standard. [Butler Snow, Pre-suit Notice Under UCC § 2-607(3)]

## (d) Good Faith Under § 1-304

### The Good Faith Obligation

UCC § 1-304 provides: "Every contract or duty within the Uniform Commercial Code imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement." UCC § 1-201(20) defines "good faith" as "honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing."

### Buyer's Conduct Raises Serious Good Faith Concerns

The Connecticut Law Review Note, "Good Faith Rejection of Goods in a Falling Market," explains: "All aspects of the performance of a contract must be performed in 'good faith.' Since the rejection of goods is an aspect of performance, rejection—even if otherwise rightful—must be performed in good faith."

The buyer's pattern of conduct is troubling: (1) installed and operated all widgets for 8 weeks; (2) painted 600 of them black; (3) sent emails praising performance; (4) only then raised the color issue while demanding a 40% refund and seeking to keep the widgets. This pattern appears to be what Professor Summers describes as "commercial bad faith"—seizing upon a trivial defect after having the full benefit of the goods' use.

Professor Lawrence argues that "a buyer's insistence upon rejection for a minor contract deviation in order to avoid an unfavorable bargain is an unfair use of buyers' rejection rights." While the facts here don't suggest a falling market, the buyer's delay and contradictory conduct (praising, painting, then complaining) strongly suggest bad faith.

### Indicia of Good Faith Failure

- **Delay.** Waiting 8 weeks to raise an immediately apparent defect suggests the buyer was not genuinely dissatisfied with the color but is opportunistically seeking a price reduction.
- **Painting.** Painting 600 widgets black during the 8-week period, without any communication with the seller about the color issue, is inconsistent with a good-faith intent to reject or revoke.
- **Contradictory Conduct.** Sending emails praising widget performance while later claiming the color substantially impairs value is inherently contradictory.
- **Demand for 40% Refund.** Demanding a 40% refund while keeping the goods, for a purely cosmetic color deviation, suggests the buyer is seeking a windfall rather than compensation for actual injury.

## (e) Cure Rights Under § 2-508 and Available Remedies

### Seller's Right to Cure: Timing and Reasonable Grounds

UCC § 2-508 provides two cure scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Time for Performance Has Not Expired (§ 2-508(1)).** If the seller delivered non-conforming goods before the contract delivery date, the seller has an absolute right to cure within the contract time. This may or may not apply depending on whether the contract delivery date has passed.

**Scenario 2: Time for Performance Has Expired (§ 2-508(2)).** The seller may still cure if the seller had "reasonable grounds to believe" the non-conforming tender would be acceptable. The leading case of T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison Company of New York held: "The seller is not protected unless he had 'reasonable grounds to believe' that the tender would be acceptable. Such reasonable grounds can lie in prior course of dealing, course of performance or usage of trade as well as in the particular circumstances surrounding the making of the contract." [T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison, 57 N.Y.2d 574 (1982)]

### Seller Had Reasonable Grounds to Believe Tender Would Be Acceptable

Several factors support the seller's reasonable belief:

- **Industry Practice.** Industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components. This is a "usage of trade" under UCC § 1-205 that the seller could reasonably rely upon.
- **Functional Specifications.** The contract emphasized performance criteria (load capacity, durability). The seller delivered goods meeting all such criteria. The color reference was a specification, but it was secondary to performance.
- **No Prior Objections.** The buyer never raised color issues in prior dealings, emails, or communications.
- **Reasonable Cure Offered.** The seller offered immediate replacement with a two-week lead time. In Wilson v. Scampoli, the court held that a seller had the right to substitute a conforming tender, finding that the buyer's refusal to allow cure was wrongful.

### Interaction: Cure vs. Acceptance by Conduct

A critical analytical point: § 2-508 cure rights are triggered by **rejection** of goods. If the buyer has already **accepted** the goods under § 2-606, the cure provisions of § 2-508 are not directly triggered. However, as a practical matter:

1. If the buyer attempts to reject (which is precluded by acceptance), the seller would have cure rights under § 2-508(2).
2. If the buyer revokes acceptance (which is unlikely to succeed), most courts hold the seller has no right to cure. The Kansas Comment to 84-2-508 states: "A seller has no right to cure following revocation of acceptance." [Kansas Revisor of Statutes, 84-2-508]
3. Since the buyer has accepted by conduct and cannot effectively reject or revoke, the cure question becomes moot as to the buyer's rejection rights, but relevant to the overall dispute resolution.

### The Buyer's Demand for 40% Refund While Retaining Widgets: Unsupported

The buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping the widgets is not supported by UCC damage principles.

**Section 2-714: Damages for Accepted Goods.** Under § 2-714(2), "the measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted." For a color-only deviation where functionality is unaffected:

- The difference in market value between navy widgets and charcoal gray widgets is likely minimal or zero if the goods are custom-made for a specific customer and the color does not affect their utility.
- The buyer's act of painting 600 widgets black is strong evidence that the color difference did not cause substantial value impairment.
- The proper measure would be the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets (or the market value difference, which for functional components with minor color variation is likely negligible).

In Chatlos Systems, Inc. v. National Cash Register Corporation, the court held that "the correct measure of damages is the difference between the fair market value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted." The contract price is not controlling. [Chatlos Systems v. NCR, 670 F.2d 1304 (3d Cir. 1982)]

**Section 2-717: Deduction of Damages from Price.** The buyer may deduct "all or any part of the damages resulting from any breach" from remaining payments under § 2-717. However, this deduction is limited to **actual damages**, not a subjective percentage. The buyer cannot unilaterally deduct 40% unless that represents actual proven damages. The D.C. Code commentary explains: "The buyer must give notice of his intention to withhold all or part of the price if he wishes to avoid a default." Additionally, the § 2-607(3) notice requirement must first be satisfied. [D.C. Law Library, § 28:2-717; FindLaw, New York UCC § 2-717]

**No Right to Reject or Revoke.** Since the buyer has accepted the goods and cannot effectively reject or revoke, the buyer's remedies are limited to damages under § 2-714 and deduction under § 2-717. The buyer cannot obtain a 40% refund via rejection or revocation.

---

## Available Remedies Summary

### Buyer's Available Remedies (Post-Acceptance)

Since the buyer has accepted the goods by conduct and cannot reject or revoke, the available remedies are:

1. **Damages Under § 2-714.** The buyer may recover damages for breach of express warranty (color specification). The measure is the difference in value between navy widgets and charcoal gray widgets at time of acceptance. For functionally identical goods where industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable, this difference is likely small or negligible. The cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets (to match the 600 the buyer already painted) might be a reasonable measure. [Cornell LII, UCC § 2-714]

2. **Deduction Under § 2-717.** The buyer may deduct actual damages from any remaining price due, provided proper notice is given under both § 2-607(3) (notice of breach) and § 2-717 (notice of intention to deduct). The deduction is limited to actual proven damages, not an arbitrary 40%.

3. **Incidental and Consequential Damages Under § 2-714(3).** In a proper case, the buyer may recover incidental and consequential damages (e.g., costs of repainting, if not already done). However, consequential damages may be limited or excluded by the contract under § 2-719.

### Seller's Available Remedies and Position

1. **Right to Payment.** Since the buyer has accepted the goods, the seller is entitled to the contract price, subject to any offset for proven damages. UCC § 2-607(1) provides that "the buyer must pay at the contract rate for any goods accepted."

2. **Cure Offer.** The seller's offer of immediate replacement with a two-week lead time is commercially reasonable. If the buyer rejects this offer and the seller incurs additional storage, handling, or other costs, the seller may have a claim against the buyer for those costs.

3. **No Right to Cure Under Revocation.** If the buyer attempts revocation (which is unlikely to succeed), the seller has no right to cure under most court interpretations. [Quarles & Brady, Supply Chain Survival Series]

### Specific Performance (§ 2-716) — Not Applicable

Specific performance is not relevant here, as the buyer seeks to keep the goods (not force delivery), and the goods are not unique in a way that would justify specific performance.

---

## Conclusion

The buyer did **not** effectively reject the goods under the perfect tender rule (§ 2-601). Instead, the buyer **accepted** the goods by conduct under § 2-606 through three independent grounds: (a) signifying conformity by praising performance via email; (b) failing to make an effective rejection within a reasonable time (8-week delay, especially with a 10-day inspection clause); and (c) painting 600 widgets black, an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership.

The buyer has **no right to revoke acceptance** under § 2-608. The color deviation (navy vs. charcoal gray) does not substantially impair the value of functional components where industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable. Moreover, the buyer's painting of 600 widgets constitutes a "substantial change in condition" that bars revocation.

The buyer's **notice of rejection was untimely and ineffective**. The 8-week delay is unreasonable for a visually apparent defect, the 10-day contractual inspection period reinforces this conclusion, and the emails praising performance waived any objections.

The buyer's conduct raises **serious good faith concerns** under § 1-304. Using, praising, and painting the goods for 8 weeks before raising a color objection suggests opportunistic behavior rather than genuine dissatisfaction.

Regarding **cure rights and remedies**: If the buyer's rejection were effective (which it is not), the seller would have cure rights under § 2-508(2) given industry practice and the seller's reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable. The seller's offer of immediate replacement within two weeks is a reasonable cure. The buyer's demand for a 40% refund while keeping the widgets is unsupported by UCC damage principles. The proper remedy is damages under § 2-714 measured by the difference in value between navy and charcoal gray widgets (likely minimal), which may be deducted from the price under § 2-717.

### Recommended Course

The buyer should either: (1) accept the seller's cure offer of replacement widgets; or (2) retain the widgets and seek damages under § 2-714 for the actual difference in value (likely the cost to repaint the remaining 400 widgets), subject to providing proper notice under § 2-607(3) and § 2-717. The buyer is not entitled to reject the goods, revoke acceptance, or unilaterally demand a 40% refund.
