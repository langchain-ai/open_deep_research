# Part I: Statutory Precision & Evolution of Dual Legal Status

## 1. Barbados: The Evolution of Chattel Classification (1661–1730s)

### 1A. The Foundational 1661 Barbados Slave Code

The Barbados Slave Code, formally titled **"An Act for the better ordering and governing of Negroes,"** was passed on September 24, 1661, by the Parliament of Barbados, under Governor Humphrey Walrond. It was the first comprehensive slave code in the Anglo-Atlantic world [1][2][3].

**The original 1661 manuscript has not survived.** The earliest surviving copy is the amended 1667 manuscript version [1][4].

**Precise Preamble Language on Classification:**

The preamble contains this exact statutory language, preserved in the 1667 manuscript:

> "...soe farr to protect them as wee doe mens' other goods & chattels..."

> "...an heathenish, brutish and an uncertaine dangerous kind of people..."

The preamble states that the law's purpose was to "protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels," establishing that Black slaves were to be treated as chattel property in the island's court [1][2][3][4].

The complete preamble text (from the Huntington Library 1667 manuscript) reads:

> "Former Lawes being in many clauses imperfect, and not fully comprehending the true Constitution of this Government in relation of their Slaves... an heathenish, brutish, and an uncertaine dangerous kind of people... We are not to leave them to the arbitrary, cruel, and outrageous wills of every evil disposed person, but soe farr to protect them as we doe many other goods and Chattels... have thought good to renewe and revive whatsoever we have found..."

**Key Provisions of the 1661 Code:**

- **Partus sequitur ventrem established:** "All children borne in this country shall be held bond or free only according to the condition of the mother" [4]
- **Violence by slaves:** "If any Negro or slave whatsoever shall offer any violence to any Christian by striking or the like, such Negro or slave shall for his or her first offence be severely whipped by the Constable. For his second offence of that nature he shall be severely whipped, his nose slit, and be burned in some part of his face with a hot iron" [3][4]
- **Trial by peers denied:** "And being brutish slaves, [they] deserve not, for the baseness of their condition, to be tried by the legal trial of twelve men of their peers, as the subjects of England are" [3]
- **Master's immunity for killing own slave:** "And it is further enacted and ordained that if any Negro or other slave under punishment by his master unfortunately shall suffer in life or member, which seldom happens, no person whatsoever shall be liable to any fine therefore" [3]
- **Willful killing of own slave:** If any man shall "of wantonness, or only of Bloody Mindedness, or Cruel Intention, willfully kill a Negro or other Slave of his own, he shall pay into the Publick Treasury... And he shall further by the next Justice of the Peace, be bound to the good Behaviour" [4]
- **Clothing requirement:** Masters required to provide each slave with one set of clothing per year, but no standards for diet, housing, or working conditions [1][4]

### 1B. Re-enactments: 1676, 1682, 1688

The Barbados Assembly reenacted the slave code, with minor modifications, in three subsequent acts:

| Year | Title | Nature of Changes |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| 1676 | "A Supplemental Act to a Former Act for the Better Ordering and Governing of Negroes" | Minor modifications, primarily clarifying provisions |
| 1682 | "An Act for the Governing of Negroes" | Minor modifications, expanding from 23 to 55 clauses |
| 1688 | "An Act for the Governing of Negroes" | Minor modifications, reaffirming existing framework |

The original code expanded from 23 clauses to 55 in the reenactments [1][4]. The amendments were minor and did not alter the fundamental classification of enslaved people as chattel property [1][3]. The "goods and Chattels" classification language remained consistent across all reenactments.

### 1C. Barbados Statutes in the 1730s

**No comprehensive consolidation or amendment of the core slave code occurred in the 1730s.** The 1688 reenactment remained in force throughout the decade.

The only specific Barbados statute from the 1730s identified is:

- **1731:** "An Act for the punishment of Run-away Slaves, and of Slaves who shall willfully entertain, harbour and conceal any Run-away Slaves" [5]

This act focused on the specific problem of runaway slaves and those who harbored them, reflecting ongoing concerns about slave control and escape. It did not alter the fundamental property classification.

### 1D. Functional Consequences of "Chattel" Classification in Barbados

The classification of enslaved people as **"goods and Chattels"** (movable personal property) carried specific legal consequences under 18th-century English property law:

**Debt Recovery:**
- Enslaved people could be seized by creditors to satisfy debts, just like any other movable goods [6]
- They could be "slotted into bills of sale, attachments, executions, and claims for conversion" — the ordinary legal mechanisms for movable property [6]
- This allowed speedier seizure and sale through streamlined court proceedings, without the cumbersome real actions required for real estate

**Inheritance:**
- As chattels, enslaved people passed to executors/administrators of an estate, not directly to heirs by descent
- They could be bequeathed by will as personalty, without the restrictions that applied to real estate (such as dower rights or entail)
- However, custom frequently treated slaves as passing according to the patterns of real estate even when legally classified as chattels — Handler notes that "none of the features was ever defined in any law; rather, they were implicit in any Barbados law that mentioned slaves" [7]

**Taxation:**
- Enslaved people could be taxed as personal property, with different rates and assessment methods than real estate

**Sale:**
- Enslaved people could be sold by simple transfer of possession, without the formalities (deeds, recording) required for real estate transfers
- They could be sold at auction more easily

**Rights of Masters:**
- The "chattel" classification gave masters near-absolute property rights, with the state reluctant to interfere with a master's treatment of his own property
- The 1661 code provided that if a master killed his own slave, he would only pay a fine to the Treasury and be bound to good behavior — no serious criminal liability [4]

**Insolvency:**
- As chattels, slaves were fully available for the satisfaction of creditors' claims, making them more liquid and easily convertible to cash than real estate [6]

## 2. Jamaica: The Shift from Chattel to Real Estate Classification (1664–1730s)

### 2A. The 1664 Jamaica Slave Code

**Full Title:** "An Act for the better ordering and Governing of Negro Slaves" [8][9]

**Date:** Passed November 2, 1664, consented to by Governor Thomas Modyford [8][9]

**Relation to Barbados Code:** The 1664 Jamaica Slave Code was based on the Barbados Slave Act of 1661. Governor Thomas Modyford — a former Barbados planter and slaveholder who had been Speaker of the Barbados Assembly — played a pivotal role. He traveled from Barbados to Jamaica, bringing several hundred fellow Bajan planters who accelerated Jamaica's transition to plantation agriculture [8][9].

**Classification Language in the Preamble:** The 1664 code did **not** contain explicit property classification language designating enslaved people as chattels or real estate in its operative clauses [9]. The preamble closely tracked Barbados's language:

> "...an heathenish brutish & an uncertaine & dangerous kind of people"

The full preamble states:

> "these formal Laws being in many clauses imperfect & not fully comprehending the true constitution of this Government in relation to theyre Slaves especially the Negroes an heathenish brutish & an uncertaine & dangerous kind of people... Yet wee well know by the right rule of reason & order wee are not to leave them to the arbitrary cruell and outrageous will of every evill disposed person But soe farr to protect them as wee doe mens' other goods & chattels & alsoe some what further as being created men though without the knowledge of God in theis world" [8][9]

**Critical Distinction:** This passage uses the phrase "mens' other goods & chattels" in the **rhetorical preamble** rather than in operative clauses. The code is not typically cited as having formally classified enslaved people as chattel property by statute — the language establishes a comparison for protective purposes rather than a legal definition [8][9].

**Key Provisions of the 1664 Code:**

- **Violence against Christians:** First offense = severely whipped; second offense = severely whipped, nose slit, burned in some part of the face; third offense = such greater corporal punishment as they shall think meet to inflict [8][9]
- **Movement restrictions:** No Master or Mistress shall give slaves leave on Sundays, holy days, or in the night time to go out of plantations without a ticket [8][9]
- **Weapon searches:** Overseers to search slave quarters biweekly for runaway slaves, weapons, and stolen goods [8][9]
- **Runaways:** Must be returned within set time frames under threat of heavy fines [8][9]
- **Reward for slave collaborators:** Slaves who help capture fugitives may receive freedom or rewards with a "badge of a redd crosse on his Arme whereby hee may be knowne & cherished" [8][9]
- **Insurrection:** If any Negro shall make insurrection or rise in rebellion, the Governor shall proceed by Marshall Law and punish by Death or other paines as their crimes shall deserve [8][9]
- **Master's immunity for punishment death:** If any slave under punishment by his Master for running away or any other crime shall suffer in life or member, no person whatsoever shall be accountable to any Law therefore [8][9]
- **Willful killing of own slave:** If any man shall wantonly or of cruel intention willfully kill a Slave of his own, he shall pay to the King 30 pounds sterling [8][9]
- **Annual reading requirement:** The code was to be "read & published in all respective parish Churches in this Island the first Sunday in March & the first Sunday in September in every yeare ensueing" [8][9]

### 2B. The 1696 Jamaica Act: The Shift to Real Estate Classification

**Full Title:** "An Act for the better Order and Government of Slaves" [10][11]

**Key Classification Clause:**

> "for payment of Debts and Legacies all Slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other Goods and Chattles are" [10][11]

**The Real Estate Designation:** This is a critical point of scholarly interpretation. Historian Edward B. Rugemer states: "The 1696 Jamaican slave code designated enslaved people as real estate, making them integral to economic transactions like debts and dowries" [6][12]. The actual text of the 1696 Act treats slaves "as all other Goods and Chattles" for debt payment purposes [10][11], but the scholarly consensus (Rugemer, Handler) is that this act designated enslaved people as real estate for inheritance and descent purposes. This parallels the Virginia 1705 Act ("An Act declaring Slaves to be real Estate"), which declared slaves real estate for inheritance but still made them liable for debts as personal estate [6][13].

**Key Provisions of the 1696 Act:**

- No person shall give leave to any slave to go out of their plantation without a Ticket or White Servant [10][11]
- All Slaves shall have Cloths once every year, on or before the Twenty Fifth Day of December, upon Penalty of Five Shillings [10][11]
- If any slave shall offer any Violence to any White Person, such Slave shall be punished at the Discretion of Two Justices and Three Freeholders, who may inflict Death or any other Punishment [10][11]
- If any Slave or Slaves shall run away and continue but for the space of twelve months, shall be deemed rebellious, and such slave or slaves so taken as a punishment for their crime, shall be transported [10][11]
- No Slave shall be free by becoming a Christian [10][11]
- **Critical classification clause:** "And for payment of Debts and Legacies all Slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other Goods and Chattles are" [10][11]
- All Masters and Mistresses shall endeavor the Instruction of their Slaves in the Principles of the Christian Religion [10][11]

### 2C. The 1717 Jamaica Act: Punishment and Compensation

**Full Title:** "An Act for the more effectual punishing of Crimes committed by Slaves" [14][15]

**Key Provisions:**

- **Compensation cap:** No one Slave shall be appraised at above the Sum of Forty Pounds (for compensation purposes when judicially executed) [14][15]
- **Restriction on master's power:** No Slave shall be dismembered at the Will and Pleasure of his Master... under the Penalty of One hundred Pounds [14][15]
- **State-ordered dismemberment:** If any Slave shall run away... such Slave to be punished, by cutting off one of the Feet [14][15]
- **Gaming restrictions:** No Retailers of Rum and Rum-punch shall suffer any Negroe, or other Slave to game or use any Sort of Play within their houses... under the Penalty of forty Shillings [14][15]
- **Assembly restrictions:** No Proprietor, Attorney, or Overseer shall suffer any Number of strange Negroes, exceeding Five, to assemble on his plantation... under the Penalties... Ten Pounds or Five Pounds [14][15]
- **Free Black identification requirement:** Free Mulatto, Negro, or Indian shall furnish with a Certificate and a publick Badge of a blue Cross to be worn upon the Right Shoulder [14][15]
- **Drumming prohibition:** Every Justice of the Peace, or Military Officer to enter into any Plantation, Settlement, or any other Place to suppress and prevent all Drumming and other noises [14][15]

**Compensation Mechanism:** The 1717 Act established a structured legal approach for punishing crimes committed by enslaved people, including capital crimes punishable by death, with procedures for appraising and compensating slave owners for executed slaves valued up to forty pounds [14][15].

### 2D. Jamaica Statutes in the 1730s

The following Jamaica statutes from the 1730s have been identified:

- **1725:** "An Act to inflict further and other Punishments on the Transgressors... for the preventing the transportation of Slaves" [5]
- **1739:** "An Act for confirming the Articles executed by Colonel John Guthrie, Lieutenant Francis Sadler, and Cudjoe the Commander of the Rebels... and for granting Freedom to Five Negroes who were Guides to Parties" [5][6]

The 1739 Act confirmed the peace treaty with the Maroons, ending the First Maroon War (1728–1739) [6]. This was a significant event as the Jamaican assembly formally recognized Maroon autonomy in exchange for their assistance in capturing runaway slaves.

**No comprehensive consolidated slave code was enacted in Jamaica during the 1730s.** The 1696 Act (as amended by the 1717 Act and subsequent legislation) remained the governing framework throughout the decade.

### 2E. Functional Consequences of "Real Estate" Classification in Jamaica

The classification of enslaved people as **real estate** (rather than chattel) carried different legal consequences under 18th-century English property law:

**Inheritance and Descent:**
- As real estate, enslaved people would "descend to the Heirs and Widows of Persons departing this Life, as Lands of Inheritance in Fee Simple" (using the parallel language of the Virginia 1705 Act) [6][13]
- This meant enslaved people would pass to the legal heir (typically the eldest son under primogeniture) rather than being distributed by executors as personalty
- Widows would receive a dower interest (life estate in one-third) in enslaved people, just as they would in land

**Debt Recovery:**
- Under traditional English law, real estate was protected from unsecured creditors, whereas chattels were not [6]
- However, the 1696 Act included the proviso making slaves liable for debts "as all other Goods and Chattles" — a compromise provision [10][11]
- **The 1732 British Debt Recovery Act (5 Geo. II, c. 28)** overrode local classifications by requiring that "houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates" be liable to seizure for debts [16][17]

**Dower Rights:**
- A widow's dower right (life estate in one-third of the deceased husband's real property) applied to enslaved people classified as real estate
- This meant a widow had the use of one-third of the enslaved people during her lifetime, with the remainder to the heirs

**Sale:**
- As real estate, enslaved people could not be sold as easily as chattels — they required more formal conveyancing processes (deeds, recording)
- However, the 1696 Act removed the requirement for written records of slave sales [10][11]
- The 1732 Debt Recovery Act largely nullified the practical difference by requiring execution sales of slaves and land as chattels for debt [16][17]

**Entail:**
- Where slaves were classified as real estate, they could be entailed (limited to a specific line of heirs, preventing sale by the current holder)
- This protected slave wealth within families across generations but limited the ability to sell or mortgage individual enslaved people

**The Purpose of Real Estate Classification:**
- "The purpose of declaring slaves to be real estate if their owner died without leaving a will was to prevent court-appointed executors from selling them off to satisfy debts" [6][12]
- It ensured that enslaved people (representing enormous capital value) remained within the family rather than being liquidated by court officials

## 3. Comparative Summary: Chattel (Barbados) vs. Real Estate (Jamaica)

| Legal Consequence | Barbados (Chattel) | Jamaica (Real Estate) | Effect of 1732 Debt Recovery Act |
|-------------------|-------------------|----------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Intestate descent** | To executors for distribution among next of kin | Directly to heir at law (eldest son) | Overridden for debt — creditors get priority |
| **Dower for widows** | None — widows get personalty share | Life estate in 1/3 of slaves | Not directly overridden |
| **Debt recovery** | Full seizure for debts | Protected from unsecured creditors | Made slaves liable as chattel across all colonies |
| **Sale** | By simple transfer or auction | Required formal deed | Required execution sales for debt |
| **Entail** | Not possible (chattel) | Possible (real estate) | Not directly affected |
| **Taxation** | As personal property | As real property | Not directly affected |

## 4. The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729)

### 4A. Primary Manuscript Source

**Location:** Northumberland Archives [43][44][45]

**Description:** The original contemporaneous document containing the Yorke-Talbot Opinion is preserved in the Northumberland Archives. A PDF transcription of the original is available from the Northumberland Archives website [44].

**Citation for the manuscript:**
Yorke-Talbot Opinion (24 Jan. 1729), Northumberland Archives, [shelfmark].

### 4B. Standard Printed Versions

**Hargrave's State Trials:**
The opinion is cited in Hargrave's State Trials, specifically in the printed report of the Somerset case (State Trials, 20:70) [46].

**Cobbett's Complete Collection of State Trials:**
The opinion appears in T.B. Howell (ed.), Cobbett's Complete Collection of State Trials and Proceedings for High Treason and Other Crimes and Misdemeanors [46].

**Citation for the printed version:**
Opinion of Attorney General Yorke and Solicitor General Talbot (24 Jan. 1729), in T.B. Howell, A Complete Collection of State Trials, vol. __ (London, 1816), col(s). __.

### 4C. Full Text of the Opinion

> "We are of opinion that a slave, coming from the West Indies to Great Britain or Ireland, with or without his master, doth not become free; And that his master's property or right in him is not thereby determined or varied; And that baptism doth not bestow freedom upon him, or make any alteration in his temporal condition in these Kingdoms. We are also of opinion that his master may legally compel him to return again to the plantations." — Yorke and Talbot, 1729 [44][45]

### 4D. Scholarly Discussion

The opinion was first published in the Boston Gazette and widely circulated in colonial newspapers [44]. It was sought by slave merchants following judicial decisions by Lord Chief Justice Holt (Smith v. Gould, 1706), which challenged the legal basis of slavery in England [45]. It was "widely cited to support slavery under common law for nearly 40 years" [45]. As Nicholas Leah notes, the opinion was "not a court judgment" but had significant practical influence [44].

# Part III: Deepened Analytical Framing — Constitutional Significance & Cautions

## 1. Warning Against Misusing the Molasses Act (1733)

### 1A. The Molasses Act Contains No Language About Enslaved People

The Molasses Act of 1733 (6 Geo. II, c. 13) is **entirely a trade regulation and revenue statute**. Its text contains **no mention whatsoever of enslaved people, slavery, or the slave trade** [28][29][47].

**What the Act actually does:**
- Imposes duties on foreign rum (9d/gallon), molasses (6d/gallon), and sugar (5s/hundredweight) [28][29]
- Requires proper customs entries and payment of duties before goods can be landed [28][29]
- Imposes penalties including forfeiture of goods, fines, and imprisonment for smuggling [28][29]
- Was set for a duration of five years from June 24, 1733 [28]

### 1B. Why It Is a Common Error to Cite the Molasses Act for Property-Status Claims

**The error:** Some non-specialist researchers have attempted to use the Molasses Act as evidence that Parliament was legislating on the legal status of enslaved people in the 1730s, or that the Act somehow recognized enslaved people as property.

**Why this is wrong:**
1. The Act's text contains zero language about persons, property, chattels, or legal status
2. The Act is about **trade duties on commodities** (rum, molasses, sugar), not about the legal status of the laborers who produced those commodities
3. It is a serious scholarly error to cite this Act in connection with the property-status of enslaved people

### 1C. What Can and Cannot Be Said About the Molasses Act

**Cannot be said:**
- That the Molasses Act dealt with enslaved people as property
- That it classified slaves as chattel
- That it regulated slavery in any direct way

**Can be said:**
- The Act was part of the broader mercantilist system that sustained the Atlantic slave economy
- The molasses trade was integral to the Triangle Trade: molasses from the West Indies was distilled into rum in New England, which was shipped to Africa to purchase enslaved people, who were then sold in the West Indies and the South
- As John Adams reflected in 1818, molasses was "an essential ingredient in American independence" [29]
- The Act protected British West Indian sugar planters who relied entirely on enslaved labor
- But the Act itself was about trade duties, not slave status, and should not be cited for property-status claims

## 2. The Colonial Debts Act of 1732: Imperial Legal Harmonization

### 2A. What the Act Did

The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 (5 Geo. II, c. 7), formally titled "An act for the more easy recovery of debts in his Majesty's plantations and colonies in America," was one of the most significant Parliamentary interventions into colonial property law in the 1730s [16][17].

**The critical provision regarding enslaved people:**

> "the houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates within the plantations belonging to any person indebted shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts... shall be assets for the satisfaction thereof... and shall be subject to the like remedies... for seizing, extending, selling or disposing... towards satisfaction of such debts" [16][17]

**Mechanism of operation:**
1. A British creditor could make an affidavit before a magistrate in Britain
2. The affidavit was transmitted to the colonial court
3. It had the same force as a judgment of an English court
4. The colonial court was required to authorize seizure and sale of the debtor's lands, houses, and enslaved people [16][17]

### 2B. How It Functioned as a Tool of Imperial Legal Harmonization

**1. It dismantled English inheritance protections for land:** Traditional English law had protected freehold land from unsecured creditor claims during a debtor's life and through inheritance proceedings. The English legal system had historically shielded family land from economic fluctuations. The 1732 Act abolished these protections for the colonies [48][49].

**2. It treated real property, houses, and slaves equivalently to chattel property for debt recovery:** As Claire Priest explains: "The 1732 Debt Recovery Act abolished legal distinctions between land and chattel property in relation to creditor claims and required courts to treat slaves as chattel in debt proceedings, facilitating creditor protections and expanding credit markets" [48][49].

**3. It standardized creditor-friendly laws across all colonies:** Before 1732, colonial assemblies had varied laws and some had actively obstructed debt collection suits. The Act overrode these local variations and imposed a uniform imperial framework [16][48].

**4. It expanded trade credit by London merchants:** By the early 1730s, London merchants had significantly increased the level of credit and loan services to American and West Indian colonial plantation operators. The Act gave those merchants legal assurance of accessing colonial assets (including enslaved people) in the event of default [16][50].

### 2C. How the Act Integrated the Empire's Legal Architecture

The Act functioned as a **unifying legal mechanism** across the fragmented colonial slave codes:

- While each colony had its own slave codes with different property classifications (chattel in Barbados, real estate in Jamaica), the 1732 Act created a uniform imperial framework for treating enslaved people as assets that could be reached by creditors across colonial boundaries [16][48]
- A British merchant could use the affidavit procedure to reach enslaved people held as assets in **any British colony**, regardless of that colony's particular slave code's rules about property classification [16]
- The Act thereby served as a bridge between English common law and colonial slave codes, integrating the empire's legal architecture for credit and debt recovery despite the fragmented local codes

### 2D. Longevity of the Act

The clause respecting the seizure of Negro slaves for purposes of satisfying debts was repealed in 1797, but the remainder of the Act relating to the seizure of land to settle debts remained in force in many parts of Canada well into the 20th century [16].

## 3. Parliamentary Supremacy vs. Colonial Autonomy on Slavery

### 3A. The General Pattern: Parliament Rarely Legislated Directly on Slavery

In the 1730s, the British Parliament **generally did not directly legislate on the institution of slavery in the colonies**. The regulation of enslaved people was left largely to colonial assemblies. Each colony developed its own slave codes, beginning with Barbados in 1661 [1][51].

As one source notes: "In the 1661 Barbados slave law, the first comprehensive slave code of the Anglo-Atlantic world, lawmakers admitted that 'in all the body of [English] lawe [there is] noe tract to guide us where to walk nor any rule sett as how to governe such slaves'" [1][4].

The result was a **patchwork of colonial slave codes**, not a unified imperial slave code [51].

### 3B. The Limits of Colonial Legislative Power

Despite the absence of direct Parliamentary legislation, colonial assemblies did **not** have free rein. Colonial slave laws operated within the constraints of English common law. As Holly Brewer's scholarship demonstrates: "American slavery therefore did not emerge 'beyond the line' of English justice, but within it" [52].

The English common law had evolved over the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries to incorporate slavery as a legal institution. English high court judges "provided the legal foundation for an imperial common law of slavery that allowed people to be deemed absolute property" [52]. This was done through case law, notably the 1677 case **Butts v. Penny**, which established precedents treating enslaved people as absolute property recoverable like goods [52][53].

As Brewer argues: "When Parliament failed to pass an imperial slave code with such provisions, Charles II turned to the courts. English common law became an instrument—the best the Stuart kings of England had—to create new laws, and to both expand their own power and to legitimate slavery. The definition of people as property was closely connected to absolutism, both in theory and practice" [52].

### 3C. The Colonial Debts Act as a Key Exception

The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 represented a **key exception** to the pattern of Parliamentary non-intervention — a clear assertion that Parliament could and would regulate colonial property law, including the legal status of enslaved people. As Thomas Jefferson noted in his annotated copy, the Act represented "Britain's latest imperial encroachment within the existing body of colonial trade restrictions already in place" [17].

The Act asserted metropolitan authority by:
1. Overriding colonial laws that had protected local debtors or obstructed suits
2. Imposing a uniform legal rule across all colonies about what property was liable for debts
3. Explicitly including enslaved people (using the term "negroes") alongside land and houses as assets subject to seizure [16][17]

### 3D. The Constitutional Relationship Between English Common Law and Colonial Slave Codes

The relationship was complex and contested:

- The English Bill of Rights of 1689 had established parliamentary sovereignty and codified basic rights for all subjects [54]
- Colonial slave codes were seen as "local" law — enactments of colonial assemblies that operated within the framework of, but were subordinate to, Parliamentary authority [51][54]
- However, the issue of whether Parliament could or would override local slave law was largely theoretical until the **Somerset case of 1772**, when Lord Mansfield ruled that slavery must be authorized by "positive law" (i.e., statutory enactment) rather than by common law [55][56]

**The 1730s legal environment thus existed in a state of ambiguity:**
- Colonial slave codes provided "positive law" authorization for slavery in the colonies
- English common law had evolved through court decisions to recognize enslaved people as property
- Parliament had not passed any comprehensive imperial slave code
- The Colonial Debts Act (1732) was one of the rare Parliamentary acts that touched on the legal treatment of enslaved people, but only indirectly through property/debt law
- The constitutional relationship between English and colonial law regarding slavery remained ambiguous and contested

### 3E. Caution for the Novelist: Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Claiming the Molasses Act was about slavery or slave property:** This is incorrect. The Molasses Act contains no language about enslaved people. It was a trade duty on molasses, rum, and sugar imports [28][29]. It is a serious error to cite it as evidence of Parliament legislating on slavery/slave property.

2. **Assuming Parliament regularly legislated on slavery in the colonies in the 1730s:** In fact, Parliament rarely did so in this period. Slavery regulation was left largely to colonial assemblies [1][51].

3. **Treating colonial slave codes as wholly autonomous from English law:** While colonial assemblies enacted slave codes, these operated within the constraints of English common law [52].

4. **Anachronistically applying Somerset (1772) backward:** The Somerset ruling that slavery required positive law was not the law in the 1730s. In this period, English common law had evolved to recognize enslaved people as property through cases like Butts v. Penny (1677) [52][55].

## 4. Retained Content from Previous Report: South Carolina 1740 Negro Act

The South Carolina Negro Act of 1740, while enacted just after the 1730s, remains the most explicit statutory language classifying enslaved people as chattel property in any British colony [57][58].

**Exact statutory language:**

> "All negroes, Indians (free Indians in amity with this government excepted), mulattoes or mestizos... shall be and they are hereby declared to be and remain for ever hereafter absolute slaves, and shall follow the condition of the mother; and shall be deemed, taken, and adjudged to be chattels personal in the hands of their owners and possessors, and their executors, administrators, and assigns, to all intents, constructions, and purposes whatsoever" [57][58]

**Source:** The Statutes at Large of South Carolina, vol. 7, ed. David J. McCord (Columbia, S.C.: A.S. Johnston, 1840) [58].

This Act was passed in direct response to the Stono Rebellion of September 1739. It contains comprehensive provisions including:
- The principle of *partus sequitur ventrem* (status follows the mother)
- Punishment for striking white persons (death for third offense)
- Summary trials by justices and freeholders
- Prohibition on teaching slaves to write (£100 forfeit)
- Maximum 15 hours of labor per day
- Prohibition on firearms without license

## 6. Practical Guidance for the Novel

### 6A. Key Facts for Your Novel's Timeline

| Year | Event | Relevance |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 1729 | Yorke-Talbot Opinion | Legal opinion that slaves remained property in England; not a court ruling but influential |
| 1731 | Barbados Runaway Slaves Act | Focused on punishment of runaways; no change to property classification |
| 1732 | Colonial Debts Act (5 Geo. II, c. 7) | Made "houses, lands, negroes" liable for debts across all colonies |
| 1733 | Molasses Act (6 Geo. II, c. 13) | Trade duties only; no language about enslaved people |
| 1739 | Stono Rebellion (South Carolina) | Led to the 1740 Negro Act |
| 1739 | Jamaica Maroon Treaty | Ended First Maroon War; Maroons granted autonomy in exchange for returning runaways |

### 6B. What Characters Could Legitimately Debate

**An English lawyer arguing that a slave brought to England is free:**
- Could cite Smith v. Gould (1706), where Lord Chief Justice Holt ruled "as soon as a negro comes to England he is free"
- Could argue that "England was too pure an Air for Slaves to breathe in"
- The legal situation was genuinely uncertain — this ambiguity was not resolved until Somerset (1772)

**A colonial planter insisting the slave remains property:**
- Could cite the Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729) that "a slave coming from the West Indies... doth not become free"
- Could argue that baptism does not confer freedom
- The opinion, while not a court judgment, was widely cited for nearly 40 years

**A merchant discussing the Colonial Debts Act:**
- Could explain that the 1732 Act made enslaved people reachable by creditors across colonial jurisdictions
- Could note that it overrode local property classifications, treating slaves as chattel for debt recovery
- Thomas Jefferson later described similar acts as "one of the Highest Acts of Legislature that one Country could exercise over another"

### 6C. The Three Defining Features of Slave Status (Handler)

Jerome Handler identifies three features that implicitly defined slave status across the British colonies:
1. **Chattel property** — enslavement meant being owned as property
2. **Lifetime (or permanent) servitude** — slavery was not a term-limited condition
3. **Inheritance of slave condition from an enslaved mother** — *partus sequitur ventrem*

Handler notes that "none of the features was ever defined in any law; rather, they were implicit in any Barbados law that mentioned slaves" [7].
