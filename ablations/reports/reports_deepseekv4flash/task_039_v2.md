# Independent Directors Under NASDAQ Listing Standards: Comprehensive Research Report

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive and technically precise analysis of the definition of an independent director under NASDAQ listing standards, addressing all eight areas specified in the research brief. The analysis is based exclusively on primary sources, including the official NASDAQ Listing Center Rulebook (Rule 5600 Series), SEC releases approving NASDAQ rule changes, SEC Rule 10A-3, and authoritative legal practice guides. All rule numbers, thresholds, time periods, and exceptions are explicitly enumerated.

## Section 1: Eligibility Criteria — The Core Definition Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 1.1 The Two-Part Test for Independence

Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2), an "Independent Director" is defined through a two-part test that requires satisfaction of both an objective component and a subjective component [1][8][9]:

**Part A — The Subjective Affirmative Determination by the Board:**
> "Independent Director" means a person other than an Executive Officer or employee of the Company **or any other individual having a relationship which, in the opinion of the Company's board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment** in carrying out the responsibilities of a director.

**IM-5605** elaborates: "The board has a responsibility to make an affirmative determination that no such relationships exist through the application of Rule 5605(a)(2)." [1]

**Part B — The Objective Bright-Line Criteria:**
Rule 5605(a)(2) continues: "The following persons shall not be considered independent:" — listing paragraphs (A) through (G) which set forth per se disqualifications. These are objective measures that "provide transparency to investors and Companies, facilitate uniform application of the rules, and ease administration." [1][8]

### 1.2 Board Process Requirements

NASDAQ expects a robust board process for making independence determinations, typically including [3][4][9]:
- Annual director and officer questionnaires
- A relationship inventory
- A committee overlay review (to assess additional criteria for audit, compensation, and nominating committees)
- A board resolution reflecting the affirmative determinations

**Critical Note:** The bright-line tests are not determinative. A director who meets all bright-line criteria is not automatically presumed independent — the board must still make an affirmative determination that no material relationship exists. [3][9]

### 1.3 Factors Considered in the Materiality Determination

In assessing whether a "material relationship" exists, the board should consider all relevant facts and circumstances, including [1][8]:
- **Stock ownership:** Ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board finding of independence. "Because Nasdaq does not believe that ownership of Company stock by itself would preclude a board finding of independence, it is not included in the aforementioned objective factors." [1]
- **Family relationships:** Any familial relationship that could create a sense of obligation or loyalty must be evaluated
- **Business relationships:** Direct or indirect business relationships between the director (or an affiliate of the director) and the company or its affiliates
- **Professional relationships:** Service as legal counsel, consultant, or other professional service provider
- **Charitable relationships:** Significant charitable contributions by the company to an organization with which the director is affiliated
- **Past relationships:** The board considers both current and past relationships through the lookback periods codified in the bright-line rules

### 1.4 Scope of "Company" and "Executive Officer"

Per IM-5605 [1]:
- **"Company"** includes any parent or subsidiary of the Company. "The term 'parent or subsidiary' is intended to cover entities the Company controls and consolidates with the Company's financial statements as filed with the Commission (but not if the Company reflects such entity solely as an investment in its financial statements)."
- **"Executive Officer"** means those officers covered in Rule 16a-1(f) under the Act.

### 1.5 Definition of "Family Member" (Post-2020 Amendment)

Per Rule 5605(a)(2), as amended effective February 13, 2020 (SR-NASDAQ-2019-049) [1][5][6][7]:

> **"Family Member"** means a person's **spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers and fathers-in-law, sons and daughters-in-law, brothers and sisters-in-law, and anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home.**

The complete enumerated list of all relationships constituting a "Family Member" is:
- Spouse
- Parents
- Children (excluding stepchildren — see 2020 Amendment below)
- Siblings
- Mothers and fathers-in-law
- Sons and daughters-in-law
- Brothers and sisters-in-law
- Anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares such person's home

**The 2020 Amendment (SR-NASDAQ-2019-049, effective February 13, 2020):** [5][6][7]

The amendment made two key changes to the prior definition (which included "a person's spouse, parents, children and siblings, whether by blood, marriage or adoption, or anyone residing in such person's home"):

**Change 1 — Stepchildren Excluded:** The new definition excludes stepchildren from the per se Family Member definition. Nasdaq's rationale: "Nasdaq believes that a director's relationship with their stepchildren may or may not interfere with the director's exercise of independent judgment based on the particular facts and circumstances of the situation." However, if a stepchild shares the director's home, they are still captured under the "anyone who shares such person's home" prong. [5][6][7]

**Change 2 — Domestic Employees Excluded:** The new definition excludes domestic employees who share a director's home. Nasdaq's rationale: "Nasdaq believes that the definition of a Family Member should not include a domestic employee who shares a director's home because this definition is intended to capture familial, not commercial, relationships." [5][6]

The amended definition is now "identical to NYSE's definition of an immediate family member." [5][7]

The amendment filing explicitly states: "the proposed rule change to Listing Rule 5605(a)(2) will not affect the additional independence criteria for audit committee members set forth in Listing Rule 5605(c)(2)." [5]

**IM-5605 adds:** "In the context of the definition of Family Member under Rule 5605(a)(2), the reference to marriage is intended to capture relationships specified in the Rule (parents, children and siblings) that arise as a result of marriage, such as 'in-law' relationships." [1]

## Section 2: Disqualification Criteria — The Complete Bright-Line Disqualifications Under Rule 5605(a)(2)

The following persons shall **not** be considered independent under Rule 5605(a)(2), paragraphs (A) through (G) [1][8][9]:

### 2.1 Employment Relationships — Paragraph (A)

> "(A) a director who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the Company"

**Lookback Period:** Three years, commencing on the date the relationship ceases. [1]

**Exception — Interim Executive Officer Employment:** IM-5605 provides: "For purposes of paragraph (A) of the Rule, employment by a director as an Executive Officer on an interim basis shall not disqualify that director from being considered independent following such employment, provided the interim employment did not last longer than one year. A director would not be considered independent while serving as an interim officer." [1]

### 2.2 Compensation Threshold — Paragraph (B)

> "(B) a director who accepted or who has a Family Member who accepted any compensation from the Company in excess of $120,000 during any period of twelve consecutive months within the three years preceding the determination of independence, other than the following:
> - (i) compensation for board or board committee service;
> - (ii) compensation paid to a Family Member who is an employee (other than an Executive Officer) of the Company; or
> - (iii) benefits under a tax-qualified retirement plan, or non-discretionary compensation.
>
> Provided, however, that in addition to the requirements contained in this paragraph (B), audit committee members are also subject to additional, more stringent requirements under Rule 5605(c)(2)." [1]

**Complete List of Three Exclusions/Exceptions from the $120,000 Compensation Threshold:**

1. **Compensation for board or board committee service** — Director and committee fees are excluded. IM-5605 clarifies: "Paragraph (B) of the Rule is generally intended to capture situations where a compensation is made directly to (or for the benefit of) the director or a Family Member of the director. For example, consulting or personal service contracts with a director or Family Member of the director would be analyzed under paragraph (B) of the Rule." [1]

2. **Compensation paid to a Family Member who is an employee (other than an Executive Officer) of the Company** — If a director's child (who is not an executive officer) works for the company, that salary is not counted toward the $120,000 threshold. [1]

3. **Benefits under a tax-qualified retirement plan, or non-discretionary compensation** — Standard retirement benefits and non-discretionary compensation arrangements are excluded. Staff Interpretation Letter 2012-2 confirms that SERP payments that are legal obligations not contingent on continued service are "non-discretionary" and excluded. [8][9]

**Additional IM-5605 Guidance on What Is Not Considered Disqualifying Compensation:** [1]
- Non-preferential payments made in the ordinary course of providing business services (such as payments of interest or proceeds related to banking services or loans by a Company that is a financial institution, or payment of claims on a policy by a Company that is an insurance company)
- Payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities
- Loans permitted under Section 13(k) of the Act
- All of the above are excluded "as long as the payments are non-compensatory in nature."

**What IS considered indirect compensation:** "Political contributions to the campaign of a director or a Family Member of the director would be considered indirect compensation under paragraph (B)." [1]

**Interim Executive Officer Compensation:** IM-5605 provides: "Similarly, for purposes of paragraph (B) of the Rule, compensation received by a director for former service as an interim Executive Officer need not be considered as compensation in determining independence after such service, provided such interim employment did not last longer than one year." However, the board must still consider whether such former employment and compensation would interfere with independent judgment. [1]

### 2.3 Family Member of Executive Officer — Paragraph (C)

> "(C) a director who is a Family Member of an individual who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the Company as an Executive Officer" [1]

**Lookback Period:** Three years, commencing on the date the Executive Officer's employment relationship ceases. [1]

### 2.4 Business Relationships — Paragraph (D)

> "(D) a director who is, or has a Family Member who is, a partner in, or a controlling Shareholder or an Executive Officer of, any organization to which the Company made, or from which the Company received, payments for property or services in the current or any of the past three fiscal years that exceed 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues for that year, or **$200,000, whichever is more**, other than the following:
> - (i) payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities; or
> - (ii) payments under non-discretionary charitable contribution matching programs." [1]

**Two Thresholds (whichever is more):**
- 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues for that year, OR
- $200,000

**Two Exclusions/Exceptions from the Business Relationship Disqualification:**

1. **Payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities** — Passive investment returns are excluded.
2. **Payments under non-discretionary charitable contribution matching programs** — Matching gift programs are excluded.

**IM-5605 Detailed Guidance on Paragraph (D):** [1]
- "Paragraph (D) of the Rule is generally intended to capture payments to an entity with which the director or Family Member of the director is affiliated by serving as a partner, controlling Shareholder or Executive Officer of such entity."
- "Under exceptional circumstances, such as where a director has direct, significant business holdings, it may be appropriate to apply the corporate measurements in paragraph (D), rather than the individual measurements of paragraph (B). Issuers should contact Nasdaq if they wish to apply the Rule in this manner."
- "The reference to a partner in paragraph (D) is not intended to include limited partners."
- "The independence requirements of paragraph (D) of the Rule are broader than Rule 10A-3(e)(8) under the Act."
- "Under paragraph (D), a director who is, or who has a Family Member who is, an Executive Officer of a charitable organization may not be considered independent if the Company makes payments to the charity in excess of the greater of 5% of the charity's revenues or $200,000."
- "Nasdaq encourages Companies to consider other situations where a director or their Family Member and the Company each have a relationship with the same charity when assessing director independence."

**Comparison with NYSE:** [9] Nasdaq's threshold (greater of 5% or $200,000) is more stringent than NYSE's (greater of $1 million or 2% of the other company's consolidated gross revenues).

**Law firm analysis on legal payments:** "For purposes of determining whether a lawyer is eligible to serve on an audit committee, Rule 10A-3 under the Act generally provides that any partner in a law firm that receives payments from the issuer is ineligible to serve on that issuer's audit committee. In determining whether a director may be considered independent for purposes other than the audit committee, payments to a law firm would generally be considered under Rule 5605(a)(2), which looks to whether the payment exceeds the greater of 5% of the recipient's gross revenues or $200,000; however, if the firm is a sole proprietorship, Rule 5605(a)(2)(B), which looks to whether the payment exceeds $120,000, applies."

### 2.5 Interlocking Directorates / Compensation Committee Interlocks — Paragraph (E)

> "(E) a director of the Company who is, or has a Family Member who is, employed as an Executive Officer of another entity where at any time during the past three years any of the Executive Officers of the Company serve on the compensation committee of such other entity" [1]

**Lookback Period:** Three years, commencing on the date the interlocking relationship ceases. [1]

This captures "compensation committee interlocks" — where a company's executive officers serve on the compensation committee of another entity that employs the director (or the director's family member) as an executive officer.

### 2.6 Auditor Affiliation — Paragraph (F)

> "(F) a director who is, or has a Family Member who is, a current partner of the Company's outside auditor, or was a partner or employee of the Company's outside auditor who worked on the Company's audit at any time during any of the past three years" [1]

**Lookback Period:** 
- **Current partners:** Always disqualified (no lookback needed for current status)
- **Former partners or employees who worked on the audit:** Three years

### 2.7 Investment Company Special Rule — Paragraph (G)

> "(G) in the case of an investment company, in lieu of paragraphs (A)-(F), a director who is an 'interested person' of the Company as defined in Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act of 1940, other than in his or her capacity as a member of the board of directors or any board committee." [1]

IM-5605 confirms: "Paragraph (G) of the Rule provides a different measurement for independence for investment companies in order to harmonize with the Investment Company Act of 1940." [1]

### 2.8 Audit Committee-Specific Additional Disqualifications — Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)

Under Rule 5605(c)(2)(A), each audit committee member must meet heightened requirements beyond the general independence criteria [1][8][9]:
- (i) be an Independent Director as defined under Rule 5605(a)(2);
- (ii) meet the criteria for independence set forth in **Rule 10A-3(b)(1) under the Act** (subject to exemptions in Rule 10A-3(c)) — meaning they must not accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee from the Company other than for board service, and must not be an affiliated person of the Company;
- (iii) **not have participated in the preparation of the financial statements** of the Company or any current subsidiary of the Company at any time during the past three years; and
- (iv) **be able to read and understand fundamental financial statements**, including a Company's balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement.

Additionally, each Company must have at least one member who has "past employment experience in finance or accounting, requisite professional certification in accounting, or any other comparable experience or background which results in the individual's financial sophistication, including being or having been a chief executive officer, chief financial officer or other senior officer with financial oversight responsibilities." [1]

IM-5605-4 provides: "A director who qualifies as an audit committee financial expert under Item 407(d)(5)(ii) and (iii) of Regulation S-K is presumed to qualify as a financially sophisticated audit committee member under Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)." [1]

### 2.9 Exceptional and Limited Circumstances Exception for Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)(2)(B)

One director who is not independent may be appointed to the audit committee if [1][8]:
- Not currently an Executive Officer or employee or a Family Member of an Executive Officer;
- The board determines membership is required by the best interests of the Company and its Shareholders;
- Disclosure is made under Item 407(d)(2) of Regulation S-K;
- The member serves no longer than two years and may not chair the committee.

## Section 3: Operational Mechanics of Lookback Periods

### 3.1 Summary Table of All Lookback Periods and Their Mechanics

| Disqualification Type | Rule Citation | Lookback Period | Type of Period | Key Calculation Point |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Employment (A)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(A) | 3 years | Rolling period commencing on date relationship ceases | 3 years forward from termination of employment |
| **Compensation (B)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(B) | Any 12 consecutive months within the 3 years preceding determination | Rolling 3-year window, testing all possible 12-consecutive-month periods | Date of independence determination |
| **Family Member of Executive Officer (C)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(C) | 3 years | Rolling period commencing on date relationship ceases | 3 years forward from cessation of family member's employment |
| **Business Relationships (D)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(D) | Current or any of the past 3 fiscal years | Fixed fiscal year periods (completed fiscal years) | Current FY + 3 preceding completed FYs |
| **Compensation Committee Interlocks (E)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(E) | At any time during the past 3 years | Rolling period commencing on date relationship ceases | 3 years forward from when interlock ceased |
| **Auditor Affiliation (F)** | Rule 5605(a)(2)(F) | At any time during any of the past 3 years | Rolling period commencing on date relationship ceases | 3 years forward from when auditor relationship ceased |
| **Financial Statement Preparation (audit committee only)** | Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(iii) | At any time during the past 3 years | Rolling 3-year lookback from date of determination | Date of independence determination for audit committee service |

### 3.2 Detailed Mechanics for Each Category

#### (A) Employment — Rolling Period Commencing on Cessation Date

IM-5605 states: "The three year look-back periods referenced in paragraphs (A), (C), (E) and (F) of the Rule commence on the date the relationship ceases. For example, a director employed by the Company is not independent until three years after such employment terminates." [1]

**Key Nuance:** This is a **forward-looking disqualification period** that starts when the relationship ends. If employment terminates on January 1, 2024, the director cannot be considered independent until January 2, 2027. The clock does not reset — it is a fixed three-year period running forward from the termination date.

**Interim Employment Exception:** Interim employment as an Executive Officer lasting no longer than one year does not trigger the three-year disqualification after termination. However, the director is not independent while serving as an interim officer. [1]

#### (B) Compensation — Rolling 3-Year Window with 12-Consecutive-Month Testing

The rule requires the board to test "any period of twelve consecutive months within the three years preceding the determination of independence." [1] This creates a more complex calculation than a fixed period because it requires testing every possible 12-consecutive-month period within the preceding 36 months.

**Example:** If the determination is made on June 1, 2024, the board examines compensation received from June 1, 2021 to June 1, 2024. Within that window, every possible 12-consecutive-month period must be tested (e.g., June 2021–May 2022, July 2021–June 2022, ..., June 2023–May 2024). If any such period shows covered compensation exceeding $120,000, the director is disqualified.

**How Compensation Is Measured:** IM-5605 clarifies: "Paragraph (B) of the Rule is generally intended to capture situations where a compensation is made directly to (or for the benefit of) the director or a Family Member of the director." This includes consulting contracts, personal service contracts, and political contributions to the director's campaign. [1]

#### (C) Family Member of Executive Officer — Rolling Period Commencing on Cessation Date

The three-year lookback "commence[s] on the date the relationship ceases." [1] If the Executive Officer's employment terminates on January 1, 2024, the director who is a Family Member cannot be considered independent until January 2, 2027.

#### (D) Business Relationships — Fixed Fiscal Year Period

This is a **fixed-period fiscal year lookback**, not a rolling window. The rule evaluates the "current or any of the past three fiscal years." [1] This is a completed fiscal year test, not a rolling 36-month lookback.

**Example:** If the independence determination is made in November 2024, and the company's fiscal year ends December 31, the board examines payments in the current fiscal year (FY2024, which may not yet be complete) and the past three full fiscal years (FY2023, FY2022, FY2021). If the payment threshold was exceeded in any one of those fiscal years, the director is disqualified.

Staff Interpretation Letter 2010-10 confirms: payments are evaluated "in the current year or any of the last three fiscal years." [8]

#### (E) Compensation Committee Interlocks — Rolling Period Commencing on Cessation Date

The three-year lookback "commence[s] on the date the relationship ceases." [1] If the interlocking compensation committee arrangement ends on January 1, 2024, the director cannot be considered independent until January 2, 2027.

#### (F) Auditor Affiliation — Rolling Period Commencing on Cessation Date

The three-year lookback "commence[s] on the date the relationship ceases." [1] For current partners, there is no lookback — they are always disqualified. For former partners or employees who worked on the audit, the three-year period runs from when the auditor relationship ceased or when they stopped working on the company's audit.

#### (c)(2)(A)(iii) Financial Statement Preparation — Rolling 3-Year Lookback

This is a rolling three-year lookback measured from the date of the independence determination. The phrase "at any time during the past three years" indicates a rolling period. [1]

**Interaction with Interim Employment Exception:** IM-5605 clarifies that even if the interim employment exception under paragraph (A) applies, "if the director participated in the preparation of the Company's financial statements while serving as an interim Executive Officer, Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(iii) would preclude service on the audit committee for three years." [1] This is a separate and additional disqualification that runs concurrently with but independently from the general independence lookback.

### 3.3 How Lookback Periods Interact With Each Other

Based on IM-5605, the lookback periods operate **independently** but can run **concurrently**. A person may have multiple disqualifying relationships simultaneously, and each lookback period is measured independently. A director cannot be deemed independent until ALL lookback periods have expired for ALL disqualifying relationships. [1]

**Staff Interpretation Guidance on Specific Interactions:** [8]
- **Acquisition scenarios (Staff Letter 2008-17):** Employment by a target company prior to acquisition is not counted for independence purposes after the target becomes a wholly-owned subsidiary.
- **SERP payments (Staff Letter 2008-22):** SERP payments to a director's father are non-discretionary and excluded from the $120,000 limit.
- **Business holdings (Staff Letter 2010-5):** Under exceptional circumstances, payments to a director's company may be analyzed under paragraph (D) rather than paragraph (B) if the director has direct, significant business holdings.

## Section 4: Types of Companies Required to Have Independent Directors

### 4.1 Majority Independent Board Requirement — Rule 5605(b)(1)

Under Rule 5605(b)(1), a majority of the board of directors must be comprised of Independent Directors as defined in Rule 5605(a)(2). [1][9][10][12]

This requirement applies generally to all NASDAQ-listed companies, with specific exemptions and phase-in provisions described below.

**Executive Sessions (Rule 5605(b)(2)):** Independent Directors must have regularly scheduled meetings at which only Independent Directors are present ("executive sessions"), contemplated to occur at least twice a year. [1]

### 4.2 Controlled Companies — Rule 5615(c)

**Definition:** A "Controlled Company" is a company of which **more than 50% of the voting power for the election of directors** is held by an individual, a group, or another company. [10][12][15]

For a group to exist, the shareholders must have filed a notice that they are acting as a group (e.g., a Schedule 13D). The calculation of voting power held by a group can include shares covered under voting agreements relating to the election of directors. [10]

**Exemptions (What Controlled Companies CAN Exempt):** [10][12][15]
Under Rule 5615(c), controlled companies are **exempt** from:
- **Rule 5605(b)** — Majority independent board requirement
- **Rule 5605(d)** — Independent compensation committee requirements
- **Rule 5605(e)** — Independent nominating committee requirements

**Limitations (What Controlled Companies CANNOT Exempt):** [10][12][15]
- **Audit Committee Independence (Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3):** Controlled companies are NOT exempt. The audit committee must consist of at least three members, each of whom must be independent under the heightened independence criteria of Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1). [10]
- **Executive Sessions (Rule 5605(b)(2)):** Controlled companies are **required** to hold executive sessions of independent directors. [10][12]
- **Code of Conduct:** Controlled companies are not exempt from the requirement to adopt and publicly disclose a code of conduct.
- **Related Party Transaction Review:** The audit committee or another independent body must review and approve all related party transactions.
- **Notification of Noncompliance (Rule 5625):** Controlled companies must comply.
- **Quorum Requirements (Rule 5620(c)):** Controlled companies must comply with the requirement to provide for a quorum of at least 33 1/3 percent of outstanding shares.

**Disclosure Requirements:** [10]
- Companies other than FPIs must disclose in their proxy statement that they are a Controlled Company, the basis for that determination, and the corporate governance standards with which they do not comply (per Instruction 1 to Item 407(a) of Regulation S-K).
- FPIs must disclose in their annual report on Form 20-F or Form 40-F.
- A company that is eligible to rely on the Controlled Company exemption but chooses not to has no special disclosure obligations.

**Transition Upon Ceasing to Be a Controlled Company:** [10][20][23]
- **Immediately:** One independent member on the compensation committee and nominating committee.
- **Within 90 days:** A majority of independent members on the compensation and nominating committees.
- **Within one year:** All independent members on the compensation and nominating committees, AND a majority independent board (Rule 5605(b)).
- **Audit Committee:** Must comply with audit committee requirements **as of the date it ceased to be a Controlled Company** — no phase-in.

The applicable phase-in periods are computed **beginning on the date the company ceases to be a Controlled Company** (amended Rule 5615(c)(3), renumbered as Rule 5615(b)(7)). [20][23]

### 4.3 Foreign Private Issuers — Rule 5615(a)(3)

Under Rule 5615(a)(3), a Foreign Private Issuer (FPI) is permitted to follow its **home country practice in lieu of** the requirements of the Rule 5600 Series and Listing Rule 5250(d), subject to certain conditions. [7][9][10][12]

**Definition of "Home Country":** The jurisdiction of the company's legal incorporation or principal listing. [7]

**Conditions for Relying on Home Country Practice Exemption:** [7][10]
1. Submit a written statement from independent home country counsel certifying that its home country practices are **not prohibited** by home country law.
2. Publicly disclose each specific Nasdaq requirement it does not follow and describe the home country practice it follows instead (in SEC filings or, if not filing, on its website).
3. Failure to provide required disclosure may result in delisting unless promptly cured.

**What CAN Be Exempted Under Home Country Practice:** [7][9][10][12]
- Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b))
- Independent Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d))
- Independent Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e))
- Director Nomination Process
- Shareholder Approval for Equity Compensation
- Meeting Quorum Requirements (Rule 5620(c))

**What CANNOT Be Exempted — Mandatory Compliance Requirements:** [7][9][10][12]
- **Audit Committee Independence (Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3):** FPIs must have an audit committee composed of at least three members who satisfy the independence requirements of SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1). Audit committee members must meet the independence criteria in Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(ii) and the committee must satisfy Rule 5605(c)(3). [9][10]
- **Notification of Noncompliance (Rule 5625)**
- **Voting Rights (Rule 5640)** — Prohibition on unfairly discriminatory voting rights
- **Code of Conduct** — Must adopt a code of conduct applicable to all directors, officers, and employees
- **Related Party Transaction Review** — Must have appropriate review and oversight by the audit committee or another independent body
- **Executive Sessions** — FPI must comply with the requirement that independent directors hold executive sessions

**Transition When an FPI Ceases to Be an FPI:** [20][21][22]
A company that ceases to qualify as an FPI under SEC rules shall have **six months from the Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date** (the end of its most recently completed second fiscal quarter) to comply with:
- Majority independent board requirement (Rule 5605(b))
- Independent compensation committee requirements (Rule 5605(d)(2) and (e)(1)(B))
- Audit committee requirements (Rule 5605(c)(2)), including the three-person audit committee requirement

**Critical Caveat:** During the phase-in period, the company must have an audit committee that satisfies Rule 5605(c)(3) and members must meet the criteria for independence referenced in Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(ii) (Rule 10A-3(b)(1)). The Rule 10A-3 independence standard must be **continually complied with** — no phase-in is permitted for that specific requirement. [20]

### 4.4 Investment Companies and Business Development Companies (BDCs)

**Registered Management Investment Companies:** [10][11][12]
Per SR-NASDAQ-2019-039, Nasdaq exempts management investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940 from Rules 5605(b), (d), (e), and 5610. This means registered management investment companies are **exempt** from:
- Majority independent board requirement (Rule 5605(b))
- Independent compensation committee requirements (Rule 5605(d))
- Independent nominating committee requirements (Rule 5605(e))

However, registered investment companies **must** have an audit committee whose members meet Exchange Act Rule 10A-3 independence criteria, though they do not need to meet the general Nasdaq independence requirements. ICA-registered closed-end management investment companies are not required to make the audit committee charter available on their website. [12]

The exemptions also extend to asset-backed issuers, unit investment trusts, and cooperative entities such as agricultural cooperatives. [10]

**Business Development Companies (BDCs):** [11]
BDCs are **treated differently** from registered investment companies. According to Nasdaq's FAQ:

> "Must a business development company comply with the compensation committee requirements? Yes. Business development companies are required to comply with **all of the provisions of the Rule 5600 Series**, including the compensation committee requirements in Rule 5605(d) and IM 5605-6." [11]

This means BDCs are **not exempt** from:
- Majority independent board requirements (Rule 5605(b))
- Independent compensation committee requirements (Rule 5605(d))
- Independent nominating committee requirements (Rule 5605(e))

BDCs must comply with all Rule 5600 Series provisions, including audit committee independence requirements. [11][12]

**Summary Distinction:** Registered management investment companies are exempt from the independence requirements under the Rule 5600 Series (except for audit committee Rule 10A-3 independence), while **BDCs are fully subject** to all Rule 5600 Series requirements.

### 4.5 Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs)

Nasdaq provides certain special provisions for Smaller Reporting Companies: [11][12][16]

**Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d)(5)):** [11][12]
- SRCs are **exempt** from the **enhanced independence requirements** for compensation committee members (the additional factors the board must consider under Rule 5605(d)(2) when determining independence for compensation committee members).
- SRCs are **exempt** from the responsibility to assess the **independence of compensation committee advisers** (Rule 5605(d)(3)).
- SRCs must still comply with the general requirement to have a compensation committee composed of independent directors (at least two members).
- SRCs may adopt a **board resolution** that specifies the compensation committee's responsibilities **in lieu of adopting a written compensation committee charter**. [12][16]

**Phase-In When Ceasing to Be an SRC (Rule 5615(b)(5)):** [16][20]
When a company ceases to be an SRC, the Nasdaq rules provide a transition period:
- One member meeting enhanced independence requirements: within 6 months following the beginning of the year after loss of SRC status
- A majority: within 9 months
- All members: within 12 months

**No Exemption from Other Requirements:** SRCs are not exempt from the majority independent board requirement, audit committee independence requirements, or nominating committee requirements under Rule 5605.

### 4.6 Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs)

EGCs are defined under Section 2(a)(19) of the Securities Act as companies with total annual gross revenues of less than **$1.235 billion** during their most recently completed fiscal year, and that had not sold common equity securities under a registration statement as of December 8, 2011. [17][18]

An EGC retains its status until the **earliest of**: [17][18]
1. The last day of the fiscal year in which total annual gross revenues are $1.235 billion or more
2. The last day of the fiscal year following the **five-year anniversary** of the first sale of common equity securities under an effective registration statement
3. The date on which the issuer has issued more than **$1 billion in non-convertible debt** in the previous three years
4. The date on which the issuer becomes a **large accelerated filer**

**EGCs Are Not Exempt from Nasdaq Independence Requirements:** EGCs do not receive special exemptions from Nasdaq's board independence or committee independence requirements. The relief EGCs receive relates to **SEC disclosure requirements** (e.g., scaled executive compensation disclosure, two years of audited financials instead of three), not Nasdaq corporate governance listing standards. [17][18]

EGCs are subject to the same phase-in periods as other IPO companies under Rule 5615(b). [20]

### 4.7 Historical Grandfathering Clauses

**Pre-2003 Binding Obligation for Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e)(4)):** [10][12]
Under Rule 5605(e)(4), a company is exempt from the independent nominating committee process if it is subject to a **binding obligation** establishing a different nomination process that was **in effect prior to November 4, 2003** and is inconsistent with the requirement. [12]

**Grandfathering of Pre-Existing Equity Compensation Plans (2003 Rules):** [7]
Under the original 2003 Nasdaq corporate governance rules, shareholder approval became mandatory for most equity compensation plans after June 30, 2003. However, there was **grandfathering of pre-existing plans** unless they were materially amended.

**Transition Rules for Companies Exempt from Now-Vacated Diversity Rules:**
On December 11, 2024, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, sitting en banc, vacated the SEC's approval of Nasdaq's board diversity-related requirements set forth in Nasdaq Listing Rules 5605(f) and 5606. Companies that were previously required to comply with these rules no longer have that obligation.

## Section 5: Phase-In Requirements Under Rule 5615(b)

*Note: On August 26, 2024, the SEC approved amendments (Release No. 34-100816, File No. SR-NASDAQ-2024-019) to Nasdaq Rules 5605, 5615, and 5810, which clarify and modify phase-in schedules for certain corporate governance requirements and clarify the applicability of cure periods. The following reflects the amended rules.* [20][21][22][23]

### 5.1 IPO Companies — Complete Phase-In Schedule Under Rule 5615(b)(1)

**Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)):**
- **12 months from the Listing Date** to comply. [20][22]

**Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c)(2)) — Independence Phase-In:**
- **By the Listing Date:** At least one member must satisfy the independence requirements.
- **Within 90 days of the effective date of the registration statement:** A majority of members must satisfy the requirements.
- **Within one year of the effective date of the registration statement:** All members must satisfy the requirements. [20][21][22]

**Audit Committee — Size Phase-In (3-member minimum):**
- **By the Listing Date:** At least one member.
- **Within 90 days of the Listing Date:** At least two members.
- **Within one year of the Listing Date:** At least three members. [20][21][22]

**Critical Caveat:** If a company chooses to have only one member on the audit committee initially, that member must be independent and meet the financial sophistication requirement. The phase-in does **not** grant an exemption from the requirement that at least one member has past accounting or finance experience. [20][22]

**Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d)) — Independence Phase-In:**
- **By the earlier of the date the IPO closes or five business days from the Listing Date:** At least one independent member.
- **Within 90 days of the Listing Date:** A majority of independent members.
- **Within one year of the Listing Date:** Fully independent committee. [20][22]

**Compensation Committee — Size Phase-In (2-member minimum):**
- **By the Listing Date:** At least one member.
- **Within one year of the Listing Date:** At least two members. [20][22]

**Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e)) — Phase-In:**
- **By the earlier of the date the IPO closes or five business days from the Listing Date:** At least one independent member.
- **Within 90 days of the Listing Date:** A majority of independent members.
- **Within one year of the Listing Date:** Fully independent committee (or alternative: a majority of Independent Directors may discharge the nominations committee responsibilities under Rule 5605(e)). [20][22]

### 5.2 Carve-Out and Spin-Off Transactions — Rule 5615(b)(4)

Companies listing in connection with a carve-out or spin-off transaction receive the same phase-in schedule as IPO companies, with one key difference for the first independent director deadline on compensation and nominating committees: [20][22]

**Key Difference from IPOs:** For carve-out/spin-off companies, the first independent director on compensation and nominations committees must be appointed **by the date the transaction closes** (not by 5 business days post-listing as with IPOs), accommodating board meetings between listing and transaction close. [20][22]

**Majority Independent Board:** 12 months from Listing Date.

**Audit Committee (Independence and Size):** Same as IPO schedule (1 member by Listing Date, majority within 90 days, fully compliant within one year; size: 1 by Listing Date, 2 within 90 days, 3 within one year).

**Compensation Committee:** 1 independent by close of transaction, majority independent within 90 days, fully independent within one year. Size: 1 by close of transaction, 2 within one year.

**Nominating Committee:** 1 independent by close of transaction, majority independent within 90 days, fully independent within one year.

### 5.3 Companies Emerging from Bankruptcy — Rule 5615(b)(2)

**Audit Committee:** Must comply with audit committee composition requirements set forth in Rule 5605(c)(2) **by the Listing Date**, unless an exemption is available pursuant to Rule 10A-3. [20][21][22] **No phase-in is permitted for audit committee compliance for bankruptcy emergence companies.**

**Other Requirements:** Phase-in periods are available for the majority independent board, independent compensation committee, and independent nominations committee requirements. All applicable phase-in periods are computed beginning on the Listing Date. [20][22]

### 5.4 Companies Transferring from Other National Securities Exchanges — Rule 5615(b)(3)(A)

The phase-in period in Rule 5615(b)(3) applies **only to companies that transfer securities registered pursuant to Section 12(b) of the Act from another national securities exchange to Nasdaq**. [20][22]

- **If the prior exchange had a substantially similar requirement:** The company is afforded the **balance of any grace period** afforded by the other market.
- **If the prior exchange did NOT have a substantially similar requirement:** The company is afforded **one year from the date of listing on Nasdaq** to comply.

### 5.5 Section 12(g) Companies — Rule 5615(b)(3)(B)

Companies with securities registered under Section 12(g) that list on Nasdaq: [20][22]

**Audit Committee (Independence):** Must satisfy the audit committee requirements except for the three-member minimum **by the Listing Date** (unless an exemption under Rule 10A-3 applies). Only directors who are independent under Rule 5605(a)(2) and meet Rule 10A-3(b)(1) criteria may serve during the transition period.

**Audit Committee (Size):** Phase-in permitted:
- At least **one** independent director member as of the date of listing
- **Two** independent director members within **90 days**
- **Three** independent director members within **one year**

**Other Requirements:**
- Majority independent board: 12 months from Listing Date
- Compensation committee: One independent upon listing, majority within 90 days, fully independent within one year; size: at least one at listing, at least two within one year
- Nominating committee: One independent upon listing, majority within 90 days, fully independent within one year

### 5.6 Special Provisions — Important Limitation on Cure Periods Following Phase-In

Per the 2024 Amendments (codified in Rules 5605(b)(1)(B), 5605(c)(4)(C), 5605(d)(4)(B), and 5810(c)(3)(E)): [20][21][22][23]

**The Core Rule:** A company relying on any phase-in period in Rule 5615(b) is **not eligible for a cure period** immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period, **unless** the company complied with the applicable requirement during such phase-in period but fell out of compliance after having complied before the end of the phase-in period.

**Applicable to these specific requirements:**
- Audit committee composition requirement in Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)
- Compensation committee composition requirement in Rule 5605(d)(2)(A)
- Majority independent board requirement in Rule 5605(b)(1) [20][22]

**The "Compliance During Phase-In" Exception:** If a company demonstrated compliance during the phase-in period but subsequently fell out of compliance:
1. The company would **not be considered deficient** until the end of the phase-in period.
2. For computing the cure period, the event causing the deficiency is the event causing the fall-out of compliance (not the end of the phase-in period). [20][22]

**Consequence of Non-Compliance:** If a company allows the phase-in period to run out without demonstrating compliance, Nasdaq will issue a **Staff Delisting Determination letter** to delist the company's securities. [20][22]

## Section 6: Cure Periods for Noncompliance

### 6.1 General Cure Period — One Vacancy or Loss of Independence

For companies that become noncompliant with the independent director or committee requirements due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control (e.g., death, resignation, or disqualification of a director) [1][10][20]:

- **General Rule:** The company has until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting** or **one year from the occurrence of the event** that caused the failure to regain compliance.
- **Minimum Guarantee:** If the next annual shareholders meeting occurs **less than 180 days** after the event, the company has **180 days from the event** to cure.

### 6.2 Majority Independent Board — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(b)(1)(A))

If a Company fails to comply with the majority independent board requirement due to **one vacancy, or one director ceases to be independent due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control**: [1][20]

- The Company shall regain compliance by the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event.
- **Provided, however, that if the annual shareholders meeting occurs no later than 180 days following the event**, the Company shall instead have **180 days from such event to regain compliance**.
- The Company must provide notice to Nasdaq immediately upon learning of the event.

### 6.3 Audit Committee — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(c)(4))

**(A) Loss of Independence:** If an audit committee member ceases to be independent for reasons outside the member's reasonable control, the member may remain on the audit committee until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event. [1][20]

**(B) One Vacancy:** If the Company fails to comply due to **one vacancy** on the audit committee (and the cure period in paragraph (A) is not being relied upon for another member), the Company has until the **earlier of the next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event. If the annual shareholders meeting occurs no later than 180 days following the event, the Company has **180 days** from the event. [1][20]

### 6.4 Compensation Committee — Specific Cure Period (Rule 5605(d)(4) and Rule 5810(c)(3)(E))

If a Company fails to meet the compensation committee composition requirement under Rule 5605(d)(2)(A) due to **one vacancy, or one compensation committee member ceases to be independent due to circumstances beyond the member's reasonable control**: [1][20][23]

- The Company has until the **earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year** from the occurrence of the event.
- If the annual shareholders meeting occurs sooner than **180 days** after the event, the Company has **180 days** from the event.

### 6.5 Unavailability of Cure Periods After Phase-In (2024 Amendments)

As codified in the 2024 Amendments, companies relying on phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) **cannot use cure periods to extend the phase-in period** if they never achieved compliance during the phase-in. This applies to:
- Majority independent board (Rule 5605(b)(1)(B))
- Audit committee composition (Rule 5605(c)(4)(C))
- Compensation committee composition (Rule 5605(d)(4)(B)) [20][21][22][23]

## Section 7: Committee Composition Requirements

### 7.1 Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)

[1][9][12]

**Size:** At least three members.

**Independence Standards:**
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2)
- Each member must meet the heightened independence criteria of SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1) (no consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company; no affiliation with the company or subsidiaries)

**Financial Qualifications:**
- Each member must be "able to read and understand fundamental financial statements" (financial literacy)
- At least one member must have "financial sophistication" — past employment experience in finance or accounting, professional certification in accounting, or other comparable experience (including being or having been a CEO, CFO, or senior officer with financial oversight responsibilities)
- A director who qualifies as an audit committee financial expert under Item 407(d)(5)(ii) and (iii) of Regulation S-K is presumed to qualify as financially sophisticated

**Additional Disqualification (Rule 5605(c)(2)(A)(iii)):**
- Must not have participated in the preparation of the company's financial statements at any time during the past three years

**Charter Requirements (Rule 5605(c)(1)):**
- Formal written audit committee charter
- Must specify scope of responsibilities, including structure, processes, and membership requirements
- Must specify responsibility for overseeing the outside auditor's independence
- Must specify purpose of overseeing accounting and financial reporting processes
- Must specify responsibilities and authority set forth in Rule 10A-3(b)(2), (3), (4) and (5)

**Committee Responsibilities (Rule 5605(c)(3)):**
- Directly responsible for the appointment, compensation, retention, and oversight of the independent auditor (Rule 10A-3(b)(2))
- Must establish procedures for receiving and handling complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, and auditing matters, including confidential anonymous submissions (Rule 10A-3(b)(3))
- Authority to engage independent counsel and other advisers (Rule 10A-3(b)(4))
- Appropriate funding as determined by the audit committee (Rule 10A-3(b)(5))

### 7.2 Compensation Committee — Rule 5605(d)

[1][9][12]

**Size:** At least two members.

**Independence Standards:**
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2)
- The board must consider all factors specifically relevant to determining whether a director has a relationship to the Company which is material to that director's ability to be independent from management, including:
  - (i) the source of compensation of such director, including any consulting, advisory or other compensatory fee paid by the Company to such director
  - (ii) whether such director is affiliated with the Company, a subsidiary of the Company or an affiliate of a subsidiary of the Company

**Exception for Non-Independent Member (Rule 5605(d)(2)(B)):**
- If the committee has at least three members, one non-independent director (who is not an Executive Officer or employee or a Family Member of an Executive Officer) may be appointed
- The board must determine membership is required by the best interests of the Company and its Shareholders
- Maximum of two years
- Company must disclose the nature of the relationship and the reasons for the determination

**Charter Requirements (Rule 5605(d)(1)):**
- Formal written compensation committee charter
- Must specify scope of responsibilities, including structure, processes, and membership requirements
- Must specify responsibility for determining or recommending CEO and Executive Officer compensation
- Must specify that the CEO may not be present during voting or deliberations on his or her compensation
- Must specify responsibilities and authority under Rule 5605(d)(3)

**Committee Responsibilities and Authority (Rule 5605(d)(3)):**
- Authority to retain compensation consultants, legal counsel, and other advisers in its sole discretion
- Directly responsible for appointment, compensation, and oversight of any such advisers
- Company must provide appropriate funding for reasonable compensation to such advisers
- Before selecting or receiving advice from a compensation consultant, the committee must consider **six independence factors**:
  - (i) provision of other services to the Company by the adviser's firm
  - (ii) fees received from the Company as a percentage of total revenue
  - (iii) policies and procedures to prevent conflicts of interest
  - (iv) business or personal relationship with a committee member
  - (v) stock of the Company owned by the adviser
  - (vi) business or personal relationship with an Executive Officer of the Company

**Smaller Reporting Company Treatment (Rule 5605(d)(5)):**
- SRCs are exempt from:
  - The enhanced independence requirements for compensation committee members under Rule 5605(d)(2)(A)
  - The responsibility to assess independence of compensation committee advisers under Rule 5605(d)(3)
- SRCs must still have a compensation committee of at least two independent directors
- SRCs may adopt a board resolution specifying committee responsibilities instead of a formal charter

### 7.3 Nominating Committee — Rule 5605(e)

[1][9][12]

**Composition Requirements (Rule 5605(e)(1)):**
Director nominees must either be selected, or recommended for the board of directors' selection, either by:
- (i) a **nominating committee comprised solely of Independent Directors**; or
- (ii) **Independent Directors constituting a majority of the board's independent directors in a vote in which only Independent Directors participate**

**Exception for Non-Independent Member (Rule 5605(e)(3)):**
- In exceptional and limited circumstances, if the committee has at least three members, one non-independent director may be appointed
- Maximum of two years

**Charter Requirements:**
Must address, by written committee charter provision or board resolution:
- The process for board selection of nominees for election by shareholders
- Such other matters relating to director nominations as may be required under federal securities laws (such as policy regarding consideration of director candidates proposed by securityholders)

## Section 8: Key Regulatory Developments

### 8.1 2024 Amendments to Phase-In and Cure Periods (SR-NASDAQ-2024-019)

On August 26, 2024, the SEC approved amendments to Nasdaq Rules 5605, 5615, and 5810. [20][21][22][23]

**Key Changes:**

1. **Codification of Cure Period Unavailability Following Phase-In Periods:** Amended Rules 5605(b)(1)(B), 5605(c)(4)(C), 5605(d)(4)(B), and 5810(c)(3)(E) to codify that a company relying on any phase-in period is not eligible for a cure period immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period unless it complied during the phase-in period but fell out of compliance before its expiration. [20][22]

2. **Codification of Phase-In Compliance Policy:** Codified the policy that a company demonstrating compliance during a phase-in period but subsequently falling out of compliance before its expiration would not be considered deficient until the end of the phase-in period. [20][22]

3. **IPO Phase-In Clarifications:** Restated phase-in provisions for IPOs, including specific schedule for audit committee size phase-in (1 member by Listing Date, 2 within 90 days, 3 within one year). [20][22]

4. **Bankruptcy Emergence Codification:** Codified that companies emerging from bankruptcy must comply with audit committee requirements by the Listing Date. [20][21][22]

5. **Section 12(g) Companies:** Provided specific phase-in schedules for companies with securities registered under Section 12(g) that list on Nasdaq. [20][22]

6. **Carve-Out and Spin-Off Transactions (New Rule 5615(b)(4)):** Extended phase-in schedules to companies listing via carve-out or spin-off transactions. [20][22]

7. **FPI Cessation Transition:** Clarified that a company ceasing to be an FPI has six months from the Foreign Private Issuer Determination Date to comply with domestic requirements. [20][22]

8. **Compensation Committee Cure Period (Rule 5810(c)(3)(E)):** Amended to provide specific cure period for one vacancy or one member ceasing to be independent (earlier of next annual meeting or one year, with 180-day minimum). [20][22][23]

9. **Renumbering of Rules:** Rules 5615(c)(1), 5615(c)(2), and 5615(c)(3) renumbered as 5615(a)(7)(A), 5615(a)(7)(B), and 5615(b)(7). Rule 5615(b)(4) renumbered to 5615(b)(5). [20][22]

### 8.2 SEC Rule 10A-3 Requirements

SEC Rule 10A-3, adopted pursuant to Section 301 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, establishes the baseline independence requirements for audit committee members that NASDAQ must enforce through its listing standards: [1][9]

- No acceptance of any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company (other than director or committee fees)
- No affiliation with the company or any of its subsidiaries
- These requirements are in addition to the general independence standards under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

### 8.3 Diversity Rule Vacatur (December 11, 2024)

On December 11, 2024, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, sitting en banc, vacated the SEC's approval of Nasdaq's board diversity-related requirements set forth in Nasdaq Listing Rules 5605(f) and 5606. Companies are no longer required to comply with these rules.

### 8.4 Family Member Definition Amendment (February 2020)

As detailed in Section 1.5, the SEC approved Nasdaq's amended proposal to modify the definition of "family member" in Rule 5605(a)(2) to exclude stepchildren (unless they reside with the director) and domestic employees. [5][6][7]
