# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms: California Cap-and-Invest, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market (2020–2024)

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive comparative analysis examines three major carbon pricing systems—California's Cap-and-Invest Program, the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China's national carbon market—across seven critical dimensions from 2020 to 2024. Each system operates within distinct political and economic contexts, with unique design features that shape their effectiveness in reducing greenhouse gas emissions, generating revenue, maintaining compliance, and responding to economic disruptions.

The EU ETS represents the most mature system with the highest carbon prices (annual average of approximately €80–84/tCO2e in 2023), driven by structural reforms including the Market Stability Reserve (MSR) and the accelerated Linear Reduction Factor. California's program generates substantial cumulative revenue ($34.5 billion) directed toward climate investments and community benefits, though it faces challenges related to allowance oversupply and declining prices. China's newer national market, launched in July 2021, has achieved remarkably high compliance rates (99.98% for the third compliance cycle) and is rapidly expanding sectoral coverage to become the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage.

| TNAC between 400 and 833 million | No action taken |
| TNAC < 400 million | MSR releases **100 million allowances** into market |

- **Cumulative transaction value (2021–2024):** 43.03 billion yuan (~$5.93 billion USD) [22]

**Price Formation Structure:**
- **Intensity-based cap (tradable performance standard):** Allowances are allocated based on output-based benchmarks tied to production levels, not an absolute emissions cap. The supply of allowances is endogenous — tied to actual production output times sectoral intensity benchmarks [3][4][19][20].
- **Two-step allocation:** Pre-allocation of 70% of previous year's verified emissions, followed by ex-post adjustment based on verified current-year output [21][25]
- **Trading methods:** Listed trading (±10% price limit), block trading (±30% limit, minimum 100,000 tonnes), and one-way bidding [3]
- **Participants:** Only key emitting entities can trade — no institutional investors, financial institutions, or individual investors are currently permitted [3][7]
- **No derivatives:** Only spot trading is permitted [3][7]
- **Banking:** Permitted with quantitative limits [3]
- **Borrowing:** Temporarily allowed during the second compliance cycle (2021–2022) — entities could borrow up to 50% of allowance shortage from 2023 vintage; **not continued** into the third cycle [3]

**Explicit Statement on Cap Type:**

**The China National ETS operates on an intensity-based cap (tradable performance standard), NOT an absolute cap.** This is a critical structural distinction from the EU ETS and California Cap-and-Invest [3][4][19][20][21].

"The National ETS adopts an output-based benchmark allocation approach without an absolute cap." [4] Allowances are allocated based on facility output and a benchmark emissions intensity, creating an endogenous relationship between output and allowances. The cap has grown from approximately 4,500 MtCO2 in 2019–2020 to an estimated 8,000 MtCO2 in 2024 [3].

**Achieved Intensity Reduction Metrics:**

| Metric | Value | Time Period | Source |
|--------|-------|-------------|--------|
| Carbon intensity reduction in fossil-fuel power generation | **10.8%** | 2018 → 2024 | [3] |
| Overall electricity generation emissions intensity reduction | **8.8%** | 2018 → 2023 | [6] |
| Carbon intensity reduction in thermal power sector | **2.38–2.4%** | 2018 → 2023 | [4][6] |
| Academic estimate: total carbon reduction due to ETS (pilot + national) | Up to **18.2%** | 2013–2020 | [40] |
| Power sector emissions reduction (modeled) | **13%** | 2013–2020 | [40] |

**Important Caveat — Total Emissions Continue to Rise:**

China's total CO2 emissions continue to increase. The ETS's intensity-based design means that while emissions per unit of output decline, total emissions can rise with economic growth and industrial output expansion. The system has been characterized by "generous allowances, lack of an absolute emissions cap, allowance surpluses, and limited trading liquidity" [3][21][42].

Under the NBER/RFF modeling by Goulder et al., "the current benchmarks are five percent less stringent than efficiency-maximizing levels" [19][23].

**Planned Shift from Intensity-Based to Absolute Target:**

In November 2025 (beyond primary review period but contextually relevant), China submitted its **2035 NDC**, committing to reduce economy-wide GHG emissions by **7–10% from peak levels by 2035** — the first time China has committed to reducing its absolute GHG emissions [3].

The Chinese government issued landmark 2025 policy guidance outlining a transition from an intensity-based cap to an absolute cap [3]. By 2027 the system should cover all major industrial sectors, and by 2030 it will have shifted from controls based on carbon intensity to controls based on absolute emissions [23].

## 5. COVID-19 Economic Disruption Management

### 5.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

**Determination: RULES-BASED — No Discretionary Interventions**

**CARB did NOT deploy any emergency discretionary interventions in response to COVID-19.** The program's existing rules-based mechanisms operated automatically [7][10][12].

**Auction Performance During COVID-19:**

| Auction | Date | Settlement Price | % of Allowances Sold | Revenue Generated |
|---------|------|-----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Joint #22 | Feb 2020 | $16.68 | Near 100% | Pre-pandemic baseline |
| Joint #23 | **May 2020** | **$16.68** (floor) | **~35%** | **~$25 million** (collapse) |
| Joint #24 | Aug 2020 | $16.68 (floor) | 83% | $474 million |
| Joint #25 | Nov 2020 | $16.68 (floor) | 100% | $587 million |

**Sources:** [4][10][12][13]

**Mechanism: Self-Ratcheting (Unsold Allowance Rules Under Section 95911(f)(3)(D))**

The program's self-ratcheting mechanism functioned as an automatic stabilizer [70][10]:

- When auctions are undersubscribed, unsold allowances are removed from current circulation
- If California state-owned allowances remain unsold for more than **24 months**, they are transferred into the Allowance Price Containment Reserve (APCR)
- The maximum number of unsold allowances that can be returned to a future auction is **25%** of the California allowances offered at that auction
- If previously unsold state-owned allowances were re-designated but still remain unsold, they cannot be re-designated until two consecutive auctions settle above the auction reserve price

This provides "two levels of temporary cap tightening" [10].

**No Suspension of Auctions:**

The program did not suspend any auctions during COVID-19. All quarterly auctions continued on schedule throughout the pandemic [2][3].

**Deferred 2020 Emissions:**

Under existing rules (Section 95856(d) of the Cap-and-Trade Regulation), any deferred 2020 covered emissions (an existing mechanism for the final year of a compliance period) were included with the full CP4 compliance obligation due November 1, 2024. This was an **existing regulation provision**, not a COVID-specific intervention [12][65].

**Analysis Distinguishing COVID Impact from Structural Oversupply (Cullenward Analysis):**

According to Danny Cullenward (Stanford lecturer, CalEPA Independent Emissions Market Advisory Committee member) [12][47]:

- "Faulty market design is the real culprit, not the pandemic."
- The market surplus was already **227 million allowances** at the end of 2018 and was "likely approaching 300 million now" (as of May 2020) [12]
- "Because it needed a two-thirds vote, the bill (AB 398) required major concessions to the fossil fuel industry" — including maintaining excess allowances in circulation
- The COVID-19 pandemic's impact on emissions is "insufficient to explain the auction shortfall; instead, it underscores persistent structural design flaws" [12]
- Estimates showed a **30% oversupply** (over 200 million tons) during the second compliance period (2015–2017) [11]

**Post-Pandemic Recovery:**

- Prices began rising from February 2023 due to post-pandemic industrial recovery and expectations of stricter future regulations [2][4][10]
- Emissions across tracked sectors dropped **8% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels** [28]
- 2023–24 fiscal year saw peak auction revenues of **$8.1 billion** [49]

**Determination: RULES-BASED — No Discretionary Interventions**

**The EU ETS did NOT deploy any specific discretionary interventions in response to COVID-19.** The system relied entirely on its pre-existing rules-based Market Stability Reserve (MSR) mechanism [3][12][16].

- Auction volumes in 2021 were reduced under the same legal basis

**No Emergency Price Floor or Market Intervention:**

The system maintained its design of no price floor and no price ceiling. The volume-based MSR was the only mechanism operating. No emergency policy measures beyond the MSR were identified [3][16].

**Market Response to COVID:**

| Phase | Period | Price Movement | Driver |
|-------|--------|---------------|--------|
| Initial shock | March–April 2020 | Fell to **~€15** | Demand collapse from economic shutdown |

**Academic Analysis of COVID Impact:**

- EUA prices showed an inverted U-shaped relationship with new confirmed COVID-19 cases and deaths: initially increasing then decreasing as the pandemic worsened [71]
- Government response policies (stringency index) had a 'stop-loss' effect, supporting market stability [71]
- The **€750 billion EU Recovery Plan**, emphasizing green transition, supported longer-term market recovery [71]
- "The negative demand shock due to COVID-19 mainly decreases cumulative emissions with limited price impact" [71]

**REPowerEU Plan (Distinguished from COVID — Energy Crisis Response):**

The REPowerEU plan was launched in **May 2022** as a response to the energy crisis triggered by **Russia's invasion of Ukraine** (NOT COVID) [11][12]:
- Mobilizes close to **€300 billion** in investments [11]
- Three pillars: Saving Energy, Diversifying Energy Supplies, Accelerating Renewables [11]

## 6. Interaction with Complementary Policies

### 6.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

California's climate policy architecture positions the Cap-and-Invest program as a **"backstop mechanism"** alongside sector-specific regulations. Approximately **75% of emission reductions** are expected from complementary policies, while the cap-and-trade scheme acts as a guarantee to meet overall emissions caps [7][9][73].

**Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) and SB 100:**

SB 100 (2018) establishes California's target of **100% renewable and zero-carbon electricity by December 31, 2045** [74]:

| Target Year | RPS Requirement |
|-------------|----------------|
| 2024 | 44% |
| 2027 | 52% |
| 2030 | 60% |
| 2045 | 100% |

In 2023, in-state solar generation increased 6.9%; fossil fuel generation dropped 3.9% [1]. In 2021, hydropower generation decreased by 13.5 TWh (24.3%) due to drought, partially offset by increased solar and wind [3].

**Interaction with Cap-and-Invest ("Waterbed Effect" within California):**

The "waterbed effect" occurs when overlapping policies (RPS, LCFS) reduce emissions below the cap, creating surplus allowances that can be banked and used later, potentially allowing emissions to rise elsewhere in the cap-and-trade system. Renewable deployment reduces emissions in the covered sector, freeing up allowances for use elsewhere or banking.

The cap-and-trade program is designed as a "backstop" to ensure overall emissions reduction targets are met, but the majority of actual reductions are expected to come from complementary policies [9]. C2ES states: "California's cap-and-trade program complements broader state policies such as renewable portfolio standards, low carbon fuel standards, and vehicle emissions regulations, serving as a backstop to ensure overall emission reduction targets are met" [9].

**Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS):**

Transportation accounts for the largest share of emissions (37.1% in 2023). The LCFS works alongside cap-and-trade to reduce transportation emissions through lifecycle carbon intensity reduction rather than direct combustion emissions [75][76]:

- Transportation emissions decreased **4.6%** in 2023 due to increased renewable diesel use and growth in zero-emission vehicles
- **975,700** light-duty battery electric vehicles in California in 2023 (49.6% growth from 2022) [1]
- In 2022, **647,000** light-duty BEVs (43.8% growth from 2021) [3]
- Some LCFS fuel substitution costs reach up to **$945 per MTCO2e** compared to the highest allowance price of ~$102 per MTCO2e (ARB 2010 analysis) [75]

**Schatzki & Stavins (2014) finding:** Overlapping policies targeting the same capped sources generally do not yield additional environmental benefits and may undermine economic efficiency [75]. However, more recent analysis finds that LCFS and cap-and-trade programs together reduce demand for carbon allowances and provide complementary incentives [76].

**2022 Scoping Plan Targets (Adopted December 15, 2022):**

- **48% below 1990 levels by 2030** (more ambitious than the statutory 40%) [19][29]
- **85% below 1990 by 2045**
- **Carbon neutrality by 2045**
- 94% reduction in liquid petroleum use by 2045
- 86% drop in total fossil fuel demand by 2045 relative to 2022
- SB 1020 mandating 90% renewable and zero-carbon electricity by 2035

**LAO Finding on Cap-and-Trade's Role:**

The Legislative Analyst's Office found that the 2022 Scoping Plan "**lacks a clear strategy for meeting 2030 GHG goals**" and that the "**Cap‑and‑Trade Program is not currently positioned to close 2030 emissions gap**" due to allowance oversupply and banking provisions (~310 million banked allowances) [19].

The LAO explained: "Due to allowance banking, there will be surplus permits enabling emissions beyond target levels, possibly leading to low allowance prices and volatile auction revenues" [19].

The LAO recommended the Legislature:
1. Require CARB to clarify its 2030 plans, including the role of cap-and-trade
2. Consider modifying the cap-and-trade program to reduce allowance supply
3. Limit offset usage
4. Extend the program beyond 2030 to ensure alignment with legislative goals [19]

## 7. Leakage Concerns and Border Adjustments

### 7.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

**Carbon Leakage Rates from Ex-Ante Modeling:**

**Fowlie, Reguant & Ryan (2016) — CARB-funded study** [81]:

| Metric | Finding |
|--------|---------|
| For median industry: 10% increase in domestic energy prices (proxy for carbon pricing) | 4–10% reduction in domestic production; 3–9% decrease in exports; 2–4% increase in imports |
| Industries with export declines exceeding 20% under $10/ton CO2 price | Cement, lime, industrial gas, wet corn milling, nitrogen fertilizer, iron and steel |
| Estimated upper bounds on market transfer rates (proxy for leakage potential) | Typically below **20%** |

**Fowlie & Reguant (2022) — Published academic paper** [82]:

| Metric | Finding |
|--------|---------|
| Aggregate leakage rate (median estimate) | **46%** |
| Interpretation | Nearly half of the domestic emissions reductions from a regional carbon pricing policy could be offset by increased emissions outside the regulated jurisdiction |
| Output-based subsidies targeted on leakage risk measures | "Significantly reduce this leakage risk" |
| Current practice of coarsely targeting subsidies on emissions intensity and trade exposure | "Delivers a small fraction of leakage mitigation benefits while incurring significant costs" |

**Free Allowance Allocation Methodology by Leakage Risk Classification:**

Industrial sectors are divided into three leakage classifications with corresponding free allowance allocations [5][9][81]:

| Leakage Risk | Allocation Level | Duration |
|-------------|-----------------|----------|
| **High** (emissions-intensive, trade-exposed; e.g., oil and gas extraction and refining) | **100%** free allocation | Throughout compliance periods (maintained through 2030 under AB 398) |
| **Medium** | Declining: **75%** (CP2) → **50%** (CP3) | Declining over compliance periods |
| **Low** | Declining: **50%** (CP2) → **30%** (CP3) | Declining over compliance periods |

**Two Allocation Methodologies:**
- **Product-based:** Applicable to sectors like oil and gas extraction and refining
- **Energy-based:** For sectors without product benchmarks (e.g., food processing)

**Cap Adjustment Factor:** The program employs emission efficiency benchmarks and a cap adjustment factor, which declines from **0.981 in 2013 to 0.511 in 2030**, meaning industrial facilities receive allocations that decline annually in proportion to the overall cap [7][81].

**Vintage 2026 Allocation Breakdown:**
Total of 133,823,322 allowances allocated [5]:
- Electrical Distribution Utilities (EDUs): **54.4%**
- Natural Gas Suppliers: **23.2%**
- Industrial sectors: **~22%**

**Linkage with Québec (Implemented and Operating Throughout 2020–2024):**

California and Québec have operated a joint carbon market since **January 2014** [5][6][7]:
- Joint quarterly auctions using dual-currency system
- Mutual acceptance of compliance instruments
- **November 2024 compliance event:** Québec entities surrendered 13.3 million California-issued offset credits
- **Linkage with Ontario** (2018) terminated after Ontario's cap-and-trade system was cancelled

**Linkage with Washington (Under Discussion — NOT Implemented as of End 2024):**

In March and September 2024, joint statements from Québec, California, and Washington affirmed their commitment to explore potential linkage of Washington's carbon market to the California-Québec market [5][6]. As of end 2024, this was **under discussion but not implemented**.

**Border Adjustment Proposals (Under Consideration — NOT Implemented):**

Economists have shown that full auctioning of emissions allowances, together with a combination of border carbon price adjustment and export rebates, can be effective in mitigating emissions leakage [81]. The Climate Center's 2023 policy brief recommends gradually eliminating free allowances to better incentivize decarbonization, noting that "free allowances subsidize polluting firms, diluting incentives for carbon reductions" [83]. As of end 2024, **no border carbon adjustment mechanism had been adopted in California.**

## 8. Synthesis and Key Cross-Cutting Lessons

### 8.1 Price Stability Mechanisms Matter — But Design Philosophy Differs Fundamentally

The three systems demonstrate fundamentally different approaches to price stability:

**Volume-based (EU):** The MSR operates through automatic volume adjustments based on the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation (TNAC). It absorbed ~379 million allowances during COVID without any discretionary intervention. The invalidation of 2.5 billion allowances in 2023 permanently tightened the cap. This approach has been highly effective but produces volatile prices (€15 to €105.73 range).

**Price-based (California):** The program features explicit price floors ($16.68 in 2020, increasing 5%+inflation annually to $27.94 in 2026), a two-tier APCR ($41.40/$53.20 in 2021 escalating annually), and a price ceiling ($65 in 2021 escalating to $102.52 in 2026). The self-ratcheting mechanism (Section 95911(f)(3)(D)) for unsold allowances provides additional supply-side adjustment. However, neither the APCR nor the price ceiling has been triggered to date, and the program faces structural oversupply.

**Neither (China):** China's ETS has no formal price stabilization mechanism during the 2020–2024 period. Price discovery is limited by the absence of institutional investors and derivatives, and the intensity-based cap means allowance supply automatically adjusts to production levels. As auctioning is introduced post-2025, price volatility will likely increase.

### 8.2 Revenue Allocation Shapes Political Sustainability

**California's community benefit requirements** have built broad political support: the statutory 35% minimum for disadvantaged communities has been exceeded (actual achievement ~73%, $9.2 billion invested). This has created a powerful coalition of environmental justice advocates supporting program continuation.

**EU's dedicated climate funds** (Innovation Fund ~€40B, Modernisation Fund €57B, Social Climate Fund €86.7B) demonstrate how revenue recycling can address distributional concerns and build member state support. The shift from "encouraged" 50% to "obliged" 100% allocation (from June 2023) significantly strengthens the climate alignment of revenue use.

**China's lack of auction revenues** means no such political economy dynamics have yet developed. The planned National Carbon Emission Trading Fund and eventual auctioning will need to address revenue distribution carefully to maintain stakeholder support.

### 8.3 Compliance Exceeding 99% with Verified MRV Is Achievable Across All Systems

All three jurisdictions achieve near-perfect compliance rates through different enforcement mechanisms:

- **California:** Strict liability (3:1 penalty ratio for non-surrender, up to $10,000+/day per violation) with public enforcement transparency. The compliance exceeding 99% with verified MRV is maintained through CARB's robust monitoring and reporting systems.

- **EU ETS:** €100/tonne penalty plus continued liability, with public disclosure of non-compliant operators. Aviation sector compliance exceeds 99.5% and stationary installations achieve near-100% compliance.

- **China:** Remarkable progression from 99.5% (first cycle) to 99.98% (third cycle). The post-May 2024 penalty regime (5–10× average market price) has dramatically strengthened enforcement, as evidenced by the first major penalty of RMB 424 million in March 2026. The pre-May 2024 regime's ineffectiveness (RMB 20,000–30,000 cap) demonstrates that penalty credibility is essential for compliance.

### 8.4 The Intensity-Based vs. Absolute Cap Distinction Is Foundational

**China's intensity-based cap (tradable performance standard)** means that while emissions per unit of output decline, total emissions continue to rise. The cap has grown from ~4,500 MtCO2 (2019–2020) to ~8,000 MtCO2 (2024). This is a fundamentally different approach from the absolute caps of California and the EU.

**California and the EU** use declining absolute caps that ensure total emissions decline over time regardless of economic output. California's cap declines ~4% annually; the EU's LRF has accelerated from 1.74% to 4.3% (2024–2027).

**The planned shift** in China from intensity-based to absolute targets (as per the 2035 NDC and 2025 policy guidance) represents a critical evolution. By 2030, China aims to shift from controlling carbon intensity to controlling absolute emissions.

### 8.5 The "Waterbed Effect" Requires Active Management

The waterbed effect — where overlapping policies reduce emissions below the cap, creating surplus allowances that can be banked and used later — is managed differently in each system:

- **California:** The waterbed effect is largely unaddressed. ~310 million banked allowances represent a significant risk to achieving 2030 targets. The LAO found that cap-and-trade "is not currently positioned to close 2030 emissions gap."

- **EU ETS:** The MSR's invalidation mechanism (2.5 billion allowances invalidated in 2023, 381 million in 2024, 271 million in 2025) permanently removes surplus allowances, directly addressing the waterbed effect. Germany's cancellation of 514,000 allowances further demonstrates member-state-level action.

- **China:** The waterbed effect is less relevant under the intensity-based cap, as allowance supply automatically adjusts to production. However, as the system transitions to absolute caps, surplus management will become critical.

### 8.6 Border Adjustments Represent the Frontier of Carbon Pricing Innovation

The EU's CBAM is the world's most ambitious border carbon adjustment mechanism, with its transitional phase (reporting only) operating from October 2023 and the definitive phase (financial obligations) beginning January 2026. This represents a paradigm shift from free allocation to carbon pricing at the border.

**Key implications:**
- **For China:** The EU CBAM represents both a threat (estimated costs of €146M–€3.4B by 2034) and an opportunity (catalyst for domestic ETS strengthening). China's dual response — international engagement at WTO/COP29 and domestic policy strengthening (sectoral expansion, enhanced penalties, strengthened MRV) — demonstrates strategic adaptation.

- **For California:** Despite being under consideration, no border carbon adjustment has been adopted. The output-based allocation for EITE industries (100% free allocation for high-leakage-risk sectors) remains the primary anti-leakage mechanism.

- **The future:** CBAM pricing at €75.36/tonne (April 2026) sets a benchmark for global carbon pricing. As more jurisdictions consider border adjustments, the interaction between domestic carbon prices and trade measures will become increasingly important.

### 8.7 COVID-19 Response Design Reveals System Maturity

The COVID-19 pandemic served as a stress test for each system:

- **California and the EU** demonstrated the resilience of **rules-based automatic stabilizers** — no discretionary interventions were needed. The self-ratcheting mechanism and MSR operated as designed, absorbing surplus allowances and maintaining program integrity.

- **China's discretionary interventions** (launch delay, temporary borrowing, generous allocation, coal/gas flexibility measures) reflected its status as a newly established system navigating simultaneous pandemic and launch challenges. The borrowing mechanism for the second compliance cycle was particularly notable — allowed up to 50% of allowance shortage — but was not continued into the third cycle, indicating system maturation.

### 8.8 Complementary Policies Are Essential but Require Coordination

All three systems demonstrate that **carbon pricing alone is insufficient** — complementary policies are essential for deep decarbonization:

- **California:** ~75% of emission reductions expected from complementary policies (RPS, LCFS, SB 100), with cap-and-invest as a backstop. The LAO finding that cap-and-trade "is not currently positioned to close 2030 emissions gap" highlights the coordination challenge.

- **EU:** RED III (42.5% renewables), EED (11.7% efficiency improvement), ESR (national targets), and REPowerEU (€300B) work alongside the ETS. The MSR's invalidation mechanism addresses the waterbed effect that could otherwise undermine these policies.

- **China:** The "1+N" framework coordinates decarbonization across sectors. The shift from "dual control of energy" to "dual control of carbon" (2024) signals policy evolution toward total carbon emission control. Power market reforms are essential for transmitting carbon price signals.
