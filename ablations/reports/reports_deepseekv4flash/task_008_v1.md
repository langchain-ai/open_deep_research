# Comprehensive Comparison: Bosch Serie 8 WTX88M20AU vs LG DVH9-09W vs Samsung DV90T6240LH Heat Pump Dryers

## Executive Summary

This report provides a detailed comparison of three heat pump dryers for a Melbourne household in a heritage-listed terrace house with limited ventilation, shared walls, and 6-8 laundry loads per week. After extensive research across energy consumption, drying performance, noise output, reliability, installation requirements, and total cost of ownership, a clear recommendation emerges:

**The LG DVH9-09W is the most suitable choice for your situation**, offering the best balance of performance, reliability, warranty coverage, and value for a Melbourne terrace house. The Bosch Serie 8 WTX88M20AU, while superior in energy efficiency, is **discontinued** and difficult to purchase new. The Samsung DV90T6240LH is a **UK/European model not sold in Australia**; its closest Australian equivalent is the Samsung DV91T6440LE.

### Analysis for Terrace House Installation

For a heritage-listed terrace with shared walls, noise transmission is a critical concern. The difference of 1 dB between the Bosch (62 dB) and Samsung (63 dB) is **barely perceptible to human hearing** - a 3 dB difference represents a doubling of sound intensity, so the 1 dB gap is marginal [18].

**Key findings for noise in attached housing:**

- **Bosch WTX88M20AU**: The **AntiVibration Side Panels** are specifically designed to reduce vibration transmission - this is a genuine advantage for terrace housing. The heavier weight (55.5 kg) also helps dampen vibration. The 24-hour delayed start allows timing drying for non-sensitive hours [14][15].

- **LG DVH9-09W**: While no official dB rating was found, the Dual Inverter technology provides smoother, quieter operation than standard motors. At 57 kg (the heaviest model), it has the best inherent stability for reducing vibration transmission. The Gentle Care mode uses lower heat, which likely corresponds to lower operational noise [19].

- **Samsung DV90T6240LH**: At 63 dB(A), it is marginally louder than the Bosch. At 50 kg (lightest), it may transmit more vibration through floors and walls. However, the Digital Inverter motor provides smoother operation, and the alarm can be muted for overnight operation [16][17].

**General guidance for terrace houses:** All three are heat pump dryers, which are inherently quieter than vented or condenser dryers. Installing anti-vibration pads under any of these models would further reduce noise transmission to neighbours [18].

### Common Failure Points for Heat Pump Dryers

Based on Australian repair service data and user reports:

1. **Condenser/Lint Buildup**: The most common issue. Blocked condensers cause extended drying times and eventual failure. Samsung models without self-cleaning condensers are most susceptible [31].

2. **Moisture Sensors**: Dirty or failed sensors lead to inconsistent drying - over-drying or under-drying. All three models can experience this, but LG has received specific criticism for sensor performance [12].

3. **Compressor Failure**: The most expensive repair ($500-$1,000+). Heat pump dryers use sealed refrigerant systems that are costly to repair when they fail [30].

4. **Drum Bearings and Belts**: Worn bearings cause noise and vibration. Samsung's lighter construction (50 kg) may be more prone to bearing wear over time.

5. **Control Board Failures**: Electronic control boards can fail, particularly in models with complex smart features. Samsung has received reports of control board issues [25].

### Samsung Melting Issue (Safety Concern)

A significant safety issue has been reported on certain Samsung heat pump dryer models (DV90T8240SH/S2 and potentially others): **"Users have reported a safety issue involving melting of internal rubber components causing rattling and scraping noises, along with a thick heap of melted black goo at the back of the machine"** [32]. The machine reportedly displayed no error code and no failsafe stopped the machine. Samsung Australia has apparently been responsive in some cases, but this raises concern about long-term reliability.

### Average Lifespan

- **Expected lifespan**: Quality heat pump dryers typically last **10-12 years** with proper maintenance [33].
- **Bosch Series 8**: Some users report failures after only 2-3 years, while others report 10+ years of satisfactory service [24].
- **LG**: One user reported their previous LG dryer served faithfully for approximately 9 years [12].
- **Samsung**: Mixed reports, with some users experiencing issues within 2-3 years and others reporting satisfactory performance [25].

### Heritage Overlay Considerations

**Critical finding: Heat pump dryers require NO external structural modifications, making them generally exempt from heritage overlay permit requirements.**

Key points for heritage-listed terrace houses:

1. **No external venting needed**: All three models are fully self-condensing, so no vent holes need to be cut through heritage walls, windows, or roofs [34].

2. **No external unit**: Unlike split-system air conditioners requiring outdoor compressor units, heat pump dryers are single, self-contained indoor appliances. There is no external component visible from the street [34].

3. **Internal alterations exempt**: The Heritage Overlay specifically targets external alterations visible from public areas. Internal installation of appliances does not require a planning permit [38].

4. **Planning permit required only for**: Demolition, construction of buildings, external alterations, painting external surfaces, signage - none of which apply to internal dryer installation [38].

5. **Humidity management**: Since heat pump dryers do not vent moist air into the room, they are ideal for terrace houses with limited ventilation. The condensed water can be plumbed directly to a drain - all three models include drain hoses [4][22][16].
