# Comparative Analysis of Maternal Mortality Rates and Prenatal Care Access: Rohingya Refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh vs. Karen Refugees in Mae La Camp, Thailand (2017–2023)

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of maternal health outcomes and healthcare delivery models for two distinct refugee populations in Southeast Asia: Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, and Karen refugees in Mae La camp, Thailand. The Rohingya crisis, which saw a massive influx of over 725,000 people beginning in August 2017, has resulted in approximately 963,000 refugees living in congested camps in Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar district [12]. In contrast, Mae La camp, established in 1984, is the largest of nine recognized camps along the Thai-Myanmar border, housing approximately 34,000–46,000 ethnic Karen refugees in a protracted displacement situation spanning over three decades [1].

The analysis covers the period from 2017 to 2023, examining four key health indicators—antenatal care completion, skilled birth attendance, postpartum hemorrhage incidence, and neonatal mortality—while exploring how fundamentally different healthcare delivery models correlate with health outcomes. The report also assesses the impact of the 2021 UNFPA funding reduction on continuity of maternal health services in both settings.

---

## Section 1: Population Context and Camp Profiles

### 1.1 Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh — Rohingya Refugee Context

As of September 2023, the Rohingya refugee population in Cox's Bazar camps was approximately 963,303 individuals [12]. The population comprises Forcibly Displaced Myanmar Nationals (FDMN) who fled waves of ethnic cleansing and violence in Myanmar's Rakhine State, with the largest exodus occurring after August 25, 2017. Women and girls represent approximately 52% of the refugee population, with an estimated 19,500 pregnant women and 325,000 women of reproductive age in need of maternal health services at the height of the crisis [5].

The camps are located in the Ukhia and Teknaf Upazilas of Cox's Bazar District, a region already characterized by weak health infrastructure relative to the scale of need. The refugees live in 33 overcrowded camps with limited privacy, poor sanitation, and challenging terrain that impedes access to health facilities. The Government of Bangladesh maintains strict control over camp governance and does not permit refugees to work legally or move freely outside the camps [5].

### 1.2 Mae La Camp, Thailand — Karen Refugee Context

Mae La refugee camp (also known as Beh Klaw) was established in 1984 in Tha Song Yang District, Tak Province, approximately 8 kilometers from the Thai-Myanmar border and 57 kilometers from Mae Sot [1]. As of 2019, the camp housed 35,373 persons of concern to UNHCR, with 93% of ethnic Karen origin, 51% female, and 60% aged 18 and above [4]. By August 2022, the population had declined to approximately 34,164 due to ongoing third-country resettlement [5].

Over 96,000 refugees had been resettled from Mae La to countries including the United States, Australia, Canada, Finland, and Norway since 2004 [2]. Thailand is not a signatory to the 1951 Refugee Convention and does not have domestic legislation regulating refugee status, meaning refugees live under a policy of temporary shelter overseen by the Ministry of Interior rather than formal integration [2].

## Section 2: Health Indicators Comparison

### 2.1 Antenatal Care (ANC) Visit Completion Rates (4+ Visits)

**Cox's Bazar (Rohingya):**
- A 2025 cross-sectional study of 415 women in Camp-4 found that 71.57% had at least four ANC visits [3].
- The "Intergenerational Risk and Resilience of Rohingya in Displacement" (iRRRd) study, with 2,322 women recruited between 2023–2024, reported an average of 6.9 ANC visits (SD = 2.6), with 80.8% initiating care in the first trimester [10].
- The March 2024 Health Sector Bulletin reported ANC 4 coverage "hovering around 77%" based on Health Sector 4W data, though a separate WHO concurrent monitoring survey found that only 43% of children under three were born in health facilities, raising questions about data consistency [15].
- Each additional year of maternal formal education increased the number of visits by 1.5% and reduced delayed initiation by 12.1% [10].

**Mae La (Karen):**
- The UNHCR Health Information System 2018 report for Mae La camp found antenatal care coverage at 91%, exceeding the >90% standard [3].
- A peer-reviewed retrospective analysis of two long-term refugee camps (Mae Ra Ma Luang and Mae La Oon) found that "more than 90% of pregnant women attended four or more antenatal care visits" throughout the 2000–2018 period [6][7].
- The Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU) cohort, tracking approximately 94,645 pregnancies over nearly four decades, provided ANC to approximately 90% of women in Mae La camp [8].

**Comparative Assessment:** Mae La demonstrates consistently higher ANC 4+ completion rates (91% vs. 71–77% in Cox's Bazar). The protracted nature of Mae La's healthcare system, with over three decades of institutional experience, likely contributes to this difference. However, the 2023–2024 iRRRd data from Cox's Bazar showing 80.8% first-trimester initiation and 6.9 average visits suggests substantial recent improvements in ANC engagement among Rohingya women.

### 2.2 Skilled Birth Attendance (SBA) Percentages

**Cox's Bazar (Rohingya):**
- The UNHCR Annual Public Health Global Review 2024 documented a dramatic increase: "Between 2018 and 2024, the proportion of births attended by skilled healthcare providers in Rohingya refugee camps rose from 12% to 88%" [14].
- The UNHCR 2023 review reported SBA "stable at 93%" [23].
- A 2025 cross-sectional study in Camp-4 found 69.4% reported skilled birth attendants during delivery [3].
- Historical context: In February 2018, only 22% of births were facility-based [11]. The WHO Health Sector Bulletin for April–June 2021 reported facility-based delivery sustained at 67% despite COVID-19 disruptions [1].
- The March 2024 Health Sector Bulletin reported facility-based delivery "exceeding 80%" but noted conflicting data from WHO monitoring showing only 43% facility births [15].

**Mae La (Karen):**
- The UNHCR HIS 2018 report recorded skilled birth attendance at 95% of all births in Mae La camp [3].
- The Malteser International camps (MRML and MLO) reported 80% of deliveries with a skilled birth attendant [6][7].
- SMRU provides delivery by skilled birth attendants for approximately 2,500 women annually in Mae La camp [8].
- A PLOS ONE study (2016) documented that training local Karen and Burman women as SBAs from 2008–2015 led to a 25% increase in clinic births, a 16.4% reduction in episiotomy use, and 99% usage of partograms during labor [9].

**Comparative Assessment:** Mae La's SBA rate (95%) consistently exceeds Cox's Bazar (69–88%, depending on data source). However, the rapid improvement in Cox's Bazar from 12% (2018) to 88% (2024) represents one of the fastest SBA increases documented in a humanitarian setting, demonstrating what intensive NGO-led interventions can achieve. The discrepancy between different Cox's Bazar data sources (UNHCR vs. WHO monitoring) underscores methodological challenges in measuring this indicator.

### 2.3 Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Incidence and Maternal Mortality

**Cox's Bazar (Rohingya):**
- The 2020 UNFPA MPMSR report documented 144 deaths among women of reproductive age, with 31% (45) classified as maternal deaths. The major cause was obstetric hemorrhage (26.7%), followed by hypertensive disorders (20%) and pregnancy-related infections (13.3%) [2].
- The 2022 MPMSR report recorded 83 maternal deaths, with hemorrhage accounting for 44% and hypertensive disorders for 31% [7].
- The 2023 MPMSR report documented 57 maternal deaths, with obstetric hemorrhage (35%) and hypertensive disorders (33%) as primary causes [24].
- The September 2023 Health Sector Bulletin estimated the Maternal Mortality Rate near 300 per 100,000 live births, though noted this may represent improved surveillance rather than worsening outcomes [12].
- The March 2024 Health Sector Bulletin reported 24 confirmed maternal deaths in the first three months of 2024, with causes including non-obstetric complications (six deaths), obstetric hemorrhage (one death), and hypertensive disorders (one death) [15].

**Mae La (Karen):**
- Between 2004–2018 in the Malteser camps, ten maternal deaths relating to 12,961 live births were documented, yielding a Maternal Mortality Ratio of 126.5 per 100,000 live births [6][7].
- The SMRU cohort (94,645 pregnancies) reported a maternal mortality ratio of 176 per 100,000 live births [12].
- A quality of intrapartum care study in Mae La found that postpartum hemorrhage was the most common observed complication, but SBAs "followed the protocol but were slow to recognize severity and take action" [10].
- The Ibis Reproductive Health assessment noted that "post-partum hemorrhage and unsafe abortion are the most commonly reported causes of maternal mortality and morbidity for cross-border populations" [11].

**Comparative Assessment:** Direct comparison of PPH incidence is difficult due to different surveillance methodologies. However, the data suggest that hemorrhage is a leading cause of maternal death in both settings. The Cox's Bazar MMR of 179–300 per 100,000 is substantially higher than Mae La's 126–176 per 100,000. Notably, the Mae La MMR did not show a declining trend over the study period despite high ANC coverage and improved anemia control, suggesting that the "three delays" model—particularly delays in reaching care due to distance to referral hospitals—remains a critical challenge even in the more established camp system.

### 2.4 Neonatal Mortality Within 28 Days

**Cox's Bazar (Rohingya):**
- The BMJ Global Health study (September 2017 to December 2018) estimated the neonatal mortality rate at 27.0 per 1,000 live births (95% CI: 22.3–31.8) [8].
- The September 2023 Health Sector Bulletin reported infant mortality at 25.2 per 1,000 live births [12].
- The 2023 MPMSR report documented 304 perinatal deaths from August to December 2023, with 78% occurring in facilities, primarily due to preterm complications, birth asphyxia, and intrauterine growth retardation [24].
- 76.3% of neonatal deaths occurred at home or in the community [8].

**Mae La (Karen):**
- The neonatal mortality rate in Mae La camp declined from 21.8 to 10.7 deaths per 1,000 live births between 2009 and 2011—a 51% reduction—following the introduction of a Special Care Baby Unit (SCBU) [13].
- In the Malteser camps (MRML and MLO), neonatal mortality declined by 62% from 2003–2018, reaching 7.7 per 1,000 live births [6][7].
- A 10-year mixed methods review (2008–2017) involving 2,319 preterm births found mortality reduced by 68% in very preterm and 53% in moderate preterm neonates [15].
- The New Humanitarian reported that Mae La's neonatal mortality rate of 12 per 1,000 live births (2010) outperformed Myanmar's national rate of 33 per 1,000 and approached Thailand's rate of 8 per 1,000 [14].

**Comparative Assessment:** This indicator shows the most dramatic disparity between the two settings. Mae La's neonatal mortality (7.7–12 per 1,000 live births) is substantially lower than Cox's Bazar (25–27 per 1,000 live births). The establishment of a low-cost, refugee-staffed SCBU in Mae La, costing approximately $7.27 per day per infant, was associated with a 51% reduction in neonatal mortality [13]. No comparable specialized neonatal care unit existed in Cox's Bazar during the study period, with the first such facility—the Save the Children Maternal and Child Hospital—only opening in early 2026 [20].

## Section 3: Healthcare Delivery Models

### 3.1 Cox's Bazar: NGO-Operated Field Hospital Model

**Structure and Governance:**
The health response in Cox's Bazar is coordinated by the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare (MoHFW) and the Health Sector, delivering the Minimum Package of Essential Health Services (MPEHS) through a three-tier network: 93 health posts, 46 primary health care centers, and 5 secondary care field hospitals, supplemented by approximately 1,400 community health workers [2]. Over 100 partner organizations are involved, including UN agencies (WHO, UNFPA), international NGOs (MSF, BRAC, Save the Children, IOM), and national organizations.

**Key Actors and Facilities:**

- **MSF (Médecins Sans Frontières):** Operates eight health facilities in Cox's Bazar providing emergency care, sexual and reproductive health services, mental health support, and treatment for diseases including hepatitis C, dengue, and scabies. The Goyalmara Mother and Child Health Facility, a 68-bed facility built in 2018, provides 24/7 services including neonatal intensive care, maternity care, and sexual and reproductive health services. MSF assisted 3,930 births in 2024 [4][6][7].

- **UNFPA:** Leads the sexual and reproductive health response, supporting 100–300 midwives deployed in the camps and 46 women-friendly safe spaces. UNFPA supplies more than 90% of reproductive health materials in the camps [13]. The UNFPA-supported Hope Field Hospital serves as the main 24/7 comprehensive emergency obstetric and newborn care (CEmONC) provider, conducting 1,126 cesarean sections and treating 1,822 obstetric emergencies in 2022 [17].

- **BRAC:** Operates 12–13 health facilities serving over 300,000 refugees, focusing on sexual and reproductive health and rights, mental health, nutrition, and gender equality. Between March 2022 and March 2025, BRAC provided sexual and reproductive health services to 3,593 adolescents, delivered 342,940 outpatient consultations, and vaccinated 106,403 adults against cholera [9].

- **IOM:** Inaugurated the first-ever CEmONC services in the Rohingya camps in August 2025 at its Primary Health Centre in Camp-24, Leda, Teknaf, featuring a modern operating theatre, specialist doctors, and 24/7 blood transfusion services [16].

- **Save the Children:** Opened a 59-bed Maternal and Child Hospital in Kutupalong in early 2026, providing 24/7 emergency obstetric and newborn care including cesarean sections and ultrasound diagnostics—services previously inaccessible within the camp [6][7].

**Midwife-to-Population Ratio:**
- As of March 2024, the Health Sector Bulletin reported 466 midwives in the camps [15]. With a population of approximately 963,303, this yields approximately 1 midwife per 2,067 people.
- UNFPA deployed nearly 300 midwives to the camps in 2022, reaching over 240,000 women [18].
- The Health Sector Strategic Plan 2022–2024 notes that only 51% of required skilled birth attendants are available [2].

**Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC) Availability:**
- The March 2024 Health Sector Bulletin reported only 1 facility with active CEmONC services [15].
- A 2018 UNFPA expert interview described a woman experiencing birth complications who "was referred to three different facilities before she could access lifesaving care. Her complete referral journey was over six hours" [21].
- A Community-based Referral Transportation System study documented that poor road infrastructure, especially during rainy seasons when "the wheels get stuck into the mud," frequently delayed emergency transport [22].
- The Three Delays Model analysis of 2023 MPMSR data showed 53% of maternal deaths involved delays in decision-making to seek care (Delay 1), 5% delays reaching care (Delay 2), and 42% delays receiving adequate care within facilities (Delay 3) [24].

### 3.2 Mae La Camp: Thai Ministry of Public Health Integration Model

**Structure and Governance:**
The healthcare model in Mae La is best characterized as a "state-oversight, NGO-implemented parallel system" with integration into the Thai national health system at the secondary referral level. The Ministry of Interior maintains overall camp administration through a Camp Commander (Deputy District Officer), while the Ministry of Public Health (MOPH) provides oversight of disease surveillance, vaccination protocols, and referral standards [2][3].

**Key Features of the MOPH Integration Model:**

- **Vaccination:** The camp follows the Thai National EPI policy, with vaccines provided free by the Thai MOPH [3].
- **Disease Surveillance:** Reports are submitted directly to local health authorities and the Bureau of Epidemiology at the Thai MOPH, with close WHO cooperation [3].
- **Referral Agreements:** A Memorandum of Agreement for full payment of secondary care exists between Malteser International and Thai hospitals. Patients requiring higher-level care are referred to Tha Song Yang Hospital (approximately 57 km away) or Mae Sot General Hospital [3][22].
- **Thai Government Support:** In August 2025, Thailand's Tak Province Public Health Department announced monthly financial support of 2,500 THB to 80 health workers serving Mae La, Umpiem, and Nupo camps, initiated after international humanitarian aid cuts [6].

**Key Health Providers:**

- **Shoklo Malaria Research Unit (SMRU):** Established in 1986, SMRU provides delivery by skilled birth attendants for approximately 2,500 women annually in Mae La. The SMRU clinic manages most obstetric emergencies except cesarean sections, which are referred to Thai hospitals. The cesarean section rate is approximately 5% [8][25].

- **Malteser International (MI):** Has provided integrated primary health care services since 1993 in the Mae Ra Ma Luang and Mae La Oon camps (approximately 20,000 total population). MI employed 26 midwives, 23 MCH assistants, and 4 facilitators for mother support groups in 2018 [6][7].

- **Planned Parenthood Association of Thailand (PPAT):** In partnership with UNFPA since 2012, PPAT provides integrated reproductive health services including family planning, gender-based violence care, and peer education [26][27].

- **Karen Women's Organisation (KWO):** Operates a TBA training and support program since 2005, providing hygiene supplies and training. In 2023, 57 TBAs received training and supplies, assisting 815 women with births [6].

**Midwife-to-Population Ratio:**
- According to the UNHCR HIS 2018 report, Mae La had 3 medical doctors and 72 qualified nurses serving 39,061 refugees—approximately 1.84 qualified nurses per 1,000 population [3].
- This is below the WHO-recommended minimum threshold of 2.5 medical staff per 1,000 people for adequate primary care coverage [18][19].
- The camp also employed 64 community health workers [3].

**Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC) Availability:**
- No comprehensive EmOC (CEmONC) is available within Mae La camp. Vacuum extraction is not performed in the camps [6][7].
- The SMRU clinic provides Basic EmOC but refers all cesarean sections to Tha Song Yang Hospital (57 km, approximately 56 minutes by taxi) [21][22].
- In the Malteser camps, 93% of deliveries (12,849/13,746) occurred within the camps, with only 7% (897/13,746) requiring emergency secondary care referral [6][7].
- Referral distance to the nearest hospital in Mae Sariang takes 3.5–7 hours depending on season, with roads obstructed by fallen trees and landslides during the rainy season [6][7].

### 3.3 Comparative Assessment of Delivery Models

| Dimension | Cox's Bazar (NGO Field Hospital Model) | Mae La (MOPH Integration Model) |
|---|---|---|
| **System maturity** | Emergency response (since 2017) | Protracted system (since 1984) |
| **Governance** | Government oversight + Health Sector coordination of 100+ partners | Ministry of Interior + MOPH + NGO implementers (smaller number of partners) |
| **In-camp CEmONC** | 1–5 facilities with surgical capability; Hope Field Hospital main provider | No in-camp CEmONC; all cesarean sections referred to Thai hospitals |
| **Referral distance** | 1–3 hours within camps; variable road conditions; "six-hour referral journeys" documented | 57 km to Tha Song Yang Hospital; 3.5–7 hours during rainy season |
| **Ambulance availability** | RTMI ambulances (9 in March 2024); Referral Hubs | Thai hospital ambulances; MoA for secondary care payments |
| **Midwives per population** | ~466 midwives for ~963,303 population (1:2,067) | 72 nurses+midwives for 39,061 population (1:542) |
| **Health information system** | Complex; multiple parallel systems; data quality discrepancies documented | UNHCR HIS with direct MOPH reporting |
| **Community health workers** | ~1,400 deployed (1 per ~688 population) | 64 CHWs for 39,061 population (1 per 610 population) |

**Key Model Differences:**
The Cox's Bazar model relies on a vast number of international NGOs providing services through a standardized Minimum Package of Essential Health Services, with the government maintaining oversight. This model achieved rapid scale-up but faces challenges of coordination, staff retention (described as "high turnover and poor retention" in the PLOS ONE study), and data quality [5].

The Mae La model operates under a smaller, more integrated system where NGOs work in long-term partnership with Thai health authorities. The Thai MOPH provides vaccination supplies, disease surveillance protocols, and referral hospital access, creating a more sustainable framework. However, the absence of in-camp CEmONC capability remains a critical gap, with all cesarean sections requiring external referral.

## Section 4: Cultural Mediation Practices for Traditional Birth Attendants

### 4.1 Cox's Bazar: Engaging Rohingya Dai Ma

Traditional birth attendants (dai ma) play a culturally central role in Rohingya maternal health. Research by Translators without Borders in September 2021 found that nine of ten pregnant women interviewed planned to use a dai ma for their upcoming births, valuing privacy, family presence, and trust in familiar attendants [2].

**Key Cultural Barriers:**
- Mistrust of health facilities rooted in negative experiences in Myanmar and circulating rumors that "newborns are killed or sold at these health facilities" [16].
- Strict gender norms: women cannot interact with male healthcare providers, and male-dominated family decision-making requires husband, in-law, and parental permission for facility births [18].
- Cultural beliefs: some women avoid night hospital visits due to fears of spirits (jinn) [18].
- Language barriers: Arabic and Bengali language differences pose communication challenges, though Rohingya-speaking volunteers have improved outreach [5].

**Intervention Strategies:**
- **Community Partners International (CPI) and Green Hill TBA Training (2021–2022):** This innovative project trained Rohingya TBAs to refer women to health facilities rather than conduct home deliveries. Initial resistance from TBAs, who relied on home deliveries for livelihood and cultural acceptance, shifted as they understood the risks. The number of women choosing facility-based births in the project catchment area rose from 33% in 2020 to over 95% in 2022—a dramatic transformation [16].
- **UNFPA midwives** in the camps work alongside TBAs, who are described as "a very essential link from the community to the facility" [3].
- **Community health workers (1,437 deployed)** conduct household visits in the Rohingya language, distributing health promotion materials and overcoming linguistic barriers [4].

### 4.2 Mae La: Integrating TBAs into the Health System

In Karen refugee culture, approximately 80% of women traditionally deliver at home assisted by TBAs [6]. The response has been to engage TBAs as cultural mediators rather than replace them.

**Key Cultural Factors:**
- Strong Karen cultural preferences for home births with traditional midwives, reinforced by spiritual beliefs including the concept of 37 kla (essential spirits) [14].
- Postpartum practices involve extended rest and specific dietary restrictions [14].
- The EthnoMed profile notes that "a study conducted with Karen women in a refugee camp revealed that home births with the use of traditional midwives was preferred" [14].

**Intervention Strategies:**
- **Mother Support Groups (MSGs) led by TBAs:** Established in 2012, these groups use TBAs' knowledge, experience, and reputation within the community to teach the importance of ANC and exclusive breastfeeding [6][7].
- **KWO TBA Training:** Since 2005, KWO has provided hygiene supplies, single-use maternity kits, and annual training. KWO created the first Karen language TBA Training Manual, developed with Mae Tao Clinic and Burma Medical Association [6].
- **SMRU Birth Attendant Training:** From 2008–2015, SMRU trained local Karen and Burman women as skilled birth attendants through a six-month curriculum adapted from WHO guidelines, leading to a 25% increase in clinic births and 16.4% decrease in episiotomy rates [9].
- **Migrant Health Workers (MHWs) and Migrant Health Volunteers (MHVs):** Since 2003, Thailand has deployed MHWs as interpreters in health facilities and MHVs as cultural mediators within migrant communities. MHWs were formally recognized as Language Coordinators in 2016 [13].

### 4.3 Comparative Analysis of Cultural Mediation Approaches

| Dimension | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) |
|---|---|---|
| **TBA terminology** | Dai ma | Traditional Birth Attendant (TBA) |
| **Key organizations** | CPI, Green Hill, UNFPA, TWB | KWO, SMRU, Malteser International |
| **Training focus** | Referral to facilities (non-clinical role) | Safe delivery support + cultural mediation |
| **Language resources** | Rohingya-speaking volunteers, face-to-face home visits | First Karen language TBA Training Manual |
| **Facility birth impact** | 33% → 95% in CPI catchment (2020–2022) | 80% SBA overall; 25% increase in clinic births post-training |
| **Community engagement** | Family decision-making; husband/in-law permission required | MSGs led by TBAs; community education events |

**Key Insight:** Both settings demonstrate that TBAs can be effective conduits for improving maternal health outcomes when engaged respectfully and provided with evidence-based information in culturally appropriate ways. The Rohingya model produced a more dramatic facility birth rate increase over a shorter period, while the Karen model achieved greater integration of TBAs as ongoing members of the health system rather than temporary referral agents.

## Section 5: Midwife-to-Population Ratios and Emergency Obstetric Care Access

### 5.1 Midwife-to-Population Ratios

**Cox's Bazar:**
- March 2024 data: 466 midwives for approximately 963,303 refugees = **1 midwife per 2,067 people** [15].
- UNFPA deployed nearly 300 midwives by 2022, reaching over 240,000 women [18].
- The Health Sector Strategic Plan notes that only 51% of required skilled birth attendants are available [2].
- A 2025 PMC study recommended "strengthening midwifery and community-based care" over increasing physicians due to resource constraints [3].

**Mae La:**
- UNHCR HIS 2018: 72 qualified nurses (including midwives) + 3 doctors for 39,061 refugees = **1 nurse/midwife per 542 people** [3].
- This is approximately 1.84 qualified nurses per 1,000 population, below the WHO threshold of 2.5 per 1,000 for adequate primary care coverage [18][19].
- Malteser camps (MRML and MLO): 26 midwives for approximately 20,000 total population = **1 midwife per 769 people** [6][7].

**Comparative Assessment:** Mae La consistently demonstrates a higher density of midwifery and nursing staff relative to population, which correlates with its better health outcomes. However, both settings fall short of WHO-recommended staffing thresholds for universal health coverage (4.45 doctors, nurses, and midwives per 1,000 people).

### 5.2 Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC) Availability Within 2-Hour Transport Radius

**Cox's Bazar:**
- Only 1 facility with active CEmONC services in March 2024 [15].
- Only 17% of the 200 health facilities in the camps have 24/7 access [9].
- Documented cases of women being referred to three different facilities over six hours before accessing lifesaving care [21].
- "Ambulances cannot access much of the camps due to terrain, forcing women to be carried out on makeshift stretchers" [11].
- The BMJ Global Health study found that "camps closer to a secondary health facility compared with a labour room/sexual and reproductive health unit had a lower neonatal mortality rate" [8].

**Mae La:**
- No in-camp CEmONC capability; cesarean sections referred to Tha Song Yang Hospital (57 km, ~56 minutes) [21][22].
- Referral distance to Mae Sariang Hospital: 80 km, 3.5–7 hours depending on season [6][7].
- 93% of deliveries occur within camps, with only 7% requiring referral [6][7].
- The Malteser study noted that "the distance to the referral hospital" was a likely factor in the ten maternal deaths documented, with six dying in Thai hospitals after delays [6][7].

**Comparative Assessment:** Neither setting fully meets the WHO "EWENE" target of 80% of countries having more than 50% of their population able to access EmOC within 2 hours. Mae La's referral system is more predictable given the MoA with Thai hospitals, but the distance is substantial (3.5–7 hours during rainy season). Cox's Bazar has greater absolute availability of CEmONC (Hope Field Hospital) but faces unpredictable transport barriers due to terrain and poor road conditions.

## Section 6: Funding and Policy Impact — UNFPA Funding Reduction

### 6.1 The 2021 UNFPA Funding Crisis: Global Context

In April 2021, the UK government announced an 85% reduction in funding to the UNFPA Supplies programme, cutting committed funding from £154 million to approximately £23 million, and reducing core funding by 60% from £20 million to £8 million [1][2][3][4]. The withdrawn £130 million ($180 million USD) would have helped prevent approximately 250,000 maternal and child deaths, 14.6 million unintended pregnancies, and 4.3 million unsafe abortions [1][2][5].

UNFPA Executive Director Dr. Natalia Kanem stated: "When funding stops, women and girls suffer, especially the poor, those living in remote, underserved communities and through humanitarian crises" [3][5].

UNFPA's 2021 global humanitarian appeal sought $818 million to support 54 million women and youth, with $19 million specifically appealed for Bangladesh to maintain reproductive health and protection services for Rohingya refugees in Cox's Bazar [6][7].

---

### 6.2 Impact on Cox's Bazar (Rohingya Response)

**Pre-2021 Funding Gaps:**
Even before the 2021 cuts, funding gaps in 2019 forced service scaling down, risking care for 318,500 women and girls of reproductive age and 31,200 pregnant women [13]. The 2021 Joint Response Plan sought $943 million but achieved only 73.1% funding, with 26.9% ($253.5 million) unmet [18][19].

**Quantified Impacts:**
- **Maternal deaths in 2021:** 96 confirmed maternal deaths were recorded in the camps, with obstetric hemorrhage (29.2%) and hypertensive disorders (23%) as leading causes [16].
- **Three Delays Analysis:** 31.6% of deaths involved Delay 1 (preferring informal care, poor awareness); 45.1% involved Delay 3 (poor quality care at facilities) [16].
- **Midwife support:** UNFPA warned in 2025 that it could only support 47% of targeted midwives globally. By 2026, an estimated 315,000 women of reproductive age could lose access to antenatal, family planning, and safe delivery services [15][23].
- **Health facility closures:** By 2025, at least 11 health centers had closed in Cox's Bazar due to funding shortfalls, along with specialist care reductions and layoffs of 1,200 teachers [22].
- **Increasing population pressure:** Since late 2024, approximately 160,000 new refugees have fled escalating violence in Myanmar, further straining resources [22].

**Cascade Effects:**
The funding reductions occurred during a period of compounding crises: COVID-19 pandemic restrictions, Cyclone Bulbul (November 2019), and overwhelmed local health systems. UNFPA adapted by distributing PPE, reproductive health kits, and implementing hygiene protocols. However, the funding gap directly limited the number of midwives that could be deployed and the availability of emergency obstetric care.

---

### 6.3 Impact on Mae La Camp (Thailand)

**UNFPA Partnership in Mae La:**
Since 2012, UNFPA has partnered with PPAT to provide integrated reproductive health services in five refugee camps. Between January and November 2013, over 55,000 refugees received direct services, and more than 18,300 were trained in related health topics [26][27]. This partnership led to significant improvements in family planning acceptance, with UNFPA noting that "before the clinic gained wide acceptance, many refugees simply did not know about the health benefits of family planning and sexual and reproductive health services" [26].

**Post-2021 Developments:**
- Since the 2021 military coup in Myanmar, the refugee population in Thai border camps has grown by 35% (nearly 30,000 new arrivals), increasing demand for services while funding declined [32][33][34].
- UNFPA Thailand's 2024 achievements included operationalizing the Prevention and Management of Adolescent Pregnancy Act, developing national fertility policy, and establishing standard operating procedures for One Stop Crisis Centres [38].
- UNFPA extended the "Safe Birth For All" intervention along the Thai-Myanmar border in June 2025 [37].

**The 2025 Funding Crisis (Compounding Effect):**
In January 2025, the U.S. government ordered a 90-day suspension of foreign aid, followed by permanent cuts to 83% of USAID programs. The U.S. previously covered 60% of total costs for food and healthcare in Thai border refugee camps [32][33]. This led to:
- Closure of IRC-run hospitals at Mae La and Umpiem camps, disrupting maternal care, vaccinations, and sanitation programs [36].
- Food aid cuts: standard household allowances reduced from 275 baht to 57 baht per adult monthly [33][34].
- Thai hospitals absorbing patients: Dr. Worawit Tantiwattanasap, Director of Umphang Hospital, stated that "hospitals must follow humanitarian and human rights principles and treat emergency cases, including labor and delivery, without charging fees" [36].
- The Border Consortium warned in February 2025 it could only guarantee food aid until end of March 2025 [34].

---

### 6.4 Comparative Funding Impact Assessment

| Dimension | Cox's Bazar | Mae La |
|---|---|---|
| **Funding baseline** | $943M JRP (2021); 73.1% funded | US covered 60% of total costs; $20M TBC request (2024) |
| **UNFPA 2021 impact** | Direct reduction in SRH services; midwife deployment limited | Service continuation through PPAT partnership; programmatic resilience |
| **Post-2021 refugee influx** | ~160,000 new arrivals since late 2024 (increasing strain) | 35% population increase (~30,000) since 2021 coup |
| **Health facility impact** | 11 health centers closed by 2025; specialist care reduced | IRC hospitals at Mae La and Umpiem closed (2025) |
| **Coping mechanisms** | Reduced SRH services; increased mortality risk | Illegal labor, smuggling, child labor, theft |
| **Government response** | Government maintains camp control; international donors fill gap | Thai MOPH providing 2,500 THB/month per health worker (2025) |

**Key Finding:**
The 2021 UNFPA funding reduction had a more direct and immediate impact in Cox's Bazar, where the entire health system depends on international humanitarian funding. In Mae La, the longer-established partnership model and Thai government infrastructure provided greater programmatic resilience, but the cumulative effect of US funding cuts in 2025 has proven catastrophic, causing hospital closures and food aid reductions that directly threaten maternal health services.

## Section 7: Summary and Conclusions

### 7.1 Comparative Health Indicator Summary

| Indicator | Cox's Bazar (Rohingya) | Mae La (Karen) | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ANC 4+ visits** | 71–77% (increasing) | 91% (consistently high) | Mae La superior |
| **Skilled birth attendance** | 69–88% (rapidly increasing from 12% baseline) | 95% (stable, high) | Mae La superior; Cox's Bazar shows dramatic improvement |
| **Maternal mortality ratio** | 179–300 per 100,000 | 126–176 per 100,000 | Mae La lower |
| **Neonatal mortality (28 days)** | 25–27 per 1,000 | 7.7–12 per 1,000 | Mae La substantially lower |
| **CEmONC availability** | Limited (1–5 facilities); 17% facilities 24/7 | None in-camp; all refer to Thai hospitals | Neither adequate |

### 7.2 Key Conclusions

1. **Protracted systems outperform emergency responses:** Mae La's 40-year health system consistently produces better maternal and neonatal outcomes than Cox's Bazar's 7-year emergency response. Time, institutional memory, and community trust are critical factors.

2. **In-camp CEmONC availability is critical for reducing maternal mortality:** The absence of comprehensive emergency obstetric care within Mae La remains a significant gap despite otherwise strong indicators, mirroring Cox's Bazar's challenges. Neither setting fully meets the WHO 2-hour EmOC access target.

3. **TBAs are an underutilized resource:** Both settings demonstrate that culturally respectful engagement of traditional birth attendants can dramatically improve facility-based delivery rates when TBAs are trained as referral agents and cultural mediators rather than being bypassed.

4. **Data quality challenges persist:** Conflicting data sources in Cox's Bazar (UNHCR reporting 93% SBA vs. WHO monitoring suggesting 43% facility births) highlight the need for harmonized health information systems.

5. **Funding volatility threatens all progress:** The 2021 UNFPA cuts and 2025 US aid reductions demonstrate that even well-established health systems like Mae La's are vulnerable to donor funding shocks, with direct consequences for maternal survival.
