# Comprehensive Management Plan for 68-Year-Old Man with Recurrent Near-Syncope, ECG Abnormalities, and Insurance Barriers

## Overview: Clinical Scenario Summary

This is a 68-year-old man presenting to the ED for the third time in one month with near-syncope and fatigue. His ECG shows sinus bradycardia (HR 52), occasional dropped beats, and a PR interval that varies between 180-240 milliseconds. Labs are unremarkable (Hb 13.8, Cr 1.1, K+ 4.2, Mg 2.0, TSH 2.4, troponin normal). He takes metoprolol 50 mg BID and atorvastatin. His daughter reports two witnessed episodes at home where he "went pale and couldn't talk" for approximately 30 seconds with spontaneous recovery. Outpatient Holter monitoring was ordered 3 weeks ago but denied twice by insurance (appeal pending), and cardiology has a 6-week wait for new patients.

This report addresses three critical questions: (1) the optimal immediate cardiac monitoring strategy that can be arranged from the ED, (2) evidence-based clinical thresholds that warrant mandatory admission versus outpatient workup, and (3) the appropriate disposition and monitoring plan per current guidelines.

## Part 1: Optimal Immediate Cardiac Monitoring Strategy from the ED

### 1A. Clinical Risk Stratification: This Patient Is HIGH RISK

Before discussing monitoring options, it is critical to recognize that this patient meets **high-risk criteria** by multiple validated risk stratification tools and guidelines:

**San Francisco Syncope Rule (SFSR):** This patient has an abnormal ECG, which alone classifies him as high-risk by the SFSR. The SFSR has a pooled sensitivity of 87% (95% CI 79-93%) and specificity of 52% (95% CI 43-62%) for predicting serious outcomes within 30 days [San Francisco Syncope Rule to Predict Short-Term Serious Outcomes: A Systematic Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3193123).

**Canadian Syncope Risk Score (CSRS):** Based on age >65, recurrent episodes, abnormal ECG, and no clear vasovagal predisposition, this patient would likely score in the **medium to high-risk range (score 2-4)** corresponding to a 30-day serious adverse event rate of 8-19% [Canadian Syncope Risk Score](https://reference.medscape.com/calculator/383/canadian-syncope-risk-score-csrs). The CSRS validation study found that 91.7% of arrhythmic outcomes in medium and high-risk patients were identified within 15 days [Multicenter ED Validation of the Canadian Syncope Risk Score](https://todd-roat.squarespace.com/s/Cards_Multicenter-Emergency-Department-Validation-of-the-Canadian-Syncope-Risk-Score.pdf).

**ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guidelines:** This patient has multiple high-risk features including older age (>60), male sex, abnormal ECG (sinus bradycardia, variable PR interval, dropped beats), and recurrent episodes. The guidelines state: "The presence of one or more serious medical conditions is the key determinant for further in-hospital management of patients after syncope" [2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Patients With Syncope](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000499).

**ESC 2018 Syncope Guidelines:** This patient has persistent sinus bradycardia and ECG abnormalities, which are classified as high-risk features warranting "an intensive diagnostic approach" and potential admission [2018 ESC Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Syncope](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/39/21/1883/4939241).

## Part 2: Evidence-Based Clinical Thresholds for Admission vs. Outpatient Management

### 2A. Thresholds That Warrant Mandatory Admission

Based on the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline, ESC 2018 Syncope Guidelines, and ACC/AHA/HRS 2017 Syncope Guidelines, the following thresholds warrant **mandatory admission and inpatient evaluation**:

#### Sinus Node Dysfunction (Sick Sinus Syndrome)

| Threshold | Guideline | Recommendation |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Sinus pauses >3 seconds** | 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline | Defines sinus node dysfunction; symptomatic pauses warrant admission and potential pacing [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline on the Evaluation and Management of Patients with Bradycardia and Cardiac Conduction Delay](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| **Sinus pauses >5 seconds** | Research literature | Represents a critical threshold for sudden death risk; may warrant ICU-level monitoring |
| **Symptomatic sinus bradycardia <40 bpm** | ESC 2018 | High-risk feature requiring admission [2018 ESC Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Syncope](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/39/21/1883/4939241) |

#### Atrioventricular (AV) Block

| Threshold | Guideline | Recommendation |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Mobitz II second-degree AV block** | ACC/AHA/HRS (Class I) | Permanent pacing recommended regardless of symptoms; "constitutes a medical emergency, even in minimally symptomatic patients, because of the risk of sudden death" [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline on the Evaluation and Management of Patients with Bradycardia and Cardiac Conduction Delay](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| **High-grade AV block** | ACC/AHA/HRS (Class I) | Permanent pacing recommended regardless of symptoms |
| **Complete (third-degree) AV block** | ACC/AHA/HRS (Class I) | Permanent pacing recommended regardless of symptoms; "improves survival especially if syncope has occurred" |
| **2:1 AV block** | Cannot be classified as Mobitz I or II | QRS width is suggestive but not definitive; requires EPS for localization; admission warranted for evaluation |
| **Marked first-degree AV block (PR >300 ms)** | ACC/AHA/HRS (Class IIa) | Pacemaker implantation is reasonable if associated with hemodynamic compromise or pacemaker syndrome symptoms |

#### Other High-Risk Findings

| Finding | Guideline | Recommendation |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| **PR >240 ms with QRS >120 ms or fascicular block** | ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline | Specific high-risk marker requiring attention |
| **Bifascicular block + unexplained syncope** | ACC/AHA/HRS (Class I) | EPS recommended; pacing indicated if HV >70 ms or inducible high-grade AV block |
| **Nonsustained ventricular tachycardia** | ESC 2018 | High-risk feature requiring admission |
| **Elevated troponin (>99th percentile)** | CSRS (2 points) | Warrants admission for evaluation of myocardial ischemia |
| **Structural heart disease** | ACC/AHA/HRS | "Syncope in patients with structural heart disease is a serious finding" with 60.6% rate of death, readmission, or ED visits within 30 days |

### 2B. Thresholds That Can Be Managed with Expedited Outpatient Workup

| Finding | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| **First-degree AV block (PR 200-240 ms) without symptoms** | Outpatient evaluation is appropriate; risk of progression to high-grade block is very low |
| **Isolated sinus bradycardia (HR 50-60 bpm) without symptoms** | Outpatient management is appropriate |
| **Sinus pauses <3 seconds in asymptomatic patients** | Outpatient evaluation; pauses >2 seconds are common on Holter and generally benign |
| **Reflex (vasovagal) syncope** | ACC/AHA/HRS: "Patients with presumed reflex-mediated syncope can be managed in the outpatient setting" (Class IIa) |
| **Low-risk CSRS (-3 to 0)** | 30-day serious adverse event rate ≤0.9%; safe for outpatient management |
| **Mobitz I (Wenckebach) in asymptomatic patients** | Can be managed outpatient if nocturnal and in young, healthy individuals; in older patients or those with symptoms, admission warranted |

### 2C. Application to This Patient

This patient's ECG shows:
- Sinus bradycardia (HR 52) — below 60 bpm, but above 40 bpm
- PR interval varying 180-240 ms — crosses the threshold for first-degree AV block (>200 ms)
- Occasional dropped beats — concerning for intermittent second-degree AV block

The **PR interval of 240 ms** is at the specific high-risk threshold identified in the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline. The **varying PR interval** (180-240 ms) with **dropped beats** is most consistent with intermittent second-degree AV block (likely Mobitz I/Wenckebach, but Mobitz II cannot be excluded without analyzing multiple consecutive conducted beats). The combination of **sinus bradycardia + PR prolongation + dropped beats + symptoms (near-syncope, fatigue)** warrants admission for inpatient monitoring.

The episode of pallor and inability to speak for ~30 seconds with spontaneous recovery is most consistent with **convulsive syncope** (syncope with brief seizure-like activity due to cerebral hypoperfusion) rather than seizure or TIA. Key supporting features: pallor (classic syncope sign), brief duration (~30 seconds, typical for syncope), spontaneous recovery without post-ictal confusion, and a plausible arrhythmic mechanism given the ECG abnormalities.

### 3B. Specific Disposition Plan for This Patient

#### Step 1: Admit for Inpatient Telemetry (Class I Recommendation)

This patient meets high-risk criteria and should be admitted for continuous cardiac monitoring. The 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guideline gives a Class I recommendation for hospital evaluation and treatment for patients with a serious medical condition potentially relevant to the cause of syncope [2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Patients With Syncope](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000499).

**During admission:**

1. **Hold metoprolol** in a monitored setting. Beta-blocker use is associated with OR 2.8 for sick sinus syndrome [ACC/AHA/HRS Guidelines on Beta-Blockers and AV Block](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628). About 85% of patients with drug-induced bradyarrhythmia may eventually require permanent pacing. Holding the medication will help determine whether the conduction abnormalities are drug-induced or intrinsic.

2. **Obtain echocardiogram** to evaluate for structural heart disease (LVEF, valvular disease, wall motion abnormalities).

3. **Perform serial troponin** testing to rule out myocardial ischemia.

4. **Continuous telemetry monitoring** for at least 24-48 hours to capture intermittent high-grade AV block or symptomatic pauses.

5. **Evaluate QRS duration and QTc** on ECG to complete CSRS risk stratification.

#### Step 2: If High-Grade AV Block or Symptomatic Pauses Are Documented

- **If Mobitz II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block is documented** — Even if it resolves after holding metoprolol, the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline recommends permanent pacing regardless of symptoms if not due to reversible causes. However, if the block clearly resolves off metoprolol and the patient is asymptomatic off the medication, a trial off beta-blockers with outpatient monitoring (14-day patch) may be reasonable before committing to pacemaker.

- **If sinus pauses >3 seconds are documented with symptoms** — This meets the definition of sinus node dysfunction and warrants permanent pacing.

- **If no high-grade block is documented but symptoms persist** — The patient likely has intermittent high-grade AV block or sick sinus syndrome that was not captured during the inpatient stay. The next step would be prolonged outpatient monitoring:

#### Step 3: Arrange Appropriate Outpatient Monitoring Before Discharge

Since the outpatient Holter was denied by insurance and cardiology is 6 weeks out, the following strategy is recommended:

**Primary strategy: Apply a 14-day patch monitor (Zio XT or CAM) upon discharge from the ED or hospital.**

- **Zio XT**: Use iRhythm's Direct Bill program. The company bills insurance directly and offers discounted self-pay rates, payment plans, and financial assistance for patients experiencing hardship. Call 1.888.693.2401 for benefit verification and enrollment. The patch provides continuous monitoring for up to 14 days and captures the window in which 91.7% of arrhythmic outcomes occur in medium/high-risk patients.

- **CAM Patch**: Alternatively, the Carnation Ambulatory Monitor can be applied at the bedside. Published ED data (Overlake Medical Center, 412 patients, 2021) showed that applying the CAM patch at discharge reduced time to diagnosis from 21 days to 7 days and reduced 30-day ED readmissions from 40% to 7% [Carnation Ambulatory Monitor](https://www.bardydiagnostics.com/cam).

**Secondary strategy: Initiate the insurance appeal with clinical documentation.**

The appeal should include:
- Citations from the ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guidelines (Class I recommendation for cardiac monitoring)
- Documentation of three ED visits in one month
- Documentation of witnessed episodes (pallor, inability to speak)
- ECG evidence of sinus bradycardia, variable PR interval, and dropped beats
- The patient's age (68) and male sex as high-risk features
- The 6-week wait for cardiology as a significant delay in care

**If the 14-day patch is non-diagnostic but symptoms persist:**

The next step would be:
1. **Mobile Cardiac Outpatient Telemetry (MCOT)** for up to 30 days with real-time monitoring — though this typically requires prior failed 14-day monitoring for insurance coverage
2. **Implantable Loop Recorder (ILR)** for up to 3 years of monitoring — the ACC/AHA/HRS guideline gives this a Class IIa recommendation for syncope suspected to be of arrhythmic etiology

#### Step 4: Safety Net Planning

Regardless of disposition, the following safety net measures should be implemented:

- **Return precautions**: Instruct the patient and family to return to the ED immediately for syncope, near-syncope, chest pain, palpitations, dyspnea, or any episode of loss of consciousness
- **Driving restrictions**: Discuss driving restrictions given the risk of recurrent syncope; the patient should not drive until the diagnosis is established and treatment is initiated
- **Family supervision**: Advise family members to stay with the patient when possible, especially during high-risk activities (driving, climbing stairs, bathing)
- **Fall precautions**: Review home safety measures to prevent injury from falls
- **Beta-blocker management**: If the patient is discharged before a definitive diagnosis is made, discuss with cardiology whether to continue, hold, or taper metoprolol. If held, the medication should be tapered (not abruptly discontinued) to avoid rebound tachycardia and hypertension.
