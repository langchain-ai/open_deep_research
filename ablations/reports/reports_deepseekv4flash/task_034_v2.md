# Vertical Integration Strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (ofi) in Cocoa Processing (2018–2026): An Extensively Revised and Deepened Analysis

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive, extensively revised analysis of the vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam Food Ingredients (ofi) in cocoa processing from 2018 to 2026, incorporating the latest quantitative disclosures (2025–2026 data), sector-wide baselines from independent initiatives, differentiated buyer archetype mapping, and forward-looking regulatory and competitive dynamics.

The three companies collectively process approximately 60% of the world's cocoa. The analysis reveals a sector undergoing transformative convergence driven by the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), evolving sustainability disclosure obligations, and unprecedented cocoa price volatility (prices surged to ~$12,000/tonne in late 2024 before collapsing toward $4,000/tonne by mid-2025).

**Key findings include:**

- **Olam/ofi leads in explicit direct-sourcing disclosure** (67% direct, disclosed in 2020), while Barry Callebaut's ~40% direct figure and Cargill's ~60% direct figure come from third-party or historical sources. All face significant traceability challenges in indirect supply chains.
- **Cargill has invested the most in West African processing capacity** during the analysis window ($113 million plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan).
- **Farmer income evidence remains dominated by company-commissioned research.** The gap between poverty line metrics (~$2.15/day) and living income benchmarks ($6,372–$8,039/year) remains vast. Independent studies show only 4–13% of farmers achieve living income.
- **All three companies are EUDR-ready in their direct supply chains,** but none have publicly disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions. Independent audits (AidEnvironment, Mighty Earth, Global Witness) have documented deforestation linked to all three processors' supply chains.
- **Ferrero's published tier-1 supplier list confirms Olam/ofi's absence** from its direct supply chain while Barry Callebaut and Cargill are included – a significant competitive finding.
- **The EUDR deadline is now December 30, 2026** (large operators), with a mandated simplification review by April 2026 creating regulatory uncertainty.
- **Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate division split** remains under active consideration but unconfirmed. Cargill's "flexibility" strategy and Olam's AtSource platform differentiation represent key competitive vectors.

## 1. Direct-Sourced vs. Broker-Purchased Cocoa Percentages (2025–2026 Data)

### 1.1 Barry Callebaut

Barry Callebaut does **not formally disclose** a standalone percentage for direct-sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa in its 2024/25 Annual Social and Environmental Impact Report or GRI Report. The most specific publicly available figure remains from a 2021 IDH-funded case study, indicating approximately **40% of cocoa through direct supply chains** traceable to farm level, implying ~60% through indirect channels.

The 2024/25 Impact Report clarifies the distinction: "We have achieved full traceability to farm level for all cocoa volumes sourced directly for the European market – the world's largest chocolate market. For indirectly sourced volumes, we rely on our suppliers and are actively working with them to meet the same rigorous traceability standards we apply to our own direct sourcing" [[1]](https://www.barry-callebaut.com/system/files/2025-11/Annual%20Social%20and%20Environmental%20Impact%20Report%202024_25.pdf).

The company sources directly from smallholder farmers in Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Cameroon, Indonesia, Brazil, Ecuador, and Nigeria, "as well as from third-party suppliers from the main cocoa-producing countries" [[2]](https://www.barry-callebaut.com/system/files/2025-11/GRI%20report%202024_25.pdf).

**EUDR supply chain complexity context:** A single 40ft container (25 MT) of cocoa can involve "likely min. 400 farms"; a standard Bill of Lading (250 MT) involves approx. 4,000 farms; a bulk shipment (5,000 MT) involves approx. 80,000 farms [[3]](https://www.callebaut.com/sites/default/files/2025-01/EUDR%20Introduction%20for%20Distributors%20and%20Artisans%20-%20Callebaut.pdf).

**Data gap:** Barry Callebaut has not updated its direct-sourcing percentage since the 2021 IDH study. The traceability percentages reported (78.9%, 91%, etc.) refer to traceability within the direct supply chain, not the share of direct vs. indirect sourcing.

### 1.2 Cargill

Cargill **does not publicly disclose** the specific percentage or volume of cocoa sourced directly from farmer cooperatives versus through third-party brokers/intermediaries in its 2024 or 2025 Cocoa Impact Reports.

The clearest available data comes from an IDH/C-Lever.org case study (2021), indicating that Cargill's **direct supply chain comprises approximately 60% of its cocoa sourcing**, with the remaining 40% being indirect [[4]](https://www.cargill.com/sustainability/cocoa/the-cargill-cocoa-promise-10-year-anniversary). The U.S. Department of Labor's April 2026 report corroborates that "around 40–60% of cocoa moves through indirect supply chains with limited traceability" [[5]](https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/ILAB/CLCCG-2024-Framework-of-Action-ENG-CLEAN.pdf).

Cargill sources cocoa directly from six countries (Brazil, Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire, Ecuador, Ghana, Indonesia) and indirectly from 19 other origins [[6]](https://www.cargill.com/sustainability/doc/1451373894196/2025-cargill-impact-report-cocoa.pdf).

**Sustainability program coverage:** KPMG N.V. provides limited assurance on sustainability KPIs for the Cargill Cocoa Promise, which "accounts for about one third of the cocoa we source" [[7]](https://www.cargill.com/sustainability/doc/1432271059750/2024-impact-report-cocoa.pdf). Between January and June 2024, 54% of Cargill's cocoa volumes were sourced sustainably [[8]](https://www.kakaoforum.de/wp-content/uploads/Roadmap_Cargill.pdf).

**Data gap:** Cargill has never explicitly disclosed a direct vs. indirect sourcing ratio. The 60/40 figure is a decade-old estimate.

### 1.3 Olam (Olam Food Ingredients/ofi)

Olam remains the **only one of the three companies to have explicitly disclosed a direct-sourcing percentage**. On September 22, 2020, Olam Cocoa announced that it had achieved 100% traceability for its directly sourced cocoa supply, which constitutes **approximately two-thirds (66.7%) of its global cocoa purchases** across nine countries. The remaining one-third (approximately 33.3%) is sourced indirectly through traders and intermediaries [[9]](https://www.olamgroup.com/news/all-news/press-release/olam-cocoa-hits-100-percent-traceability-target-across-its-direct-global-supply-chain.html).

Andrew Brooks, ofi's Global Head of Cocoa Sustainability, stated: "Demand for traceable cocoa is certainly becoming a key requirement for most customers, particularly as consumers take more of an interest in where their food comes from." He acknowledged that the indirect supply chain presents challenges "that require collaboration across industry, NGOs, governments, and regulatory frameworks" [[10]](https://www.foodnavigator.com/Article/2020/09/22/Olam-Cocoa-hits-100-traceability-in-direct-cocoa-supplies-This-is-a-crucial-step-towards-eradicating-child-labour-and-deforestation).

**Updated disclosure status:** As of 2024, ofi's LinkedIn page confirms "100% of our direct cocoa bean supply chain is traceable" using proprietary technology [[11]](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ofi-group_cocoa-carbon-partnerships-activity-7404460459405312001-mH3A). However, the 67/33 direct/indirect split has **not been publicly updated since 2020**. Olam Group's subsequent annual reports do not contain a line-item disclosure of this ratio.

**Critical caveat:** The U.S. Department of Labor (April 2026) notes that "co-mingling of cocoa beans begins at or near the first point of aggregation and continues at every subsequent stage of the supply chain, making farm-level traceability structurally difficult to maintain even where it is attempted" [[5]](https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/ILAB/CLCCG-2024-Framework-of-Action-ENG-CLEAN.pdf).

## 5. Farmer Income Metrics Under Sustainability Programs (2024–2026 Data with Methodological Rigor)

### 5.1 Overview of Evidence Quality

**Critical finding:** No truly independent, peer-reviewed academic study or standalone NGO impact assessment was found that specifically quantifies farmer income changes under any of the three companies' programs using rigorous counterfactual methods (randomized controlled trials, difference-in-difference, or propensity score matching). The available evidence comes from company-commissioned reports, company-partnered research, or industry-wide studies that do not isolate individual company impacts.

### 5.2 Barry Callebaut

**Company-Reported Data (PwC-audited):**
- **557,739 cocoa farmers lifted above the poverty line** in 2024/25, a 30% increase from 428,997 in 2023/24
- Uses the World Bank international poverty line benchmark (~$2.15/day, updated to ~$3.00/day) [[36]](https://www.barry-callebaut.com/en-US/sustainability/strategy/Prospering-Farmers)

**Independent Verification:** PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) provides assurance on Forever Chocolate metrics [[1]](https://www.barry-callebaut.com/system/files/2025-11/Annual%20Social%20and%20Environmental%20Impact%20Report%202024_25.pdf).

**Agri-Logic White Paper "Harvesting Insights: What Drives Farmer Prosperity?" (Published February 2026):**
*Co-funded by Barry Callebaut, IDH, and Rainforest Alliance – not fully independent.*

- **Percentage of farmers achieving Living Income by country (2023/24 season):**
  - Vietnam: 40-45%
  - Cameroon: 40-45%

## 6. EUDR Compliance Responses and Mapping Milestones (2025–2026 Data)

### 6.1 Regulatory Background and Current Timeline

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) – Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 – requires commodities placed on or exported from the EU market to be "deforestation-free" (cutoff date: December 31, 2020) and traceable to the specific plot with geolocation data.

**Current binding deadlines (as of May 2026):**
- **December 30, 2026:** For large and medium operators (previously December 30, 2025, then postponed)
- **June 30, 2027:** For micro and small enterprises [[46]](https://taxnews.ey.com/news/2026-0237-eu-deforestation-regulation-application-postponed-to-30-december-2026)

**Key developments:**
- Postponement enacted under **Regulation (EU) 2025/2650**
- Mandated **simplification review** requires European Commission to assess impacts by **April 30, 2026** – could trigger further legislative changes [[47]](https://www.foodingredientsfirst.com/news/eudr-delay-april-review-uncertainty.html)
- Only the **operator who first places** an EUDR-relevant commodity on the market will file a Due Diligence Statement (DDS); downstream operators no longer submit separate statements [[46]](https://taxnews.ey.com/news/2026-0237-eu-deforestation-regulation-application-postponed-to-30-december-2026)

**Non-compliance penalties:** Fines of at least 4% of annual EU turnover, market bans, product confiscation [[48]](https://www.coolset.com/academy/the-eu-deforestation-regulation-eudr-what-businesses-need-to-know-and-do)

## 9. Forward-Looking Regulatory and Competitive Dynamics

### 9.1 EUDR Implementation: The Compliance Limbo

**Current Status:** The EUDR application date for large operators is **December 30, 2026**. A mandated simplification review by **April 30, 2026** could trigger further legislative changes, leaving companies in "compliance limbo" – uncertain whether to invest in systems for rules that may change again [[47]](https://www.foodingredientsfirst.com/news/eudr-delay-april-review-uncertainty.html).

**Key Simplifications Adopted:**
- Only the initial operator filing a DDS; downstream operators only need to retain DDS reference numbers
- Simplified one-off declarations for micro/small primary operators in low-risk countries
- Printed books, newspapers, pictures removed from scope [[46]](https://taxnews.ey.com/news/2026-0237-eu-deforestation-regulation-application-postponed-to-30-december-2026)

**Competitive Implications:**
- Companies with advanced traceability systems (all three processors) have a buffer period but face uncertainty about requirement changes
- **Barry Callebaut's position:** Strong advocate for no further delay; states readiness regardless of timeline
- **Cargill's position:** Publicly confident of compliance by deadline
- **Olam's position:** Supports schedule but concerned about smallholder readiness

**Critical Competitive Dynamic:** Companies unable to provide EUDR-compliant traceability risk deselection from EU market procurement shortlists. Traceability is transitioning from competitive advantage to **baseline qualification requirement**.

### 9.2 EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

**Current Status:** Approved May 2024, came into force July 25, 2024. Significantly amended via **Omnibus I** (adopted February 24, 2026, published February 26, 2026) [[83]](https://commission.europa.eu).

**Key Changes:**
- **Scope drastically reduced:** Now applies to companies with **more than 5,000 employees and €1.5 billion net turnover** (down from original threshold of ~1,000 employees and €450 million)
- **Transposition deadline extended:** Member States must transpose by **July 2028** (originally July 2026)
- **Operational compliance:** Extended toward **July 2029**
- **Mandatory transition plans** for climate change mitigation **removed** under Omnibus I
- Regular due diligence reviews reduced from annually to **once every five years** [[84]](https://www.corporate-sustainability-due-diligence-directive.com)

**Implications for Cocoa Processors:**
- All three processors (Barry Callebaut: ~CHF 14.8 billion revenue; Cargill Cocoa: significant global turnover; Olam Group: S$56.2 billion) will be subject to CSDDD
- Must conduct human rights and environmental due diligence throughout operations, subsidiaries, and business partner chains
- Must address adverse impacts such as child labor, pollution, and biodiversity loss across cocoa supply chains
- Penalties: Fines up to **3% of net worldwide turnover**; injunction measures; compensation for damages

**Competitive Implication:** Companies with established human rights due diligence systems (Olam's OFIS/AtSource with FLA monitoring; Barry Callebaut's PwC-verified systems; Cargill's KPMG-assured reporting) have a compliance advantage over smaller processors.

### 9.3 EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and ESRS E4 (Biodiversity)

**Current Status:** Under Omnibus I (political agreement December 9, 2025), CSRD now applies only to **largest companies** (more than 1,000 employees). "Stop-the-clock" Directive (April 14, 2025) postponed reporting for wave two and three companies. Omnibus proposal suggests postponing CSRD implementation by **two years** (to reporting in 2028 for the 2027 financial year) [[85]](https://normative.io/insight/csddd).

**ESRS E4 – Biodiversity and Ecosystems Disclosure Requirements:** [[86]](https://www.efrag.org)

**Key Changes Post-Omnibus (December 2025 Draft):**
- Biodiversity transition plan disclosure now **conditional upon existence** of such plan; companies not mandated to develop plans solely for reporting
- Policies, targets, and actions only need disclosure if they **already exist** and are relevant to material impacts
- **Aggregation across sites** acceptable when it improves clarity
- Stronger emphasis on **materiality** as a filter

**Key Disclosure Requirements (Where Material):**
- How the undertaking affects biodiversity and ecosystems (material impacts, risks, opportunities)
- Plans and capacity to adapt strategy in line with **Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework**
- **Financial effects** related to biodiversity risks and opportunities
- Site-specific disclosures for sites affecting biodiversity-sensitive areas
- **125 data points** across six biodiversity-specific sections covering full value chain

**Implications for Cocoa Processors:**
- All three processors are likely subject to CSRD due to employee counts >1,000 and EU market presence
- Must disclose deforestation impacts, biodiversity dependencies, and alignment with global biodiversity goals
- ESRS E4 requires traceability of products and disclosure of actions to address biodiversity impacts – reinforcing EUDR compliance investments
- Companies with established TNFD reporting (ofi published its first TNFD Report in 2025) have first-mover advantage [[87]](https://assets.ctfassets.net/yzlb24ni1fxz/4qouj6guavX0FRhawKMXIL/acfed0b609aa2a9ca473508bfc61491b/ofi_Group_Limited_TNFD_Report_2025.pdf)

### 9.4 West African Cocoa Sector Regulations

**Ghana's Cocoa Crisis and Reform Package (February 2026):**
- Farmgate price cut by nearly **29%** from GH₵3,625 to GH₵2,587 per 64-kg bag (approximately $3,580/MT)
- Driven by collapse in global cocoa prices from ~$12,000/tonne to ~$3,500–4,100/tonne by February 2026
- COCOBOD faced outstanding arrears exceeding **GH₵10 billion** and total liabilities estimated at **GH₵60 billion**
- Reform package includes: market-aligned price reset, bond-based financing model, proposed legislation guaranteeing farmers minimum of **70% of FOB price**, **50% local processing target**, forensic audit of COCOBOD finances [[88]](https://www.confectionerynews.com)

**Côte d'Ivoire Considers Following Ghana:**
- Senior official: "We have put all options on the table and discussions are progressing well. Courageous and realistic decisions will be taken soon"
- ICCIG Executive Secretary Alex Assanvo emphasized need for measures to prevent structural damage and defended the Living Income Differential (LID) policy [[89]](https://www.reuters.com)

**Living Income Differential (LID) Analysis:**
- USD 400 per tonne markup on FOB price, introduced in 2019 for 2020/21 harvest season
- European Commission's JRC analysis: Effects range from "negligible to substantially positive," varying with scenarios
- Ghana benefits more than Côte d'Ivoire under most scenarios
- Côte d'Ivoire needs additional **supply controls** to reach target prices
- May **significantly affect government revenues negatively** in both countries
- Implementation of complementary cocoa supply control measures essential for creating best outcomes [[90]](https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu)

**African Regional Standard for Sustainable Cocoa (ARS-1000):**
- Introduced in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana since **October 2024**
- Mandates: no deforestation, national traceability systems, social responsibility including child labor elimination efforts

**Competitive Implications:**
- Farmgate price volatility creates income uncertainty for farmers, potentially undermining sustainability program impacts
- Government price cuts may reduce farmer participation in certification programs
- Processors with direct supply relationships and farmer support programs (all three) better positioned to maintain supply stability during regulatory turbulence
- LID at risk of erosion if governments cannot enforce it amid price declines

### 9.5 Barry Callebaut's Potential Cocoa/Chocolate Division Split

**Status as of May 2026:** **Rumors remain unconfirmed.** Multiple financial media outlets treat it as an active strategic consideration, but no official announcement has been made.

**Timeline of Reports:**
- **Late 2025:** Reuters reported Barry Callebaut considering separating cocoa processing division from chocolate operations
- **December 2025:** Shares surged up to 6.5% on news; Kepler Cheuvreux noted "around two-thirds of the cocoa division's gross sales come from internal transfers, making a clean carve-out challenging" [[91]](https://www.reuters.com)
- **January 9, 2026:** Confectionery News reported rationale: reducing exposure to volatile cocoa commodity prices, creating more stable, higher-margin chocolate operation; different risk profiles may appeal to distinct investors
- **April 16, 2026 (Half-Year Results):** New CEO Hein Schumacher (appointed Jan 26, 2026): "We have significant work to do to reinvigorate the company after a turbulent period of industry disruption and transformation." Announced "Focus for Growth" plan with full update expected in June 2026 [[26]](https://www.barry-callebaut.com/system/files/2026-04/Barry-Callebaut-Group-Half-Year-Results-Fiscal-Year%202025-26.pdf)

**Strategic Rationale:**
- Cocoa business is "very volatile and capital intensive" (Kepler Cheuvreux)
- Separation would leave "a more stable, profitable and less capital intensive operation"
- Potential for long-term supply deal between separated entities
- Could optimize financing by appealing to investors with distinct risk preferences

**Potential Split Benefits:**
- Chocolate division could achieve higher valuation multiples (consumer products)
- Cocoa division could focus on commodity processing efficiency
- Reduced exposure to cocoa terminal market volatility (prices ranged from ~$5,300 to ~$9,425/tonne in FY 2024/25)

**Competitive Implications:**
- A split could **disrupt customer relationships** – premium chocolate manufacturers who value integrated supply may seek alternatives
- Could **benefit Cargill and Olam** if Barry Callebaut's customers diversify suppliers during transition
- Could also **create opportunities** for Barry Callebaut's chocolate division to source from competitors (Cargill, Olam) if internal supply agreements are not renewed
- Michael van der Bom of Cargill described "flexibility" as key, potentially positioning Cargill to capture disaffected Barry Callebaut customers

### 9.6 Cargill's "Flexibility" Strategy

**Core Strategic Positioning:** Cargill has articulated a strategy centered on **flexibility** as the defining competitive advantage. Michiel van der Bom, product line director for cocoa and chocolate at Cargill: "What matters now is flexibility. Our role is to help customers with options that reflect today's realities and the uncertainty ahead" [[30]](https://www.cargill.com/food-beverage/emea/news/flexibility-is-key-to-navigating-todays-cocoa-and-chocolate-market).

   - **67% lower carbon footprint** (third-party verified LCA)
   - Free from major allergens (dairy, soy, peanuts, tree nuts); vegan, Kosher, Halal
   - Cargill is **exclusive global B2B distributor** for Voyage Foods [[92]](https://www.cargill.com)

3. **Cell-Based Cacao (Kokomodo partnership):**
   - Proof-of-concept partnership supported by EIT Food (European Institute of Innovation and Technology)
   - "Cultivated cacao is real cacao grown from selected non-GMO cacao cell lines using cellular agriculture"
   - Kokomodo acquired by Pluri in 2025 (71% stake for $4.5 million)
   - Targeting FDA GRAS by 2026 and potential market entry by **2027** [[93]](https://www.cargill.com)

**Implications for Premium Chocolate Manufacturers:**
- More options: traditional certified cocoa, cocoa alternatives (NextCoa), future cell-based ingredients
- Ability to manage cost structures while maintaining product quality and sustainability commitments
- "Volatility, uncertainty, complexity and ambiguity have become familiar features of the cocoa market"
- Cargill positions itself as "a trusted end-to-end chocolate solutions provider"

### 9.7 Olam/ofi's AtSource Platform Differentiation and Cocoa Compass Narrative

**AtSource Integrated Platform:** Sustainability management system created by Olam Group in 2018, actively monitoring **10 core sustainability topics** related to **12 UN SDGs** [[94]](https://qa-atsource.olamnet.com/atsource.html).

**Three Tiers:**
- **AtSourceV:** Traceability, risk and performance assessment; FSA Silver equivalence
- **AtSource+:** Granular metrics, advanced environmental footprints, annual third-party verification; **FSA Gold equivalence** (highest-level recognition)
- **AtSource∞:** Aspirational level focusing on landscape regeneration

**Competitive Differentiation:**
- Over **100 environmental and social metrics** on carbon, social impact, water use – supporting ESG reporting for global brands
- **Carbon Scenario Planner** – helps customers assess carbon reduction pathways
- **AI-powered Carbon Stock Monitoring tool** – high-quality data on forest cover and tree loss
- **Cocoa Farmer Income Tool** – assesses living income and guides targeted support
- **SAI Platform FSA equivalence** – reduces audit burden for farmers, validates the program globally

**Cocoa Compass Living Income Narrative:**
- **Headline achievement:** 155,000 farmers (45% of program participants) earning a living income – **surpassing 2030 target six years early** [[13]](https://assets.ctfassets.net/yzlb24ni1fxz/701vWnha1u6CIE8r8kHL6b/a27daac5d82c54c512dcea36ef70d960/ofi_Cocoa_Compass_Report_FINAL_09_Dec_2025.pdf)
- Developed **first-ever living income reference values for Cameroon, Nigeria, and Papua New Guinea**
- ofi frames itself as "the preferred partner for positive change"

**Critical Caveat:** The living income achievement was "largely driven by cocoa market price increases" (prices peaked at ~$12,000/tonne in late 2024) – raising questions about sustainability if prices decline. The 2025/26 price plunge to ~$4,000/tonne may materially reduce the number of farmers earning living income absent program-driven improvements.

**Competitive Positioning:**
- **Advantage:** AtSource provides differentiation for sustainability-focused clients who need ESG data for their own reporting (CSRD, CSDDD compliance)
- **Advantage:** First-mover in living income measurement with three new reference countries
- **Risk:** If living income achievement was primarily price-driven, the metric is vulnerable to market cycles
- **Risk:** Ferrero's exclusion from tier-1 supplier list remains a significant competitive gap

## 13. Conclusions and Competitive Outlook

### 13.1 Convergence and Differentiation

The vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have converged significantly over the 2018–2026 period, driven by three primary forces: EUDR compliance requirements, premium chocolate manufacturers' demands for verifiably sustainable cocoa, and unprecedented price volatility. However, important differentiation remains.

**On direct sourcing:** Olam leads with the only explicit disclosure of a 67% direct sourcing ratio (2020). Barry Callebaut's ~40% direct sourcing figure and Cargill's ~60% estimate suggest significant room for growth in direct supply chain control. None of the three companies has updated their direct-sourcing disclosure since 2020–2021.

**On traceability and mapping:** All three companies have achieved 100% polygon mapping or traceability in their direct supply chains. Barry Callebaut's 1.5 million farms mapped, Cargill's 100% of Promise supply chain in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana, and Olam's 100% of sustainability programs mapped represent significant investments. However, indirect supply chains remain a critical vulnerability for all three.

**On capital investments:** Cargill invested the most in West African processing capacity during the 2018–2024 period ($113 million plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan). Olam's major investments predated the analysis window, though Olam Agri's $200 million expansion signals renewed commitment.

**On farmer income:** All three companies report positive income impacts, but the evidence base relies entirely on company-commissioned or co-authored research. The gap between "poverty line" metrics (~$2.15–$3.00/day) and "living income" benchmarks ($6,372–$8,039/year) remains vast. Independent studies consistently show that only 4–13% of farmers achieve living income levels.

**On EUDR compliance:** All three companies have invested heavily in traceability systems, satellite monitoring, and polygon mapping. Barry Callebaut and Cargill appear most advanced in farm mapping, while Olam benefits from its earlier 2020 traceability milestone. None have disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions, which remains a critical transparency gap. Independent audits have documented deforestation linkages for all three processors.

**On competitive positioning:** Ferrero's public supplier list confirms Barry Callebaut and Cargill as tier-1 suppliers while Olam is absent – a significant competitive blow. Mondelēz works with all three processors but has a notably deep partnership with Barry Callebaut through Cocoa Life. Lindt's confidentiality on processor relationships prevents direct comparison.

### 13.2 Forward-Looking Competitive Dynamics

**Regulatory landscape:** The EUDR delay to December 2026, with a mandated simplification review by April 2026, creates regulatory uncertainty. The CSDDD's narrowed scope (5,000+ employees) still captures all three processors. CSRD/ESRS E4 will require biodiversity disclosures that reinforce traceability investments.

**West African reforms:** Ghana's 29% farmgate price cut and Côte d'Ivoire's potential following suit create income uncertainty for farmers, potentially undermining sustainability program impacts. The LID faces erosion risk amid price declines.

**Corporate restructuring:** Barry Callebaut's potential cocoa/chocolate split (unconfirmed but actively explored) could reshape competitive dynamics. If implemented, it may benefit Cargill and Olam as customers diversify suppliers. Cargill's "flexibility" strategy (portfolio approach, cocoa-free NextCoa, cell-based cacao) positions it to capture market share from disrupted competitors. Olam's AtSource platform and Cocoa Compass living income narrative provide differentiation for sustainability-focused clients.

**The next phase of competition** will likely center on:
1. **Innovation capabilities** (Barry Callebaut's Chocolate Academies and proprietary products; Cargill's NextCoa and cell-based cacao)
2. **Platform-based services** (Olam's AtSource with 100+ sustainability metrics)
3. **Flexibility in formulation and supply chain management** (Cargill's portfolio approach)
4. **Capacity to absorb price volatility** (Barry Callebaut's potential split; Cargill's private ownership; Olam's ofi IPO exploration)

**The strategic differentiation among these three processors is narrowing** as sustainability and traceability become baseline requirements rather than competitive advantages. Companies that can demonstrate independent verification of farmer income impacts, transparent disclosure of non-compliant volumes, and innovative solutions for cocoa price volatility will have the strongest competitive position in premium chocolate manufacturing contracts.
