# New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market Analysis (2018–2024, with Outlook to 2031)

## Executive Summary

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market, valued at approximately **NZD $1.8 billion** in 2025–2026 (IBISWorld ANZSIC C2221), has experienced a complex trajectory from 2018 to 2024. The industry, comprising approximately **267 businesses** and employing around **4,100–5,000 workers**, processes roughly **85,000–100,000 tonnes of structural steel annually** against a capacity that expanded from **120,000 to 150,000 tonnes** between 2018 and 2023. The sector faces a pivotal moment: near-term headwinds from reduced construction activity and import competition are balanced by a substantial **$275 billion national infrastructure pipeline**, growing demand for green steel solutions, and structural advantages in seismic-resistant construction.

### 1.1 Revenue (NZD) — Data Sourcing Methodology

#### Step-by-Step Instructions for Stats NZ Query

The Annual Enterprise Survey (AES) is New Zealand's most comprehensive source of financial statistics, covering over 60% of New Zealand's GDP [1]. The AES provides annual financial performance information about businesses operating within New Zealand, collected from businesses with balance dates between 1 October in one year and 30 September the following year [1].

**Critical note:** The AES is designed predominantly at the **four-digit ANZSIC level (109 industries)** [2]. ANZSIC06 code C2221 (Structural Steel Fabricating) is a six-digit class that sits within a broader manufacturing grouping. Specific financial data for C2221 at the six-digit level is **not publicly available in a disaggregated format** from Stats NZ's standard AES public releases. Obtaining C2221-specific revenue would require a **customized data request** from Stats NZ's Customised Data Services.

**To query AES data via Stats NZ platforms:**

1. **Via NZ.Stat (nzdotstat.stats.govt.nz):**
   - Navigate to "Industry" subject area
   - Select "Annual Enterprise Survey (AES)" dataset
   - Filter by ANZSIC06 industry classification
   - Search for "Structural Steel Fabricating" or code "C2221"
   - Note that data may only be available at the four-digit level (C222)

2. **Via InfoShare (infoshare.stats.govt.nz):**
   - Select "Industry" categories
   - Choose "Annual Enterprise Survey (AES)" subject area
   - Filter by ANZSIC06 code

   - C2221-level breakdown may require a customized data extraction

3. **Via Stats NZ website (stats.govt.nz):**
   - Go to "Topics" > "Industries"
   - Browse "Annual Enterprise Survey" information releases

4. **Via Figure.NZ (figure.nz):**
   - Provides user-friendly visualizations of Stats NZ AES data

### 1.3 Fabricated Tonnage — Triangulation Approach

**Explicit acknowledgment:** Stats NZ does **NOT** publish a direct tonnage time series for structural steel fabrication. No official government statistical series exists that directly measures the tonnage of structural steel fabricated annually in New Zealand.

#### Proposed Triangulation Methodology

Combining multiple data sources, annual tonnage estimates can be constructed as follows:

##### (a) SCNZ Industry Capacity/Utilization Surveys

SCNZ publishes tri-annual "Structural Steel Industry Updates" based on a **quarterly fabricator forward-workload survey**. The methodology involves:
- Using reported total capacity (120,000–150,000 tonnes) as the denominator
- Using reported spare capacity percentages to calculate actual tonnage: Actual = Capacity × (1 - Spare %)
- Cross-referencing with the "approximately 100,000 tonnes" figure mentioned in multiple years

**Compiled capacity and fabrication table:**

| Year/Period | Stated Capacity | Spare Capacity % | Implied Fabrication | SCNZ Stated "~100,000t" |
|-------------|----------------|------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| 2019 (peak) | 120,000t | ~17% (implied) | ~100,000t | ✓ Yes |
| Jun 2020 | 120,000t | 47% | ~63,600t | ✓ Also stated |
| Dec 2021 | 120,000t | 35% | ~78,000t | ✓ Also stated |
| Mar 2022 | 120,000t | 37% | ~75,600t | Also stated |
| Sep 2023 (yr end) | 120,000t | 29% | ~85,200t | ✓ Also stated |
| Mar 2024 (Mar 23 rep) | 120,000t | 28% | ~86,400t | ✓ Also stated |
| Mar 2024 (Sep 23 rep) | 120,000–150,000t | 41% | ~70,800–88,500t | ✓ Also stated |

**Critical observation:** SCNZ consistently uses the rounded figure of "approximately 100,000 tonnes" across nearly all reports regardless of spare capacity calculations. The implied fabrication from capacity × (1 - spare capacity) yields numbers ranging from ~63,600t to ~100,000t, suggesting the "100,000t" is a benchmark/rounded industry reference rather than a precisely calculated figure. The 100,000t figure appears to be the long-run average throughput.

**What is included in SCNZ figures:** The SCNZ tonnage figures cover **structural steel only** — fabricated beams, columns, trusses, connections, and other load-bearing steel components used in buildings and bridges. This is confirmed by SCNZ's consistent reference to "structural steel" being "turned into buildings and bridges" [11][12][13][14].

**What is excluded:** SCNZ does **not** include reinforcing steel (rebar, mesh) in its tonnage reporting. Reinforcing steel is a completely separate supply chain dominated by Pacific Steel (NZ) Ltd, which produces approximately 250,000 tonnes annually of reinforcing products [15][16]. The PBU (Pacific Steel) tonnage falls under ANZSIC C2110 (Iron and Steel Manufacturing) or a separate classification from structural steel fabrication (C2221).

**Capacity growth over time:**
- 2018: "annual capacity (~110,000 tonnes)" per SCNZ 111:2018 guide [17]
- 2020–2023: Capacity rose from **120,000 to 150,000 tonnes** over five years [13][14]
- This 25% increase reflects investment in state-of-the-art plant, machinery, and technology

##### (b) Trade Statistics — HS Code 7308

HS Code 7308 covers "Structures (excluding prefabricated buildings of heading 9406) and parts of structures (for example, bridges and bridge-sections, lock-gates, towers, lattice masts, roofs, roofing frameworks, doors, windows and their frames, thresholds for doors, shutters, balustrades, pillars and columns) of iron or steel" [18].

**Available trade data points:**

- **2024:** New Zealand exported structures and parts of structures of iron or steel (HS 730890) totaling **US$42.11 million** with a total quantity of **12,546 tonnes** [19]. Top destinations: Japan (US$14.82M, 4,918t), United States (US$8.97M, 2,340t), Australia (US$7.29M, 1,712t).

- **2024:** NZ imports of structures and parts of structures (HS 730890) were approximately **US$5.54 million** with a total quantity of **1,651 tonnes** [19]. Top suppliers: Australia (US$1.86M, 460t), China (US$1.82M, 468t).

- **2025:** NZ imports from China under full HS 7308: **US$25.57 million** [20].

- **2025:** NZ exports to the US under HS 7308: **US$4.07 million** [21].

**Methodology for using trade data:**
- Extract annual export values and quantities (kg) for HS 7308 from Stats NZ OMT datasets
- Convert to tonnage by dividing kg by 1,000
- Note that NZ steel exports are relatively small compared to domestic consumption
- The triangulation combines: Domestic estimated tonnage + Export tonnage - Import tonnage (as a check) to estimate net market consumption

**Net market consumption estimate (structural steel only):**
- Domestic fabrication: ~85,000–100,000t
- Imports (estimated via HS 7308): ~5,000–10,000t
- Exports: ~12,500t
- Net consumption: approximately **77,500–97,500 tonnes** per year

##### (c) Major Project Disclosures

Major infrastructure projects often disclose estimated steel tonnages. Key projects with disclosed tonnages (detailed in Section 4 of this report) include:
- City Rail Link: 4,000t structural steel, ~25,000–31,600t reinforcing steel
- New Dunedin Hospital: ~1,600 units structural steel (outpatient building), 490+ tonnes reinforcing steel (inpatient foundations)
- Te Kaha Stadium: 9,000 tonnes structural steel (roof)
- Christchurch Hospital Tower C: 1,500 tonnes structural steel

**Proposed overall triangulation formula:**
- **Estimated Annual Tonnage = SCNZ Survey Actual Tonnage (from capacity × utilization) + Trade Adjustment (exports - imports of fabricated structures) + Major Project Adjustment**

**Data gaps acknowledged:**
- SCNZ surveys cover SCNZ member fabricators; non-member fabricators may account for some additional volume
- The "approximately 100,000 tonnes" figure is rounded and may vary year-to-year
- Imported prefabricated steel structures (which compete with domestic fabrication) are not captured in SCNZ tonnage figures
- HS code 7308 includes items beyond "structural steel fabrication" as defined by ANZSIC C2221 (e.g., scaffolding, doors, windows)

## 2. Top 5 Structural Steel Fabricators — Ranking with Methodology

### 2.1 Companies Office Data Extraction Methodology

#### General Search Process

The NZ Companies Register (companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz) is maintained by the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment [22]. Users can search for companies by name, company number, or New Zealand Business Number (NZBN).

**Step-by-step search methodology:**

1. **Search:** Enter company name, NZBN, or company number into the search bar
2. **Verify entity:** Confirm company status (Registered/Removed), date registered, entity type
3. **Extract identification:** Record NZBN, company number, and trading names
4. **Navigate to "Documents" tab** for the company
5. **Determine filing status:**
   - "Large" companies (total assets > NZ$22M or total revenue > NZ$11M, or having one or more large subsidiaries) must file audited financial statements [23]
   - "Exempt" (small) companies are NOT required to file financial statements — this is the most common status for private structural steel fabricators
6. **If full statements filed, extract:** Revenue/Turnover, Total Assets, Profit/(Loss) Before Tax
7. **If exempt (no financial statements filed):** Use alternative estimation methods

#### Alternative Estimation Methods (for Private Companies)

For private companies that file exempt financial statements and do not disclose revenue:
- **Industry reports:** IBISWorld provides market share data (Culham Engineering identified as largest market share holder in C2221)
- **Third-party data aggregators:** RocketReach, Growjo, ZoomInfo, Prospeo.io, Adapt.io, LeadIQ (vary widely in accuracy)
- **Revenue-per-employee benchmarks:** Applied to known employee counts
- **Self-reported figures:** From company websites, press releases, or government contract awards
- **Reverse engineering from market share:** If total market size ($1.8B) and market share are known, revenue can be estimated

## 3. Tariff Analysis with Corrected Causal Reasoning

### 3.1 New Zealand's Tariff Environment for Steel Products

#### MFN Rates for Structural Steel Products

New Zealand's tariff regime for steel products is characterized by relatively low Most Favoured Nation (MFN) rates:

| HS Code Range | Product Description | Standard MFN Rate | Preferential Rate (FTA) |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| HS 7207 | Semi-finished products of iron or non-alloy steel | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7208 | Flat-rolled products, hot-rolled, width ≥600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7209 | Flat-rolled products, cold-rolled, width ≥600mm | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7210 | Flat-rolled products, coated (tin, zinc, Al-Zn etc.) | **5%** | **0%** |
| HS 7213 | Bars and rods, hot-rolled, in irregular coils | **5%** | **0%** |

[47][48][49]

The simple average MFN applied tariff rate for non-agricultural products is **2.0%** (2024), with **57.1% of tariff lines already duty-free under MFN** [50].

#### Why Tariffs Are NOT a Meaningful Competitive Buffer

New Zealand is party to **14 free trade agreements (FTAs)** worldwide, accounting for over **70% of trade** in 2023 [51]. The key FTA partners relevant to steel trade include:

- **China** (NZ-China FTA, 2008) — All tariffs for Chinese exports to NZ eliminated by 2016. China is NZ's largest steel import source [52].
- **Australia** (CER, 1983) — Zero-tariff trade for decades [51].
- **Korea** (Korea-NZ FTA, 2015) — Tariffs eliminated on ~98% of goods, including steel [53].
- **Japan** (CPTPP) — All industrial goods tariffs eliminated under CPTPP [54].
- **EU** (EU-NZ FTA, effective 1 May 2024) — Removes duties on all EU goods exported to NZ at entry into force [55].
- **UK** (NZ-UK FTA, effective 31 May 2023) — Comprehensive tariff elimination on industrial goods [56].
- **CPTPP** — Covers Canada, Mexico, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, Brunei, Chile, Peru [54].
- **RCEP** — Includes China, Japan, Korea, Australia, ASEAN countries [57].
- **AANZFTA** — ASEAN-Australia-New Zealand Free Trade Area [47].
- **India** — Landmark FTA announced in 2026, eliminates tariffs on 95% of NZ exports [58].

**Practical effect:** Imports of structural steel from virtually all major steel-exporting nations enter New Zealand at **0% tariff**, making the 5% MFN rate applicable only to a small residual set of countries without FTAs. **Tariffs provide no meaningful competitive buffer for New Zealand steel fabricators against imports from any significant source country.**

In 2024, New Zealand imported $360M of Iron & steel (HS Chapter 72), with major sources including South Korea, China, Japan, and Australia [59].

### 3.2 Anti-Dumping Duties

#### Active Anti-Dumping Duties on Steel

**Aluminium–zinc coated steel from the Republic of Korea:** This is the only currently active anti-dumping duty on a steel product in New Zealand. The duty was initially determined by the Minister of Commerce on March 28, 2022, with imposition suspended until December 31, 2022. The suspension lapsed and the duty became payable from **January 1, 2023**. It will remain in force until **March 29, 2027** [60].

**Key insight:** This duty applies to a specific coated steel product used mainly in roofing and cladding, **not** structural steel fabrication products such as beams, columns, trusses, or fabricated structural assemblies. The impact on structural steel fabrication margins is **minimal**.

#### Terminated Anti-Dumping Duties

**Galvanised wire from Malaysia and China:** The anti-dumping duties on galvanised wire were **terminated on October 30, 2025** [61].

**Hollow steel sections from China and Malaysia:** The provisional antidumping duty was terminated on November 21, 2018. Notably, **no antidumping duties were imposed on imports from China** [62].

#### Countervailing Duties

There are currently **no goods subject to countervailing duties** in New Zealand [60].

### 3.3 Corrected Causal Reasoning: Drivers of Competitive Dynamics

**In New Zealand's low/zero-tariff environment (0% for virtually all FTA partners), fabricator margins are primarily driven by:**

1. **Global steel prices** — Global steel prices have experienced significant volatility. Hot-rolled coil (HRC) steel prices as of May 27, 2026 are at $1,166 USD/T, up 39.64% year-over-year [63]. The SteelBenchmarker price for USA HRB reached $1,155 per tonne (May 13, 2026), up substantially from the pandemic-era low [64].

2. **NZD/USD exchange rates** — The NZD has weakened significantly. As of May 27, 2026, the NZD/USD rate is 0.5894, down 1.86% year-over-year [65]. A weaker NZD increases the NZD cost of USD-denominated steel and shipping.

3. **Ocean freight costs** — Freight costs have surged dramatically:
   - **Shanghai Containerized Freight Index (SCFI):** 2,218 points as of May 25, 2026, up **39.85% year-over-year** [66]
   - **Baltic Dry Index (BDI):** 3,085 points as of May 26, 2026, up **138.04% year-over-year** [67]

4. **Supplier pricing dynamics** — Steel & Tube's April 2026 Procurement Update notes that Middle East conflicts have increased energy, freight, and insurance costs, altering supplier terms from CFR to FOB to manage risk. Expected near-term price movements: Carbon Steel 4–9%, Aluminium 8–12%, Stainless 5–6% [68].

**Competitive pressure from imports is a function of global steel price dynamics and freight costs, not local tariff policy.** The combination of soaring shipping costs (SCFI +39.85% YoY, BDI +138.04% YoY) and a weakening NZD (down 1.86% YoY) creates significant cost pressure for NZ import-dependent steel fabricators.

### 3.4 US Tariff Escalation Effects

#### 50% US Steel Tariff (June 2025)

On June 4, 2025 (NZT), US tariffs on steel and aluminium imports doubled to **50%** under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act [69]. BCG estimated the new 50% tariffs add $50 billion in costs [70]. The initial 25% tariffs had already driven up US prices for steel by 77% compared to EU prices between February and May 2025 [70].

**Impact on global steel supply dynamics:** The tariffs "could lead to a redistribution of trade flows to less protected markets" (Fitch Ratings, June 6, 2025) [71]. Steel diverted from the US market flows to Asia-Pacific, affecting NZ steel pricing dynamics by increasing supply in non-US markets, potentially putting downward pressure on import prices.

#### 15% Reciprocal Tariff on NZ (August 2025)

On August 8, 2025, new tariffs were announced on New Zealand exports: 25% for autos and auto parts (NZ$56 million) and 50% for steel and aluminium [72]. The US applied a baseline 10% reciprocal tariff to NZ on April 2, 2025, rising to **15%** on August 7, 2025 [73].

**Cost to NZ:** The 10% baseline tariff cost NZ nearly **$900 million/year**; at 15%, that rises to approximately **$1.35 billion annually** [73].

**Implications for NZ steel fabricators:**
- **Indirect export competitiveness:** NZ fabricators who are subcontractors to projects involving goods exported to the US face a competitive disadvantage due to the combined 50% steel tariff + 15% reciprocal tariff
- **Global supply diversion:** Steel that would have gone to the US is being diverted to other markets including Asia-Pacific, affecting NZ pricing
- **US price support:** Rising US domestic prices (US HRC at $1,166/tonne by May 2026) create a price umbrella that allows global steel prices to find support at higher levels [63][71]

## 5. Strategic Risks and Opportunities

### 5.1 Top 3 Strategic Risks

#### Risk 1: Competition from Imported Fabricated Steel

**Nature of the Risk:** New Zealand structural steel fabricators face sustained competition from cheaper imported fabricated steel, particularly from China. IBISWorld describes the level of competition as **"high and increasing"** [7].

**Evidence:**
- New Zealand imports from China of articles of iron or steel reached **US$367.92 million** in 2025 [129]
- A Beca technical paper on "Imported Structural Steel Issues & Considerations" documents significant challenges: NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing, increasing project costs despite initial cost savings [130]
- The collapse of Challenge Steel (Christchurch, ~2018) illustrates the risks: debts of $10 million; quality assurance costs negated price advantage of Chinese steel [131]

**Corrected causal reasoning:** The competitive pressure from imports is a function of **global steel price dynamics and freight costs, not local tariff policy**. In NZ's zero-tariff environment (0% for all FTA partners), the 5% MFN rate is largely irrelevant. Key drivers:

1. **NZD/USD exchange rate:** NZD at 0.5894 (May 27, 2026), down 1.86% YoY. A weaker NZD increases the cost of USD-denominated imported steel, providing a natural buffer for domestic fabricators [65].

2. **Ocean freight costs:** SCFI at 2,218 points (up 39.85% YoY); BDI at 3,085 points (up 138.04% YoY). Soaring freight costs make imported steel more expensive in NZD terms [66][67].

3. **Global steel prices:** HRC at $1,166 USD/T (up 39.64% YoY). Global steel price increases raise the base cost of imports [63].

**Mitigating factors:**
- NZ's Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC) programme covering 92.5% of output provides quality assurance advantage [13]
- Seismic performance requirements favour local expertise and certified materials
- Quality compliance costs for imported steel (testing, certification) reduce price differential

#### Risk 2: Labour and Skills Shortages

**Nature of the Risk:** New Zealand faces a chronic shortage of skilled welders, fabricators, and engineering professionals, constraining capacity and increasing labour costs.

**Evidence:**
- Engineering New Zealand reports: "New Zealand is experiencing a long-term engineering skills shortage, and this problem is increasing." An estimated **1,500–2,300 additional engineers** are needed annually [132]
- Welding and fabrication roles (Fitter Welder, Welder, Metal Fabricator, Pressure Welder) were added to the **Green List (Tier 1 Straight to Residence pathway)** as of August 2025, reflecting acute shortages [133]
- Despite unemployment rising to 5.2–5.3%, "skilled construction roles remain hard to fill" (RLB Infrastructure Forecast Q3 2025) [134]
- The average business in structural steel fabricating now employs **fewer workers than five years ago**, despite revenue per employee increasing [8]

**Impact on fabricators:**
- Increases project costs and reduces competitiveness against imported steel
- Limits ability to scale production when infrastructure project pipeline accelerates
- Creates dependency on immigration policy and international labour markets

#### Risk 3: Global Trade Policy Uncertainty and Supply Chain Concentration

**Nature of the Risk:** NZ steel fabricators face dual exposure: (1) global trade tensions affecting steel input costs and export competitiveness, and (2) concentration of supply chains through China and ASEAN.

**Evidence:**
- **US tariff escalation (50% on steel, 15% reciprocal on NZ):** Creates global trade flow distortions. Steel diverted from US markets flows to Asia-Pacific, affecting NZ pricing dynamics [69][72][73]
- **Supply chain concentration:** A Treasury-commissioned report notes NZ's "physical remoteness means that New Zealand's supply chains face unique physical connectivity risks, with long and thin servicing from shipping lines and airlines" [135]
- **Steel & Tube FY25 results** illustrate market impact: revenue fell 20% to $385.4M, volumes down 12% YoY, net loss of $24.4M. CEO Mark Malpass described "a very challenging bottom of cycle trading environment" [26]

**Impact on fabricators:**
- Global trade fragmentation creates uncertainty in steel pricing and availability
- Indirect exposure via NZ exporters facing US tariffs reduces overall economic activity
- Supply chain concentration (China accounts for 16% of NZ imports) creates vulnerability to disruptions

### 5.2 Top 3 Strategic Opportunities

#### Opportunity 1: Major Infrastructure Project Pipeline

**Nature of the Opportunity:** New Zealand's National Infrastructure Pipeline represents an unprecedented volume of projects with significant structural steel demand, providing a multi-year demand horizon.

**Evidence:**
- **$275 billion** in projects across nearly 12,000 initiatives (September 2025) [74]
- **$181 billion** with committed or identified funding
- **$61 billion** currently under construction
- Between June and September 2025, **$28 billion** worth of projects progressed to confirmed funding
- **RoNS programme:** 17 prioritised roads, projected cost $40–50 billion [77]

**Steel & Tube CEO Mark Malpass:** "We are starting to see early signs of recovery, and expect activity to continue to improve through FY26 as the benefits of lower interest rates take effect and stimulate confidence, spending and investment, along with Government-backed construction projects." [26]

**Strategic implication:** Local fabricators with capacity, certification, and project-delivery capability are well-positioned to capture substantial work over the next 5–7 years.

#### Opportunity 2: Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication and Seismic Retrofit Demand

**Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication:**
- **National Green Steel Limited** is developing NZ's first fully integrated structural steel manufacturing plant at Hampton Downs using EAF technology on recycled steel. Target: 200,000 tonnes annually [91][92][93]
- The **Sustainable Steel Council NZ (SSC)** has over 80 active members, developing a **Steel RoadMap to Carbon Zero 2050** [136]
- **HERA's $10.3 million MBIE Endeavour-funded Construction 4.0** programme engages 50+ researchers on low-carbon design, AI-based quality monitoring, and circular economy systems [137]

**Seismic Retrofit Demand:**
- Structural steel holds >50% of NZ's multi-level construction market, >80% in Christchurch [11][12][13]
- Wellington has ~600 earthquake-prone buildings requiring strengthening [126]
- HERA's $9.47 million Endeavour-funded research aims to develop systems so buildings suffer **no structural damage under severe earthquakes** [137]

**Strategic implication:** Fabricators that invest in low-carbon production capabilities and SFC certification position themselves for premium pricing and preferred-supplier status on government and institutional projects with sustainability mandates.

#### Opportunity 3: Modular/Prefabricated Construction and Technology Adoption

**Evidence:**
- Global modular construction market: **US$173.5 billion (2025) projected to US$302.0 billion by 2035**, CAGR 5.7%. Steel leads material usage with **47.8% share** [138]
- Structural steel offers **10-15% shorter project timelines** compared to alternative materials [11]
- **HERA's Construction 4.0 programme** ($10.3M) includes pioneering dynamic Life Cycle Assessment, AI-based quality monitoring, and Fab4.0Lab for advanced manufacturing [137]
- **D&H Steel Construction** embraces Industry 4.0 including robotics and data-driven decision-making [44][45]
- **Steel & Tube** reports adoption of 3D modelling and digital innovation investment [26]

**Strategic implication:** Fabricators that invest in automation, BIM integration, and modular fabrication capabilities can capture margin improvements, reduce labour dependency, and differentiate themselves on speed and quality.

## Conclusion and Outlook

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market enters mid-2026 at an inflection point. The industry has weathered a period of declining construction activity (market size down 6% in 2025), compressed margins, and persistent skills shortages. Steel & Tube Holdings, the largest player, reported a net loss of $24.4M in FY2025 with volumes down 12% YoY [26].

However, the structural drivers are strongly positive:

- **$275 billion infrastructure pipeline** provides multi-year demand visibility, with $61 billion already under construction and $20 billion scheduled to start within 12 months [74]
- **Green steel** (National Green Steel Plant at Hampton Downs, 200,000 tonnes capacity) and **seismic retrofit** requirements create premium market segments
- **Technology adoption** (robotics, BIM, modular construction) offers pathways to improved productivity
- **Spare capacity** of 28–41% provides near-term buffer, but the volume of pipeline projects (250,000–500,000 tonnes structural steel over 2026–2031) exceeds current annual production capacity [14]

The principal risks — import competition, labour shortages, and trade policy uncertainty — require active management through investment in technology, quality certification, and workforce development. The key analysis correction in this report is that competitive pressure from imports is a function of **global steel prices, NZD/USD exchange rates, and ocean freight costs, not local tariff policy**, which is effectively zero for all significant trading partners.

Fabricators that successfully navigate these challenges will be well-positioned to benefit from New Zealand's substantial infrastructure investment programme over the next decade.
