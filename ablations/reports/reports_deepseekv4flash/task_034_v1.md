# Vertical Integration Strategies of the Top Three Global Cocoa Processors (2018–2024): A Comprehensive Analysis

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes the vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam (via Olam Food Ingredients/ofi) from 2018 to 2024, focusing on upstream investments in West African cocoa farming cooperatives and sustainability certification programs. The three companies collectively process approximately 60% of the world's cocoa, with Barry Callebaut holding roughly 25% of the global industrial chocolate market, Cargill at approximately 15.3%, and Olam as the third-largest processor.

The analysis reveals a sector in profound transition. All three processors have invested heavily in direct sourcing infrastructure, origin-country processing facilities, and digital traceability systems—driven primarily by the impending EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) and growing demand from premium chocolate manufacturers for verifiably sustainable cocoa. However, significant gaps remain in independent verification of farmer income impacts, and none of the three companies have publicly disclosed specific volumes of non-compliant cocoa excluded from their supply chains.

## 1. Percentage of Direct-Sourced Versus Broker-Purchased Cocoa

### 1.1 Barry Callebaut

Barry Callebaut does **not** formally disclose a standalone percentage for direct-sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa in its annual reports or sustainability reports. The most specific publicly available figure comes from a 2021 IDH-funded case study, which states that Barry Callebaut sources **approximately 40% of its cocoa through a direct supply chain** traceable to the farm level, implying that approximately 60% is sourced through indirect channels (third-party brokers/intermediaries) [1].

The company's reporting focuses on traceability within its direct supply chain rather than the share of direct versus indirect sourcing. In the 2017/18 Forever Chocolate Progress Report, Barry Callebaut noted sourcing 44% of cocoa beans through sustainability programs [2]. By 2022/23, the company reported that mapping covered 547,804 cocoa farm plots, allowing traceability to farm level of 78.9% of its **direct supply** [3]. As of 2025, the company had mapped over 1.5 million cocoa farms [4].

**Key distinction:** The traceability percentages (78.9%, 91%, etc.) refer to the proportion of the *direct supply chain* that is traceable to farm level, NOT the percentage of total cocoa that is direct-sourced. The 40% direct-sourcing figure has not been updated in recent annual reports.

### 1.2 Cargill

Cargill **does not publicly disclose** the specific percentage or volume of cocoa sourced directly from farmer cooperatives versus through third-party brokers/intermediaries in its annual reports or sustainability reports. This is a significant transparency gap.

What is known from available sources:

- Cargill sources cocoa **directly** from six countries (Brazil, Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire, Ecuador, Ghana, and Indonesia) and **indirectly** from 19 other origins [5].
- In a 2012 presentation at the World Cocoa Conference, Cargill stated that **80% of beans were sourced through cooperatives**—but this figure is over a decade old [6].
- Cargill's Promise Cocoa program encompasses 178 farmer organizations and 211,434 certified farmers as of 2024 [7].
- Between January and June 2024, **54% of Cargill's cocoa volumes were sourced sustainably** [8].

Industry-wide data from Trase (2024) provides context: only **48% of Côte d'Ivoire's cocoa export volume can be traced to specific production departments** due to indirect sourcing through intermediaries. Across the entire industry, only **44% of cocoa beans from Côte d'Ivoire can be traced to cooperatives**; indirect sourcing accounts for approximately **24%**, and **32%** come from undisclosed suppliers [9].

### 1.3 Olam (Olam Food Ingredients/ofi)

Olam is the **only one of the three companies to have explicitly disclosed a direct-sourcing percentage**. On September 22, 2020, Olam Cocoa announced that it had achieved 100% traceability for its directly sourced cocoa supply, which constitutes **approximately two-thirds (66.7%)** of its global cocoa purchases across nine countries. The remaining **one-third (approximately 33.3%)** is sourced indirectly through traders and intermediaries [10].

Andrew Brooks, Olam's Chief Sustainability Officer, stated: "Demand for traceable cocoa is certainly becoming a key requirement for most customers, particularly as consumers take more of an interest in where their food comes from." He also noted that the indirect supply chain presents challenges "that require collaboration across industry, NGOs, governments, and regulatory frameworks" [10].

**Important caveat:** This 67/33 split has not been publicly updated since 2020. Olam Group's subsequent annual reports do not contain a line-item disclosure of this ratio.

## 2. Capital Expenditures on Origin-Country Processing Facilities in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana

### 2.1 Barry Callebaut

**Côte d'Ivoire:**

- **SACO Grinding Unit Expansion – Abidjan (2019):** Barry Callebaut invested **CHF 55 million (approximately $55.2 million)** to expand its Société Africaine de Cacao (SACO) plant in Abidjan. The new grinding unit increased cocoa bean processing capacity by over 40%, doubling the Abidjan facility's capacity from 50,000 tonnes to 100,000 tonnes. Combined with San Pedro's expansion to 130,000 tonnes, total capacity in Côte d'Ivoire reached approximately 230,000 tonnes by 2022 [11][12]. The expansion created 45 new direct jobs and 120 indirect jobs [11].

- **San Pedro Capacity Expansion:** A 10,000-tonne expansion brought the San Pedro facility to 130,000 tonnes total capacity (this expansion predates 2018 but provides context for the company's overall Ivorian footprint) [13].

**Ghana:**

- **No major new grinding capacity expansion in Ghana was identified within the 2018–2024 window.** The Tema facility's last major capacity expansion was the second processing line installed in 2006/2007, which brought capacity to 60,000 tonnes annually [14].

- **Solar Power Expansion – Tema (2023–2024):** Barry Callebaut invested in a 640 kWp solar photovoltaic installation at its Tema facility. Phase 1 (0.5 MW solar farm) accounts for 6% of the site's electricity needs; Phase 2 aims to expand solar penetration to 14%. The project is expected to create or secure nearly 100 local jobs [15].

**Strategic context:** The company launched the "BC Next Level" program (CHF 500 million through 2025) for digitalization, operational efficiency, and sustainability, though this is not exclusively West Africa-focused [16].

### 2.2 Cargill

**Côte d'Ivoire:**

- **Yopougon Processing Facility Expansion ($100 million):** Announced December 2019 and completed November 2021, this expansion increased production capacity by **50%** (some sources state "doubled" capacity). A significant portion was dedicated to producing Gerkens® deeply rich brown cocoa powders. The Yopougon plant is the **largest single cocoa-grinding plant in Africa**. The expansion created 85–100 full-time local jobs and hundreds of indirect jobs [17][18].

Harold Poelma, president of Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate, stated: "This investment will serve as a catalyst for the establishment of a broader, local agri-food industry, as we shift a greater share of our global grinding activities to the countries of origin" [17].

**Ghana:**

- **Tema Processing Facility Expansion ($13 million):** Announced December 2019, this expansion increased capacity by **20%** to meet customer demand for high-value cocoa powders [19].

**Sustainability and Traceability Programs:**

- Cargill allocated **$12.3 million over three years** for sustainability and traceability programs: $7.7 million in Côte d'Ivoire and $3.4 million in Ghana. This included new farm registration, GPS mapping, nutritional programs, community healthcare, farm rehabilitation support, and Child Labor Monitoring System expansion [20].

- In November 2021, Cargill committed an additional **$13 million** for expanded programming, including nearly $6 million for farmer coaching and training and $3.5 million to expand traceability efforts [21].

### 2.3 Olam

**Côte d'Ivoire:**

Olam's processing investments in Côte d'Ivoire largely predate the 2018–2024 analysis window:

- **Abidjan Cocoa Processing Plant (2010–2012):** A **$43.5 million** greenfield facility with capacity to process approximately 60,000 metric tonnes of cocoa beans annually [22].
- **San Pedro Olam Cocoa Processing Facility (2014):** A **$75 million** investment with 75,000 metric tonnes per year grinding capacity, among the most advanced cocoa processing plants in Africa at the time. Created employment for 450 local people [23].
- **Additional FMO/Proparco/DEG-financed plant:** A greenfield cocoa processing plant in San Pedro with capacity for 69,000 MT of cocoa beans annually, with a total project cost of **EUR 50 million**. This project is wholly owned by Olam International [24].

By January 2017, Olam Cocoa reported that it had doubled its processing capacity in Côte d'Ivoire to 150,000 MT with utilization improving to over 85% [25].

**Ghana:**

**No cocoa-specific processing investments in Ghana between 2018 and 2024 were clearly itemized in public sources.** Olam Group's 2024 Annual Report notes capacity expansions in pasta and wheat flour production in Ghana, as well as a $40 million pasta production plant in Tema, but these are not cocoa-specific investments [26].

**Global context:** Following the 2016 acquisition of ADM's cocoa business for $1.2 billion, Olam Cocoa became a top-three global cocoa processor with processing capacity of 700,000 metric tonnes across eight factories worldwide [25].

## 3. Documented Farmer Income Changes Under Sustainability Programs

### 3.1 Overview of Evidence Quality

**Critical finding:** No truly independent, peer-reviewed academic study or standalone NGO impact assessment was found that specifically quantifies farmer income changes under any of the three companies' programs using rigorous counterfactual methods (randomized controlled trials, difference-in-difference, or propensity score matching). The available evidence comes from company-commissioned reports, company-partnered research, or industry-wide studies that do not isolate individual company impacts.

### 3.2 Barry Callebaut

**Company-reported data (PwC-audited):**

- **Forever Chocolate / Cocoa Horizons:** Barry Callebaut reports that **557,739 cocoa farmers in its supply chain have risen above the poverty line** in 2024/25, a 30% increase from 428,997 in 2023/24. The company uses the World Bank international extreme poverty line (~$2.15/day) [27].

- **Cocoa Horizons Snapshot 2022/23:** The Foundation supported over 247,000 farmers, with 50% of farmers delivering full data living above the World Bank's poverty line. CHF 50 million was raised, funding farmer premiums (40%), productivity (28%), community (13%), and environmental efforts (11%) [28].

**Multi-stakeholder research (co-authored, not fully independent):**

The **Agri-Logic White Paper (2023)** — co-funded by Barry Callebaut, IDH, and Rainforest Alliance — analyzed 561 farmers across 12 cocoa-producing regions in Côte d'Ivoire. Key findings:

- Average yields reached 546 kg/ha in 2021/22 (a 60% increase attributed mainly to better rainfall)
- **Less than 10% of surveyed farmers** produced yields sufficient for a living income (defined as ~800 kg/ha)
- Approximately two-thirds of farmers lived above the poverty line in 2021/22, compared to 40% in 2020/21
- The prevalence of child work declined from 61% of farms in 2018/19 to 31% in 2021/22
- Precipitation, fertilizer use, cocoa farm size, and yields explained 84% of income variance [29]

**Important methodological note:** Barry Callebaut reports farmers "lifted out of poverty" using the World Bank extreme poverty line (~$2.15/day), while the living income benchmark is a much higher standard (~$6,904/year for a household of seven in Côte d'Ivoire). The company's own collaborative research shows less than 10% of farmers achieve yields sufficient for living income.

### 3.3 Cargill

**Company-partnered data:**

The **CARE-Cargill Partnership "A Decade of Impact" (2008–2020)** reported:

- In Ghana's Rural Education Project: **25% increase in cocoa yields** and a **29% increase in average farmer income**
- In Ghana's PROCOCO program: a **fivefold increase in financial service access** and **tripled farmer incomes**
- Over 236,000 people reached directly [30]

**Cargill's own data (2025 Impact Report):**

- In Brazil: **32% average productivity increase** and **160% income growth** from technical assistance programs
- 94,000+ farmers received one-on-one coaching
- 244,364 farmers enrolled globally in 2021 [31]

**Independent/third-party assessments:**

The **IDH (Sustainable Trade Initiative) SDM Analysis of Cargill in Côte d'Ivoire (2021)** found that despite Cargill's support, **average cocoa farmer incomes remain below living income levels**: approximately **$2,700 average versus $5,676 living income** benchmark. The report also found that **29% of Cargill's farmers have plantations over 25 years old**, necessitating renovation to prevent income declines [32].

The **Impact Institute Living Income Analysis (2021–2022)** — while not Cargill-specific — surveyed 102 cocoa-farming households in San Pedro and found average annual household income of **$3,132 (45% of the living income benchmark of $6,904)**, with **94% of households earning below the living income benchmark** and 33% below the extreme poverty line [33].

### 3.4 Olam

**Company-reported data (Cocoa Compass metrics):**

Olam's Cocoa Compass initiative, launched in 2019, set a target of **150,000 cocoa farmers achieving a living income by 2030** (defined as $7,300 per year). In 2022, ofi reported that **over 30,168 cocoa farmers** in its supply chain were earning a living income [34].

ofi's 2024 Cocoa Compass Impact Report claims to have **reached its 2030 cocoa living income target early**, with **155,000 farmers earning a living income, representing 45% of farmers in its global cocoa sustainability programs** [35].

The Cocoa Compass 2020/21 report also stated that farmers in nine countries increased productivity to **635 kg per hectare (+9% from 2019/20)** and that Olam paid **$136 million in sustainability premiums** to farmers and farming cooperatives [36].

**Independent assessment:**

The **Fair Labor Association (FLA) Assessment (May 2022)** — "Progress in Addressing Child Labor in Olam's Cocoa Supply Chain in Côte d'Ivoire" — interviewed 451 individuals across 10 communities. Key findings:

- **72% of interviewed producers believed child labor is decreasing** in their communities
- However, **44% of producers were still at risk** of using children under 13 in farm work
- Hazardous tasks persist among underage workers
- The report noted persistent structural challenges: fluctuating cocoa prices, small landholdings, labor shortages, and cultural factors sustaining child labor [37]

**Important caveat:** The FLA assessment focused on child labor perceptions rather than income quantification. No independent study isolating Olam-specific farmer income changes using rigorous counterfactual methods was found.

### 3.5 Comparative Summary

|--------|----------------|---------|------|

## 4. EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) Compliance Responses

### 4.1 Regulatory Background

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) — Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 — was adopted to combat the EU's impact on global deforestation. The regulation requires that commodities placed on or exported from the EU market be "deforestation-free" (with a cutoff date of December 30, 2020) and traceable to the specific plot with geolocation data [38].

**Timeline:** Originally effective December 30, 2024, implementation was delayed. The current timeline sets December 30, 2025 for large operators and June 30, 2026 for micro- and small enterprises. A further proposed delay to December 2026 for large companies has been under debate [38].

**Penalties for non-compliance:** Fines up to 4% of annual EU turnover, confiscation of goods and profits, temporary exclusion from public procurement [38].

### 4.2 Barry Callebaut

**Public position:** Barry Callebaut has been a vocal advocate for EUDR implementation without further delay. The company states: "We remain committed to being compliant with the EUDR requirements by the application date (30/12/2025)" and "respectfully opposes the proposal to delay the regulation" [39].

**Traceability systems:**

- **Katchilè System:** A proprietary SAP-based tool launched in 2015, featuring mobile phone-based data collection, data hosting, and report generation [1].
- **Starling Satellite Monitoring:** Partners with Airbus and Earthworm Foundation to use very high-resolution optical imagery (30cm resolution) for continuous forest cover monitoring and deforestation detection [40].
- **Polygon Mapping:** Barry Callebaut mapped over 1.5 million cocoa farms as of 2025, achieving the level of traceability required for EUDR [4].

**Mapping completion rates:**

| Year | Farms Mapped | Traceability Achievement |
|------|-------------|-------------------------|
| 2018/19 | 295,383 farms | Census interviews with 229,142 farmers |
| 2020 | — | 100% traceability in direct supply chain (40% of total sourcing) |
| 2022/23 | 547,804 plots | 78.9% traceability to farm level in direct supply |
| 2024 | 260,511 farms (CFI period) | 91% farmer traceability |
| 2025 | 1.5 million+ farms | "Achieved level of traceability required for EUDR" |

[1][3][4][41]

**Due diligence approach:** Barry Callebaut implements a three-tiered due diligence process covering country, supplier, and farm levels, using seven formalized tools: country and supplier risk assessments, on-farm risk assessments, satellite-based deforestation monitoring, yield control, grievance mechanisms, and stakeholder engagement [42].

**Non-compliant volume exclusions:** **No specific tonnages or percentages of excluded volumes were disclosed.** Barry Callebaut states it can "identify farms located in protected forest areas and exclude cocoa sourced from those regions" but does not disclose the specific volume excluded [1].

**Independent audit:** Barry Callebaut's traceability data is verified by PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) [1]. The company was ranked #1 in Packaged Foods by Sustainalytics for five consecutive years and received an 'A' rating from CDP for forest stewardship [3].

### 4.3 Cargill

**Public position:** Cargill initiated a two-phase project starting in early 2023 to prepare for EUDR compliance. David Maton, EUDR Cocoa and Chocolate Project Lead, stated: "Our supply chains will meet the EUDR requirements when the regulation takes effect" [43]. Cargill has published EUDR Supplier Terms and Conditions and a Supplier EUDR Data Privacy Addendum [44].

**Traceability systems:**

- **CocoaWise™ Platform:** Includes GPS farm polygon mapping and digital barcoding via CocoaWise™ BeanTracker. Farmers benefit from CocoaWise™ ProFarm and ProCoop tools [45].
- **Satelligence Partnership (August 2023):** Cargill partnered with Satelligence to deploy near-real-time deforestation risk monitoring across its global cocoa supply chains, using AI and machine learning to analyze satellite imagery daily [46].
- **Meridia Partnership:** For first-mile data collection and verification [47].

**Mapping completion rates:**

| Year | Achievement |
|------|-------------|
| 2022 | 90% of farmers in Côte d'Ivoire and 69% in Ghana fully GPS polygon-mapped in direct sustainable supply chain |
| 2023/24 | 123,432 farms mapped; deforestation risks assessed over 295,060 hectares |
| 2025 | **100% of farmers** in Promise supply chain in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana polygon-mapped and monitored for deforestation risk |

[47][48][49]

**Deforestation results:** Cargill reports that "gross primary forest loss within mapped supply chains has been held to **less than 0.01% since 2014**" [47].

**Due diligence and TRACES integration:** Cargill explicitly describes submitting Due Diligence Statements (DDS) through the EU's TRACES platform. The company expects to "provide customers with DDS reference and verification numbers confidentially via email in parallel with product delivery" [43].

**Non-compliant volume exclusions:** **No specific tonnages or percentages of excluded cocoa volumes were disclosed.** However, AidEnvironment's Compliance Checker documented **two potential cocoa-related noncompliance instances** under EUDR involving Cargill (one in Côte d'Ivoire and one in Brazil) [50].

**Independent audit:** KPMG provided "limited assurance that the sustainability indicators reported are materially accurate for crop year 2024–2025" for Cargill's Cocoa Impact Report [31].

### 4.4 Olam

**Public position:** Olam was a co-signatory of a November 2025 coalition statement urging EUDR implementation on schedule. Christopher Stewart, Global Head of Sustainability Impact at ofi, stated: "Whilst we are confident our systems are robust within our own supply chains, we are concerned that many smallholders in producing countries will not be ready for the EUDR" [51].

**Traceability systems:**

- **Olam Farmer Information System (OFIS):** Olam's proprietary farm-level data collection system enabling traceability and transparency. By mid-2017, OFIS had mapped over 93,000 farmers covering 244,000 hectares [52].
- **AtSource Platform:** ofi's sustainability management system providing over 100 sustainability metrics, supporting EUDR compliance with detailed due diligence statements formatted for EU TRACES [53].
- **Satelligence Partnership / Forest Loss Risk Index (FLRI):** Developed jointly by Olam Food Ingredients and Satelligence in 2018, FLRI is an operational, automated vegetation change alert system covering **more than 600,000 farms** worldwide using 10m resolution satellite imagery [54].

**Mapping completion rates:**

[10][34][55]

**Living income achievement:** Olam reports that its Cocoa Compass program reached its 2030 target early, with **155,000 farmers earning a living income** (45% of farmers in global cocoa sustainability programs) [35].

**Non-compliant volume exclusions:** **No specific tonnages or percentages of excluded cocoa volumes were disclosed.** A Global Witness November 2025 investigation named ofi (Olam) as one of the traders sourcing cocoa from Liberia, where Liberia's cocoa belt lost over 250,000 hectares of forest between 2021 and 2024. The report notes that supply chains using "mass balance" blending systems mix certified sustainable cocoa with untraceable supply [56].

## 5. Competitive Positioning in Premium Chocolate Manufacturing Contracts

### 5.1 Global Market Structure

The global cocoa processing market is highly concentrated. According to multiple sources, approximately 60% of global cocoa processing is conducted by Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam [57]. Barry Callebaut holds approximately 25% of the global open market for industrial chocolate, the largest volume share among peers, with annual sales of approximately CHF 14.8 billion in fiscal year 2024/25 and over 60 production facilities globally [58].

The industrial chocolate market data shows:
- **Barry Callebaut:** ~21.6–25% market share
- **Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate:** ~15.3% market share
- **Olam (ofi):** Part of the top-three group controlling ~60% combined [59][60]

The EU industrial chocolate market, valued at USD 18.50 billion in 2025, is projected to grow at 4.3% CAGR to reach USD 29.40 billion by 2036. The report notes that "rising cocoa price volatility and strict EU regulations are reshaping procurement strategies toward shorter contracts and diversified sourcing" and that "leading companies focus on vertical integration, sustainable sourcing, traceability, and processing innovation to maintain competitive advantage" [61].

### 5.2 Premium Chocolate Client Relationships: Verified Partnerships

#### Lindt & Sprüngli

Lindt & Sprüngli **does NOT publicly disclose** specific processor-level supplier names (Barry Callebaut, Cargill, Olam) in its public reports. The company operates the "Lindt & Sprüngli Farming Program," its proprietary responsible cocoa sourcing program.

From Lindt & Sprüngli's 2024 Sustainability Report:
- **84.2%** of cocoa products sourced through the Farming Program or other responsible sourcing programs
- **61.3%** of cocoa beans sourced through the Farming Program
- Program active in seven countries with **118,000 farmers**
- Investment of **CHF 33.6 million** into responsible cocoa sourcing in 2024 [62]

Lindt's five principles for responsible cocoa sourcing include: long-term supplier partnerships, deploying the Farming Program, ensuring traceability from farm to factory, continuous program improvement via monitoring and external verification, and engaging in multi-stakeholder initiatives [62].

**No public announcement of a specific partnership, contract win, or exclusive agreement with any of the three processors was found.** Lindt keeps its processor-level relationships confidential.

#### Ferrero

Ferrero publicly disclosed its **tier 1 cocoa suppliers** for September 2022–August 2023 [63]. This disclosure provides the clearest competitive insight:

**Confirmed tier 1 cocoa suppliers for Ferrero:**
- ✅ **Barry Callebaut** — listed as a tier 1 supplier
- ✅ **Cargill** — listed as a tier 1 supplier
- ✅ **Blommer** — also listed
- ✅ **ETG/Beyond Beans** — also listed
- ❌ **Olam** — **NOT present** in Ferrero's tier 1 supplier list

This is a significant competitive finding: **Olam is absent from Ferrero's direct supply chain** for the disclosed period, while both Barry Callebaut and Cargill have confirmed supply relationships.

Ferrero reports that **99% of its cocoa is sourced through independently managed sustainability standards** (Rainforest Alliance, Cocoa Horizons, Fairtrade). The company has mapped more than 90% of its key ingredients to their origins, with traceability for cocoa reaching 97% [64].

#### Mondelēz International

Mondelēz works with **all three processors**, with a notably deep and long-standing partnership with Barry Callebaut.

**Barry Callebaut relationship:** Mondelēz expanded its Cocoa Life sustainability program in 2015 through a partnership with Barry Callebaut and the NGO Solidaridad. Cathy Pieters, director of Mondelēz's Cocoa Life program, stated: "It's really a big step up. We are now working with all our suppliers on the ground" [65].

**Cargill and Olam relationships:** The AidEnvironment Compliance Checker (December 2025) confirms that Mondelēz sources largely through tier-1 traders including **Barry Callebaut, Cargill, ECOM, and Olam** [66]. This confirms Olam does have a supply relationship with Mondelēz (unlike with Ferrero).

Mondelēz reported that by the end of 2024, **approximately 91% of cocoa volume for its chocolate brands** was sourced through Cocoa Life. The program has trained or coached ~178,000 farmers on Good Agricultural Practices and covers ~89% of Cocoa Life communities with Child Labor Monitoring & Remediation Systems [67].

**2025 Olam-Mondelēz partnership:** On April 21, 2021, Mondelēz International and Olam Food Ingredients announced a partnership in Indonesia to create the **world's largest sustainable commercial cocoa farm**, spanning 2,000 hectares on Seram Island in Maluku province. This initiative combines Mondelēz's Cocoa Life program ($400 million investment since 2012) and OFI's Cocoa Compass ambition [68].

### 5.4 Competitive Dynamics and Strategic Differentiation

**Vertical integration as competitive advantage:** According to UNCTAD, the "cocoa–chocolate chain is marked by a high degree of vertical integration and significant concentration at various stages along the supply chain," creating "the potential for the exercise of oligopsonistic or monopsonistic power in cocoa purchasing" [69].

MarketsandMarkets reports that "industry leaders invest heavily in sustainable sourcing, digital traceability, and flavor innovation to meet evolving regulatory and environmental standards" and that the market is "moderately consolidated, with very few global players that carry heavy market shares due to integrated supply chains, robust sourcing networks, and expansive distribution capacity" [70].

**Key competitive factors for premium chocolate contracts:**

1. **Traceability and EUDR compliance:** The ability to provide fully traceable, deforestation-free cocoa is rapidly becoming a prerequisite rather than a differentiator. Ferrero and Mondelēz both require high levels of traceability; Lindt aims for first-mile traceability for 100% of cocoa products by 2025 [62][64][67].

2. **Sustainability certification:** Ferrero sources 99% of cocoa through independently managed sustainability standards; Lindt is transitioning all volumes to Rainforest Alliance Certification; Mondelēz sources 91% through Cocoa Life [62][64][67].

3. **Innovation capabilities:** Barry Callebaut's 26 Chocolate Academy centers, innovations like Ruby chocolate and WholeFruit chocolate, and its "Top Chocolate Confectionery Trends" report position it as a strategic innovation partner [58][60].

4. **Financial stability and scale:** Barry Callebaut's scale (66 factories, 138 countries served) and Cargill's privately held financial strength provide stability for long-term contracts. Olam's recent ofi EBIT growth of 29.1% demonstrates strong operational performance [71].

5. **Price and volatility management:** All three processors face challenges from historic cocoa price volatility (prices rose 218% from October 2022 to March 2025). Barry Callebaut's potential split of its cocoa and chocolate divisions (under consideration as of 2025) could reshape competitive dynamics [72][73].

**Rabobank/Euromonitor/McKinsey reports:** **No publicly available reports from these three organizations specifically analyzing the competitive positioning of these three processors in relation to premium chocolate manufacturers were found.** These reports are typically proprietary, subscriber-only content not indexed in general web search results.

### 5.5 Emerging Competitive Threats and Strategic Shifts

- **Barry Callebaut's potential split:** Reuters reported in 2025 that Barry Callebaut is considering separating its global cocoa business from its chocolate operations, marking "one of the most consequential strategic shifts in Barry Callebaut's modern history." A separation could "help protect the group from commodity price swings" [72][73].

- **Cargill's flexibility strategy:** In early 2026, Cargill emphasized "flexibility" as a competitive advantage, advocating a "portfolio approach" providing tailored solutions to chocolate manufacturers. The company upgraded European production facilities in Deventer, Berlin, and Mouscron to enhance formulation flexibility [74].

- **Olam's integrated platform advantage:** Olam's AtSource platform, providing over 100 sustainability metrics and direct EU TRACES integration, positions the company as a technology-enabled sustainability partner. The Cocoa Compass program's early achievement of its living income target (155,000 farmers) provides a strong narrative for sustainability-focused clients [35][53].

---

## 6. Key Data Gaps and Transparency Limitations

The following data points are **not publicly disclosed** by any of the three companies:

1. **Direct vs. broker sourcing percentages:** Only Olam has explicitly disclosed this (67/33 split, in 2020). Barry Callebaut's 40% direct figure comes from a third-party case study. Cargill has no public disclosure.

2. **Farmer income changes:** No company has an independent, peer-reviewed evaluation of its programs using rigorous counterfactual methods.

3. **Non-compliant volume exclusions:** None of the three companies has disclosed specific tonnages or percentages of cocoa excluded from supply chains due to deforestation or traceability concerns.

4. **Rabobank/Euromonitor/McKinsey reports:** These proprietary reports are not publicly accessible.

5. **Lindt's processor partners:** Lindt does not publicly disclose its tier 1 processor-level suppliers.

6. **Cargill's total cocoa volume from West Africa:** Not publicly disclosed.

---

## 7. Conclusions

The vertical integration strategies of Barry Callebaut, Cargill, and Olam have converged significantly over the 2018–2024 period, driven by three primary forces: the EUDR's traceability requirements, premium chocolate manufacturers' demands for verifiably sustainable cocoa, and the need to manage unprecedented price volatility.

**On direct sourcing:** Olam leads with the only explicit disclosure of a 67% direct sourcing ratio. Barry Callebaut's ~40% direct sourcing figure (from 2020) suggests significant room for growth in direct supply chain control. Cargill's lack of disclosure on this metric is a notable transparency gap.

**On capital investments:** Cargill has invested the most in West African processing capacity during the 2018–2024 period ($113 million plus $12.3 million in sustainability programs), followed by Barry Callebaut (CHF 55 million in Abidjan plus solar in Tema). Olam's major investments predate the analysis window.

**On farmer income:** All three companies report positive income impacts, but the evidence base relies entirely on company-commissioned or co-authored research. The gap between "poverty line" metrics (~$2.15/day) and "living income" benchmarks (~$6,904/year) remains vast, with collaborative research suggesting fewer than 10% of farmers achieve living income levels.

**On EUDR compliance:** All three companies have invested heavily in traceability systems, satellite monitoring, and polygon mapping. Barry Callebaut and Cargill appear most advanced in farm mapping (100% of direct supply chains), while Olam benefits from its earlier 2020 traceability milestone. None have disclosed specific non-compliant volume exclusions, which remains a critical transparency gap.

**On competitive positioning:** Ferrero's public supplier list confirms Barry Callebaut and Cargill as tier 1 suppliers while Olam is absent, representing a significant competitive blow to Olam. Mondelēz works with all three processors but has a notably deep partnership with Barry Callebaut through Cocoa Life. Lindt's confidentiality on processor relationships prevents direct comparison.

The strategic differentiation among these three processors is narrowing as sustainability and traceability become baseline requirements rather than competitive advantages. The next phase of competition will likely center on innovation capabilities (Barry Callebaut's Chocolate Academies and proprietary products), platform-based services (Olam's AtSource), and flexibility in formulation and supply chain management (Cargill's portfolio approach).
