# Comparative Analysis of Carbon Pricing Mechanisms: California Cap-and-Invest, EU ETS, and China National Carbon Market (2020–2024)

## Executive Summary

This revised report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of three major carbon pricing systems—California's Cap-and-Invest Program (formerly Cap-and-Trade), the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), and China's national carbon market—across seven critical dimensions from 2020 to 2024. Each system operates within distinct political and economic contexts, with unique design features that shape their effectiveness. The analysis systematically addresses quantitative precision and completeness, source rigor, the distinction between implemented versus announced measures, and the specifics of COVID-19 economic disruption responses.

The EU ETS represents the most mature system with the highest carbon prices (annual average of €84/tCO2e in 2023), driven by structural reforms including the Market Stability Reserve (MSR). California's program generates substantial cumulative revenue ($34.5 billion) directed toward climate investments and community benefits. China's newer national market, launched in July 2021, has achieved remarkably high compliance rates (99.98% for the third compliance cycle) and is rapidly expanding sectoral coverage to become the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage.

**Market Stability Reserve (MSR) Mechanism:**

The MSR is the primary price stability tool in the EU ETS, operating through **volume-based adjustments** rather than direct price intervention. It operates automatically based on the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation (TNAC) [16][17].

**MSR Thresholds and Intake Rates (operating during 2020–2024):**

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| TNAC > 1,096 million | MSR withholds **24%** of TNAC |
| TNAC between 833 and 1,096 million | Variable percentage (difference between TNAC and 833 million) |
| TNAC between 400 and 833 million | No action taken |
| TNAC < 400 million | MSR releases **100 million allowances** into market |

**MSR Operation by Year:**

| Year | TNAC (million allowances) | Intake/Release | Action Taken |
|------|--------------------------|----------------|--------------|
| 2020 | ~1,580 | ~379 million placed in MSR (24% of 1,580) | Continued surplus reduction [18] |
| 2021 | ~1,547 | ~371 million placed in MSR | Surplus declining [19] |
| 2022 | 1,134.8 | 272.4 million placed in MSR [16] | |
| 2023 | 1,111.7 | 266.8 million placed in MSR [16] | **2.5 billion allowances invalidated** on Jan 1, 2023 |
| 2024 | ~1,140 | 381 million allowances invalidated on Jan 1, 2024 [16] | Invalidation threshold fixed at 400 million |

**Invalidation Mechanism (from 2023 onward):**

Starting in 2023, allowances held in the MSR above a defined threshold are permanently removed and invalidated [16]:
- **January 1, 2023:** 2.5 billion allowances invalidation (threshold = 2022 auction volume)
- **January 1, 2024:** 381 million allowances invalidation (threshold fixed at 400 million from 2024)
- **January 1, 2025:** 271 million allowances invalidation

The invalidation threshold was set at 400 million allowances from 2024 onward under the Fit for 55 revision [17].

**MSR Intake Rate Change (Implemented vs. Adopted for Future):**

| Period | Intake Rate | Legal Basis |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| 2019–2023 | 24% (doubled from 12%) | Directive 2018/410 |
| 2024–2030 | 24% (extended) | Decision (EU) 2023/852 (Fit for 55) |
| 2031 onward | 12% (planned reversion) | Decision (EU) 2023/852 |

**Key Point:** The 24% intake rate and minimum 200 million annual placement were *extended* to 2030 under the 2023 revision (adopted April 19, 2023) and were in force for 2024. The 12% reversion from 2031 is a *planned future* measure [17].

**Backloading History (pre-2020, impact felt during 2020–2024):**

The MSR was preceded by a "backloading" measure (2014–2016) that delayed the auctioning of 900 million allowances [20]. These allowances were eventually transferred to the MSR and contributed to the 2.5 billion allowances invalidated on January 1, 2023, demonstrating that pre-2020 structural decisions continued to affect the 2020–2024 period.

- **Opening price (July 16, 2021):** 48 yuan/tCO2

- **Lowest price (October 27, 2021):** 38.50 yuan/tCO2

**2022 (Inter-Cycle Period):**

Between the first and second compliance cycles, trading volumes declined substantially as there was no immediate compliance deadline pressure. Allowance prices remained relatively stable in the 40–60 yuan/tCO2 range.

**2023 (Second Compliance Cycle):**

Prices steadily increased throughout 2023 as the second compliance cycle approached its deadline. By the end of 2023, CEAs reached approximately 79 yuan/tCO2 ($11 USD/tonne), a 65% increase from the opening price on the first trading day [21][22].

**2024 (Third Compliance Cycle):**

- **April 24, 2024:** Closing price exceeded 100 yuan/tCO2 for the first time
- **Average price for 2024:** ~98 yuan/tCO2 ($13.37 USD/tonne) [22]
- **All-time high (November 13, 2024):** 106.02 yuan/tCO2 (daily high price) [24]
- **All-time high opening (November 14, 2024):** 105.35 yuan/tCO2
- **Year-end closing price (2024):** 97.49 yuan/tCO2 ($14 USD/tonne) [22]
- **2024 transaction volume:** 189 million tonnes
- **2024 transaction value:** 18.11 billion yuan ($2.49 billion USD) [22]
- **Cumulative transaction volume (2021–2024):** ~630 million tonnes
- **Cumulative transaction value (2021–2024):** 43.03 billion yuan ($5.93 billion USD)

**Price Formation Structure:**

China's ETS uses an **intensity-based cap** rather than an absolute emissions cap. Allowances are allocated based on output-based benchmarks tied to production levels [21][25]:

- **Two-step allocation:** Pre-allocation of 70% of previous year's verified emissions, followed by ex-post adjustment based on verified current-year output
- **Trading methods:** Listed trading (±10% price limit), block trading (±30% limit, minimum 100,000 tonnes), and one-way bidding
- **Participants:** Only key emitting entities can trade — no institutional investors, financial institutions, or individual investors are currently permitted in the national ETS [21]
- **No derivatives:** Only spot trading is allowed

**Regional Pilot Market Prices (for context):**

China's seven regional pilot markets (Beijing, Chongqing, Guangdong, Hubei, Shanghai, Tianjin, Shenzhen) demonstrated significant price variation, generally trading at lower levels than the national market [27].

**Achieved Reductions vs. Baseline Projections:**

- Statewide emissions dropped below the 2020 target of 431 MMTCO2e in 2014 (to 428.3 MMTCO2e) and have remained below it since [28]

**Cumulative Reductions:** California's overall GHG emissions have declined by approximately 14% since program inception (2013–2023), with the most significant reductions occurring in the electricity sector [7][28].

**Important Clarification:** China's National ETS uses an **intensity-based cap**, not an absolute emissions cap. Total allowances are calculated bottom-up based on actual production output multiplied by sectoral intensity benchmarks. This means total emissions can rise with higher production even as emissions intensity declines [21][25].

**Achieved Emissions Reductions (Intensity-Based):**

| Metric | Value | Time Period | Source |
|--------|-------|-------------|--------|
| Carbon intensity reduction in thermal power sector | 2.38% | 2018 → 2023 | [22][39] |
| Overall electricity generation emissions intensity reduction | 8.78% | 2018 → 2023 | [39] |
| Carbon intensity reduction in fossil-fuel power generation | 10.8% | 2018 → 2024 | [22] |
| Academic estimate: total carbon reduction due to ETS | Up to 18.2% | 2013–2020 | [40] |
| Power sector emissions reduction (modeled) | 13% | 2013–2020 | [40] |

**Methodology for Baseline:** The baseline for intensity reduction is relative to 2018 levels in the MEE Progress Reports. Unlike absolute cap systems, China's system measures progress through emissions intensity (CO2 per unit of output) rather than absolute emissions reductions [22][39].

**Important Caveat — Total Emissions Continuing to Rise:**

China's total CO2 emissions continue to increase. The ETS's intensity-based design means that while emissions per unit of output decline, total emissions can increase with economic growth and industrial output expansion. China's emissions are projected to increase by 1.9% annually through 2025 [41]. The system has been characterized by "generous allowances, lack of an absolute emissions cap, allowance surpluses, and limited trading liquidity" [21][42].

**Future Trajectory — Shift from Intensity to Absolute Target:**

In November 2025, China submitted its 2035 NDC, committing to reduce economy-wide GHG emissions by 7–10% from peak levels by 2035 — the first time China has committed to reducing its absolute GHG emissions [43]. This marks a significant shift from intensity-based targets toward an absolute reduction commitment. The "dual control of carbon" system (replacing "dual control of energy" in 2024) also signals a policy evolution toward total carbon emission control [22].

### 3.3 China National Carbon Market

**Critical Clarification — No Auction Revenues During 2020–2024:**

Allowances in the China National Carbon Market are **100% freely allocated** using an output-based approach during Phase 1 (2021–2025) [21][25]. The government has generated **no direct revenue from allowance sales**. Primary revenue comes from transaction fees from trading, which are minimal in scale.

**Planned Auctioning (Announced, Not Implemented):**

The Interim Regulations for the Management of Carbon Emissions Trading (State Council Decree No. 775), effective May 1, 2024, state that "auctioning is to be introduced and gradually expanded" as a method of allowance allocation [59][60].

| Source | Recommendation/Timeline |
|--------|------------------------|
| IEA | Phased introduction starting with 5–10% in power sector, increasing to ~25% by 2035 [61] |
| IEA Estimate | Auctioning could generate ~USD 39 billion annually by 2035 |
| IMF Estimate | Partial auctioning could generate ~0.2% of GDP (~$40 billion) by 2030 [62] |
| August 2025 Government Opinion | Outlines roadmap for implementing allowance auctions [63] |

**National Carbon Emission Trading Fund (Planned, Not Implemented):**

The regulations reference a "National Carbon Emission Trading Fund" that would manage revenues from the scheme, but no specific timeline for establishment has been given.

**Penalty Revenues (Negligible During 2020–2024):**

Under the pre-May 2024 penalty regime, fines were capped at RMB 30,000 (~$4,600) for non-compliance, generating negligible revenue. The first major penalty under the new regime (RMB 424 million, ~$59 million) was imposed in March 2026, which is outside the review period [64].

**Regional Pilot Revenues (Historical Context):**

Regional pilots have experimented with partial auctioning (e.g., Guangdong's 2016 auction at $1.48/ton), but the national system has not yet followed suit. Pilots generated cumulative trading value of CNY 7.68 billion by 2019 [27].

## 5. COVID-19 Economic Disruption Management

### 5.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

**Determination: Rules-Based, Not Discretionary**

CARB did **not** deploy any emergency discretionary interventions in response to COVID-19. The program's existing rules-based mechanisms operated automatically [7][47].

**Auction Performance During COVID-19:**

| Auction | Date | Settlement Price | % of Allowances Sold | Notes |
|---------|------|-----------------|---------------------|-------|
| Joint #22 | Feb 2020 | $16.68 | Near 100% | Pre-pandemic baseline |
| Joint #23 | **May 2020** | **$16.68** | **~35%** | **Collapse** — only $25 million raised [4][47] |
| Joint #24 | Aug 2020 | $16.68 | Below 100% | Continued low demand |
| Joint #25 | Nov 2020 | $16.68 | Below 100% | All 2020 auctions at floor price |

**Mechanism: Self-Ratcheting (Unsold Allowance Rules)**

The program's self-ratcheting mechanism functioned as an automatic stabilizer [70]:

- When auctions are undersubscribed, unsold allowances are removed from current circulation
- If California state-owned allowances remain unsold for more than 24 months, they are transferred into the Allowance Price Containment Reserve
- **Section 95911(f)(3)(D):** The maximum number of unsold allowances that can be returned to a future auction is **25%** of the California allowances offered at that auction
- If previously unsold state-owned allowances were re-designated but still remain unsold, they cannot be re-designated until two consecutive auctions settle above the auction reserve price

**Analysis of COVID Impact vs. Structural Oversupply:**

According to a contemporaneous analysis by Danny Cullenward (CalEPA Independent Emissions Market Advisory Committee member) [47]:
"A recent study estimated that even if the U.S. maintains stay-at-home orders through July as well as gathering restrictions through December, emissions would fall by only 11.5%. Normalized to California's cap-and-trade emissions, that reduction is equal to about 37 million allowances. For comparison, the market surplus was already 227 million at the end of 2018 – the most recent year for which data are available – and that number is likely approaching 300 million now. There's no serious argument that the pandemic is responsible for the auction shortfall. Faulty market design is the real culprit."

**No Suspension of Auctions:**

The program continued quarterly auctions throughout the pandemic period. All four 2020 auctions were held on their regular schedules [2].

**Deferred 2020 Emissions:**

Pursuant to section 95856(d) of the Cap-and-Trade Regulation, any deferred 2020 covered emissions (an existing mechanism for the final year of a compliance period) were included with the full CP4 compliance obligation due November 1, 2024. This was an **existing regulation provision**, not a COVID-specific intervention [65].

**Post-Pandemic Recovery:**

- Prices began rising from February 2023 due to post-pandemic industrial recovery and expectations of stricter future regulations [5]
- Emissions across tracked sectors dropped 8% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels [28]
- 2023–24 fiscal year saw peak auction revenues of $8.1 billion [49]

### 5.2 European Union Emissions Trading System

**Determination: Rules-Based, Not Discretionary**

The EU ETS did **not** deploy any specific discretionary interventions in response to COVID-19. The system relied entirely on its pre-existing rules-based Market Stability Reserve mechanism [17][71].

**MSR Automatic Stabilizer Operation During COVID:**

| Year | TNAC (million) | MSR Intake (million) | Impact |
|------|---------------|---------------------|--------|
| 2020 | ~1,580 | ~379 (24% of TNAC) | Automatically absorbed surplus as demand fell |
| 2021 | ~1,547 | ~371 (24% of TNAC) | Continued surplus absorption |

**Effects of MSR Operation:**

- The MSR cut auction volumes by approximately **30–40% in 2019 and 2020** [17]
- Based on the surplus and revised legislation for Phase 4, the number of allowances auctioned was reduced by around **375 million in 2020** [16]
- Auction volumes in 2021 were reduced under the same legal basis

**No Emergency Price Floor or Market Intervention:**

The system maintained its design of no price floor and no price ceiling. The volume-based MSR was the only mechanism operating. No emergency policy measures beyond the MSR were identified [16][71].

**Market Response to COVID:**

| Phase | Period | Price Movement | Driver |
|-------|--------|---------------|--------|
| Initial shock | March–April 2020 | Prices fell to ~€15 | Demand collapse from economic shutdown |
| Recovery | May–Dec 2020 | Prices recovered to ~€30 | MSR absorption, reduced supply |
| Post-COVID surge | 2021–2022 | Prices rose to €50–100 | MSR tightening, energy crisis, green recovery |

**Academic Analysis:**

A study examining COVID-19's impact on the EU ETS found [71]:
- EUA prices showed an inverted U-shaped relationship with new confirmed COVID-19 cases and deaths
- Government response policies (stringency index) were more effective than fiscal policies in mitigating negative impact in the short term
- The €750 billion EU Recovery Plan, emphasizing green transition, supported longer-term market recovery

**Post-COVID/Energy Crisis Period (2022–2023):**

The Russian war against Ukraine starting February 2022 caused significant energy market disruptions, with gas market disruptions leading to decreased activity in gas-dependent industries [32]. The MSR continued to operate automatically throughout this period. The REPowerEU plan (May 2022) was a discretionary policy response to the energy crisis, involving the auction of 93,280,000 allowances in 2026 via transfer from the Innovation Fund [37].

## 6. Interaction with Complementary Policies

### 6.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

California's climate policy architecture positions the Cap-and-Invest program as a "backstop mechanism" alongside sector-specific regulations. Approximately **75% of emission reductions** are expected from complementary policies, while the cap-and-trade scheme acts as a guarantee to meet overall emissions caps [7][73].

**Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) and SB 100:**

SB 100 (2018) establishes California's target of 100% renewable and zero-carbon electricity by December 31, 2045 [74]:

| Target Year | RPS Requirement |
|-------------|----------------|
| 2024 | 44% |
| 2027 | 52% |
| 2030 | 60% |
| 2045 | 100% |

**Interaction with Cap-and-Invest:** The RPS reduces allowance demand from the electricity sector, which can depress allowance prices but does not reduce total emissions because the cap is fixed. This creates a "waterbed effect" within California — renewable deployment reduces emissions in the covered sector, freeing up allowances for use elsewhere or banking.

**Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS):**

The Cap-and-Invest program and LCFS both cover fuel suppliers but create different incentives [75][76]:
- Cap-and-Invest covers only direct combustion emissions
- LCFS focuses on lifecycle carbon intensity reduction
- Some LCFS fuel substitution costs reach up to **$945 per MTCO2e** compared to the highest allowance price of $102 per MTCO2e (ARB 2010 analysis)
- **Schatzki & Stavins (2014) finding:** Overlapping policies targeting the same capped sources generally do not yield additional environmental benefits and may undermine economic efficiency [75]
- **More recent analysis:** LCFS and cap-and-trade programs together reduce demand for carbon allowances and provide complementary incentives [76]

**2022 Scoping Plan Integration (Adopted December 15, 2022):**

The plan emphasizes aggressive transition across all sectors [29]:
- 94% reduction in liquid petroleum use by 2045
- 86% drop in total fossil fuel demand by 2045 relative to 2022
- SB 1020 mandating 90% renewable and zero-carbon electricity by 2035

**Policy Coordination Challenge:**

ARB estimated that complementary policies would achieve **~80%** of the emissions reductions needed for the 2020 target, with cap-and-trade responsible for **~22.5%** (18 MtCO2e) [7]. The LAO found that the cap-and-trade program "is currently not stringent enough to close the emissions gap by 2030" due to an oversupply of allowances and the ability to bank unused allowances [30].

### 6.2 European Union Emissions Trading System

**Renewable Energy Directive (RED III):**

The revised Renewable Energy Directive targets a **42.5%** share of renewables in the EU's overall energy consumption by 2030, with ambition to reach 45% [37]:
- Extends transport emission intensity reduction targets to aviation
- Mandates fuel distributors to reduce greenhouse gas intensity by 14.5% by 2030
- Increased deployment of renewables drove the **24% decline** in electricity production emissions in 2023

**Energy Efficiency Directive (EED):**

The REPowerEU plan includes updates to the EED to support energy efficiency improvements, targeting an **11.7%** energy efficiency improvement [37].

**Effort Sharing Regulation (ESR):**

Covers non-ETS sectors (transport, buildings, agriculture, waste). Member States have national emissions targets under the ESR. Complementary national emissions targets should be extended beyond 2030 [37].

**Coal Phase-Out Dates (as of 2024):**

| Country | Coal Phase-Out Target | Status |
|---------|----------------------|--------|
| Germany | **2030** (accelerated from 2038) | Accelerated timeline announced |
| RWE (German utility) | **2030** | Brought forward from 2038 |
| EU overall | Various dates | 14 Member States have committed to coal phase-out by 2030 or earlier |

**Germany's Cancellation of EU ETS Allowances:**

Germany cancelled over 500,000 emission allowances from the EU ETS (514,000 tonnes CO2) to secure the climate benefits of its coal phase-out, countering the "waterbed effect" [79].

**REPowerEU Plan (Launched May 2022):**

Strategic response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine [37]:
- Aims to phase out Russian fossil fuel imports and accelerate the clean energy transition
- EU has mobilized close to **€300 billion** to fund the plan
- REPowerEU auction volumes include **93,280,000 allowances** in 2026

**EU 2040 Climate Target (Amended March 2026):**

Legally binding target of reducing net greenhouse gas emissions by **90% by 2040** relative to 1990 levels [37]:
- Includes a domestic reduction of 85%
- Allows up to 5% international carbon credits
- Council also agreed to a 2035 emissions reduction target between 66.25% and 72.5%
- Estimated capital investment of ~€700 billion annually from 2031–2040 (~3% of EU GDP)

**ETS2 (Buildings and Road Transport):**

A new emissions trading system for buildings, road transport, and additional sectors was created under Fit for 55. Key features [37][80]:
- **Start date:** 2027 (or delayed to 2028 if energy prices are deemed exceptionally high)
- **Cap:** Declining from 2027
- **LRF:** 5.10% initially, 5.38% from 2028
- **Status at end of 2024:** Adopted but **not yet operational** — will begin in 2027

### 6.3 China National Carbon Market

**"Dual Carbon" Goals (Announced September 2020):**

President Xi Jinping announced China's "dual carbon" targets at the UN General Assembly [43]:
- **Carbon peak** before 2030
- **Carbon neutrality** before 2060

**14th Five-Year Plan (2021–2025) — Binding Targets:**

| Target | Value | Type |
|--------|-------|------|
| Carbon intensity reduction | **18%** by 2025 (vs. 2020) | Binding |
| Energy intensity reduction | **13.5%** by 2025 (vs. 2020) | Binding |
| Non-fossil energy in energy mix | **20%** | Non-binding |

**"1+N" Climate Policy Framework:**

Coordinates decarbonization efforts across sectors with guiding opinions and detailed action plans, managed by the Leading Small Group and aligned with the National Development and Reform Commission [43].

**Coal Phase-Down Commitment:**

China continues significant coal plant development despite climate goals. Coal consumption reached a new high in 2020. Key policy positions [43]:
- Xi Jinping advocates a "build before breaking" approach — expanding low-carbon energy sources before reducing fossil fuel use
- China has committed to **phasing down coal consumption** during the 15th Five-Year Plan period (2026–2030)
- This is a "phase-down" (not phase-out) — a critical distinction from the EU and California

**Shift from "Dual Control of Energy" to "Dual Control of Carbon" (2024):**

In 2024, China's "dual control of carbon" system replaced "dual control of energy," shifting focus from energy consumption targets to carbon intensity and total emissions [22]. This policy evolution aligns with the ETS's role and signals a transition toward absolute emission controls.

**Power Market Reforms:**

China's power sector is heavily regulated with government-controlled dispatch and fixed tariffs, limiting the carbon price's impact on dispatch decisions and electricity prices [42]:
- Recent reforms aim to expand spot trading markets and incorporate carbon costs in mid- and long-term contracts
- Goal of establishing a unified national power market by **2030**
- Inter-ministerial coordination among MEE, NDRC, NEA, and MIIT is vital to align power sector reform and ETS development

**CCER Voluntary Carbon Market (Relaunched January 22, 2024):**

The Chinese Certified Emission Reduction (CCER) program was suspended in March 2017 and officially relaunched on January 22, 2024 [21][22]:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| New account registrations (2024) | Over 4,500 |
| Offset limit under ETS rules | Up to **5%** of annual emissions |
| Methodologies covered | Forestry carbon sequestration, renewable energy, methane abatement, industrial energy efficiency |
| First-cycle CCER use | >32 million tonnes, valued at >900 million yuan |

## 7. Leakage Concerns and Border Adjustments

### 7.1 California Cap-and-Invest Program

**Emissions Leakage Evidence:**

Ex-ante modeling studies report carbon leakage rates ranging from **-8.25% to 90%**, with an average of **27.27%** [81]. The rate tends to be lower when emissions coverage is broader and anti-leakage measures are included.

**Academic Findings (Fowlie, Reguant & Ryan, 2016) [82]:**

For the median industry, a 10% increase in domestic energy prices is associated with:
- **4–10%** reduction in domestic production
- **3–9%** decrease in exports
- **2–4%** increase in imports
- For cement, lime, industrial gas, wet corn milling, nitrogen fertilizer, iron and steel: estimated negative impacts on export volumes of **20% or greater**

**Free Allowance Allocation Methodology:**

Industrial sectors are divided into three leakage classifications with corresponding free allowance allocations [6][81]:

| Leakage Risk | Allocation Level | Duration |
|-------------|-----------------|----------|
| **High** (e.g., oil and gas) | **100%** free allocation | Throughout compliance periods |
| **Medium** | 75% (2nd CP) → 50% (3rd CP) | Declining |
| **Low** | 50% (2nd CP) → 30% (3rd CP) | Declining |

**Two Allocation Methodologies:**
- **Product-based:** Applicable to sectors like oil and gas extraction and refining
- **Energy-based:** For sectors without product benchmarks (e.g., food processing)

**Cap Adjustment Factor:**

The program employs emission efficiency benchmarks and a cap adjustment factor, which declines from **0.981 in 2013 to 0.511 in 2030**, meaning industrial facilities receive allocations that decline annually in proportion to the overall cap [7][81].

**Vintage 2026 Allocation Breakdown:**

Total of 133,823,322 allowances allocated [6]:
- Electrical Distribution Utilities (EDUs): **54.4%**
- Natural Gas Suppliers: **23.2%**
- Industrial sectors: **~22%**

**Linkage with Québec (Implemented and Operating Throughout 2020–2024):**

California and Québec have operated a joint carbon market since January 2014 [6][7]:
- Joint quarterly auctions using dual-currency system
- Mutual acceptance of compliance instruments
- **November 2024 compliance event:** Québec entities surrendered 13.3 million California-issued offset credits
- **Linkage with Ontario** (2018) terminated after Ontario's cap-and-trade system was cancelled

**Linkage with Washington (Under Discussion — NOT Implemented as of End 2024):**

In March and September 2024, joint statements from Québec, California, and Washington affirmed their commitment to explore potential linkage of Washington's carbon market to the California-Québec market [6]. As of end 2024, this was **under discussion but not implemented**.

**Border Adjustment Proposals (Under Consideration — NOT Implemented):**

Economists have shown that full auctioning of emissions allowances, together with a combination of border carbon price adjustment and export rebates, can be effective in mitigating emissions leakage [81]. The Climate Center's 2023 policy brief recommends gradually eliminating free allowances to better incentivize decarbonization, noting that "free allowances subsidize polluting firms, diluting incentives for carbon reductions" [83]. As of end 2024, no border carbon adjustment mechanism had been adopted in California.

### 7.2 European Union Emissions Trading System

**Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM):**

**Legislative Timeline:**

| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| **July 2021** | CBAM proposal adopted by European Commission |
| **December 2022** | Political agreement reached between EU institutions |
| **May 2023** | CBAM published in Official Journal (Regulation 2023/956) |
| **October 1, 2023** | **Transitional phase begins** — reporting only, no financial obligations |
| **January 1, 2026** | **Definitive/compliance phase begins** |
| **2026–2034** | CBAM charge phases in (2.5% to 100% of embedded emissions) |
| **2034** | Full coverage; free allowances fully phased out |

**Source:** [37][84]

**Sectors Covered (Initial Scope):**

| Sector | Status in 2020–2024 |
|--------|-------------------|
| Cement | Transitional phase (Oct 2023–Dec 2025): reporting only |
| Iron and Steel | Transitional phase: reporting only |
| Aluminium | Transitional phase: reporting only |
| Fertilisers | Transitional phase: reporting only |
| Electricity | Transitional phase: reporting only |
| Hydrogen | Transitional phase: reporting only |

**Important Distinction — Transitional vs. Definitive Phase:**

| Aspect | Transitional Phase (Oct 2023 – Dec 2025) | Definitive Phase (From Jan 2026) |
|--------|------------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| **Obligation** | Quarterly reporting of embedded emissions only | Purchase and surrender of CBAM certificates |
| **Financial Adjustment** | **None** — no payment required | Full financial obligation based on EUA auction prices |
| **Penalties** | Up to €50/tonne for non-reporting | €100/tonne for non-surrender (same as EU ETS) |
| **Default Values** | Allowed (until June 2024) | Actual values required from 2026 |
| **Declarant Status** | Not required | Importers must apply for authorized declarant status |

**CBAM Pricing Mechanism (From 2026):**

| Period | Certificate Price Calculation |
|--------|------------------------------|
| 2026 | Based on **quarterly average** of EUA auction prices |
| From 2027 | Based on **weekly average** of EUA auction prices |

**First Official CBAM Price:** €75.36 per tonne of CO2 (published April 7, 2026) [85].

**Free Allowance Phase-Out for CBAM Sectors:**

| Year | Free Allocation Reduction | CBAM Coverage |
|------|-------------------------|---------------|
| 2026 | -2.5% | 2.5% |
| 2027 | -2.5% | 5% |
| 2028–2033 | Gradually phased out | Gradual increase |
| 2034 | **0% free allocation** | **100% embedded emissions** |

**Temporary Decarbonization Fund:**
Funded by 25% of CBAM revenues for 2028–2029, supporting decarbonization investments in energy-intensive industries [84].

**Leakage Evidence:**

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| "No factual evidence for carbon leakage has been found for the past two ETS periods (2005–2007 and 2008–2012)" | European Commission [37] |
| Current compensation rules result in "substantial overcompensation and poorly target firms with the highest marginal relocation risk" | NBER firm-level analysis [86] |
| Interviews with 761 manufacturing firms across six European countries "reveal low average downsizing risk but substantial variation across sectors and firms" | Academic study |

**Free Allocation in Practice (2023):**

- **528 million allowances** allocated for free to industry across the EU
- Covering **84%** of total industry emissions
- In Germany, industrial free allocation coverage estimated at **96%** after adjusting for waste gas and heat imports [32]

**China's Response to CBAM (Dual Approach):**

2. **Domestic policy strengthening:** Accelerating ETS development to prepare enterprises for global carbon pricing

## Conclusion

The three carbon pricing systems analyzed represent different stages of maturity and operate within distinct economic and political contexts, yet all demonstrate that carbon pricing can be an effective tool for reducing greenhouse gas emissions when properly designed and enforced.

**California's Cap-and-Invest Program** represents a mature, hybrid system with comprehensive price containment mechanisms (floors, ceilings, and reserves), substantial revenue generation ($34.5 billion cumulative) directed toward climate investments and community benefits (73% to disadvantaged communities), and near-perfect compliance rates. All four COVID-19-era auctions settled at the floor price of $16.68, but the program's rules-based self-ratcheting mechanism operated without discretionary intervention. The program faces challenges regarding allowance oversupply and the stringency of its 2030 targets, as highlighted by the Legislative Analyst's Office. The AB 398 price containment structure (two-tier APCR and price ceiling) was fully implemented from 2021 onward, though neither has been triggered to date.

**The European Union Emissions Trading System** stands as the most established carbon market globally, demonstrating the longest track record of emissions reductions (47% since 2005, reaching 51% in 2024 preliminary data) and the most ambitious recent reforms through the Fit for 55 package. The MSR proved effective as an automatic stabilizer during the COVID-19 pandemic, absorbing ~379 million allowances in 2020 without any discretionary intervention. The system's high and volatile carbon prices (annual average of €84/tCO2e in 2023, peaking at €105.73 in February 2023) drive significant investment. The CBAM is the most ambitious border adjustment mechanism globally — its transitional phase (reporting only) operated from October 2023, with the definitive compliance phase (financial obligations) beginning January 2026. The system generated €265.7 billion in cumulative revenue since inception.

**China's National Carbon Market** has achieved remarkable progress in a short period, rapidly becoming the world's largest carbon market by emissions coverage (~5.1 billion tonnes/year in the power sector alone). Its 99.98% compliance rate for the third compliance cycle demonstrates strong governmental enforcement capacity. Critically, the enhanced penalty regime introduced by the State Council regulations (effective May 1, 2024) replaced the previous nominal RMB 30,000 cap with fines of 5–10 times the average market price, fundamentally changing the enforcement landscape. However, the intensity-based cap design and 100% free allocation mean near-term emission reduction impacts have been limited. Planned reforms — including sectoral expansion to steel, cement, and aluminum (implemented from 2025, not yet in compliance during the review period), introduction of auctioning, and the shift from "dual control of energy" to "dual control of carbon" — promise to strengthen the system significantly.

**Key Lessons Across Systems:**

1. **Price stability mechanisms matter:** The EU's MSR and California's price floors/ceilings both provide stability through different approaches (volume-based vs. price-based). China's lack of formal price stabilization mechanisms may become more relevant as auctioning is introduced.

2. **Revenue allocation shapes political sustainability:** California's community benefit requirements (35% minimum to disadvantaged communities, achieving 73% actual) and the EU's Social Climate Fund (€86.7 billion from 2026) demonstrate how revenue use can build and maintain political support.

3. **Complementary policies are essential but require coordination:** All three systems show that carbon pricing alone is insufficient. California's overlapping RPS/LCFS policies, the EU's RED III/EED/ESR framework, and China's "1+N" system demonstrate the need for careful policy interaction management.

4. **Leakage concerns drive policy innovation:** The EU's CBAM is the most ambitious response to date, with its transitional phase operational from October 2023 and definitive phase from January 2026. California is exploring border adjustments, and China is expanding its ETS coverage to mitigate CBAM exposure for its steel, cement, and aluminum sectors.

5. **Compliance enforcement is critical:** All three systems achieve near-perfect compliance rates through different mechanisms — California's strict liability (3:1 penalty ratio), the EU's €100/tonne penalty, and China's transition from a nominal RMB 30,000 cap to fines of 5–10 times market prices.

6. **COVID-19 response design matters:** The EU and California demonstrated the resilience of rules-based automatic stabilizers (MSR and self-ratcheting, respectively), while China's discretionary interventions (temporary borrowing, generous allocation) reflected its status as a newly established system navigating simultaneous pandemic and launch challenges.
