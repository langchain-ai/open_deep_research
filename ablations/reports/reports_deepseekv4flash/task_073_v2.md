# Comprehensive Comparative Analysis of Autism Spectrum Disorder Diagnostic Criteria: DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR) and ICD-11 (2019/2022)

**Date of Analysis: May 27, 2026**

---

## Introduction

This report provides a revised and updated comprehensive comparative analysis of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnostic criteria as defined in the DSM-5 (2013, with Text Revision in 2022) and the ICD-11 (2019, effective 2022), with a specific focus on sensory processing differences. The analysis addresses six critical domains: evidence synthesis of diagnostic accuracy and prevalence, clinical guideline positions, advocacy organization perspectives, diagnostic safety risks, and reimbursement/coding implications. The ICD-11, which came into effect on January 1, 2022, represents the first major revision to the WHO's classification system in 28 years. Both systems have converged significantly by abandoning previous subcategories (Autistic Disorder, Asperger Syndrome, PDD-NOS) in favor of a unified autism spectrum diagnosis, yet important structural and operational differences remain. Critically, as of May 2026, **no direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies** comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, or specificity for ASD have been published. This fundamental evidence gap shapes all conclusions drawn in this report, particularly regarding the implications of ICD-11's broader criteria breadth, which must be explicitly framed as hypotheses requiring further validation.

## Section A: Side-by-Side Criteria Comparison

### A.1 Core Social-Communication Criteria

| **Dimension** | **DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR)** | **ICD-11 (2019/2022)** |
|---------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Criterion Structure** | Three sub-criteria (A1, A2, A3), **all three must be present** | Broad description with no fixed number of features required |
| **A1: Social-Emotional Reciprocity** | Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity, ranging from abnormal social approach and failure of normal back-and-forth conversation; to reduced sharing of interests, emotions, or affect; to failure to initiate or respond to social interactions | Persistent deficits in the ability to initiate and sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication |
| **A2: Nonverbal Communication** | Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors used for social interaction, ranging from poorly integrated verbal and nonverbal communication; to abnormalities in eye contact and body language or deficits in understanding and use of gestures; to total lack of facial expressions and nonverbal communication | Difficulties in understanding verbal and nonverbal social cues; integrating language with body language; initiating and maintaining conversations |
| **A3: Relationships** | Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships, ranging from difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts; to difficulties in sharing imaginative play or in making friends; to absence of interest in peers | Sustaining peer relationships; social awareness; empathy; sharing interests |
| **Minimum Features Required** | **All 3** social-communication criteria must be present (DSM-5-TR added explicit language: "as manifested by **all** of the following") | At least **1** social-communication feature documented alongside at least 1 repetitive behavior feature |
| **Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder** | Separate diagnosis available for those with social communication deficits not meeting full ASD criteria | No equivalent separate category |
| **Number of Possible Presentations** | Fixed polythetic approach with specific counting requirements | Approximately **300 different presentations** of autism described [Kamp-Becker, 2024] |

**Key Insight:** The DSM-5's requirement for deficits in *all three* social-communication areas creates a more restrictive diagnostic threshold compared to the ICD-11's flexible approach. The DSM-5-TR (2022) added explicit language requiring "all of the following," which the APA work group stated was intended to "maintain a high diagnostic threshold" [APA, DSM-5-TR PDF]. The ICD-11's allowance for diagnosis with only a single social-communication feature represents the most significant structural difference between the two systems.

### A.2 Sensory Processing Mention and Weighting

| **Aspect** | **DSM-5/DSM-5-TR** | **ICD-11** |
|------------|---------------------|------------|
| **Location in Criteria** | Criterion B4 (under Restricted, Repetitive Patterns of Behavior) — one of four sub-criteria | Under restricted, repetitive behaviors domain — integrated as a diagnostic requirement |
| **Exact Wording** | "Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interests in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)" | "Atypical responses to sensory stimuli" including hypersensitivity and/or hyposensitivity to sounds, light, textures (especially clothing and food), odours and tastes |
| **Required for Diagnosis?** | **No** — only 2 of 4 B criteria must be met, so B4 alone is insufficient | **No** — it is one potential manifestation of RRBs, not individually required |
| **Change from Previous Edition** | **New** in DSM-5 (2013) — not present in DSM-IV | **New** in ICD-11 — not present in ICD-10 |
| **Sensory Coverage** | Hyper-reactivity, hypo-reactivity, and unusual sensory interests explicitly named | Atypical responses to sensory stimuli including sounds, textures, lights, pain, temperature |
| **Diagnostic Utility** | Questioned by research — Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015) found only "failure to respond to noises" had sensitivity >0.75, but specificity was poor (0.34) | Broader inclusion but may come at cost of diagnostic specificity |

**Critical Update on Sensory Criteria:** The DSM-5-TR (2022) made **no changes to Criterion B4 (sensory)** . The only text revision to ASD criteria was changing Criterion A from "as manifested by the following" to "as manifested by all of the following" [WPS Publishing]. The ICD-11's inclusion of atypical sensory responses as a diagnostic requirement represents a major advance from ICD-10, where unusual sensory processing was not considered a core diagnostic feature [Greaves-Lord, Skuse & Mandy, 2023].

### A.3 Severity Specifiers

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **3 Levels of Support** rated separately for (a) social communication and (b) restricted, repetitive behaviors | **5 Profiles** based on intellectual development and functional language ability |
| **Level 1:** "Requiring Support" — difficulty initiating social interactions, rigid behavior limits functioning | **6A02.0:** Without intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 2:** "Requiring Substantial Support" — marked deficits in verbal and nonverbal communication; inflexible behavior obvious to casual observer | **6A02.1:** With intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 3:** "Requiring Very Substantial Support" — severe deficits in communication; extreme difficulty coping with change; behaviors interfere with all aspects of life | **6A02.2:** Without intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| Severity should not be used to determine service eligibility; requires individualized assessment | **6A02.3:** With intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| | **6A02.4:** With intellectual development disorder and with absence of functional language |
| | **6A02.5:** With other specified intellectual development disorder or language impairment |
| | Additional codes: 6A02.Y (Other specified) and 6A02.Z (Unspecified) |

**Key Insight:** The two systems take fundamentally different approaches to severity classification. The DSM-5 rates severity of core autism symptoms, while the ICD-11 classifies based on co-occurring intellectual disability and language impairment. The ICD-11 approach "shifts from categorical subtypes to a dimensional spectrum with functional specifiers describing intellectual functioning, language ability, and associated conditions, rather than embedded severity labels" [Neurolaunch].

### A.4 Age of Onset and Developmental Period Requirements

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion C:** "Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period" | "The onset of the disorder occurs during the developmental period – typically in early childhood – but characteristic symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed limited capacities" |
| Removed previous age-of-onset cutoff of 3 years (from DSM-IV) | No specific age cutoff — onset "during the developmental period" |
| Symptoms "usually in the second year of life," though recognition can vary | Explicitly recognizes that some individuals experience distress and social challenges later in adolescence or adulthood |
| Symptoms "may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities" | Recognizes masking/camouflaging: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" |
| **No explicit mention of regression or loss of skills** in diagnostic criteria | **Explicitly includes "loss of previously acquired skills"** (regression) as a diagnostic consideration |

**Key Insight:** The ICD-11's explicit inclusion of regression and its broader interpretation of onset timing represent meaningful differences that could affect diagnostic capture of certain populations. The ICD-11 states that "some individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder start to experience distress, impairment and overt social challenges once societal demands increase (e.g., during adolescence or adulthood)" [Greaves-Lord et al., 2023].

### A.5 Functional Impairment Thresholds

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion D:** "Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning" | "The symptoms result in significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational or other important areas of functioning" |
| **Treated as standalone criterion** (must be met independently) | **Embedded** in overall diagnostic description |
| **Criterion E:** "Symptoms are not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay" — must be ruled out | Rett syndrome explicitly excluded (LD90.4) |
| Areas specified: social, occupational, or other important areas | Areas specified: personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas — broader range than DSM-5 |
| Masking/camouflaging implicitly recognized through onset flexibility | Explicitly recognizes masking: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" — impairment can still be present |
| Observable impairment typically required | Impairment "observable in all settings, albeit with contextual variations" |

**Key Insight:** The ICD-11 includes "educational," "personal," and "family" as explicit impairment domains, which are absent from DSM-5's wording. The ICD-11's explicit recognition of compensation/masking may better capture individuals who maintain surface-level functioning but experience significant internal distress.

## Section B: Evidence Synthesis of Diagnostic Accuracy and Prevalence

### Critical Note on Available Evidence

As of May 2026, **no direct head-to-head systematic reviews, meta-analyses, or pooled analyses exist that compare the diagnostic accuracy (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, AUC) of DSM-5 (or DSM-5-TR) versus ICD-11 criteria for autism spectrum disorder.** Similarly, **no studies were found that directly compare pooled prevalence estimates between the two classification systems** using the same populations or methodological frameworks. The existing comparative literature focuses almost exclusively on DSM-5 versus DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR. **The implications of ICD-11's broader criteria breadth remain hypotheses requiring further validation through direct comparative research.** This is not an established fact but a conceptual concern raised by expert commentary.

### B.1 Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses: DSM-5 vs. DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR

#### B.1.1 Kulage et al. (2014) — Initial Meta-Analysis

- **Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Sample:** 14 studies (out of 418 identified)
- **Key Findings:**
  - Studies consistently reported decreases in ASD diagnosis (range 7.3–68.4%) using DSM-5 criteria compared to DSM-IV-TR
  - **Overall ASD diagnosis:** 31% decrease (95% CI: 20–44%, p = 0.006)
  - **Autistic Disorder:** 22% decrease (95% CI: 16–29%, p < 0.001)
  - **PDD-NOS:** 70% decrease (95% CI: 55–82%, p = 0.01)
  - The decrease in Asperger's Disorder diagnoses was not statistically significant: 70% decrease (95% CI: 26–94%, p = 0.38)
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (well-conducted systematic review with meta-analysis; limitations include heterogeneity across studies and lack of direct ICD-11 comparison)

#### B.1.2 Bennett & Goodall (2016) — 24-Study Meta-Analysis

- **Study Design:** Meta-analysis
- **Sample:** 24 studies
- **Key Findings:**
  - **35% reduction** for those previously diagnosed using DSM-IV
  - **37% reduction** for those previously diagnosed using DSM-IV-TR
  - Subgroup retention rates under DSM-5:
    - **PDD-NOS:** Only 26% (DSM-IV-TR) and 47% (DSM-IV) qualified under DSM-5
    - **Asperger Syndrome:** 55% (DSM-IV-TR) and 19% (DSM-IV) qualified under DSM-5
    - **Autistic Disorder:** 80% (DSM-IV-TR) and 87% (DSM-IV) qualified under DSM-5
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (comprehensive meta-analysis; no ICD-11 comparison)

#### B.1.3 Five-Year Follow-Up Meta-Analysis (2019)

- **Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Sample:** 33 studies
- **Key Findings:**
  - **ASD:** 20.8% decrease (95% CI: 16.0–26.7%, p < 0.001)
  - **Autistic Disorder:** 10.1% decrease (95% CI: 6.2–16.0%, p < 0.001)
  - **Asperger's Disorder:** 23.3% decrease (95% CI: 12.9–38.5%, p = 0.001)
  - **PDD-NOS:** 46.1% decrease (95% CI: 34.6–58.0%, p = 0.52) — not statistically significant
  - Only 28.8% (95% CI: 13.9–50.5%, p = 0.06) of individuals diagnosed with DSM-IV-TR but not DSM-5 ASD would qualify for Social Communication Disorder (SCD)
  - "Findings suggest smaller decreases in ASD diagnoses compared to earlier reviews"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (updated systematic review showing trend toward smaller reduction rates; still no ICD-11 comparison)

### B.2 Diagnostic Accuracy Studies for DSM-5 Criteria

#### B.2.1 Frazier et al. (2012) — Large-Scale Validation Study

- **Study Design:** Latent variable modeling using registry data
- **Sample:** 14,744 siblings (8,911 with clinically diagnosed ASD, 5,863 without ASD) aged 2–18 years from the Interactive Autism Network
- **Key Statistical Findings:**
  - **DSM-5 vs. DSM-IV-TR comparison:**
    - DSM-5 specificity: **0.97** vs. DSM-IV-TR specificity: **0.86** (superior specificity, fewer false positives)
    - DSM-5 sensitivity: **0.81** vs. DSM-IV-TR sensitivity: **0.95** (lower sensitivity, more missed cases)
  - **Relaxed DSM-5 criteria** (requiring one less symptom criterion):
    - Sensitivity: **0.93** (vs. 0.81)
    - Specificity: **0.95** (vs. 0.97, minimal reduction)
  - "Relaxed DSM-5 criteria may improve identification of ASD, decreasing societal costs through appropriate early diagnosis and maximizing intervention resources"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate to High** (large sample, robust statistical methods; retrospective data; no ICD-11 comparison)

#### B.2.2 McPartland et al. (2012) — DSM-5 Field Trial Data Analysis

- **Study Design:** Retrospective analysis of DSM-IV field trial data
- **Sample:** 933 participants (657 with clinical ASD diagnosis, 276 with non-autistic disorders)
- **Key Statistical Findings:**
  - Only **60.6%** of previously diagnosed ASD cases met DSM-5 diagnostic threshold
  - Overall specificity was high at **94.9%**
  - Sensitivity varied significantly by diagnostic subgroup:
    - Autistic Disorder: **0.76** (76%)
    - Asperger's Disorder: **0.25** (25%)
    - PDD-NOS: **0.28** (28%)
  - Sensitivity varied by cognitive ability:
    - IQ < 70: **0.70** (70%)
    - IQ ≥ 70: **0.46** (46%)
  - "Proposed DSM-5 criteria could substantially alter the composition of the autism spectrum. Revised criteria improve specificity but exclude a substantial portion of cognitively able individuals and those with ASDs other than autistic disorder"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Low to Moderate** (large sample, but retrospective application of *proposed* criteria; no direct ICD-11 comparison)

#### B.2.3 Barton et al. (2013) — Toddler-Specific Study

- **Study Design:** ROC curve analysis
- **Sample:** 422 toddlers
- **Key Statistical Findings:**
  - Optimal diagnostic threshold for toddlers: requiring **2 out of 3 social-communication criteria** and **1 out of 4 repetitive behavior criteria**
  - Sensitivity: **93%**
  - Specificity: **74%**
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Low to Moderate** (toddler-specific; no ICD-11 comparison)

### B.3 Global Prevalence Estimates (Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses)

#### B.3.1 Li et al. (2023) — Three-Level Meta-Analysis

- **Study Design:** Three-level mixed-effects meta-analysis
- **Sample:** 85 articles (317 prevalence estimates), 1994–2019, 29 countries, >120 million individuals
- **Key Findings:**
  - Pooled ASD prevalence: **0.72%** (95% CI: 0.61–0.85)
  - Regional variation: North America **1.01%** > Europe **0.73%** > Asia **0.41%**
  - Prevalence increased over time: from **0.25%** (1994–1999) to **0.99%** (2015–2019)
  - Final adjusted pooled prevalence for high-income regions (recent data): **1.18%** (95% CI: 0.92–1.48)
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (comprehensive meta-analysis; high heterogeneity; no stratification by diagnostic criteria)

#### B.3.2 Global Prevalence in Children (2025 Meta-Analysis)

- **Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Sample:** 66 studies, 2008–2024, 21,313,061 children aged 3–18 years
- **Key Findings:**
  - Global ASD prevalence: **0.77%**
  - Boys: **1.14%** (male-to-female ratio 2–3:1)
  - Regional prevalence: Australia **2.18%** (highest), Africa **1.51%**, America **1.10%**, Europe **0.71%**, Asia **0.28%**
  - "The updated DSM-5 improves diagnostic accuracy, particularly for cases that were previously undiagnosed"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (large sample size; high regional heterogeneity; no ICD-11 stratification)

#### B.3.3 Muthuka et al. (2026) — Global Prevalence with Meta-Regression

- **Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-regression
- **Sample:** 19 studies, 2004–2025, ~20.6 million participants
- **Key Findings:**
  - Pooled ASD prevalence (frequentist): **0.8%** (95% CI: 0.4–1.7%)
  - Pooled ASD prevalence (Bayesian hierarchical): **1.55%** (95% CrI: 0.75–4.1%)
  - Substantial heterogeneity: I² ≈ 100%
  - **Meta-regression identified diagnostic framework, study setting, and geographic region as significant moderators**, collectively explaining approximately **78% of between-study variance**
  - "High heterogeneity reflects differences in case ascertainment, diagnostic criteria, and surveillance infrastructure rather than true epidemiological variation"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (includes meta-regression; high heterogeneity; recognizes diagnostic framework as moderator)

### B.4 Conceptual Comparisons: DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 (No Empirical Data)

#### B.4.1 Kamp-Becker (2024) — Critical Reflection on ICD-11

- **Study Design:** Perspective article / critical reflection
- **Key Points (Quoted Verbatim):**
  - "ICD-11 allows a high variety in symptom combinations, resulting in an extremely diverse ASD picture but possibly at the expense of precision and specificity"
  - "The clinical utility is questionable as this conceptualization can hardly be differentiated from other mental disorders and autism-like traits"
  - "ICD-11 moves away from an observable, behavioral, neurodevelopmental disorder to a disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively, containing many vague and subjective concepts leading to non-falsifiable diagnoses"
  - "This bears a large danger of false positive diagnoses, increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services, and increased non-specificity of treatments"
  - "For research, the specificity of ASD will be reduced, further increasing heterogeneity, hampering replication of studies, and limiting understanding of etiology and biological pathways"
- **Evidence Status:** **Conceptual/theoretical concern** — explicitly labeled as hypothesis requiring validation. No empirical data was collected. Not a systematic review.

#### B.4.2 Greaves-Lord, Skuse & Mandy (2023) — ICD-11 Innovations

- **Study Design:** Comprehensive overview and critical reflection
- **Key Points:**
  - "Atypical responses to sensory stimuli are included as part of the diagnostic requirements in ICD-11, in contrast to ICD-10, where unusual sensory processing was not yet considered a core (diagnostic) feature"
  - "Unlike DSM-5, ICD-11 does not emphasize the criteria related to Disorders of Intellectual Development (ID; such as flipping objects, strong attachment or preoccupation with unusual objects, excessive smelling or touching of objects, echolalia, stimming)"
  - "Diagnostic classification in clinical practise should be a means to provide proper, suitable care"
- **Evidence Status:** **Conceptual overview** — provides expert analysis but no empirical comparison data

#### B.4.3 Expert Commentary (The Transmitter, circa 2018)

- **Key Expert Quotes:**
  - Catherine Lord: "I think that this is good news. It will make life simpler for people making diagnoses"
  - David Skuse: "I'd be very surprised if there would be a battle. DSM-5 was conceptually correct and ICD-11 is following a similar view"
  - Fred Volkmar: "[ICD-11] is taking some of the better parts of DSM without falling into the same pitfalls"
  - Michael B. First: "The flexibility allows clinicians to make the diagnosis upon the clinical judgment and common sense, as long as you follow the concepts in the guidelines"

### B.5 Summary of Evidence Gaps

The most critical finding is the **absence of direct DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 empirical comparisons**. The available evidence provides:
1. **Meta-analytic evidence** that DSM-5 reduces ASD diagnoses by 20.8–37% compared to DSM-IV-TR, with higher-functioning individuals and those with PDD-NOS or Asperger's most affected
2. **Prevalence meta-analyses** showing global ASD prevalence estimates of 0.72–1.55% with diagnostic framework identified as a significant moderator
3. **Conceptual critiques** raising hypotheses that ICD-11's broader criteria may increase false positives, but these remain empirically unvalidated
4. **No studies** directly comparing diagnostic outcomes when applying both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria to the same clinical population

A direct head-to-head comparison applying both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria to the same clinical population remains an urgent research priority.

## Section C: Clinical Guideline Positions

### C.1 World Health Organization (WHO)

**Official Designation:** ICD-11 code **6A02** (Autism Spectrum Disorder)

**Adoption and Implementation:**
- The ICD-11 was endorsed by the World Health Assembly at the 72nd meeting in **May 2019** and came into effect globally on **January 1, 2022** [WHO Implementation FAQ]
- As of 2025, more than 45–64 countries have adopted or begun the transition to ICD-11, including Canada, the Netherlands, Norway, Finland, and Thailand [ICD-10 Monitor; Libman Education]
- The ICD-11 is available as a global public good; WHO provides free access and tools to support full implementation
- **WHO stopped maintaining ICD-10 in 2018**; future enhancements will only appear in ICD-11

**Official Diagnostic Criteria (Quoted Verbatim):**

"Autism spectrum disorder is characterised by persistent deficits in the ability to initiate and to sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication, and by a range of restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behaviour, interests or activities that are clearly atypical or excessive for the individual's age and sociocultural context." [Find-A-Code]

"The onset of the disorder occurs during the developmental period, typically in early childhood, but symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed limited capacities." [Find-A-Code]

"Deficits are sufficiently severe to cause impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational or other important areas of functioning and are usually a pervasive feature of the individual's functioning observable in all settings." [Find-A-Code]

"Individuals along the spectrum exhibit a full range of intellectual functioning and language abilities." [Find-A-Code]

**Sensory Processing Position:**
"Atypical responses to sensory stimuli are included as part of the diagnostic requirements in ICD-11, in contrast to ICD-10, where unusual sensory processing was not yet considered a core (diagnostic) feature" [Greaves-Lord et al., 2023]

The ICD-11 specifies that restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behaviour include "atypical responses to sensory stimuli" such as hypersensitivity and/or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli, which can include unusual interests in certain sensory stimuli, sounds, light, textures (especially clothing and food), odours and tastes [Greaves-Lord et al., 2023; Autism Europe, 2018].

**Position on ICD-11 vs. DSM-5:**
No explicit WHO statement was found in formal WHO documents formally comparing ICD-11 to DSM-5 in a comparative analysis. The comparisons available come from independent academic analyses, news reporting, and expert commentary. David Skuse, professor at University College London and ICD-11 committee member, stated: "DSM-5 was conceptually correct and ICD-11 is following a similar view" [The Transmitter].

### C.2 American Psychiatric Association (APA)

**Official Publication:** DSM-5 (2013) and DSM-5-TR (March 2022)

**Key Changes in DSM-5-TR (2022):**

The APA published the DSM-5-TR (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision) in March 2022. The major change to autism diagnostic criteria involves Criterion A.

**Direct Quotation from APA Official Document:**

"The diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder is revised from the DSM-5 diagnosis."

"Criterion A phrase 'as manifested by the following' was revised to read 'as manifested by **all of the following**' to improve the intent and clarity of the wording." [APA, DSM-5-TR PDF]

"The revision by the work group was consistent with the intent of the DSM-5 work group for autism spectrum disorder to **maintain a high diagnostic threshold**." [APA, DSM-5-TR PDF]

**Other DSM-5-TR Changes:**
- The specifier language was broadened from "another neurodevelopmental, mental, or behavioral **disorder**" to "another neurodevelopmental, mental, or behavioral **problem**" — allowing clinicians to note related issues affecting well-being that may not qualify as disorders
- "Neither of these revisions is expected to have a major impact on the number of people diagnosed with autism" [WPS Publishing]

**Position on ICD-11 vs. DSM-5:**
No explicit APA statement was found formally comparing DSM-5-TR to ICD-11. The APA's DSM-5-TR material does not include a comparative analysis against WHO's ICD-11. The DSM-5-TR does note that the manual "is used internationally for clinical practice, research, education, and public health, harmonizing with the World Health Organization's International Classification of Diseases (ICD)" [DSM-5-TR PDF].

**Sensory Processing Position:**
Sensory processing differences are included in the DSM-5-TR as part of Criterion B (restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities). Specifically: "hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)" [Autism Speaks; CDC].

### C.3 American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

**Primary Clinical Report:** "Identification, Evaluation, and Management of Children With Autism Spectrum Disorder" — Published in *Pediatrics*, **January 2020** (online December 2019)

**Authors:** Susan L. Hyman and colleagues, including the Council on Children with Disabilities and Section on Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics

**Key Quotations and Recommendations:**

"ASD is a common neurodevelopmental disorder with reported prevalence in the United States of 1 in 59 children (approximately 1.7%), characterized by core deficits in 2 domains: social communication/interaction and restrictive, repetitive patterns of behavior" [PubMed ID 31843864].

"Standardized screening for ASD at 18 and 24 months of age with ongoing developmental surveillance continues to be recommended in primary care because ASD is common, can be diagnosed as young as 18 months of age, and has evidenced-based interventions that may improve function" [PubMed ID 31843864].

"The DSM-5 established a single category of ASD to replace the subtypes of autistic disorder, Asperger syndrome, and pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified in the DSM-IV" [Washington PDF of AAP Report].

"Primary care providers should be familiar with the diagnostic criteria for ASD, appropriate etiologic evaluation, and co-occurring medical and behavioral conditions that affect the child's function and quality of life" [PubMed ID 31843864].

"Shared decision making calls for collaboration with families in evaluation and choice of interventions" [PubMed ID 31843864].

**Position on ICD-11 vs. DSM-5:**
No explicit AAP statement was found endorsing ICD-11 over DSM-5 or providing guidance on which diagnostic criteria to use. The AAP's 2020 clinical report is based on and references the **DSM-5** diagnostic criteria exclusively as the reference framework for US pediatric practice.

**Sensory Processing Position:**
Sensory processing is addressed primarily as a co-occurring feature within the context of DSM-5 diagnostic criteria (Criterion B). The report notes co-occurring medical and behavioral conditions include "disorders of sleep and feeding, gastrointestinal tract symptoms, obesity, seizures, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, anxiety, and wandering" [PubMed ID 31843864]. No explicit AAP guidance was found on specific standardized sensory processing assessment tools or protocols.

**Updates Since 2020:**
- **As of May 2026, no new AAP clinical report or official update to the 2020 report has been identified**
- AAP now reports (via CDC data) that "1 in 31 children 8 years of age has been identified with a diagnosis of autism" [AAP Autism Page]
- Recommendations for screening remain at 18 and 24 months with validated tools (M-CHAT-R/F)
- AAP emphasizes that children should be referred for intervention at the time of identification and should not wait for an autism diagnostic evaluation

## Section E: Diagnostic Safety Risks

### E.1 Overview of Opposing Diagnostic Safety Profiles

A critical finding of this analysis is that DSM-5 and ICD-11 create **opposing diagnostic safety profiles** that are theoretical rather than empirically established through head-to-head comparison. The DSM-5's more restrictive counting approach primarily risks **underdiagnosis** (false negatives), while the ICD-11's broader, more permissive approach theoretically risks **overdiagnosis** (false positives). **Both of these characterizations must be understood as hypotheses requiring direct empirical validation.**

### E.2 Under-Diagnosis Risks

#### Direct Empirical Evidence (DSM-5 vs. DSM-IV-TR)

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Kulage et al. (2014) — Meta-Analysis:**
- 31% overall decrease in ASD diagnosis under DSM-5 compared to DSM-IV-TR (95% CI: 20–44%, p = 0.006)
- 70% decrease for PDD-NOS subgroup
- Sample: 14 studies, meta-analytic design

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Bennett & Goodall (2016) — Meta-Analysis:**
- 35–37% reduction for those previously diagnosed using DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR
- PDD-NOS: only 26–47% qualified under DSM-5
- Asperger Syndrome: only 19–55% qualified under DSM-5
- Sample: 24 studies, meta-analytic design

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Five-Year Follow-Up Meta-Analysis (2019):**
- 20.8% decrease for ASD overall (95% CI: 16.0–26.7%, p < 0.001)
- 23.3% decrease for Asperger's Disorder (95% CI: 12.9–38.5%, p = 0.001)
- Sample: 33 studies, meta-analytic design

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **McPartland et al. (2012):**
- Only 25% of individuals with Asperger's and 28% with PDD-NOS met proposed DSM-5 criteria
- Sample: 933 participants, retrospective field trial data analysis

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Frazier et al. (2012):**
- DSM-5 sensitivity: 0.81 vs. DSM-IV-TR sensitivity: 0.95
- Higher-functioning individuals disproportionately affected
- Sample: 14,744 siblings, latent variable modeling

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **DSM-5-TR Change (2022):**
The addition of the word "all" to social communication criteria was intended to "maintain a high diagnostic threshold" [APA, DSM-5-TR PDF]. This may theoretically increase underdiagnosis, particularly for females who mask.

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **CDC ADDM Network (2025):**
- Overall ASD prevalence: 32.2 per 1,000 (1 in 31) children aged 8 years
- Boys 3.4 times more likely to be identified than girls (49.2 vs. 14.3 per 1,000)
- Median age of earliest known ASD diagnosis: 47 months
- 39.6% of children with ASD and cognitive data classified as having intellectual disability
- Evidence of persistent underdiagnosis in females

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Kentrou, Livingston & Begeer (2024):**
- **24.6%** of autistic adults reported at least one psychiatric diagnosis they perceived as incorrect (one in four)
- **42.9%** had at least one psychiatric diagnosis before being diagnosed with autism
- Autistic women (31.7%) reported perceived misdiagnoses significantly more often than men (16.7%)
- Most frequent perceived misdiagnoses: personality disorders, anxiety disorders, mood disorders
- Sample: 1,211 Dutch autistic adults, cross-sectional survey design
- Published in eClinicalMedicine (The Lancet)

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Autism Speaks / JAMA Network Open (2024):**
- ASD diagnoses increased by **175%** over the decade 2011–2022
- Young adults aged 26–34 had the sharpest increase — a **450% rise** — suggesting many receive diagnoses in adulthood after childhood was undiagnosed
- Gender gap narrowing: 305% increase among girls and 315% among women
- Sample: Over 12.2 million people

#### Conceptual/Under-Diagnosis Concerns Under ICD-11

**[CONCEPTUAL CONCERN]** Kamp-Becker (2024) suggests that ICD-11's reliance on "inner experience" rather than observable behaviors may create new avenues for underdiagnosis when clinicians are uncertain about how to operationalize subjective criteria. This is explicitly a theoretical concern, not an empirical finding.

### E.3 Over-Diagnosis Risks

#### Conceptual/Over-Diagnosis Concerns Under ICD-11

**[CONCEPTUAL CONCERN]** **Kamp-Becker (2024) — Primary theoretical critique:**
- "This bears a **large danger of false positive diagnoses**, of further increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services and of increasing the non-specificity of treatments"
- ICD-11 "moves away from an observable, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorder to a **disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively**, containing many vague and subjective concepts leading to **non-falsifiable diagnoses**"
- With 300 possible symptom combinations and no mandatory minimum threshold, "a diagnosis can be made if 1 feature of social-communication and 1 feature of repetitive behaviors are documented, with an onset of any time in life"
- **This is a perspective article, not an empirical study. These are hypotheses, not established facts.**

**[CONCEPTUAL CONCERN]** **Autism Science Foundation Podcast:**
The broad criteria "is causing a lot of confusion in the community because the presentations are not specific to autism" [ASF Podcast].

#### Empirical Evidence on Over-Diagnosis (DSM-5 Context)

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Key Autism Services:**
52.8% of clinicians believe that over 10% of ASD diagnoses are concluded despite not fully conclusive assessments [Key Autism Services].

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Liao & Fombonne (2026) — JAMA Pediatrics:**
"The growth in autism prevalence has had harmful repercussions, diverting important services from individuals and families who need help the most. Services become diluted and the diagnosis becomes trivial, while specific problems may be overlooked in efforts to address autism."

### E.4 Sensory-Focused Presentations: Specific Safety Risks

The **different weighting of sensory criteria** between the two systems creates unique diagnostic safety risks for individuals with prominent sensory presentations:

| **Risk Type** | **Under DSM-5** | **Under ICD-11** |
|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Sensory-dominant presentation misses diagnosis** | **HIGHER RISK** — an individual with primarily sensory symptoms may not meet threshold of at least 2 of 4 B criteria unless they also have other RRBs | **LOWER RISK** — sensory sensitivities are integrated as part of RRB domain requirement |
| **Non-autistic sensory sensitivity diagnosed as autism** | **LOWER RISK** — sensory features alone insufficient for B criterion | **HIGHER RISK** — broader inclusion may capture non-autistic individuals with sensory processing disorder (SPD) or other conditions |
| **Diagnostic specificity of sensory features** | Questioned by research — sensory features "have limited utility as diagnostic features for autism in school-age and beyond" | Same concern applies — sensory features "are also frequent in other disorders" |

#### Empirical Evidence on Sensory Features as Discriminators

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015) — Foundational Study:**
- Sample: 776 school-age and adult individuals
- "In **no comparison** were the features examined predictive of autism or autism spectrum in comparison to the non-ASD sample"
- Only **"failure to respond to noises"** had sensitivity above .75, but specificity was **poor (.34)**
- "These results question the rationale for including sensory items as a diagnostic criterion for autism"
- "Sensory issues may be a source of concern and disability and may well need to be addressed as part of a comprehensive treatment program, but these results strongly question the utility of including such features in the DSM-5 definition of autism in school-age, adolescent, and adult samples"

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **ElSheikh et al. (2024) — ASD vs. ADHD Sensory Processing:**
- Sensory processing abnormalities observed in **42–88%** of children with ASD and **~50%** of children with ADHD
- ASD and ADHD differed in **all subscales except unresponsiveness, auditory filtering, and visual/auditory subscales**
- Sample: 37 ASD, 41 ADHD, 43 typically developing children aged 6–16 years
- Design: Case-control study using Short Sensory Profile and evoked potentials

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **He et al. (2021) — Disorder-Specific Tactile Alterations:**
- **Tactile detection and order judgment difficulties**: more common in ADHD
- **Tactile discrimination difficulties**: more common in ASD
- Discrimination thresholds significantly higher **only in the combined ASD+ADHD group**
- **Disorder-specific patterns** in lower-level tactile sensory processing linked to higher-level clinical symptoms
- Design: Psychophysical protocols, children with ASD, ADHD, combined ASD+ADHD, and typically developing controls

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Miezah et al. (2020) — SPD vs. ASD:**
- Children with SPD exhibited **more severe sensory processing challenges** in tactile, auditory filtering, sensation seeking, low energy/weak, and taste/smell sensitivity domains compared to children with ASD
- Children with ASD had **greater deficits in sustained and control attention** than those with SPD or TD
- Combined model of sensory and attention metrics correctly classified **76.8%** of participants
- Sample: 69 children aged 6–10 (21 SPD, 24 ASD, 24 TD)
- Design: Cross-sectional with discriminant analysis

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Carpenter et al. (2024) — Sensory Subtypes Transdiagnostically:**
- **Five sensory processing subtypes** identified: Typical Processing (30%), Intermediate/Mixed (11%), Sensory Over-Responsive (19%), Sensory Seeking (19%), Sensory Under-Responsive (20%)
- Sensory Over-Responsive group showed **distinctively elevated anxiety scores**
- Sensory Seeking and Sensory Under-Responsive groups showed **heightened ADHD scores**
- Sample: 117 children aged 8–12 from a community neurodevelopment clinic
- Design: Latent profile analysis

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Meta-Analysis on Sensory Processing in ADHD (2025):**
Sensory sensitivity was **higher in the ADHD group compared with the control group** (SMD = 1.17, 95% CI [0.75, 1.59]), observed in both adults and children [ScienceDirect, 2025].

### E.5 Populations Differentially Affected

| **Population** | **Primary Risk** | **Evidence Type** |
|----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Females** | Underdiagnosis under both systems, especially DSM-5 | Empirical: CDC ADDM (2025); Kentrou et al. (2024); DSM-5-TR change |
| **Adults** | Underdiagnosis; late recognition | Empirical: Autism Speaks/JAMA (2024) — 450% increase in young adults |
| **Higher-functioning individuals** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | Empirical: McPartland (2012); Frazier (2012); Conceptual: Kamp-Becker (2024) |
| **Culturally diverse groups** | Underdiagnosis; cultural factors affect expression and recognition | Empirical: CDC ADDM (2025) — disparities narrowing |
| **Minimally verbal / intellectual disability** | Better captured under ICD-11's detailed specifiers | Empirical: ICD-11 provides detailed guidelines distinguishing autism with/without ID |
| **Sensory-dominant presentations** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | Empirical: Grapel et al. (2015); ElSheikh et al. (2024); He et al. (2021); Miezah et al. (2020) |

### E.6 Inappropriate Interventions Resulting from Misdiagnosis

**[EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE]** **Kentrou et al. (2024):**
"Misunderstanding and misinformation around variant and differing presentations means that misdiagnosed individuals and those who do not receive a diagnosis at all are often failed by medical, education, social care and criminal justice systems."

"Misdiagnosis and lack of diagnosis can lead to severe mental health consequences, including mislabeling with stigmatized conditions such as Borderline Personality Disorder and adverse outcomes like inpatient admissions."

**[CONCEPTUAL CONCERN]** **Liao & Fombonne (2026):**
"Services become diluted and the diagnosis becomes trivial, while specific problems may be overlooked in efforts to address autism."

**[CONCEPTUAL CONCERN]** **Kamp-Becker (2024):**
Overdiagnosis leads to "limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments."

**[CLINICAL GUIDANCE]** **SPD vs. ASD Differential Diagnosis:**
SPD affects 5–16% of school-aged children; sensory symptoms overlap substantially with autism. "The distinction matters — not because one diagnosis is better or worse, but because autism and ADHD have different core features, different diagnostic criteria, and different intervention needs" [Move Up ABA].

## Section F: Reimbursement and Coding Summary

### F.1 United States: Current Status

**ICD-10-CM F84.0 — "Autistic Disorder"**

The United States currently uses **ICD-10-CM** for all healthcare billing and reimbursement. The primary autism code, **F84.0 ("Autistic disorder")**, has remained the standard since its introduction:

- **2026 Edition:** Effective October 1, 2025 [ICD10Data.com]
- **Definition:** Includes "autism spectrum disorder, Kanner's syndrome, infantile autism, and infantile psychosis"
- **Related Codes:** F84.5 (Asperger's syndrome), F84.9 (PDD unspecified)
- **Screening Code:** Z13.41 for autism screening, effective October 1, 2024

**The United States Has NOT Adopted ICD-11 for Billing**

- **No official implementation date** has been set for ICD-11 morbidity coding in the US [Libman Education]
- The National Committee on Vital and Health Statistics (NCVHS) established an ICD-11 Workgroup in 2023 to gather information and develop recommendations regarding US adoption
- **Estimated timeline:** Projected no earlier than **2027–2029** for morbidity/billing, with some experts projecting full implementation could take **10–15 years** given healthcare system complexity [Sirius Solutions; Libman Education]
- **Transition costs:** Estimated at **$1–3 billion nationally** due to complex mapping
- **Mapping challenge:** Only **23.5% of ICD-10-CM codes** could be fully represented by a single ICD-11 stem code [JAMA Health Forum, July 2023]
- **Training requirements:** WHO recommends 20 classroom hours plus 40 hours of coding practice per coder for ICD-11 proficiency; nearly 64% of US health organizations have not begun formal ICD-11 training as of 2026 [Sirius Solutions]

**Current US Payer Requirements:**

- **Private insurers uniformly require a diagnosis based on DSM-5 (or DSM-5-TR) criteria** for coverage of autism-related services
- The ICD-10-CM code F84.0 is the billing code submitted to insurers, but **the clinical diagnosis itself must be per DSM-5**
- **Priority Health (Michigan) Policy (effective March 1, 2026):** "The diagnosis of autism should be consistent with the standards of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders at the time of evaluation" [Priority Health Medical Policy]
- **Colorado Medicaid Policy:** Covered diagnoses defined by specific ICD-10-CM code ranges; psychotherapy services for ASD covered for members under 21 (effective January 1, 2024)
- **No ICD-11-based autism billing policies found** — because ICD-11 has not been adopted for billing in the US, no payer policies reference ICD-11 criteria versus DSM-5 criteria for autism billing purposes

**Key Structural Change When ICD-11 Is Adopted:**
- ICD-11 code 6A02 replaces ICD-10 F84.0 with dimensional severity specifiers, collapsing multiple autistic subtypes into a single spectrum code
- ICD-11 uses post-coordination to specify intellectual functioning and language ability levels (6A02.0 through 6A02.5)
- ICD-11 and DSM-5 criteria align closely, both adopting unified spectrum diagnoses with severity levels

### F.2 United Kingdom (NHS)

**Current Status: ICD-10 Still in Use for Morbidity Coding**

- The National Clinical Coding Standards ICD-10 5th Edition for Morbidity Coding (Version 12.0, issued April 2026) is the definitive source of clinical coding standards for use in the NHS in England [NHS England]
- SNOMED CT is the mandated terminology for clinical documentation at the point of care, while ICD-10 codes are applied retrospectively by trained coders to inpatient episodes, driving hospital funding and statistics
- **No specific ICD-11 implementation date for the UK was found**

**Diagnostic vs. Billing Code Usage:**
- In the UK, ICD-10 codes are primarily used for morbidity coding (statistics, hospital funding, and resource allocation) rather than direct billing per se
- SNOMED CT handles clinical documentation
- ICD-10 codes drive hospital tariffs and resource allocation through the Payment by Results system

**ICD-11 Transition:**
- The UK is "planning a transition to ICD-11, which brings modernization and enhanced alignment with SNOMED CT" [Tandem Health]
- The French ATIH impact study confirmed active UK engagement in ICD-11 planning at the European level

### F.3 Germany

**Current Status: ICD-10-GM 2026 Still Official**

- The currently valid version is **ICD-10-GM 2026**, published by the Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM) on behalf of the Federal Ministry of Health [BfArM]
- ICD-10-GM is the official classification for the encoding of diagnoses in inpatient and outpatient medical care in Germany
- The use of ICD-10-GM in inpatient care is laid down in § 301 Volume V of the German Social Security Code (SGB V)

**No ICD-11 Date Set:**
- BfArM explicitly states: "A specific date for introducing ICD-11 in Germany for mortality coding has not yet been determined" [BfArM]
- "For both mortality and morbidity coding ICD-10 will remain the valid official classification for Germany, until ICD-11 is introduced in the respective area of application" [BfArM]
- A German translation of ICD-11 is in preliminary release phase undergoing quality assurance with scientific medical societies
- A PubMed article (2024) notes the transition will take several years due to complexity [PubMed ID 38863176]

**Autism Coding:**
- Autism spectrum disorders are coded under the F84.x range within ICD-10-GM, consistent with WHO ICD-10
- ICD-10-GM forms the basis for the G-DRG (German Diagnosis Related Groups) and PEPP case-based fixed sum payment systems used in inpatient care

### F.4 France

**Current Status: French ICD-10 in Use**

- France currently uses its own French adaptation of ICD-10 for hospital diagnosis coding
- The French Technical Agency for Information on Hospital Care (ATIH) is leading the ICD-11 transition [ATIH 2024 Annual Report]

**Active Transition Planning:**
- ATIH conducted an impact study involving 22 interviews with hospital representatives, institutional stakeholders, software editors, and ICD-11 project leaders from Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Germany, and the United Kingdom
- A SWOT analysis of the transition was produced
- **2025 Pilot Program:** France launched a pilot calling for double coding in 10 university hospitals to prepare clinical coders and estimate financial impacts of the transition
- The strategic roadmap is targeted for definition **by 2026**
- "The deployment of ICD-11 in France will necessarily take place over a long period and will rely on good coordination with all the stakeholders involved" [PCSI Conference 2024]

**Autism Coding:**
- No specific French policies for autism diagnosis coding under ICD-11 were found, as the ICD-11 transition roadmap is still under development
- TSA (Trouble du Spectre de l'Autisme) is classified under neurodevelopmental disorders per both DSM-5 and ICD-11
- The Haute Autorité de Santé (HAS) has updated its clinical recommendations based on both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria

### F.5 Summary: Practical Impact on Reimbursement

| **System** | **Current Billing Code** | **ICD-11 Adopted for Billing?** | **Impact of ICD-11 on Coverage** |
|------------|-------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **US Private Insurance** | ICD-10-CM F84.0 | No — DSM-5 criteria required clinically | None currently; future adoption may equalize access |

**Key Takeaway:** Adopting ICD-11 criteria **does not currently affect coverage or reimbursement** in the US or most EU systems because billing codes have not transitioned. No US payer policies were found that reference ICD-11 criteria for autism billing purposes. ## Section G: Research Limitations and Evidence Gaps

### G.1 Critical Evidence Gaps

1. **No Direct Head-to-Head DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 Empirical Comparisons:** This remains the single most critical gap. As of May 2026, no published studies have directly applied both DSM-5 (or DSM-5-TR) and ICD-11 diagnostic criteria to the same clinical population and compared diagnostic accuracy (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, AUC). All existing comparative analyses are conceptual or compare DSM-5 to earlier systems (DSM-IV, DSM-IV-TR, ICD-10).

2. **No Prevalence Meta-Analyses Stratified by Diagnostic Criteria:** None of the available prevalence meta-analyses (Li et al., 2023; 2025 global prevalence; Muthuka et al., 2026) stratify their findings by DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 diagnostic criteria. While Muthuka et al. (2026) identified "diagnostic framework" as a significant moderator explaining approximately 78% of between-study variance, this analysis did not isolate DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 effects.

3. **Limited Evidence on Sensory Features as Diagnostic Discriminators:** The foundational study by Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015) — which found sensory features lack sufficient diagnostic specificity — was published before either DSM-5 or ICD-11 were fully implemented. More recent studies (ElSheikh et al., 2024; He et al., 2021; Miezah et al., 2020) provide emerging evidence of disorder-specific sensory profiles but remain small in scale and have not been replicated in large, diverse samples.

4. **No Studies on ICD-11 Impact on Service Access or Clinical Decision-Making:** As of May 2026, no empirical studies have examined how the adoption of ICD-11 criteria affects service eligibility, access to interventions, or clinical decision-making in real-world settings.

5. **Outdated NICE Quality Standard:** NICE Quality Standard QS51 has not been updated since its original publication in 2014 — over 12 years ago. This may not reflect current best practice, particularly regarding recognition of underdiagnosis in females and sensory processing assessment.

### G.2 Conflicting or Incomplete Evidence

1. **Prevalence Estimates Vary Widely:** Global ASD prevalence estimates range from 0.28% (Asia) to 2.18% (Australia) depending on region, study methodology, and diagnostic framework. High heterogeneity (I² ≈ 100%) persists even in the most recent meta-analyses.

2. **DSM-5 Reduction Rates Vary:** Meta-analyses show reduction in ASD diagnoses under DSM-5 ranging from 20.8% (2019 follow-up) to 37% (Bennett & Goodall, 2016), suggesting the effect may be moderating over time as clinicians adapt to the new criteria.

3. **DSM-5-TR Impact Unknown:** The APA stated that the DSM-5-TR changes are "not expected to have a major impact on the number of people diagnosed with autism," but this has not been empirically verified.

### G.3 Methodological Limitations of Existing Studies

1. **Retrospective Designs:** Most diagnostic accuracy studies (McPartland et al., 2012; Frazier et al., 2012) retrospectively applied DSM-5 criteria to data collected under DSM-IV, which may not reflect how criteria perform in prospective clinical application.

2. **Sample Homogeneity:** Studies are disproportionately drawn from clinical samples in high-income Western countries, limiting generalizability to diverse populations and low-resource settings.

3. **Lack of Adult Data:** Most studies focus on children; limited data exists on how DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria perform in adult populations.

4. **Self-Report Limitations:** Studies on perceived misdiagnosis (Kentrou et al., 2024) rely on self-reported diagnoses without validation from medical records.

### G.4 Areas Requiring Urgent Future Research

1. **Direct head-to-head comparison** of DSM-5-TR and ICD-11 diagnostic accuracy in the same clinical population
2. **Impact of ICD-11 breadth** on prevalence estimates, false positive rates, and service allocation
3. **Diagnostic utility of sensory features** as discriminators between ASD and other neurodevelopmental conditions (ADHD, SPD, anxiety disorders)
4. **Differential impact** of both systems on underdiagnosed populations (females, adults, culturally diverse groups)
5. **Real-world effects** of ICD-11 adoption on service access, clinical decision-making, and treatment outcomes
6. **Validation of DSM-5-TR changes** — whether the "all of the following" language affects diagnostic rates in practice

## Conclusion

The DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR) and ICD-11 (2019/2022) represent a significant convergence in autism diagnostic criteria, both adopting a unified autism spectrum diagnosis that consolidates previously separate subcategories. Both systems now include sensory processing differences as diagnostically relevant features — a major advance from ICD-10, which did not recognize sensory issues as a core diagnostic criterion.

However, important structural differences remain. The DSM-5 employs a more restrictive, counting-based approach requiring deficits in all three social-communication domains and at least two of four restricted/repetitive behavior domains. The ICD-11 takes a more flexible approach, allowing diagnosis with fewer documented features and providing clinicians greater judgment latitude. These structural differences create opposing theoretical diagnostic safety profiles: the DSM-5 primarily risks underdiagnosis (particularly for females, higher-functioning individuals, and those who mask), while the ICD-11 theoretically risks overdiagnosis (false positives due to broad criteria and lack of specificity). **Both of these characterizations must be understood as hypotheses requiring direct empirical validation.**

The evidence base for comparing the two systems remains severely limited. No direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies have been published as of May 2026, representing an urgent research priority. The available evidence — primarily studies comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria — consistently shows DSM-5 reduces ASD diagnoses by 20–37% compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R, with higher-functioning individuals and those with Asperger's or PDD-NOS most affected.

Clinical guideline organizations have taken different positions reflecting their national contexts. NICE (UK) endorses both frameworks equally. The AAP (US) uses DSM-5 as its reference framework and has not updated its 2020 clinical report. The WHO promotes ICD-11 globally, with 64 countries at various implementation stages. The APA maintains DSM-5 as the US clinical standard while acknowledging ICD codes for billing, and the DSM-5-TR clarified the social communication criterion to "maintain a high diagnostic threshold."

Advocacy organizations have not issued definitive comparative judgments. **No explicit position comparing DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 was found for any of the three major organizations (ASAN, National Autistic Society, Autism Speaks).** ASAN's history of advocacy during the DSM-5 revision suggests they would likely view ICD-11's flexibility favorably, but no formal position exists. The National Autistic Society treats both systems as equally valid under NICE guidance. Autism Speaks has not taken a public position on ICD-11.

The reimbursement landscape is currently dominated by the fact that the US has not adopted ICD-11 for billing, and most EU countries (except the UK, which uses ICD-10 for billing while planning transition) still use ICD-10 for administrative purposes. When full ICD-11 adoption occurs, the unified code 6A02 and its post-coordination severity specifiers will likely reshape service eligibility and reimbursement structures, but no payer policies currently reference ICD-11 criteria for autism.

For clinicians, the key practical consideration is that both systems can yield valid autism diagnoses, but awareness of their different risk profiles is essential. The DSM-5 requires careful attention to ensure individuals who mask or have atypical presentations are not excluded. The ICD-11 requires careful differential diagnosis to ensure individuals with autism-like traits from other conditions are not inappropriately diagnosed. In either system, clinical judgment, comprehensive multi-source information gathering, and sensitivity to diverse presentations remain paramount.
