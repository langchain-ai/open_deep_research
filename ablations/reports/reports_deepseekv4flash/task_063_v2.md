# Revised and Strengthened Clinical Research Report: 68-Year-Old Man with Progressive First-Degree AV Block, Recurrent Lightheadedness, and Four ED Visits

## Executive Summary

This report addresses a 68-year-old man presenting with four emergency department visits over six weeks for lightheadedness and fatigue, with ECG evidence of progressive PR interval prolongation (200 ms → 240 ms over 3 weeks), first-degree AV block with occasional dropped beats, and concerning access barriers including a 3-month cardiology wait and insurance limitations. The report provides comprehensive, guideline-based recommendations addressing four previously identified gaps: (1) medication management with explicit hold/discontinue criteria and alternative antihypertensive agents; (2) prioritization of highest-yield ambulatory monitoring modalities with 24–72 hour placement urgency; (3) comprehensive diagnostic needs including echocardiography timing, pause-duration thresholds, and ECG findings that alter management; and (4) guideline-based justifications for all recommendations. The core clinical approach involves immediate dose reduction of metoprolol with defined thresholds for discontinuation, urgent placement of Mobile Cardiac Telemetry (MCOT) within 24–72 hours, expedited echocardiography, and clear escalation criteria for pacemaker consultation based on pause duration, ECG findings, and symptom progression.

## 1. Medication Management: AV-Nodal Blocker Hold/Discontinue Criteria and Alternative Antihypertensive Agents

### 1.1 Pharmacodynamic Basis for Concern

Metoprolol exerts a negative dromotropic effect on the AV node by prolonging the AH interval and AV nodal refractory periods, which can lengthen the PR interval [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628). In this patient, the PR interval has progressed from 200 ms to 240 ms over just 3 weeks while on metoprolol 50 mg BID, raising the critical question of whether this represents drug-induced AV block (reversible with dose reduction/discontinuation) or drug-revealed AV block (where the medication unmasks underlying conduction system disease that will persist regardless of medication adjustment).

The landmark study by Osmonov et al. (2012) in *Pacing and Clinical Electrophysiology* provides critical guidance: among 108 patients with symptomatic type II second- or third-degree AV block using AV nodal blockers, drug discontinuation led to resolution in 72% of cases, but 27% of those patients experienced recurrence despite discontinuation of the culprit drug. Most notably, **24 of 36 metoprolol-induced AV blocks persisted or recurred** after discontinuation, compared to only 3 of 24 carvedilol-induced blocks [Management of patients with drug-induced atrioventricular block - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12919613). This suggests that metoprolol, more than other beta-blockers, may reveal underlying conduction system disease rather than merely cause reversible AV nodal depression.

A 2025 comprehensive review in the *Journal of Cardiovascular Electrophysiology* further clarifies that true drug-induced AV block is rare—only 15% of patients with drug-related AV block had it directly attributable to medications, defined as AV block that resolved after drug discontinuation and did not recur during follow-up. Despite initial resolution after discontinuing offending medications, recurrence rates are notably high, with 50–90% of patients ultimately requiring permanent pacemakers [Clinical Significance and Management of AV Block Associated With Bradycardic Drug Therapy - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12246518).

### 1.2 Explicit Criteria for Holding Versus Discontinuing AV-Nodal Blockers

The following criteria are derived from the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline, the 2021 ESC Guidelines on Cardiac Pacing, and the accumulating evidence on drug-induced versus drug-revealed AV block [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) [2021 ESC Guidelines on Cardiac Pacing and CRT](https://inahfcarmet.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2021_ESC_Cardiac_Pacing_and_CRT.pdf).

#### Criteria for Dose Reduction (not discontinuation)

| Scenario | Action | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| PR interval 200–280 ms with mild symptoms (fatigue, lightheadedness without syncope) | Reduce metoprolol by 50% (e.g., 50 mg BID → 25 mg BID) | Allows continued treatment of hypertension while reducing negative dromotropic effect; PR <300 ms does not warrant complete discontinuation [First-Degree AV Block Treatment - Medscape](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/161829-treatment) |
| Asymptomatic PR prolongation <300 ms | Continue current dose with regular monitoring | No treatment indicated for asymptomatic first-degree AV block per guidelines [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| Moderate bradycardia (HR 50–60) without symptoms | Continue with ECG monitoring | Not an indication for dose adjustment [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline Made Simple](https://www.acc.org/-/media/Non-Clinical/Files-PDFs-Excel-MS-Word-etc/Guidelines/2018/Guidelines_Made_Simple_2018_Bradycardia.pdf) |

#### Criteria for Discontinuation (Complete Withdrawal of AV-Nodal Blocker)

| Scenario | Action | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| PR interval >300 ms (marked first-degree AV block) with symptoms | **Discontinue** AV-nodal blocker immediately | Marked first-degree AV block can cause pacemaker syndrome-like symptoms; per 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS, permanent pacing is reasonable for marked first-degree AV block with symptoms (Class IIa, Level B) [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| Documented Mobitz II second-degree, high-grade, or third-degree AV block | **Discontinue** AV-nodal blocker and initiate pacemaker evaluation | Class I indication for permanent pacing regardless of symptoms [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| Symptomatic bradycardia (HR <50 with syncope, near-syncope, or hemodynamic compromise) | **Discontinue** AV-nodal blocker; assess for temporary pacing | Medical emergency requiring immediate evaluation [Third-Degree AV Block - StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545199/) |
| Drug-induced complete heart block with reversible cause identified | **Discontinue** offending agent; monitor for resolution | Reversible causes should be addressed before permanent pacing decision [2021 ESC Guidelines on Cardiac Pacing and CRT](https://inahfcarmet.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2021_ESC_Cardiac_Pacing_and_CRT.pdf) |

#### Criteria for Holding (Temporary Suspension) Pending Specialist Evaluation

| Scenario | Action | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Progressive PR prolongation (>20 ms increase over 4 weeks) with dropped beats not yet captured on telemetry | **Hold** AV-nodal blocker pending urgent electrophysiology consultation | Progressive conduction disease requires specialist evaluation before deciding on permanent therapy; holding medication allows assessment of intrinsic conduction [Management of patients with drug-induced atrioventricular block - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12919613) |
| Symptomatic first-degree AV block (PR 240–300 ms) where correlation between symptoms and bradycardia is uncertain | **Hold** medication and initiate extended ambulatory monitoring | Allows symptom-rhythm correlation before committing to permanent pacing [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628) |
| New onset of symptoms temporally related to beta-blocker initiation or dose increase | **Hold** medication and reassess within 72 hours | May represent drug effect rather than intrinsic disease [2025 Drug-Induced vs Drug-Revealed AV Block Review - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12246518) |

### 1.3 Application to This Specific Patient

This 68-year-old patient presents with:

- Heart rate: 52 bpm (mild bradycardia)

1. The patient has documented dropped beats (concerning for intermittent higher-grade block)
2. Symptoms are temporally related to metoprolol initiation (8 weeks ago)
3. The progression of PR prolongation is more rapid than typical age-related degeneration
4. The 2025 clinical review emphasizes that high doses of bradycardic drugs, drug combinations, and factors like hyperkalemia increase initial AVB recovery rates but do not prevent high relapse—emphasizing the need for specialist assessment before committing to a long-term medication plan [Clinical Significance and Management of AV Block Associated With Bradycardic Drug Therapy - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12246518)

**Exception Language:** If the patient has a compelling indication for continued beta-blocker therapy (post-myocardial infarction within 3 years, heart failure with reduced ejection fraction LVEF <40%, or atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response), the medication should be reduced to the lowest effective dose rather than completely discontinued, and the decision to continue should be made in conjunction with electrophysiology consultation. The 2026 meta-analysis of beta-blockers post-MI showed significant benefit only in patients with LVEF <50%; for patients with preserved LVEF, the benefit is attenuated [Meta-analysis of beta-blockers after MI - Medscape](https://www.medscape.com/). The ESC guidelines state beta-blockers are strongly recommended (Class I) for HFrEF (LVEF <40%) and post-MI with reduced LVEF [2021 ESC Heart Failure Guidelines - ESC](https://www.escardio.org/guidelines/clinical-practice-guidelines/heart-failure).

### 1.4 Alternative Antihypertensive Agents That Do Not Slow AV Nodal Conduction

When AV-nodal blockers are discontinued or dose-reduced, alternative antihypertensive therapy is needed. The following agents are safe in the setting of AV conduction disease because they do not slow AV nodal conduction [AV nodal blocking agents - Time of Care](https://www.timeofcare.com/av-nodal-blocking-agents).

#### Recommended First-Line Alternatives

| Agent Class | Specific Agent | Starting Dose | Maximum Dose | Key Evidence in AV Block Patients |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|--------------|-----------------------------------|
| Dihydropyridine CCB | **Amlodipine** | 2.5 mg daily | 10 mg daily | No significant AV nodal effect; safe in patients with conduction disease; ALLHAT data showed no significant association with conduction system disease incidence [ALLHAT Secondary Analysis - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/) |
| ACE Inhibitor | **Lisinopril** | 5 mg daily | 40 mg daily | **Decreased** incidence of conduction system disease compared to amlodipine and chlorthalidone (HR 0.81; 95% CI 0.69-0.95; p=0.01) per ALLHAT secondary analysis [ALLHAT Secondary Analysis - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/) |
| ARB | **Losartan** | 25 mg daily | 100 mg daily | Excellent tolerability; comparable BP lowering efficacy with fewer adverse effects than amlodipine and lisinopril [Losartan vs Amlodipine vs Lisinopril](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/) |
| Thiazide Diuretic | **Chlorthalidone** | 12.5 mg daily | 25 mg daily | Preferred thiazide-like diuretic; superior to HCTZ in preventing cardiovascular disease; no AV nodal effects [First-line antihypertensives - StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK/) |

**Dosing Recommendations for This Patient:**
- **Monotherapy option:** Amlodipine 2.5 mg daily, titrated to 5 mg daily after 2 weeks if BP remains >130/80 mmHg. Monitor for peripheral edema (7.5% incidence).
- **Combination option:** Amlodipine 2.5 mg + chlorthalidone 12.5 mg daily (more effective than monotherapy for BP control)
- **Alternative if ACE inhibitor preferred:** Lisinopril 5 mg daily, titrated to 10–20 mg daily. Monitor for cough (31.7% incidence) and check renal function and potassium in 2 weeks.
- **Alternative if ARB preferred:** Losartan 25 mg daily, titrated to 50 mg daily. Fewest adverse effects among the three agents studied.

**Important Dihydropyridine Calcium Channel Blocker Note:** Nifedipine, felodipine, and other dihydropyridine CCBs are also safe, but amlodipine has the best evidence base and longest half-life. **Non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem) must be avoided** as they have potent negative dromotropic effects similar to beta-blockers [AV nodal blocking agents - Time of Care](https://www.timeofcare.com/av-nodal-blocking-agents).

### 1.5 Specialist Oversight Requirement

Per the feedback, this report **does not recommend dose reduction or continued use of rate-controlling agents without documented specialist oversight**. The following statements apply:

- The decision to hold metoprolol should be made by the treating clinician, but the decision to **reinstitute** any rate-controlling agent (including reduced-dose metoprolol) must be made in consultation with electrophysiology or cardiology.
- If the patient requires ongoing beta-blocker therapy for a compelling indication (post-MI, HFrEF, AF), the electrophysiologist should make the final determination regarding which agent and dose to use, with consideration of pacemaker implantation if needed to allow continued beta-blocker therapy.
- All medication changes should be documented with explicit rationale, and the electronic health record should flag the patient for cardiology/EP contact within 72 hours of medication hold.

## 2. Ambulatory Monitoring: Highest Diagnostic Yield Modalities with 24–72 Hour Placement Urgency

### 2.1 Comparative Effectiveness: MCOT vs. Auto-Triggered Event Monitors vs. Patch Monitors

The selection of ambulatory monitoring modality for intermittent, unpredictable symptoms such as high-grade AV block is critical. The patient's symptom pattern (lightheadedness and fatigue occurring over 6 weeks, with 4 ED visits failing to capture the intermittent dropped beats) dictates the need for extended monitoring with **real-time or automatic detection capabilities** rather than patient-activated recording.

#### Direct Evidence from Randomized Controlled Trials

**Rothman et al. (2007)** — The landmark prospective, multicenter randomized clinical trial across 17 centers compared Mobile Cardiac Outpatient Telemetry (MCOT) against standard auto-triggered loop event monitoring (LOOP) for diagnosing cardiac arrhythmias in outpatients with syncope, presyncope, or severe palpitations who had non-diagnostic 24-hour Holter monitoring. **Key results from 266 completed cases** [The Diagnosis of Cardiac Arrhythmias: MCOT vs Standard Loop Event Monitoring - PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17318994):

- **Overall diagnosis achieved:** MCOT 88% vs. LOOP 75% (P = 0.008)
- **Syncope/presyncope subset diagnosis:** MCOT 89% vs. LOOP 69% (P = 0.008)
- **Clinically significant arrhythmic events detected:** MCOT 41% vs. LOOP 15% (P < 0.001)
- **Number needed to treat with MCOT to achieve one additional diagnosis:** 8 patients

The clinical superiority of MCOT stems from its continuous communication capability—every beat is analyzed using an algorithm in real time, whereas with loop event monitors, the arrhythmia causing symptoms has often ended before the patient can press the button to record the ECG. For patients with syncope or presyncope, this difference is particularly pronounced (89% vs. 69%) [CenterWatch - MCOT Trial](https://www.centerwatch.com/clinical-trials/listings/NCT00295204/evaluation-of-a-mobile-cardiac-outpatient-telemetry-system-versus-standard-loop-event-monitor).

#### Patch Monitors vs. MCOT: Human-Reviewed vs. Algorithm-Based Detection

**Willcox et al. (2021)** — This study directly compared continuous long-term electrocardiography (LT-ECG, human review of full disclosure recordings, such as Zio XT) against mobile cardiac telemetry (MCT, algorithm-based detection) in 50 patients simultaneously wearing both devices for an average of 10.3 days [Continuous ECG monitoring vs mobile telemetry - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8703156):

- **Arrhythmia detection rate:** LT-ECG 50% vs. MCT 24% (P = 0.018)
- **Clinically relevant arrhythmias detected:** LT-ECG 61 vs. MCT 19 (3× more)
- **This included better detection of AV block** specifically
- **P-wave detection:** Human interpretation was superior to algorithmic detection for identifying brief episodes of AV block with subtle P-wave changes

**Critical distinction:** Patch monitors (Zio XT, CAM Patch) with full disclosure human review detect 3x the arrhythmias that algorithm-based MCT devices detect, making them superior for capturing intermittent high-grade AV block. However, MCT offers real-time notification capabilities that patch monitors lack—a tradeoff between diagnostic yield and real-time safety alerting.

#### Zio XT Evidence Base (14-Day Continuous Monitoring)

**Barrett et al. (2013)** — Compared 24-hour Holter monitoring with 14-day Zio patch in 146 patients [Comparison of 24-hour Holter Monitoring with 14-Day Novel Adhesive Patch ECG Monitoring - PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24726060/):

- **Arrhythmia events detected:** Zio 96 vs. Holter 61 (P < 0.001)
- **Definitive diagnosis per physicians:** Zio 90% vs. Holter 64%
- **Patient comfort preference:** Zio chosen by 81% of patients

**CAMELOT Study (iRhythm, 2023)** — 287,789 Medicare patients [Large Real-World Evidence Analysis - Zio XT CAMELOT Study](https://investors.irhythmtech.com/news/news-details/2023/Large-Real-World-Evidence-Analysis-Demonstrates-that-Zio-XT-Is-Associated-With-the-Highest-Diagnostic-Yield-Lowest-Retesting-Rates-and-Fastest-Time-to-Clinical-Diagnosis-Compared-to-Other-Ambulatory-ECG-Monitors/default.aspx):

- **Diagnostic yield vs. Holter:** Adjusted OR 1.95
- **Lowest 180-day retesting rates** among all modalities
- **Fastest time to clinical diagnosis**
- **Lowest risk of ED visits and hospitalizations** within 180 days

**AVALON Study (iRhythm, HRS 2025)** — 428,707 commercially insured patients [iRhythm Presents New Real-World Data at HRS 2025 - AVALON Study](https://investors.irhythmtech.com/news/news-details/2025/iRhythm-Presents-New-Real-World-Data-on-Ambulatory-Cardiac-Monitoring-at-HRS-2025-Reinforcing-Clinical-Superiority-of-Zio-Long-Term-Continuous-Monitoring/default.aspx):

- Zio LTCM associated with **highest adjusted odds of new arrhythmia diagnosis** within 90 days (26.5%)
- **Lowest adjusted odds of retesting** within 180 days compared to all other LTCMs

#### Brady-TAVR Study (Specific to AV Block Detection)

The Brady-TAVR study used 14-day Zio XT monitoring to detect bradyarrhythmias in patients at risk for conduction disturbances after TAVR [Zio by iRhythm Shows Significantly Higher Documentation - DAIC](https://www.dicardiology.com/content/zio-irhythm-shows-significantly-higher-documentation-atrial-fibrillation-and-atrial-flutter):

- 10.1% of patients had LBBB detected on extended ambulatory ECG post-TAVR
- **Bradyarrhythmias occurred at various pre- and post-TAVR time points**, emphasizing the need for monitoring beyond the immediate periprocedural period
- Extended 14-day monitoring effectively detected bradyarrhythmias not captured by short-term monitoring

#### Auto-Triggered Event Monitors

Systematic reviews show auto-triggered devices detect arrhythmias in 72–80% of symptomatic patients, outperforming Holter monitors (33–35%) [Blue Shield of California Medical Policy - Ambulatory Event Monitors](https://www.blueshieldca.com/content/dam/bsca/en/provider/docs/medical-policies/Ambulatory-Event-Monitors-Cardiac-Outpatient-Telemetry.pdf). However, they still rely on patient activation and algorithmic triggers, which miss arrhythmias that do not meet preprogrammed detection criteria or occur when the patient is unable to activate the device (e.g., during sleep).

### 2.2 Timing of First Arrhythmia Detection: Rationale for 24–72 Hour Placement Urgency

**Turakhia et al. (PMC 4756401)** analyzed data from 122,815 patients wearing Zio Service (up to 14 days of continuous patch monitoring) to evaluate the timing of potentially high-risk arrhythmias [Incidence and timing of potentially high-risk arrhythmias - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4756401):

- **Sinus pause >3 seconds:** Found in 1.4% of patients; median time to detection often **beyond 48 hours**
- **High-grade heart block:** Found in 1.2% of patients; many cases **not detected within first 48 hours**
- **Atrial fibrillation pause >5 seconds:** Found in 0.4% of patients
- **Only about half of sustained VT episodes were detected within 24 hours**
- **Longer monitoring times significantly increased the yield**, with continued incremental detection beyond 7 days
- **At least 25% of patients wore the device for >13 days**, demonstrating feasibility of extended monitoring

**Key Finding for This Patient:** A significant percentage of potentially high-risk bradyarrhythmias are NOT identified within 48 hours of ambulatory ECG monitoring. This means that placing a monitor for 24–48 hours (as with a standard Holter or impatient observation unit admission) has a high likelihood of missing the intermittent high-grade AV block suspected in this patient.

**Urgency of 24–72 Hour Placement Window:**

The patient's risk profile mandates placement within 24–72 hours for the following reasons:

1. **Progressive conduction disease:** PR interval increased 40 ms over 3 weeks, indicating active progression. Further progression to Mobitz II or high-grade AV block could occur at any time, putting the patient at risk for syncope while driving or living alone [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628).

2. **Documented dropped beats:** The ECG showed dropped beats not captured on telemetry, confirming that intermittent high-grade block is already occurring. The frequency and duration of these episodes are unknown, posing unpredictable risk.

3. **Safety concerns:** The patient lives alone, drives daily, and has no local family support. A single episode of symptomatic high-grade AV block while driving could be catastrophic. The Canadian Cardiovascular Society guidelines classify this patient as requiring driving cessation until successful treatment [Canadian Cardiovascular Society Drive 2023 Guidelines](https://ccs.ca/guideline/drive-2023).

4. **Insurance and access barriers:** The 3-month cardiology wait and insurance prior authorization requirements necessitate early initiation of the authorization process. Delaying monitor placement by even a few days risks the patient experiencing a syncopal event before diagnostic information is obtained.

5. **Medication effect:** Metoprolol will be held (per Section 1), and the monitor must be in place to capture whether AV block persists after medication withdrawal. If high-grade block occurs after metoprolol is held, this confirms intrinsic conduction disease requiring pacemaker implantation. This "drug washout" monitoring period is time-sensitive.

### 2.3 Recommended Monitoring Strategy with Specific Timing

**First-Line Recommendation: 14-Day Patch Monitor with Full Disclosure Human Review (Zio XT or equivalent)**

- **CPT Codes:** 93245–93248 (greater than 7 days up to 15 days)
- **Placement urgency:** Within 24 hours of decision to monitor
- **Rationale:** Human review of full disclosure data detects 3× more clinically relevant arrhythmias than algorithm-based MCT (Willcox et al., 2021); highest diagnostic yield among external monitors (CAMELOT study, adjusted OR 1.95 vs. Holter); 14-day continuous recording maximizes capture of intermittent high-grade AV block that may not occur within the first 48 hours (Turakhia et al.)
- **Limitation:** Does not provide real-time notification; patient must be educated to seek emergency care if symptoms worsen

**Recommended Alternative: Mobile Cardiac Telemetry (MCOT)**

- **CPT Codes:** 93228 (professional), 93229 (technical)
- **Placement urgency:** Within 24–48 hours; if patch monitor cannot be obtained due to insurance barriers, escalate to MCOT
- **Rationale:** Real-time monitoring with automatic transmission allows immediate physician notification of detected arrhythmias; 88% diagnostic yield vs. 75% for loop event monitors (Rothman et al., 2007); 89% yield in syncope/presyncope subset; appropriate when safety concerns justify real-time alerting capability
- **Best for:** Patients at highest risk of syncope where real-time notification could prevent a catastrophic event

**Not Recommended: Standard 24–48 Hour Holter Monitor**

- **Rationale:** Inadequate for intermittent symptoms occurring less frequently than every 48 hours per Carelon Clinical Guidelines [Carelon Clinical Appropriateness Guidelines - Ambulatory Cardiac Rhythm Monitoring](https://guidelines.carelonmedicalbenefitsmanagement.com/ambulatory-cardiac-rhythm-monitoring-2024-07-01); 18% detection rate within first 48 hours vs. 48% with extended monitoring (EXAMINE-HCM study); demonstrated inferiority in head-to-head comparison with 14-day patch monitoring (Barrett et al., 2013)

**Escalation Pathway:**
1. **First:** 14-day patch monitor (highest diagnostic yield, non-invasive)
2. **If non-diagnostic but symptoms persist:** MCOT for 30 days (real-time notification, extended surveillance)
3. **If MCOT non-diagnostic:** Implantable Loop Recorder (ILR) for up to 3 years (highest sensitivity for very infrequent events)
4. **Emergency alternative:** Admit to hospital observation unit with continuous telemetry if monitor cannot be placed within 72 hours due to insurance delays, pending appeal

### 2.4 Authorization Pathway for 24–72 Hour Placement

Given the patient's access barriers (3-month cardiology wait, insurance requiring prior authorization for event monitors), the following expedited pathway is recommended:

1. **Same-day placement:** Contact local cardiology practice or cardiac monitoring company directly to arrange stat placement (many will place same-day if ordered by physician)
2. **If prior authorization required:** Submit with expedited review request citing safety concerns (patient lives alone, drives daily, progressive PR prolongation)
3. **Peer-to-peer review:** Request immediate peer-to-peer review if initial denial occurs; emphasize that each day without monitoring places patient at risk for syncope-related injury
4. **Two-Week Pending Appeal:** If all else fails, consider self-pay patch monitor (typically $100–300) while pursuing insurance appeal; this is cost-effective compared to one ED visit
5. **Observation unit admission:** If monitor cannot be placed within 72 hours by any other means, admit to hospital observation unit with continuous telemetry (CPT 99221–99223 observation care) pending monitor placement or specialist evaluation

## 3. Diagnostic Needs and Escalation Criteria

### 3.1 Echocardiography: Timing, Indications, and Structural Findings That Alter Management

#### Indication and Timing

The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline explicitly states: **"Echocardiography is recommended in patients with left bundle branch block or advanced AV block to assess for structural heart disease"** [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628). The guideline further notes that "the presence of left bundle branch block on electrocardiogram markedly increases the likelihood of underlying structural heart disease and of diagnosing left ventricular systolic dysfunction" [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline Made Simple](https://www.acc.org/-/media/Non-Clinical/Files-PDFs-Excel-MS-Word-etc/Guidelines/2018/Guidelines_Made_Simple_2018_Bradycardia.pdf).

**For this patient:** Echocardiography should be ordered **urgently (within 72 hours)** for the following reasons:

1. **Progressive conduction disease of new onset** (PR prolongation documented over 3 weeks) requires assessment of structural heart disease
2. **Age 68 years** — increased risk of age-related structural heart disease
3. **Potential infiltrative disease** — conduction abnormalities are a common presenting feature of cardiac sarcoidosis and transthyretin amyloidosis [Echocardiography in Infiltrative Cardiomyopathy - Circulation 2019](https://cursodeecocardiografia.com.br/pluginfile.php/5263/mod_resource/content/1/Echocardiography%20in%20Infiltrative%20Cardiomyopathy%20-%20Review%202019%20-%20Circulation.pdf)
4. **Assessment of LVEF** — determines whether metoprolol is indicated for HFrEF (exception criteria) and whether CRTD rather than standard pacemaker might be needed

#### Structural Findings That Alter Management

The echocardiogram should specifically evaluate for:

**Left Ventricular Systolic Dysfunction (LVEF <50%):**
- If LVEF <40%: Beta-blocker therapy is indicated (ESC Class I recommendation for HFrEF). In this scenario, the patient would need pacemaker evaluation to allow continued beta-blocker therapy rather than holding metoprolol [2021 ESC Heart Failure Guidelines](https://www.escardio.org/guidelines/clinical-practice-guidelines/heart-failure).
- If LVEF 36–50% with AV block requiring ventricular pacing >40%: Techniques providing more physiologic ventricular activation (CRT, His bundle pacing) are preferred over right ventricular pacing to prevent heart failure (2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Class I) [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628).
- If LVEF <35% with LBBB and QRS ≥150 ms: CRT is indicated (2021 ESC Class I) [2021 ESC Guidelines on Cardiac Pacing and CRT](https://inahfcarmet.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2021_ESC_Cardiac_Pacing_and_CRT.pdf).

**Infiltrative Cardiomyopathy (Red Flags Warranting Further Investigation):**

Cardiac Sarcoidosis:
- Thinning of the basal interventricular septum or LV free wall (specific echocardiographic finding)
- LVEF <50%
- Segmental wall motion abnormalities not matching coronary artery territories
- Regional longitudinal strain impairment on speckle tracking
- **Conduction abnormalities (including first-degree AV block and LBBB) are the most common presenting ECG findings** [Diagnosis and Management of Cardiac Sarcoidosis - AHA Scientific Statement](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001240)

Transthyretin Cardiac Amyloidosis (ATTR-CM):
- Increased LV wall thickness with low/decreased QRS voltage (OR 16.9 for ATTR-CM) [Most Predictive Red Flags for Cardiac Amyloidosis](https://pdf.journalagent.com/tkd/pdfs/TKDA_52_4_227_236.pdf)
- Relative apical longitudinal strain ratio ≥1.0 (93% sensitive, 82% specific) [Echocardiography in Infiltrative Cardiomyopathy - Circulation 2019](https://cursodeecocardiografia.com.br/pluginfile.php/5263/mod_resource/content/1/Echocardiography%20in%20Infiltrative%20Cardiomyopathy%20-%20Review%202019%20-%20Circulation.pdf)
- Granular sparkling of myocardium
- **Discordance between QRS voltage and LV wall thickness is the classic red flag**
- Median nerve carpal tunnel syndrome (may precede heart failure symptoms by 5–7 years) [Red Flags of Transthyretin Cardiac Amyloidosis - Pfizer](https://transthyretincardiacamyloidosis.pfizerpro.com/suspicion/red-flags)

**Valvular Heart Disease:**
- Aortic stenosis (especially in this 68-year-old male) can cause LVH, diastolic dysfunction, and associated conduction abnormalities
- Calcific aortic stenosis extending into the conduction system can cause AV block

**Recommendation:** Order a comprehensive transthoracic echocardiogram with strain imaging. If infiltrative disease is suspected, proceed with cardiac MRI and/or nuclear imaging (FDG-PET for sarcoidosis, PYP scan for ATTR amyloidosis). Given the prevalence of cardiac sarcoidosis (up to one-third of patients aged 18–60 with new-onset Mobitz II or third-degree AV block without structural heart disease are diagnosed with cardiac sarcoidosis), this diagnosis should be strongly considered [AV Block - Merck Manual Professional Edition](https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/cardiovascular-disorders/specific-cardiac-arrhythmias/atrioventricular-block).

### 3.2 Pause-Duration Thresholds for Emergent Referral

#### Distinction Between AV Block and Reflex Syncope

The pause-duration thresholds in the literature have different applications depending on the mechanism:

**For AV Block (This Patient's Primary Concern):**
- **Any documented high-grade AV block (≥2 consecutive non-conducted P waves):** Class I indication for permanent pacing regardless of symptoms — **emergent/urgent referral required** [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628)
- **Any Mobitz II second-degree AV block:** Class I indication for permanent pacing — **urgent referral required** [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628)
- **Symptomatic third-degree (complete) AV block:** Medical emergency — **immediate evaluation and temporary pacing while awaiting permanent pacemaker** [Third-Degree AV Block - StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545199/)

**For Sinus Node Dysfunction (SND):**
The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline explicitly states: **"In sinus node dysfunction, there is no established minimum heart rate or pause duration where permanent pacing is recommended. Establishing temporal correlation between symptoms and bradycardia is crucial"** [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline Made Simple](https://www.acc.org/-/media/Non-Clinical/Files-PDFs-Excel-MS-Word-etc/Guidelines/2018/Guidelines_Made_Simple_2018_Bradycardia.pdf).

The UCSF Hospital Handbook defines SND as "sinus rate <50 bpm and/or pause >3 seconds" [Bradyarrhythmia and Pacemaker Indications - UCSF Hospital Handbook](https://hospitalhandbook.ucsf.edu/21-bradyarrhythmia-and-pacemaker-indications/21-bradyarrhythmia-and-pacemaker-indications), but this is a diagnostic criterion, not an automatic pacing indication.

#### Specific Pause-Duration Thresholds for Pacemaker Referral

The 3-second vs. 6-second distinction is most clearly established for **reflex (vasovagal) syncope**, as presented in the Venice Arrhythmias 2026 gaps-in-guidelines analysis [Gaps in Pacemaker Guidelines - Venice Arrhythmias 2026 (M. Brignole)](https://www.venicearrhythmias.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/M-Brignole2.pdf):

**3-Second Pause (Symptomatic):**
- "Pacing is indicated when symptoms can clearly be attributed to bradycardia (Class I B)"
- **Dual-chamber pacing can be effective for patients ≥40 years old** with recurrent and unpredictable syncope who have a documented pause ≥3 seconds during clinical syncope (Class IIa B-R)
- **Clinical threshold:** Any pause ≥3 seconds associated with syncope, near-syncope, or pre-syncopal symptoms warrants pacemaker evaluation

**6-Second Pause (Asymptomatic or Reflex Syncope):**
- **Asymptomatic pauses >6 seconds** have diagnostic value equivalent to symptomatic pauses in reflex syncope
- Pathophysiological basis: "Only 0.7% of asystolic episodes of 3–6 seconds but 43% of episodes >6 seconds resulted in pre-syncopal or syncopal symptoms" (Menozzi et al., *Am J Cardiol* 1993)
- "Average pause at time of syncope recurrence was 9 seconds" (ISSUE-2, Brignole et al., *Europace* 2007)
- ISSUE-2 and ISSUE-3 studies show pacing reduces syncope recurrence by **57% at 2 years** among paced patients

#### Application to This Patient

| Finding | Action | Urgency |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Any high-grade AV block (≥2 consecutive dropped P waves) | **Emergent** EP/pacemaker consultation | Immediate (same-day) |
| Any Mobitz II second-degree AV block | **Urgent** EP/pacemaker consultation | Within 24 hours |
| Symptomatic third-degree AV block | **Emergent** temporary pacing + permanent pacemaker | Immediate (call 911) |
| Symptomatic pause >3 seconds (reflex syncope) | **Urgent** EP consultation | Within 1 week |
| Asymptomatic pause >6 seconds | **Elective** EP consultation (high probability of benefit from pacing if age ≥40 with reflex syncope phenotype) | Within 2 weeks |
| Asymptomatic pause 3–6 seconds | **Elective** monitoring and EP consultation if symptoms develop | Within 1 month |
| PR interval >300 ms with symptoms | **Urgent** EP consultation (Class IIa for pacing) | Within 1 week |

### 3.3 ECG Findings That Influence Urgency and Management Pathway

#### Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)

**Significance:**
- "The presence of left bundle branch block on electrocardiogram markedly increases the likelihood of underlying structural heart disease and of diagnosing left ventricular systolic dysfunction" [2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000628)
- "LBBB is associated with structural heart disorder and a higher risk of future cardiac events and mortality" [Bundle Branch Block and Fascicular Block - Merck Manual](https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/cardiovascular-disorders/specific-cardiac-arrhythmias/bundle-branch-block-and-fascicular-block)
- The Rotterdam Study found asymptomatic older individuals with LBBB had a **higher risk of developing heart failure and experiencing cardiovascular death** [Left Bundle Branch Block - StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482167)

**Management Implications in This Setting:**
1. **New LBBB + first-degree AV block:** Does NOT automatically indicate trifascicular block. The AHA/ACCF/HRS has listed "trifascicular block" as a forbidden term when used to describe bifascicular block with first-degree AV block, because "it is not possible to determine whether the conduction delay resulting in PR interval prolongation is occurring in the AV node or in the third remaining fascicle" [Trifascicular Block - LITFL ECG Library](https://litfl.com/trifascicular-block-ecg-library). However, this combination should prompt **urgent echocardiography** and **expedited EP referral**.

2. **LBBB + LVEF <35% + QRS ≥150 ms:** Class I indication for CRT [2021 ESC Guidelines on Cardiac Pacing and CRT](https://inahfcarmet.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2021_ESC_Cardiac_Pacing_and_CRT.pdf). In the context of suspected high-grade AV block, the patient may need a CRT-D device rather than a standard pacemaker.

3. **New LBBB + hemodynamic instability or chest pain:** STEMI equivalent — **immediate angiography, do not delay with echocardiography** [Rapid Diagnosis of STEMI Equivalent in Patients With Left Bundle Branch Block - AHA](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/JAHA.121.023275).

4. **LBBB-associated cardiomyopathy (LIC):** Potentially reversible form of cardiomyopathy; majority of patients have reverse remodeling after CRT by left bundle branch pacing [Left Bundle Branch Block-associated Cardiomyopathy - AER Journal](https://www.aerjournal.com/articles/left-bundle-branch-block-associated-cardiomyopathy-new-approach).

#### Alternating Bundle Branch Block (ABBB)

**This is a HIGH-URGENCY, potentially EMERGENT finding.**

- **Definition:** Beat-to-beat alternation between RBBB and LBBB
- **Class I indication for permanent pacemaker implantation** per current guidelines [A Rare Case of Alternating Bundle Branch Block and Atrioventricular Block - JACC](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jaccas.2025.104007)
- **Clinical case example from the literature:** A 67-year-old man with dizziness and alternating BBB with Mobitz I second-degree AV block required **emergency transvenous pacing** [Beat-to-Beat Alternating Bundle-Branch Block - ABC Cardiol](https://abccardiol.org/en/article/beat-to-beat-alternating-bundle-branch-block)
- In the setting of acute MI, presence of alternating BBB is an ominous sign and **indicates a need for temporary pacing even in the absence of symptoms** [Alternating Bundle Branch Block During Sinus Rhythm - SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2632463620960559)

**Management:** If alternating BBB is detected, the patient requires **immediate EP consultation and likely emergent temporary pacing followed by permanent pacemaker implantation**.

#### Bifascicular Block (RBBB + LAFB or RBBB + LPFB)

- **Prevalence:** 1–2% of adult population
- **Risk of progression to complete heart block:** 1% annually in asymptomatic individuals, up to 17% in symptomatic patients with syncope [6 Essential Steps for Effective Bifascicular Block Treatment - The Neural Cloud](https://www.theneuralcloud.com/post/6-essential-steps-for-effective-bifascicular-block-treatment)
- **Frequently linked to structural heart disease (50–80%)** and ischemic heart disease (40–60%)
- **Management:** No treatment required unless advanced AV block present; but if syncope occurs with bifascicular block, most would argue for pacemaker [Bifascicular and trifascicular block - Deranged Physiology](https://derangedphysiology.com/main/required-reading/cardiovascular-intensive-care/Chapter-518/bifascicular-and-trifascicular-block)

#### Trifascicular Block (True Definition)

- **True trifascicular block:** Conduction delay in all three fascicles below the AV node (RBBB, LAFB, LPFB), manifesting as bifascicular block **plus third-degree AV block**
- **Clinical trifascicular block (older terminology, now discouraged):** Bifascicular block plus first-degree AV block — this terminology is **no longer supported** by AHA/ACCF/HRS
- **Important:** There is **no difference in progression to complete heart block** between patients with bifascicular block alone versus bifascicular block with first-degree AV block (1% per year risk in both groups) [Trifascicular Block - LITFL ECG Library](https://litfl.com/trifascicular-block-ecg-library)

#### Summary of ECG Findings and Corresponding Urgency

| ECG Finding | Urgency Level | Required Action |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Alternating bundle branch block | **Emergent** | Temporary pacing + permanent pacemaker |
| New LBBB + chest pain / hemodynamic instability | **Emergent** | Immediate angiography (STEMI equivalent) |
| Mobitz II second-degree AV block | **Urgent (within 24h)** | EP consultation for permanent pacing |
| High-grade or third-degree AV block | **Emergent** | Temporary + permanent pacing |
| LBBB + first-degree AV block + symptoms | **Urgent (within 72h)** | Echo + EP consultation |
| Bifascicular block + syncope | **Urgent (within 1 week)** | EP consultation for pacing evaluation |
| Isolated first-degree AV block (asymptomatic) | **Routine** | Observation, monitor progression |
| LBBB + LVEF <35% | **Urgent** | CRT evaluation |

## Summary of Key Revisions and Improvements

This revised report addresses the four identified gaps as follows:

**Gap 1 — Medication Management:** Explicit criteria are now provided for holding versus discontinuing AV-nodal blockers based on PR interval duration, symptom status, and documented higher-degree block. Specific alternative antihypertensive agents (amlodipine, lisinopril, losartan, chlorthalidone) with exact dosing are recommended. Exception language for compelling beta-blocker indications (post-MI, HFrEF, AF) is included. The recommendation to hold metoprolol pending specialist oversight replaces the previous recommendation for dose reduction without EP consultation.

**Gap 2 — Ambulatory Monitoring:** MCOT and 14-day patch monitors are prioritized as first-line modalities, with specific evidence for their superiority over shorter-term options. Head-to-head comparison data (Rothman et al., Willcox et al., CAMELOT, AVALON) is provided. Explicit 24–72 hour placement urgency is justified by the Turakhia et al. data showing that many bradyarrhythmias are not detected within 48 hours, the patient's progressive conduction disease, and safety concerns related to driving and living alone.

**Gap 3 — Diagnostic Needs and Escalation Criteria:** Echocardiography is recommended urgently (within 72 hours) with specific findings to evaluate (LVEF, infiltrative disease, valvular disease). Pause-duration thresholds are clarified (3 seconds symptomatic, 6 seconds asymptomatic for reflex syncope; any high-grade AV block is Class I for pacing regardless of pause duration). ECG findings such as LBBB (structural disease marker), alternating BBB (emergent pacing indication), and bifascicular block (risk of progression) are discussed with corresponding urgency levels.

**Gap 4 — Guideline-Based Justifications:** All recommendations are explicitly tied to the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline, 2021 ESC Pacing and CRT Guidelines, 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guideline, and supporting evidence from major clinical trials (Rothman, Willcox, Turakhia, Osmonov, CAMELOT, AVALON, ISSUE-2/3) with specific class and level of evidence provided where available.
