# Legal Analysis: Promissory Estoppel Claim of Jordan Hayes v. Tech Company on Summary Judgment

## Executive Summary

This analysis applies Texas promissory estoppel law to determine whether software engineer Jordan Hayes can enforce a verbal promise of full remote work against a tech company on a motion for summary judgment. The central legal question is whether Hayes can overcome four significant hurdles: (1) the conflicting offer letter with an integration/merger clause; (2) the hiring manager's lack of actual authority; (3) Texas's strong at-will employment doctrine; and (4) the statute of frauds. While the claim faces substantial challenges under existing Texas law, several factual elements—including the June confirmation email, the company's acceptance of remote work through June without objection, and the disparate treatment of similarly situated employees—provide grounds to survive a motion for summary judgment and potentially prevail at trial.

---

## 1. Governing Law: Texas Promissory Estoppel

### 1.1 Elements Under Texas Law

Under Texas law, promissory estoppel requires the plaintiff to establish three elements: (1) a promise; (2) foreseeability of reliance thereon by the promisor; and (3) substantial reliance by the promisee to his detriment. [English v. Fischer, 660 S.W.2d 521 (Tex. 1983)][1]

The Texas Supreme Court in *Wheeler v. White*, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965), adopted the Restatement (First) of Contracts § 90 formulation: "A promise which the promisor should reasonably expect to induce action or forbearance of a definite and substantial character on the part of the promisee and which does induce such action or forbearance is binding if injustice can be avoided only by enforcement of the promise." [Wheeler v. White, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965)][2]

Some Texas formulations add a fourth element—that injustice can only be avoided by enforcement of the promise—drawn directly from the Restatement. The Texas Supreme Court in *Wheeler* defined the doctrine as "defensive in that it estops a promisor from denying the enforceability of the promise." [Wheeler v. White, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965)][2]

In *Frost Crushed Stone Company, Inc. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Inc.*, the Texas Supreme Court added that promissory estoppel requires "detrimental reliance which is reasonable and justified." The court also confirmed that promissory estoppel does not allow damages for anticipated profits, but does allow damages to restore the promisee to his original financial position. [Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Tex. 1995][3]

### 1.2 Damages Available

Under Texas law, damages for promissory estoppel are limited to reliance damages—the amount necessary to restore the plaintiff to the position they would have been in had they not relied on the promise. Benefit-of-the-bargain or expectation damages (including anticipated future earnings) are not recoverable. [Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Tex. 1995][3]

For Hayes, this means the $40,000 loss on the home sale, relocation costs, lease-signing expenses, and other out-of-pocket costs directly incurred in reliance on the promise would be recoverable—but not the anticipated value of the remote work arrangement itself or lost future wages.

## 2. Element (a): Whether the Verbal Promise Was "Clear and Definite"

### 2.1 The Promise Itself

The hiring manager's verbal promise during April 2024 negotiations that Hayes would have "full remote work" is the central promise at issue. Under Texas law, the promise must be "clear and definite" to support a promissory estoppel claim. [Texas Promissory Estoppel Cause of Action (TX), Practical Law][4]

The key question is whether this promise is rendered indefinite or ambiguous by the conflicting offer letter language.

### 2.2 The Conflicting Offer Letter and Merger Clause

The May 1, 2024 offer letter states "hybrid schedule per company policy" without defining remote frequency. It also includes a bolded integration/merger clause stating: "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations."

**Analysis**: The conflicting language in the offer letter creates a factual dispute about what was actually promised. However, the verbal promise of "full remote work" is specific and definite on its face—it states a clear term (no office attendance required) rather than an ambiguous statement. The conflict with the written offer letter does not make the verbal promise itself indefinite; it creates a dispute about which representation should be enforced.

### 2.3 The Confirmation Email as Evidence

The June 2024 confirmation email from the manager serves as critical written evidence of the verbal promise. Under Texas law, written documentation of a verbal promise can constitute evidence of a "clear and definite promise." In *Frost Crushed Stone*, the Texas Supreme Court specifically held that "even though no written contract was signed, the written price quote provided evidence of Frost's verbal promise." [Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Tex. 1995][3]

The email, sent from the manager's company email address using an official title, confirms the specific terms of the remote work arrangement. This is powerful evidence supporting the "clear and definite" element.

### 2.4 The Effect of the Merger Clause

Under Texas law as established in *Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential Insurance Co. of America*, 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011), a standard merger clause does not bar fraudulent inducement claims unless it contains a clear and unequivocal disclaimer of reliance. [Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential Ins. Co. of Am., 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011)][5]

The merger clause in this case states: "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations." This language disclaims the existence of prior representations, but it does not explicitly disclaim *reliance* on prior representations. Under *Italian Cowboy Partners*, this distinction is critical:

- **Standard merger clause** (disclaiming existence of other representations): Does not bar reliance-based claims
- **No-reliance clause** (explicitly disclaiming reliance on extra-contractual representations): May bar such claims

The clause here falls into the first category. It states that the written agreement supersedes prior verbal representations, but does not state that Hayes did not rely on those representations or waives any claim based on them. Under *Italian Cowboy Partners*, this is insufficient to bar a promissory estoppel claim. [Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential Ins. Co. of Am., 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011)][5]

**However**, the "contract bar" doctrine creates a separate problem. Under this doctrine, if a valid written contract covers the same subject matter as the alleged promise, promissory estoppel cannot apply because promissory estoppel only applies when the elements of a contract cannot be shown to exist. [Promissory Estoppel Cause of Action (TX), Practical Law][4] The Fifth Circuit applied this principle in *Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp.*, holding that where valid contracts exist with merger clauses, promissory estoppel claims based on prior or extraneous promises cannot override the terms of such contracts. [Clinton Growers v. Pilgrim's Pride Corp., 5th Cir. 2013][6]

In *Barkley v. Connally*, the Texas 7th Court of Appeals held that a merger clause in a Purchase and Sale Agreement barred a promissory estoppel claim, stating: "When parties have entered into a valid written integrated contract the parol evidence rule precludes enforcement of prior or contemporaneous agreements that address the same subject matter and are inconsistent with the written contract." [Barkley v. Connally, Tex. App.—Amarillo 2023][7]

**Application to Hayes**: The offer letter and the verbal promise both address the same subject matter—work location/attendance expectations. Under the contract bar doctrine, this could preclude the promissory estoppel claim because the written contract covers the same subject matter. However, the offer letter's statement ("hybrid schedule per company policy") is arguably indefinite and does not specify the frequency of remote work. If the offer letter is found to be ambiguous or incomplete on the specific issue of remote work frequency, the contract bar doctrine may not apply because the written contract does not actually cover the disputed term. This is a factual issue that would likely survive summary judgment.

### 2.5 Conclusion on Element (a)

The verbal promise was "clear and definite" in its terms. The confirmation email provides strong written evidence of the promise. The merger clause, standing alone, does not bar the claim under *Italian Cowboy Partners*. However, the contract bar doctrine presents a significant hurdle that creates a genuine issue of material fact—whether the written contract covers the same subject matter as the verbal promise.

## 3. Element (b): Whether Hayes's Reliance Was Reasonable

### 3.1 The Merger Clause and Reasonable Reliance

The merger clause stating "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations" is relevant to the reasonableness of Hayes's reliance. A reasonable employee reading this clause would be on notice that prior verbal promises may not be enforceable.

**Analysis**: Under Texas law, whether reliance is reasonable is a question of fact for the jury. [Texas Promissory Estoppel Cause of Action (TX), Practical Law][4] The merger clause creates a fact question: Was it reasonable for Hayes to rely on a verbal promise after signing a written agreement that explicitly supersedes prior verbal representations?

Several factors weigh in Hayes's favor:
- The merger clause does not explicitly disclaim reliance (under *Italian Cowboy Partners*)
- The verbal promise was made during negotiations to induce relocation—a significant life decision
- The confirmation email (sent after the offer letter) re-affirmed the promise, potentially creating a reasonable belief that the promise remained valid despite the written language

### 3.2 The Manager's Lack of Authority and Reasonable Reliance

Per HR records, the manager lacked authority to modify standard employment terms. This is a critical issue for the reasonableness of Hayes's reliance.

**Texas Agency Law Principles**: Under Texas law, an agent's authority to bind a principal can arise through: (1) actual authority, (2) apparent authority, (3) estoppel, (4) inherent power, and (5) ratification. [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8]

**Apparent Authority**: "Apparent authority is the power of an agent to act on behalf of a principal, even though not expressly or impliedly granted." [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8] It "arises when a third party reasonably believes that someone is an agent of the business" based on "the actions, or inactions, of the principal." [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8]

The "power of position" doctrine is directly relevant: "The manager of a company has the apparent authority to bind the company to contracts, regardless of whether he has actual authority." [Apparent Authority, EBSCO Research Starters][9]

**Application**: A hiring manager, by virtue of their position as a manager or department head conducting employment negotiations, has the apparent authority to make promises regarding terms and conditions of employment—including remote work arrangements. The key question is whether a reasonable job candidate would believe the hiring manager had authority to promise full remote work.

Factors supporting apparent authority:
- The manager held the position of "hiring manager" and conducted negotiations
- The manager used a company email address to send the confirmation email
- The company did not inform Hayes of any limits on the manager's authority
- The company allowed Hayes to work fully remote through June without objection (ratification)

The Restatement provides: "An agent who appears to have authority to make statements for his principal gives to his statements the weight of the principal's reputation." [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8]

**Critically**: "Even if the principal has expressly placed limitations on the agent's abilities, but these limitations are not known, then the agent will still have apparent authority to do those things." [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8] This means the employer's internal limits on the manager's authority do not negate apparent authority if Hayes was unaware of those limits.

### 3.3 Ratification by Conduct

Even if the manager lacked apparent authority, the company's conduct may have ratified the promise.

Under Texas law, ratification requires: (1) knowledge of all material facts regarding a prior, unauthorized act; and (2) conduct that demonstrates an intent to retroactively authorize the act or that is inconsistent with an intent to reject it. [BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen, Tex. 2021][10]

**Application**: The company allowed Hayes to work fully remote through June without objection. If the company knew (or should have known) that Hayes was working remotely based on the manager's promise, this acceptance of Hayes's labor without objection could constitute implied ratification.

In *BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen*, the Texas Supreme Court held that "acceptance of benefits alone may but does not necessarily imply ratification," applying a "totality of circumstances test." [BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen, Tex. 2021][10] The court specifically noted that "continued objection by a lessor post-payment can negate implied ratification." [BPX Operating Company v. Strickhausen, Tex. 2021][10]

**Application**: The company's failure to object to Hayes's remote work through June, combined with acceptance of Hayes's labor, creates a fact question on ratification. The company's later policy change (July 1) does not retroactively negate the prior ratification.

### 3.4 The Manager's Retirement

The manager's retirement in June 2024 does not affect the legal analysis. Liability attaches at the time the promise was made and at the time Hayes relied on it. The manager's subsequent retirement does not undo the legal effects of promises made while employed. The retirement could affect evidence availability, but the confirmation email provides documentary evidence independent of the manager's testimony.

### 3.5 Conclusion on Element (b)

Hayes's reliance raises substantial questions that are appropriately resolved by a jury, not on summary judgment. The apparent authority of the hiring manager, the company's ratification through acceptance of Hayes's remote work, and the fact that Hayes was unaware of the manager's internal authority limits all support a finding of reasonable reliance. However, the merger clause cuts against reasonableness and creates a genuine issue of material fact.

## 4. Element (c): Whether the Substantial Detriment Was Foreseeable

### 4.1 Foreseeability Analysis

Under Texas law, the promisor must have foreseen (or should have foreseen) that the promisee would rely on the promise to their detriment. [English v. Fischer, 660 S.W.2d 521 (Tex. 1983)][1]

**Application**: The hiring manager made the verbal promise of full remote work specifically to induce Hayes to relocate from Seattle to Austin. Relocation is a significant life event that inherently involves substantial financial commitments—selling a home, buying or leasing a new home, moving expenses, and potential loss on the home sale. The manager's explicit purpose in making the promise (to induce relocation) makes the resulting detriment highly foreseeable.

The manager should have foreseen:
- That Hayes would sell his Seattle home (potentially at a loss in a declining market or forced sale)
- That Hayes would enter into a lease in Austin
- That Hayes would incur moving expenses
- That Hayes would leave his prior employment and sever ties in Seattle

### 4.2 The Actual Detriment

Hayes sold his Seattle home at a $40,000 loss and signed a Texas lease on May 15, 2024. These are precisely the types of reliance costs that promissory estoppel is designed to remedy. In *Frost Crushed Stone*, the Texas Supreme Court awarded approximately $40,000 in reliance damages to restore the promisee to his original position. [Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Construction Co., Tex. 1995][3]

In *Hernandez v. UPS Supply Chain Solutions, Inc.*, a federal court applying Texas law granted summary judgment to an employee who relocated from Chicago to El Paso based on a job offer that was later withdrawn. The court found that "Plaintiff has satisfied his burden to show his reliance upon the job offer was reasonable, substantial, and detrimental," and awarded out-of-pocket moving expenses. [Hernandez v. UPS Supply Chain Solutions, Inc., W.D. Tex. 2007][11]

### 4.3 Conclusion on Element (c)

The detriment was clearly foreseeable. The manager made the promise for the express purpose of inducing relocation, and the resulting financial losses are the natural and expected consequences of such a relocation. This element is the strongest for Hayes.

## 5. Element (d): Whether Injustice Can Only Be Avoided by Enforcement of the Promise

### 5.1 The Justice Element Under Texas Law

The "justice element"—whether injustice can only be avoided by enforcement of the promise—is a question of law for the court, not a question of fact for the jury. [The Justice Element of Promissory Estoppel, St. John's Law Review][12] It requires the court to balance the equities between the parties.

Courts that address this element interpret it narrowly as "corrective justice," balancing loss between the promisor and promisee. [The Justice Element of Promissory Estoppel, St. John's Law Review][12] The element has received little attention from judges and is "often marginalized or overlooked by courts and scholarship, which focus primarily on promise and reliance." [The Justice Element of Promissory Estoppel, St. John's Law Review][12]

### 5.2 Factors Weighing in Hayes's Favor

**The Company's Policy Change**: The company updated its policy on July 1, 2024, requiring three office days weekly. A policy change that retroactively eliminates a promised benefit weighs in favor of enforcement, particularly when the change occurs after the employee has already relied on the original promise.

**Hayes's Prior Remote Work Without Objection**: Hayes worked fully remote through June without objection from the company. This acceptance of the arrangement for an entire month constitutes conduct consistent with the promise and inconsistent with the company's later position.

**Disparate Treatment of Similarly Situated Employees**: Two other engineers hired at the same time received written remote approvals. Under Texas law, "an employee may prove disparate treatment by showing that other employees were similarly situated for comparison purposes." [Hoffman v. Baylor Health Care System, N.D. Tex. 2014][13] The factors for determining whether employees are "similarly situated" include: (1) they shared the same supervisor; (2) were subject to the same standards; and (3) engaged in the same conduct without differentiating circumstances. [Hoffman v. Baylor Health Care System, N.D. Tex. 2014][13]

**Application**: The two other engineers hired simultaneously who received written remote approvals are directly comparable:
- They were hired at the same time
- They presumably had the same or similar roles
- They received the same benefit (remote work approval) but in written form

This disparate treatment raises an inference that Hayes was treated unfairly, strengthening the injustice element.

**The Financial Harm**: The $40,000 loss on the home sale, combined with relocation costs and lease obligations, constitutes substantial financial harm that would not have occurred but for the promise.

### 5.3 Factors Weighing Against Enforcement

**At-Will Employment**: Texas is an at-will employment state for over 135 years. [Texas At-Will Employment Doctrine][14] As one Texas appellate court held in *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc.*: "A promise to provide employment which is subject to termination at any time or for any reason [at-will] does not provide any assurances about the employer's future conduct, and does not provide a basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law." [Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc., Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1994][15]

However, *Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services* created a limited exception for pre-employment promises: "Geosource's undisputed oral promise clearly imposed a duty on Geosource to employ Roberts—but not for a fixed duration—and that duty was breached by Geosource." [Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services, Inc., Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1988][16]

The *Merricks v. Savers, Inc.* case (Massachusetts federal court, not Texas law but illustrative) addressed this distinction directly. The employer argued that because Merricks was an at-will employee, she was subject to termination at any time, but the court disagreed, "noting a pre-employment promise inducing acceptance differs from promises made during employment." [Merricks v. Savers, Inc., D. Mass. 2011][17]

**The Merger Clause**: The written offer letter's merger clause, even if not dispositive, weighs against enforcement because Hayes signed an agreement that disclaimed prior verbal representations.

**The Manager's Lack of Authority**: The fact that the manager lacked actual authority to modify standard terms weighs against enforcement, though this is mitigated by apparent authority and ratification arguments.

### 5.4 The Unauthorized Agent Problem and Promissory Estoppel

A critical tension exists in Texas law: The function of promissory estoppel is "defensive in that it estops a promisor from denying the enforceability of the promise." [Wheeler v. White, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965)][2] But if the manager lacked authority, can the company be bound as the "promisor"?

Under agency law, the answer depends on apparent authority and ratification. If the manager had apparent authority, the company is estopped from denying the promise. If the company ratified the promise through its conduct (accepting Hayes's remote work), the company is bound.

The Restatement provides: "Under general rules of agency law, principals are liable when their agents act with apparent authority." [Apparent Authority, LII / Legal Information Institute][8]

### 5.5 Conclusion on Element (d)

The injustice element is a close call that will depend on the court's assessment of equities. The company's policy change, Hayes's prior remote work without objection, and the disparate treatment of other employees all weigh heavily in favor of enforcement. However, Texas's strong at-will employment doctrine and the existence of the written merger clause weigh against enforcement. This element is appropriately decided by the court after a full factual record, making summary judgment inappropriate for the defendant.

## 6. Summary Judgment Standards

### 6.1 Standards Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a

**Traditional Motion for Summary Judgment (Rule 166a(c))**: The summary judgment movant has the burden to show there is no genuine issue of material fact and that the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. [Rule 166a, Tex. R. Civ. P.][18] If the movant bears the burden of proof at trial, it must conclusively establish every element of the claim. If the movant does not bear the burden of proof, it may negate at least one essential element. [Rule 166a, Tex. R. Civ. P.][18]

**No-Evidence Motion for Summary Judgment (Rule 166a(i))**: After adequate time for discovery, a party may move for summary judgment on the ground that there is no evidence of one or more essential elements of a claim. [No-Evidence Summary Judgment in Texas, Barsalou Law][19] The non-movant must produce "more than a scintilla" of evidence—evidence sufficient to allow reasonable minds to differ. [No-Evidence Summary Judgment in Texas, Barsalou Law][19]

### 6.2 Application to Hayes's Claim

**For a Defendant's Summary Judgment Motion**: The company would need to show either:
- There is no genuine issue of material fact on one or more elements, or
- There is no evidence to support one or more elements

**For Hayes's Summary Judgment Motion**: Hayes would need to conclusively prove all elements. This is a higher burden and likely more difficult given the factual disputes.

### 6.3 Evidence Sufficient to Survive Summary Judgment

Under *Hernandez v. UPS Supply Chain Solutions, Inc.*, the key precedent on relocation and promissory estoppel:

- The plaintiff accepted a job offer, relocated his family, terminated his prior employment, and incurred moving expenses in reliance on the promise
- The court found this constituted "substantial detrimental reliance" as a matter of law
- The court granted the plaintiff's motion for partial summary judgment and denied the employer's motion for summary judgment
- The court awarded out-of-pocket moving expenses [Hernandez v. UPS Supply Chain Solutions, Inc., W.D. Tex. 2007][11]

**Application**: Hayes has comparable evidence: relocation from Seattle to Austin, sale of a home at a $40,000 loss, signing a Texas lease, and moving expenses. This is at least "more than a scintilla" of evidence on detrimental reliance, sufficient to survive a no-evidence motion.

### 6.4 The Question of Law vs. Question of Fact

Under Texas law, the first three elements of promissory estoppel (promise, foreseeability, detrimental reliance) are questions of fact for the jury. [Texas Pattern Jury Charges - Business, Consumer, Insurance & Employment][20] The fourth element (injustice) is a question of law for the court. [The Justice Element of Promissory Estoppel, St. John's Law Review][12]

This means that summary judgment on the first three elements is appropriate only if there is no genuine issue of material fact. Given the factual disputes in this case—including what was promised, whether reliance was reasonable, and whether the manager had apparent authority—summary judgment for either party is unlikely on these elements.

The injustice element, being a question of law, is more susceptible to summary judgment. The court could rule on this element as a matter of law.

## 7. Comprehensive Analysis and Likely Outcome

### 7.1 Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths of Hayes's Case**:
- The verbal promise of "full remote work" was specific and definite
- The June confirmation email provides written evidence of the promise
- The hiring manager had apparent authority to make the promise
- The company ratified the promise by accepting Hayes's remote work through June without objection
- The $40,000 home sale loss and relocation costs constitute substantial, foreseeable detriment
- Two other engineers received written remote approvals (disparate treatment)
- The company's policy change occurred after Hayes had already relied on the promise

**Weaknesses of Hayes's Case**:
- The offer letter states "hybrid schedule per company policy" and contains a merger clause
- The manager lacked actual authority to modify standard employment terms
- Texas is a strong at-will employment state
- Texas courts have split on whether promissory estoppel applies in employment contexts
- The manager has since retired, potentially affecting evidence availability

### 7.2 Likely Outcome on Summary Judgment

**On the Company's Motion for Summary Judgment**: The company will likely fail to obtain summary judgment. There are genuine issues of material fact on all three factual elements:
- Whether the promise was clear and definite (conflicting evidence)
- Whether reliance was reasonable (merger clause vs. confirmation email)
- Whether the substantial detriment was foreseeable (clearly yes)
- Whether the injustice element is satisfied (question of law)

The court is unlikely to grant summary judgment for the company because factual disputes exist on the promise, the reasonableness of reliance, and the manager's authority.

**On Hayes's Motion for Summary Judgment**: Hayes is unlikely to prevail on his own motion for summary judgment. While the detrimental reliance element is strong, the conflicting offer letter and merger clause create genuine issues of material fact on the "clear promise" and "reasonable reliance" elements. The company also has valid defenses under the contract bar doctrine and the at-will employment doctrine that require factual development.

### 7.3 Most Likely Outcome

The most likely outcome is that the court denies both parties' motions for summary judgment, finding that genuine issues of material fact exist on the key elements. The case would proceed to trial, where a jury would determine:
- Whether the verbal promise was made and was sufficiently definite
- Whether Hayes's reliance was reasonable
- Whether the detriment was foreseeable
- The extent of Hayes's damages

The court would decide the injustice element as a matter of law based on the jury's factual findings.

### 7.4 Strategic Considerations

**For Hayes**:
- Emphasize the confirmation email as written evidence of a clear promise
- Emphasize the company's acceptance of remote work through June (ratification)
- Emphasize the disparate treatment of other engineers
- Document all out-of-pocket costs (home sale loss, moving expenses, lease costs)
- Consider whether to plead in the alternative for fraud or negligent misrepresentation based on the same facts

**For the Company**:
- Emphasize the merger clause and offer letter language
- Emphasize the manager's lack of actual authority
- Emphasize Texas's at-will employment doctrine
- Argue that the contract bar doctrine precludes the claim
- Seek to limit damages to only documented out-of-pocket expenses

## 8. Additional Considerations and Alternative Jurisdictions

### 8.1 Statute of Frauds Analysis

Texas Business and Commerce Code § 26.01(b)(6) requires agreements "not to be performed within one year" to be in writing. [Statute of Frauds in Texas][21]

**Application**: The promise of full remote work is not for a fixed duration. It is an arrangement that could theoretically be performed within one year (the company could change its policy, the employee could quit, etc.). Therefore, the statute of frauds likely does not bar this claim. However, if the promise is interpreted as an indefinite promise of remote work, the company could argue it falls within the statute.

### 8.2 Alternative Jurisdictions

The facts suggest Texas law applies given the Austin relocation and the company's operations in Texas. However, if Hayes is suing in federal court (diversity jurisdiction) or if the company is headquartered elsewhere, different state law may apply.

**Non-Texas Cases on Point**:

In *Decker v. HSBC Bank USA* (8th Circuit, Minnesota law), the court found that a promise to "work remotely from Minnesota for at least a portion of the time" was "clear and definite enough to create continuing obligations" and denied the motion to dismiss. [Decker v. HSBC Bank USA, 8th Cir.][22]

In *Merricks v. Savers, Inc.* (Massachusetts federal court), the employee was allowed to pursue a promissory estoppel claim based on a verbal pre-employment promise regarding leave time. The court found the promise sufficiently unambiguous and rejected the argument that at-will status made reliance unreasonable as a matter of law. [Merricks v. Savers, Inc., D. Mass. 2011][17]

These non-Texas cases demonstrate that courts in other jurisdictions are receptive to promissory estoppel claims based on pre-employment promises, particularly where the employee relocated in reliance on the promise.

### 8.3 The Roberts v. Collins Split in Texas

Texas appellate courts are split on whether promissory estoppel applies in the employment context:

- **Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services** (1st Dist.): Allowed promissory estoppel for pre-employment promises, holding that an employer's "undisputed oral promise clearly imposed a duty on Geosource to employ Roberts—but not for a fixed duration." [Roberts v. Geosource Drilling Services, Inc., Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 1988][16]

- **Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management** (14th Dist.): Rejected the *Roberts* approach, holding that "A promise to provide employment which is subject to termination at any time or for any reason [at-will] does not provide any assurances about the employer's future conduct, and does not provide a basis for detrimental reliance as a matter of law." [Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Management, Inc., Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1994][15]

- **Robert J. Patterson, P.C. v. Leal** (3rd Dist./Austin): "We hold that, in the context of employer and employee relations, no claim exists under Texas law for promissory estoppel when the underlying evidence consists of purely oral representations as to employment for an indefinite period." [Robert J. Patterson, P.C. v. Leal, Tex. App.—Austin 1997][23]

**Application**: The venue of this case is critical. If filed in Austin (Travis County), the *Patterson v. Leal* precedent would likely govern and bar the claim. If filed in Houston (Harris County), the split between *Roberts* (1st Dist.) and *Collins* (14th Dist.) would leave the outcome uncertain. Federal court sitting in diversity would apply Texas law and would need to predict how the Texas Supreme Court would rule.

5. Consider settlement to avoid the risk of a jury verdict on the equities
