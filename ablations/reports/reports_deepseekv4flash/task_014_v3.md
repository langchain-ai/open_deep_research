# Comprehensive Investment-Grade Analysis of NCD IPOs Open on December 8, 2025 for Retiree Fixed-Income Portfolio

**Date of Analysis Base: December 8, 2025 | Target Investor: Retiree Seeking Fixed Income**

---

## Executive Summary

This report provides a rigorously revised, investment-grade analysis of Non-Convertible Debenture (NCD) Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) open for subscription as of **December 8, 2025**, evaluated specifically for a retiree seeking fixed income. The revision addresses identified gaps in quantitative tax analysis, portfolio construction frameworks, and security enforcement analysis while retaining all valid content from the prior report.

On December 8, 2025, **four NCD public issues were open or forthcoming**: Edelweiss Financial Services Limited (CRISIL A+/Stable), Muthoot Mercantile Limited (IND BBB/Stable), KLM Axiva Finvest Limited (ACUITE BBB/IND BBB-/Stable), and Prachay Capital Limited (CRISIL BBB-/Stable, opening December 11, 2025).

### Critical Findings for Retirees

1. **Section 80TTB Does NOT Apply to NCD Interest**: Interest from NCDs is explicitly excluded from the ₹50,000 deduction available to senior citizens. This makes post-tax yields lower for NCDs than for bank FDs or SCSS for the first ₹50,000 of interest income.

2. **Post-Tax Yield Erosion is Severe**: For a retiree in the 30% tax bracket (31.2% effective after cess), a 10.10% NCD coupon yields only **6.95%** post-tax. A 13.00% Prachay Capital coupon yields **8.94%** post-tax.

3. **"Secured" Status Has Practical Limitations**: Historical recovery data for secured debenture holders in Indian defaults shows recoveries ranging from 43% to 100% depending on the case, with practical enforcement timelines of 2-6+ years.

4. **Portfolio Construction Requires Explicit Allocation**: A retiree should limit NCD exposure to **20-25%** of fixed-income portfolio, with the remainder in safer instruments. Tenor laddering should align with the current RBI repo rate of **5.25%** and the falling rate cycle.

## 1. Exact Quantitative Detail with Multiple Tax Scenarios

### 1.1 Tax Framework for NCD Interest (FY 2025-26)

#### Classification Under Income Tax Act

Interest received on Non-Convertible Debentures is taxable as **"Income from Other Sources" under Section 56(2)** of the Income Tax Act, 1961 [1]. This classification applies uniformly to both listed and unlisted NCDs.

#### TDS Applicability Under Section 193

- **Standard TDS Rate**: 10% on interest exceeding **₹10,000** per financial year (threshold established by Finance Act 2025) [1][2]
- **No PAN**: TDS at 20% if PAN not furnished [3]
- **Form 15G/15H**: Retirees with total income below taxable limit can submit these forms to avoid TDS [2]
- **Listed vs. Unlisted**: Finance Act 2023 removed the exemption for TDS on listed debentures held in Demat form, making TDS mandatory for all NCD holdings from FY 2023-24 onwards [1]

#### CRITICAL: Section 80TTB Clarification

**NCD interest does NOT qualify for Section 80TTB deduction.** Multiple authoritative sources explicitly confirm this exclusion:

- "Senior citizens **cannot claim** this deduction for interest earned on investments like **company fixed deposits, non-convertible debentures (NCDs), or bonds**" [4]
- "Interest from **corporate bonds** and instruments like PPF/NSC is **not eligible** under 80TTB as these are either exempt or covered elsewhere" [5]
- Section 80TTB applies exclusively to interest income from **deposits with banks, post offices, and cooperative societies** — corporate deposits, bonds, and NCDs are explicitly excluded [4][5][6]

This means a senior citizen with ₹1,00,000 in NCD interest and ₹50,000 in bank FD interest can claim 80TTB deduction only on the bank FD interest (up to ₹50,000), not on the NCD interest.

#### Effective Tax Rate Calculation Including Cess

**Formula**: Effective Tax Rate = Slab Rate × 1.04 (Health & Education Cess at 4%)

**Worked Examples for a Retiree in 30% Slab**:

- Step 1: Income Tax Rate = 30%
- Step 2: Cess = 4% of 30% = 1.2%
- Step 3: Total Tax Incidence = 31.2%
- Step 4: Post-tax retention = 68.8%
- Step 5: For 10.10% coupon → 10.10% × 0.688 = **6.95%**

### 1.4 Worked Example: Full Tax Calculation for a Retiree Investor

**Scenario**: Retiree aged 68, old tax regime, total income ₹12,00,000 including:
- Bank FD interest: ₹60,000
- Edelweiss NCD interest (Series IX): ₹1,01,000 (10 NCDs of ₹10,100 coupon)

**Step 1**: Gross Total Income = ₹12,00,000 + ₹60,000 + ₹1,01,000 = ₹13,61,000

**Step 2**: Section 80TTB Deduction (on bank FD interest only, up to ₹50,000) = ₹50,000
- NCD interest of ₹1,01,000 is **not eligible**

**Step 3**: Taxable Income = ₹13,61,000 - ₹50,000 = ₹13,11,000

**Step 4**: Tax Calculation (Old Regime, 60-79 years):
- Up to ₹3,00,000: Nil
- ₹3,00,001 – ₹5,00,000: ₹10,000 (5% of ₹2,00,000)
- ₹5,00,001 – ₹10,00,000: ₹1,00,000 (20% of ₹5,00,000)
- ₹10,00,001 – ₹13,11,000: ₹93,300 (30% of ₹3,11,000)
- Total Tax: ₹2,03,300
- Add Cess 4%: ₹8,132
- **Total Tax Liability: ₹2,11,432**

**Step 5**: Post-tax NCD yield:
- Tax attributable to NCD interest = ₹1,01,000 × 31.2% = ₹31,512
- Post-tax NCD income = ₹1,01,000 - ₹31,512 = **₹69,488**
- Post-tax yield = 6.95% (as calculated above)

## 5. Duration and Modified Duration Calculations

### 5.1 Methodology

All calculations assume bond priced at par (face value ₹1,000) with yield-to-maturity equal to coupon rate. Effective annual yield for monthly-pay bonds: (1 + r/n)^n - 1.

**Macaulay Duration Formula (Annual Payments)**:
MacD = [Σ (t × C)/(1+y)^t + (n × F)/(1+y)^n] / Current Bond Price

**Modified Duration**:
ModD = MacD / (1 + y)

## 8. Real-World Security Enforcement and Recovery Analysis

### 8.1 The Legal Framework: What "Secured" Actually Means

All four NCD issues in this analysis are **secured, redeemable, non-convertible debentures** with a **first-ranking pari passu charge** over identified assets of the issuer [14][24][29][38].

**Key Legal Concepts**:

- **"First Ranking Pari Passu Charge"**: Multiple lenders hold equal (pari passu) rights over the same assets, and within that class, all rank equally at the first level of priority. This means NCD holders share proportionally with other secured creditors holding the same ranking [52].

- **Fixed vs. Floating Charge**: A fixed charge is attached to specific, identifiable assets (e.g., real estate, equipment). A floating charge covers a company's general, fluctuating assets (e.g., book debts, receivables, inventory). Fixed charges have higher priority during liquidation [52].

- **Registration with ROC**: Under Section 77 of the Companies Act, 2013, a charge must be registered with the Registrar of Companies (ROC) within **30 days** of creation. Non-registration renders the charge **void against the liquidator and other creditors** — meaning the "secured" status becomes meaningless in bankruptcy [53][54].

**Practical Implications for Investors**:
1. A "secured" label in the prospectus is only as good as the charge registration and perfection
2. A pari passu charge means sharing with other creditors — not exclusive rights
3. The actual assets backing the security may be co-mingled with those securing other borrowings

### 8.2 Collateral Realization Process and Timelines

**Under SARFAESI Act, 2002** [55][56]:
- Enforceable after **90 days of default** (demand notice under Section 13(2))
- Borrower has **60 days** to object under Section 13(3A)
- Creditor may take possession and proceed with sale
- Entire process: **6-12 months** for straightforward cases

**For Listed Debt Securities**: The Enforcement of Security Interest and Recovery of Debt Laws (Amendment) Act, 2016 extends SARFAESI remedies to debenture trustees for listed debt securities without requiring NPA classification — the trustee can enforce after a **90-day notice of default** [56].

**Under IBC (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code)** [57][58]:
- A moratorium is imposed under Section 14(1)(c) once CIRP (Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process) begins — secured creditors **cannot enforce** security interests during this period
- CIRP must be completed within **180 days**, extendable by another 90 days (total 270 days)
- In practice, IBC resolutions take **2-4 years** in complex cases

**Practical Timeline from Default to Recovery** [59]:

| Stage | Typical Timeline |
|---|---|
| Default event | Day 0 |
| 90-day demand notice (SARFAESI) | Day 90 |
| Filing with NCLT / DRT | 3-6 months |
| CIRP admission / proceedings | 6-12 months |
| Resolution plan approval | 12-24 months |
| Actual recovery disbursement | 24-48 months+ |

### 8.3 Asset Valuation Haircuts

Empirical evidence from Indian defaults:

**CRISIL Recovery Risk Ratings** (for Security Receipts) [60]:
- R1+: Recovery over 150% of face value
- R2: Recovery significantly above par
- R3: Moderate recovery
- R4: Below 50% recovery
- R5: Up to 25% recovery

**ARC Cumulative Recovery Rate**: Estimated at **65-70%** in fiscal 2025, projected to touch **75-80%** in fiscal 2026 [60]

**Restructuring Recovery**: Enables up to **85% debt recovery over eight years** — higher than settlement or asset sales [60]

**JM Financial ARC**: Overall SR redemption ratio at **50%** as of December 31, 2025, with acquisitions at a high discount averaging **69%** [61]

**Senior Secured Bonds (Global Benchmark)**: Mean recovery of **57.6%** (S&P Global data) [62]

### 8.4 Pari-Passu Recovery Implications

When multiple creditors hold pari passu charges over the same assets, recoveries are shared **proportionally** based on outstanding claim amounts [52][57].

**Example**: If two sets of debenture holders (Series A: ₹100 crore, Series B: ₹50 crore) share a pari passu charge over assets worth ₹90 crore on forced sale:
- Total claims: ₹150 crore
- Recovery ratio: 60% (₹90 crore / ₹150 crore)
- Series A recovers: ₹60 crore (60% of ₹100 crore)
- Series B recovers: ₹30 crore (60% of ₹50 crore)

**CRITICAL**: If the new NCD investors are given a **second charge** (ranking behind existing secured creditors), they recover **only after** all first charge holders are fully satisfied. In the example above, if first charge holders hold ₹120 crore in claims and assets are worth ₹90 crore, second charge holders receive **zero**.

### 8.5 Historical Recovery Rates for Secured Debentures in India

**DHFL (Dewan Housing Finance)** [63][64]:
- Default: June 2019
- Resolution: June 2021 (Piramal Capital)
- Recovery for **Secured NCD Holders (Assenting Creditors)**:
  - Category 1 (up to ₹2 lakh): **100%** in cash
  - Category 4 (above ₹10 lakh): Mix of cash (~₹12,700 crore upfront) and Piramal debentures (₹19,550 crore) with 10-year maturity at 6.75% coupon
  - **Overall: 43.29% to 100%** of admitted claims depending on investment tier
- **Dissenting Creditors (Unsecured NCDs)**: Only **1.25%** of admitted claims in cash
- Timeline: ~2 years from default to resolution

**IL&FS Group** [65][66]:
- Default: September 2018
- Total External Debt: ₹99,355 crore
- Recovery as of March 2025: **₹45,281 crore** (~**46%** of total debt)
- Target recovery: ~**61%** (₹61,000 crore)
- Timeline: Still ongoing after **6.5 years**
- 197 out of 302 entities resolved as of March 2025

**Reliance Capital** [67][68]:
- Default: November 2021 (RBI superseded board)
- Total Debt: ~₹40,000 crore
- Resolution Plan: Hinduja Group's IIHL at **₹9,661 crore** (~24% recovery)
- Timeline: April 2023 (winner declared), still ongoing as of March 2025
- Debenture holders expect **100% of their dues** per promoter statements, though this is disputed

**SREI Infrastructure Finance** [69][70]:
- Default: October 2021 (RBI superseded board)
- Debenture holder base: Over 1 lakh investors
- Catalyst Trusteeship Ltd. acts as Debenture Trustee, voting on behalf of all debenture holders
- Ongoing resolution with no final recovery data available

### 8.6 Key Takeaways for Retiree Investors

1. **"Secured" is not a guarantee of full recovery**: Historical evidence shows secured debenture holders recover 43-100% depending on the case, with significant haircuts common.

2. **Timelines can extend to 6+ years**: The IL&FS case demonstrates that recovery can take well over 6 years, which is problematic for retirees needing current income.

3. **First-ranking pari passu is better than second charge**: Always verify the charge ranking — first-ranking pari passu with existing creditors provides proportional recovery, while second charge may yield zero in asset shortfalls.

4. **Charge registration is critical**: Verify that the charge is registered with ROC. Unregistered charges are void against the liquidator.

5. **Credit rating matters for recovery probability**: Higher-rated issuers (A+ vs. BBB-) have significantly lower default probabilities (0.10% vs. 0.40% for 1-year) [44].
