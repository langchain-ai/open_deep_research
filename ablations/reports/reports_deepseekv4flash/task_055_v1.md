# Primary Legal Sources and Scholarly Context for a Historical Novel Set in the 1730s British Empire

## 1. Statutory Language from the Molasses Act (1733) and Colonial Slave Laws (1730–1739)

### The Molasses Act of 1733 (6 Geo. II, c. 13)

The Molasses Act, officially titled the "Trade of Sugar Colonies Act 1732" (though enacted in 1733), contains **no mention whatsoever of enslaved people, slavery, or the slave trade**. It is purely a trade regulation and taxation statute. The full text is available at [The Statutes Project](https://statutes.org.uk/site/the-statutes/eighteenth-century/1733-6-george-2-c-13-the-molasses-act).

**Key statutory language:**

- **Preamble:** "Whereas the Welfare and Prosperity of Your Majesty's Sugar Colonies in America are of the greatest Consequence and Importance to the Trade, Navigation, and Strength of this Kingdom."

- **Duties imposed:** "upon all Rum or spirits ... of any of the Colonies or Plantations in America, not in the Possession or under the Dominion of His Majesty ... the Sum of Nine pence ... for every Gallon thereof" and "upon all Molasses or Syrups of such Foreign Produce or Manufacture ... the Sum of Six pence of like Money, for every Gallon thereof." Five shillings per hundredweight was imposed on sugar and paneles from foreign colonies.

- **Penalties:** "All such Goods ... landed or put on Shore ... without due Entry or Payment of the Duties ... shall be forfeited." Any person who "shall hinder, molest, or resist, any Custom-House Officer ... in the due Execution of his or their Duty ... shall forfeit and pay the Sum of Fifty Pounds and be imprisoned for Three Months without Bail."

The Act is integrally connected to the slave economy, however, because it protected British West Indian sugar planters who relied entirely on enslaved African labor. As historian John C. Miller noted, the Act "threatened New England with ruin, struck a blow at the economic foundations of the Middle colonies, and at the same time opened the way for the British West Indians—whom the continental colonists regarded as their worst enemies—to wax rich at the expense of their fellow subjects on the mainland." The molasses trade was a key component of the Triangular Trade (Europe-Africa-Americas), which included the slave trade. The most accessible full text is at [The Statutes Project](https://statutes.org.uk/site/the-statutes/eighteenth-century/1733-6-george-2-c-13-the-molasses-act).

### Barbados Slave Code (1661 – The Foundational Code in Force During the 1730s)

Since Barbados did not enact a new comprehensive slave code in the 1730s, the 1661 code—reenacted in 1676, 1682, and 1688—remained in force. This code contains explicit language classifying enslaved people as chattel property. It is available at [Slavery, Law, and Power: Barbados Slave Code](https://slaverylawpower.org/nhprc-sample-documents/barbados-slave-code) and [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom: Barbados 1661](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/laws/barbados-1661).

**Exact statutory language classifying enslaved people as property:**

- **Preamble:** The code described Black people as "an heathenish, brutish and an uncertaine, dangerous kind of people."
- **Purpose clause:** The law's purpose was stated as being to "protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels," establishing that Black slaves would be treated as chattel property in the island's court.
- This was the first comprehensive slave act in an English colony, explicitly treating Black slaves as chattel property. As historian Jerome S. Handler notes, the code's three defining features of slave status were "chattel property, lifetime (or permanent) servitude, and inheritance of slave condition from an enslaved mother"—though Handler argues that "none of the features was ever defined in any law; rather, they were implicit in any Barbados law that mentioned slaves."

**Key punishment and control provisions:**

- **Violence by slaves:** "If any Negro or slave whatsoever shall offer any violence to any Christian ... for his or her first offence be severely whipped ... second offence ... his nose slit and be burned in some part of his face ... next offence ... punished by death."
- **Killing slaves:** If a master killed his own slave, no penalty beyond forfeiture to the public treasury. If he killed another's slave, he "shall pay to the Owner of the Negro, double the Value, and into the Publick Treasury."
- **Runaways:** "All persons which shall hereafter take up any runaway or fugitive Slaves ... shall receive one pound sterling."
- **Movement restrictions:** "No Master ... shall give their negroes leave on sabbath days ... except ... with a ticket specifying the time of coming and returning."
- **The code allowed owners to punish slaves severely, including mutilation and burning alive, without fear of reprisal.** It authorized summary trials by justices and freeholders for crimes committed by enslaved people, bypassing jury trials.

### Jamaica – Slave Laws in the 1730s

**No specific "1733 Jamaican Consolidated Slave Act" exists.** The research identified that the Jamaican legislature in 1733 debated a case concerning racial classification ("on the difference between being white and being a mulatto"), but no comprehensive consolidated slave code from that year. However, the following acts were in force:

**The 1664 Jamaica Slave Code** (available at [Slavery, Law, and Power: Jamaica Slave Code](https://slaverylawpower.org/nhprc-sample-documents/jamaica-slave-code-governing)) was based on the Barbados model and enacted under Governor Thomas Modyford. It does not contain an explicit statutory declaration that enslaved people are "chattels" in the same manner as Barbados or South Carolina. Instead, it regulates governance and punishment without explicitly defining legal status as property in the text itself.

**Key provisions from the 1664 code:**

- **Punishment for violence against Christians:** "If any Slaves either Man or Woman shall offer any violence to any christian ... for his or her first offence ... severely whipped ... for his second offense ... burned in some part of his face."
- **Rebellion:** "If any Negro shall make Insurrection or rise in rebellion ... the Governor ... shall appoint a field officer ... to proceed by Marshall Law against the Actors ... and punish by Death or some other paines as their crimes shall deserve."
- **Movement:** "No Master or Mistress ... shall give their Slaves leave on Sundays, holydays or in the night time to go out of their Plantations without a ticket."
- **Weapon searches:** "all Overseers of plantations shall twice in every week search the slave houses for weapons."
- **Annual reading:** "this act be read & published in all respective parish Churches ... the first Sunday in March & the first Sunday in September in every year."

**The 1696 Jamaican Slave Code** designated enslaved individuals as real estate, integrating them into economic transactions—meaning slaves were classified as real property (like land) rather than just personal chattel. Jamaican historian Edward Long stated that the colonial slave codes drew from English villeinage laws, which bore "so near an affinity" to slave status.

**The 1717 Act: "An Act for the more effectual punishing of Crimes committed by Slaves"** (available at [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom: Jamaica 1717](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/laws/jamaica-1717)) contains critical provisions that would have been in effect during the 1730s:

- **Compensation for executed slaves:** "In all Cases where any Slave shall be put upon his Trial ... and shall suffer Death ... such Person shall ... certify the same ... and ... pay to the Owner ... the full Value ... such Slave or Slaves ... killed ... shall receive the full Value" – capped at forty pounds.
- **Restriction on master's power to dismember:** "No Slave shall be dismembered at the Will and Pleasure of his Master ... under the Penalty of One hundred Pounds." This was a critical distinction—the colonial assembly restricted private masters from dismembering slaves, reserving that power to the state.
- **Dismemberment for runaways:** "If any Slave ... shall run away ... and continue absent ... for the Space of Thirty Days ... such Slave to be punished, by cutting off one of the Feet ... or such other corporal Punishment."
- **Restrictions on assembly and drums:** "No Proprietor, Attorney, or Overseer ... suffer any Number of strange Negroes, exceeding Five, to assemble ... nor ... suffer any beating on Drums."

### South Carolina – The Negro Act of 1740

The **1740 South Carolina Negro Act** (available at [Laws of Enslavement and Freedom: South Carolina 1740](https://slaveryandfreedomlaws.lib.unb.ca/laws/south-carolina-1740) and [SC Pronet full text PDF](https://www.scpronet.com/modjeskaschool/wp-content/uploads/2016/03/9-an-act-for-better-ordering-slavecodes1740.pdf)) contains the **most explicit and thorough statutory language classifying enslaved people as property** in any of the colonies examined. It was passed in direct response to the Stono Rebellion of September 1739.

**Exact statutory language classifying enslaved people as chattels personal:**

> "All negroes, Indians (free Indians in amity with this government excepted), mulattoes or mestizos ... shall be and they are hereby declared to be and remain for ever hereafter absolute slaves, and shall follow the condition of the mother; and shall be deemed, taken, and adjudged to be chattels personal in the hands of their owners and possessors, and their executors, administrators, and assigns, to all intents, constructions, and purposes whatsoever."

This was the complete and total legal dehumanization—enslaved people were declared "absolute slaves" who "follow the condition of the mother" (the principle of *partus sequitur ventrem*, meaning the child inherits the status of the mother, allowing white men to rape enslaved women and produce more property without legal accountability).

**Additional key provisions from the 1740 Act:**

- **Movement:** "every slave who shall be found out of ... without a letter or ticket ... shall be punished with whipping on the bare back not exceeding 20 lashes."
- **Violence by slaves:** "if any slave shall presume to strike any white person ... for the 1st and 2d offence, suffer such punishment ... and for the 3d offence shall suffer death." "any slave who shall assault and strike any white person, such slave may be lawfully killed."
- **Summary trials:** "all crimes and offences which shall be committed by slaves ... shall be heard, examined, tried, adjudged, and finally determined by any 2 justices ... and any number of freeholders ... in the most summary and expeditious manner." Evidence of any slave, without oath, shall be admitted against another slave or free Negro in criminal cases.
- **Killing slaves:** "if any person shall wilfully murder his own slave ... shall forfeit and pay the sum of £700 current money, and shall be ... incapable of holding ... any office ... within this Province." The murder of a slave by a white person was reduced to a misdemeanor, punishable by a fine.
- **Literacy prohibition:** "No slave shall be taught to write; offenders shall forfeit one hundred pounds." Slave writing was prohibited, as were drumming and playing horns.
- **Working hours:** "no owner ... shall work or put any such slave ... to labor, more than 15 hours in 24 hours ... every such person shall forfeit any sum not exceeding or under £20."
- **Firearms:** "No slave shall carry or make use of firearms ... unless licensed by his master and ... under certain restrictions."
- **Insurrection:** "Every slave who shall raise or attempt to raise an insurrection ... shall upon conviction ... suffer death."

A 1735 South Carolina statute concerning manumission required newly manumitted slaves to leave the province within six months. The source states: "All owners of slaves ... who shall manumit or set free any slave ... shall make provision for his departure out of this province; and such slave who shall not depart ... shall lose the benefit of such manumission ... unless such manumission shall be approved ... by an order of both Houses of Assembly."

## 2. Key Differences Between British Parliamentary Law and Colonial Plantation Codes (1730s)

The decade of the 1730s represents a crucial period in the legal history of slavery in the British Empire because **English common law and colonial slave codes diverged fundamentally**. The British Parliament had passed no statutes that directly regulated colonial slavery within the 1730s—slave codes had failed to pass in Parliament. Instead, the legal foundation for slavery was being built through court decisions and colonial legislation.

### English Common Law on Slavery (circa 1706–1729)

**Smith v. Gould (1706):** Lord Chief Justice Holt rejected an assumpsit claim (a legal action for breach of contract) on the sale of a negro in England, declaring: "as soon as a negro comes to England he is free; one may be a villein in England, but not a slave." Holt argued that "a slave could not be owned as chattels in England" and that "England was too pure an Air for Slaves to breathe in."

**The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729):** This legal opinion by Sir Philip Yorke (Attorney General) and Charles Talbot (Solicitor General) was specifically sought by slave merchants alarmed by Holt's decisions. It contained three key holdings: (1) "a slave coming from the West-Indies to Great-Britain or Ireland, with or without his master, doth not become free, and that his master's property or right in him is not thereby determined or varied"; (2) "Baptism doth not bestow freedom on him, or make any alteration in his temporal condition in these kingdoms"; (3) "His master may legally compel him to return again to the plantations." Critically, this was only an opinion by Crown law officers, not a court judgment—it had "no legal precedent or courtroom authority"—yet it was "widely cited to support slavery under common law for nearly 40 years."

**The Colonial Debts Act of 1732:** This Parliamentary act made "houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates belonging to any person indebted ... liable to and chargeable with all just debts, and may be seized and sold to satisfy such debts." This was Parliament's major intervention regarding slaves—not regulating slavery per se, but facilitating the credit system by making slaves recoverable for debts. Alexander Hamilton later described this as "one of the Highest Acts of Legislature that one Country could exercise over another."

### Comparative Analysis: English Common Law vs. Colonial Slave Codes

#### A. Permissible Punishments

**English Common Law:**
- English law did not permit the whipping, branding, or dismemberment of servants as legal punishment by masters. Criminal punishment was administered by state courts.
- English homicide law applied equally to all persons—a master who killed a servant could be prosecuted for murder.
- English law had no provision for execution of servants for running away or theft from their masters (these were civil or lesser criminal matters).
- The English criminal justice system required jury trials for all felony offenses.

**Barbados Slave Code (1661/1688):**
- Permitted: severe whipping, nose-slitting, facial burning/branding, and death for enslaved persons offering violence to Christians.
- Masters who killed their own slaves faced only a fine paid to the Public Treasury.
- Dismemberment was explicitly authorized for repeat offenses.
- Capital punishment for insurrection or rebellion could be imposed by "Marshall Law" without jury trial.

**Jamaican Slave Law (1664, 1717, and subsequent acts):**
- Permitted: whipping, burning/branding on the face, dismemberment (specifically cutting off a foot for runaways absent over 30 days), and death.
- Masters were prohibited from dismembering slaves "at their Will and Pleasure" under penalty of £100—a restriction not present in the Barbados code.
- The state, however, performed dismemberment (foot amputation) as a judicial punishment for runaway slaves.
- Compensation was paid to owners for executed slaves (capped at £40).

#### B. Protections (or Lack Thereof) for Enslaved People

**English Common Law:**
- In England, "a black could take his master to court for cruel treatment, and could have a habeas corpus if restrained of his liberty" (Lord Chancellor Henley, 1762).
- The principle that "England was too pure an air for slaves to breathe" meant any enslaved person brought to England was arguably free.
- Villeinage (the closest English institution to slavery) gave villeins some legal protections—they could not be killed, had property rights, and had access to courts—all denied to colonial slaves.
- No positive law in England authorized chattel slavery.

**Barbados Slave Code:**
- "No standards for diet or housing" were provided. The only mandated provision was "one set of clothing per year."
- Slaves could not testify against white persons.
- Slaves had no right to habeas corpus or jury trial.
- Slave owners faced only a fine (not homicide prosecution) for killing their own slaves.
- The code explicitly treated slaves as goods and chattels, not persons before the law.

**Jamaican Slave Law:**
- The 1717 Act's prohibition on masters dismembering slaves at will (£100 penalty) was a formal protection—though the state performed judicial dismemberment.
- Slaves were barred from testifying against their masters.
- No right to habeas corpus or jury trial.
- Slaves could be executed based on the judgment of justices and freeholders (not a grand jury or petit jury).

#### C. Rights of Masters

**English Common Law:**
- Masters had the right to the service of their servants/apprentices but could not inflict corporal punishment beyond reasonable correction.
- Masters could not kill, maim, or permanently injure servants.
- A master's right to property did not extend to owning human beings as chattel.
- The Yorke-Talbot opinion (1729) attempted to assert a master's right to compel return of slaves to the colonies, but this was not a court ruling.

**Barbados Slave Code:**
- Masters had "near-complete dominion over the life and death of their slaves."
- Masters could kill their own slaves with only a fine (not criminal liability).
- Masters could whip, brand, and punish slaves at their discretion.
- The master's property right in slaves was fully protected by law.

**Jamaican Slave Law:**
- Masters had extensive but slightly more regulated rights than in Barbados.
- The 1717 Act restricted masters from dismembering slaves at will—a formal limitation on master's power absent from the Barbados code.
- Masters were compensated for slaves judicially executed (capped at £40).
- Slaves were classified as real estate (1696 code), giving masters additional property protections in transactions and debt recovery.

#### D. Colonial Assemblies vs. Parliament

- The British Parliament did not pass any statutes that directly regulated colonial slavery within the 1730s—"slave codes failed to pass in Parliament." Charles II had attempted to pass "an imperial slave code through Parliament" but was "unable" to do so, and instead "turned to the courts" to create precedents.
- Colonial assemblies (in both Barbados and Jamaica) had the power to enact local slave codes without direct parliamentary approval, so long as they did not conflict with English law in fundamental ways.
- The Jamaica Assembly "claimed exclusive rights to legislate internal governance, opposing British laws they viewed as infringements on colonial autonomy."
- The British crown exercised limited oversight over colonial slave legislation, though imperial authorities could disallow colonial laws that were "repugnant" to English law.
- Holly Brewer's research emphasizes that "the common law became an instrument of crown policy within a federal empire, wherein colonial legal norms had to adhere in crucial ways to that common law"—meaning the colonies were not entirely free to legislate as they wished; their laws had to conform to English common law principles, at least in theory.

## 3. Recent Peer-Reviewed Articles (2015–2025) on the Legal Dehumanization of Enslaved People

### Holly Brewer, "Creating a Common Law of Slavery for England and its New World Empire," *Law and History Review* 39, no. 4 (November 2021): 765–834.

Available at: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/law-and-history-review/article/creating-a-common-law-of-slavery-for-england-and-its-new-world-empire/8D27552070D9A6CD478BA9912DEFB26B

This is the single most important recent article for your purposes. Brewer challenges the prevailing historical consensus that English common law was confused about slavery and that slavery was only legal in England's colonies "beyond the line" of English justice. She argues that English common law evolved profoundly during the 17th and 18th centuries to become a tool of crown policy, creating an imperial common law of slavery that defined enslaved people as **absolute property**.

Key arguments:
- "English high court judges thus provided the legal foundation for an imperial common law of slavery that allowed people to be deemed absolute property."
- "The common law became an instrument of crown policy. It provided a foundation for a common law of slavery in all English colonies and for the slave trade."
- This definition of people as property was "closely connected to absolutism, both in theory and practice"—linking the absolute power of kings over subjects to that of masters over slaves.
- "Charles II helped to make the buying and selling of people as slaves both fully legal and enforceable across the empire by using the courts."
- "Parliament could not pass an imperial slave code, so the King used judicial decisions to legitimize slavery."
- "American slavery did not emerge 'beyond the line' of English justice but within an imperial legal system requiring conformity to English law."

## 4. Scholarly Debate: Was the Molasses Act (1733) Primarily About Trade Regulation or Slave Control?

The evidence strongly suggests that the dichotomy between "trade regulation" and "slave control/plantation protection" is somewhat artificial. The Molasses Act was simultaneously both: it was a trade regulation measure that served to protect the economic viability of slave-based sugar plantations. However, the debate is important because it reflects deeper historiographical questions about the relationship between British imperial capitalism and slavery.

### The Traditional Mercantilist Interpretation

The most common characterization of the Molasses Act in general historical sources is as a straightforward mercantilist trade regulation measure. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molasses_Act) states: "The act was passed not to raise revenue but to regulate trade by making British products cheaper than those from the French West Indies." [AmericanRevolution.org](https://www.americanrevolution.org/molasses-act) notes: "The Molasses Act was designed not to raise tax revenue, but to increase trade with British merchants, in locations such as the British West Indies."

Colonial agent Richard Partridge argued in 1733 that the Act was "divesting them of their rights and privileges as ye King's natural born subjects and Englishmen in levying subsidies against their consent when they... have no representatives in Parliament" and called it "an infringement on liberty and property and... a violation of the right of the subject."

### The Slavery/Plantation System Interpretation

The "trade regulation" framing cannot be separated from the slave economy, because the very sugar plantations the Act protected were entirely dependent on enslaved African labor. The British West Indian planters who agitated for the Act owned enslaved people, and the wealth generated by their plantations came from enslaved labor. Protecting their economic interests was thus inseparable from protecting the slave system that sustained them.

**Andrew Jackson O'Shaughnessy's *An Empire Divided: The American Revolution and the British Caribbean* (2000)** makes the strongest case for understanding British Caribbean colonial policy—including trade protection measures—as fundamentally shaped by the imperatives of maintaining slave societies. O'Shaughnessy argues that Caribbean planters remained loyal to Britain because they depended on British military protection against slave insurrection, and that "the crisis that evolved into an 'imperial civil war' was linked to fears of famine and slave rebellion in the Caribbean." He emphasizes "the military dependence of the planters on Britain, chiefly for protection against slave insurrection."

**Trevor Burnard and John Garrigus's *The Plantation Machine: Atlantic Capitalism in French Saint-Domingue and British Jamaica* (2016)** demonstrates how the Caribbean plantation economies functioned as integrated systems where capitalism, slavery, and trade protection were inseparable. The plantations were "complex 'machines,' finely tuned over time by planters, merchants, and officials to become more efficient at exploiting their enslaved workers and serving their empires." Their work shows that "slaves represented highly liquid forms of capital, as well as being people who produced income for their owners." The book details the smuggling trade between British North America and Saint-Domingue, showing the correlation between trade and plantation production.

**Trevor Burnard's *Planters, Merchants and Slaves* (2015)** synthesizes this understanding, arguing that the plantation system "depended on relentless brutality toward slaves, and, nonetheless, were a spectacular financial success for whites in the plantation communities, for colonial merchants, and for metropolitan investors." He argues that "slave owners adopted ameliorative measures only in an effort to improve productivity, not standards of living."

**Christian J. Koot's *Empire at the Periphery: British Colonists, Anglo-Dutch Trade, and the Development of the British Atlantic, 1621-1713* (2011)** approaches the question from the perspective of trade and commercial regulation, showing that the Molasses Act was the culmination of decades of disputes over interimperial trade between British colonists and Dutch traders. Koot argues that empires consisted not only of elites dictating imperial growth from world capitals, but also of ordinary settlers in far-flung colonial outposts who often had more in common with—and a greater reliance on—people from foreign empires. His book includes a section titled "Diverging Interests: Anglo-Dutch Trade and the Molasses Act" (pp. 215-229).

### The Eric Williams Thesis Connection

The broader historiographical framework that links trade regulation and slavery is the **Eric Williams thesis** from *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944). Williams argued that the slave trade and plantation slavery provided the capital that financed the Industrial Revolution in England, and that mercantilism and slavery were intrinsically linked. Williams stated: "The rise and fall of mercantilism [the economic system under which slave-produced sugar thrived] is the rise and fall of slavery." This thesis frames the entire British imperial economic system—including trade regulations like the Molasses Act—as fundamentally built on and sustained by enslaved labor.

The Williams thesis has been challenged by **Seymour Drescher's *Econocide* (1977)**, which argued with statistical evidence that the British Caribbean economy and slave trade were expanding around the time of abolition, and that humanitarian motives were primary. Drescher argues that the antislavery impulse went against British economic interests.

### Synthesis

The evidence strongly suggests that the dichotomy is artificial. As Barbara Solow emphasizes, no West Indian colony ever founded a successful society on the basis of free white labor—the entire British Caribbean economy was predicated on slavery. Therefore, any trade measure protecting that economy was, by extension, a measure protecting the slave system.

The debate reflects deeper questions: Was the British Empire's primary motivation economic/commercial expansion (in which slavery was a means to an end), or was the maintenance of the slave system itself a primary imperial objective? O'Shaughnessy's work suggests the latter, emphasizing how concerns about slave rebellion shaped Caribbean planters' political choices. Burnard's work emphasizes the brutal economic logic of the plantation system. Koot's work focuses on the commercial networks, showing that trade regulation was a field of contestation between imperial authorities, colonial merchants, and planters—all operating within a system that included slavery as a given.

For your novel, the important point is that the Molasses Act cannot be understood without reference to slavery, even though the text of the Act itself never mentions enslaved people. The sugar it protected, the plantations it preserved, and the triangular trade it sustained all depended on the enslavement of Africans.

## 5. Summary: Practical Guidance for Your Novel

For your historical novel set in the 1730s British Empire, here is what you need to know:

**On the legal status of enslaved people in the 1730s:**

- **In England itself:** The legal status was deeply contested. Lord Chief Justice Holt had ruled in 1706 that "as soon as a negro comes to England he is free," but the Yorke-Talbot opinion of 1729 (not a court ruling, but influential) claimed the opposite—that slaves remained property in England. This ambiguity was not resolved until the Somerset case in 1772, decades after your setting. You can use this tension in your novel—a character bringing an enslaved person to England could face legal uncertainty.

- **In Barbados (1661 code in force):** Enslaved people were explicitly classified as "goods and Chattels." Masters could kill their own slaves with only a fine. Punishments included whipping, nose-slitting, facial burning, and death for offering violence to Christians.

- **In Jamaica (1664 code, 1696 real estate classification, 1717 act in force):** Enslaved people were classified as real estate (1696 code). The 1717 act prohibited masters from dismembering slaves at will (£100 penalty), but the state could cut off a foot for runaways absent over 30 days. Owners were compensated for executed slaves (capped at £40).

- **In South Carolina (1735 manumission law; 1740 Negro Act passed just after your decade):** The 1740 Act contains the most explicit language—"deemed, taken, and adjudged to be chattels personal." This was passed in response to the Stono Rebellion of 1739, which could be referenced in your novel's timeline.

**On the difference between British law and colonial codes:**

Your characters could legitimately debate this. An English lawyer might argue that a slave brought to England is free; a colonial planter would insist the slave remains property. The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 made slaves recoverable for debts across the empire—a crucial legal mechanism. The colonial assemblies governed slavery locally, while Parliament had not directly regulated it, creating a fragmented legal landscape.

**On the Molasses Act debate:**

Your characters could represent different perspectives: a New England merchant smuggling French molasses for distilleries that produce rum for the slave trade; a British West Indian planter insisting the Act is necessary to protect legitimate (slave-produced) sugar; a British customs official trying to enforce the law against smuggling. The Act's connection to the Triangular Trade was well understood by contemporaries, even if the statutory text never mentions slavery.
