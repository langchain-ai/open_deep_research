# Independent Directors Under NASDAQ Listing Standards: A Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the definition of an independent director under the NASDAQ listing standards, addressing the eligibility criteria, disqualification criteria, and the types of companies required to have independent directors on their boards. The analysis is based on primary sources, including NASDAQ Listing Rules (particularly Rule 5605 and related rules) and SEC guidance, with specific rule numbers and concrete criteria provided throughout.

---

## Section 1: Eligibility Criteria — Who Qualifies as an Independent Director

### 1.1 Core Definition Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)

Under NASDAQ Listing Rule 5605(a)(2), an "independent director" is defined as a person who meets two fundamental conditions:

- **Not an executive officer or employee** of the company
- **No relationship** which, in the opinion of the board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director [2][4][7]

The rule text frames independence around the concept of whether any relationship would "interfere with the exercise of independent judgment" [2][4]. This definition serves as the foundational eligibility standard for all independent director determinations under NASDAQ rules.

### 1.2 The Two-Part Test for Independence

The independence determination involves two distinct and sequential components:

**Part A — Objective (Bright-Line) Criteria:** The board must first check whether the director falls within any of the specific disqualifying relationships or thresholds enumerated in NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2). If any such disqualifying relationship exists, the director is automatically precluded from being considered independent. These are objective disqualifiers that do not permit board override [1][4][9].

**Part B — Subjective Affirmative Determination:** Even if no bright-line disqualifiers apply, the board must affirmatively determine that the director has no material relationship with the listed company that would interfere with the director's exercise of independent judgment. This affirmative finding must be documented in board resolutions [2][4][8].

The board's judgment is the ultimate authority — a director is independent only if the board concludes, in its business judgment, that no relationship exists that would interfere with independent judgment [1][4][7].

### 1.3 Factors Considered in the Materiality Determination

In assessing whether a "material relationship" exists, NASDAQ guidance indicates that the board should consider all relevant facts and circumstances, including:

- **Stock ownership:** Ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board finding of independence. However, when large ownership positions are combined with other relationships, the board must consider the totality of the circumstances [1][2][4]
- **Family relationships:** Any familial relationship that could create a sense of obligation or loyalty must be evaluated [4][5][9]
- **Business relationships:** Direct or indirect business relationships between the director (or an affiliate of the director) and the company or its affiliates [9]
- **Professional relationships:** Service as legal counsel, consultant, or other professional service provider to the company [2][4]
- **Charitable relationships:** Significant charitable contributions by the company to an organization with which the director is affiliated may be considered [9]
- **Past relationships:** The board considers both current and past relationships, with specific lookback periods codified in the bright-line rules [4][9]

### 1.4 Board Process Requirements

NASDAQ expects a robust board process for making independence determinations, typically including [2][4]:

- Annual director and officer questionnaires
- A relationship inventory
- A committee overlay review (to assess additional criteria for audit, compensation, and nominating committees)
- A board resolution reflecting the affirmative determinations

These processes must comply with Regulation S-K Item 407(a) for proxy disclosure purposes [2][4].

## Section 2: Disqualification Criteria — Who Cannot Serve as an Independent Director

Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2), the following specific relationships disqualify a director from being considered independent [3][4][9]. Unless otherwise noted, the lookback period is three years preceding the determination of independence.

### 2.1 Employment Relationships — Rule 5605(c)(2)(i)

A director cannot be considered independent if:

- The director is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the company (including by any parent or subsidiary of the company) [3][4][9]
- The director is a family member of an individual who is, or at any time during the past three years was, employed by the company as an executive officer [3][4][9]

### 2.2 Compensation Threshold — Rule 5605(c)(2)(ii)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director (or a family member of the director) accepted any compensation from the company in excess of $120,000 during any period of 12 consecutive months within the three years preceding the independence determination [3][4][9].

**Exceptions to the $120,000 threshold include:**

- Director and committee fees (including reasonable director compensation and reimbursement for board-related expenses) are expressly excluded from this calculation [3][9]
- Compensation paid to a family member who is a non-executive officer employee of the company is excluded [9]
- Certain pension or deferred compensation payments that are not contingent on continued service may be excluded [9]
- Rule 10A-3 imposes even stricter compensation restrictions for audit committee members, prohibiting any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company or its subsidiaries (not just amounts over $120,000) [1][2][4][7]

### 2.3 Business Relationships — Rule 5605(c)(2)(iii)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director is, or has a family member who is, a partner in, controlling shareholder of, or executive officer of, any organization to which the listed company made, or from which the listed company received, payments in excess of the greater of 5% of the recipient's consolidated gross revenues or $200,000 in the company's most recently completed fiscal year [3][9].

This disqualification covers payments to or from any organization where the director (or family member) serves as a partner, controlling shareholder, or executive officer [3][4][9].

### 2.4 Auditor Affiliation — Rule 5605(c)(2)(iv)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director (or a family member of the director) is a current partner or employee of the company's outside auditor, or was a partner or employee of the outside auditor who worked on the company's audit at any time during the past three years [3][4][9].

### 2.5 Interlocking Directorships / Compensation Committee Relationships — Rule 5605(c)(2)(v)

A director cannot be considered independent if the director is employed by another entity where any of the listed company's executive officers serve on that other entity's compensation committee (i.e., interlocking directorships involving compensation committees) [3][9].

### 2.6 Audit Committee-Specific Disqualifications — Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) and SEC Rule 10A-3

For audit committee membership, NASDAQ imposes additional, more stringent independence requirements beyond the general director independence criteria. Under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3 [1][2][4][7]:

- Audit committee members must meet both the NASDAQ independence standards under Rule 5605(a)(2) and the SEC's audit committee independence criteria under Rule 10A-3 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 [2][4][7]
- Under SEC Rule 10A-3, audit committee members cannot accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company or its subsidiaries (other than director or committee fees) [1][2][4][7]
- Audit committee members cannot be an affiliated person of the company or any of its subsidiaries (subject to certain exceptions) [1][4][7]
- NASDAQ Rule 5605(c)(2)(A) specifically requires the audit committee to have at least three members, each of whom must be an independent director meeting both the NASDAQ and SEC independence standards [1][9][11]
- Audit committee members must also be financially sophisticated (able to read and understand basic financial statements) [9][11]
- At least one member must be an "audit committee financial expert" as defined by the SEC [9][11]

### 2.7 Definition of "Family Member" Under Rule 5605(a)(2)

For purposes of all disqualification criteria, "Family Member" means a person's [3][5][9]:

- Spouse
- Parents
- Children (including stepchildren who reside with the director)
- Siblings
- Mothers-in-law and fathers-in-law
- Sons-in-law and daughters-in-law
- Brothers-in-law and sisters-in-law
- Anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares the person's home

**Important 2020 Amendment:** In February 2020, the SEC approved NASDAQ's amended proposal to exclude stepchildren from the definition of "family member" unless they reside with the director, in which case they remain family members. This aligns NASDAQ's definition with the NYSE's definition of "immediate family member" [3][5]. Domestic employees who share a director's home are excluded from the family member definition as those relationships are typically commercial [3][5].

## Section 3: Types of Companies Required to Have Independent Directors and Composition Requirements

### 3.1 Majority Independent Board Requirement — Rule 5605(b)(1)

Under NASDAQ Rule 5605(b)(1), a majority of the board of directors must be comprised of Independent Directors as defined in Rule 5605(a)(2) [2][9][11].

This requirement applies generally to all NASDAQ-listed companies, with specific exemptions and phase-in provisions described below [9][10][12].

### 3.2 Controlled Companies — Rule 5615(c)

**Definition:** A "Controlled Company" is a company of which more than 50% of the voting power for the election of directors is held by an individual, a group, or another company [8][9][10][12].

**Exemption:** Under Rule 5615(c), controlled companies are exempt from the requirements of:
- Rule 5605(b) (majority independent board)
- Rule 5605(d) (compensation committee independence)
- Rule 5605(e) (nominating committee independence)

[8][9][10][12]

**Limitations of the Exemption:**
- Controlled companies are not exempt from the audit committee requirements under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3. The audit committee must still be fully independent [8][9][10]
- Controlled companies must still hold executive sessions of independent directors (meetings at which only independent directors are present) [9][10]
- Controlled companies must disclose their controlled company status in their SEC filings [9][10]
- When a company ceases to be a controlled company, it must comply with audit committee requirements immediately and has a phase-in period for board and committee independence beginning on the date it ceases controlled status [9][10]

### 3.3 Foreign Private Issuers — Rule 5615(a)(3)

Under NASDAQ Rule 5615(a)(3), a foreign private issuer is permitted to follow its home country practice in lieu of most NASDAQ corporate governance requirements, including the independent director requirements under Rule 5605 [9][10][12].

**Requirements for Use of Home Country Exemption:**
- The foreign private issuer must disclose in its SEC filings (typically on Form 20-F) each requirement of NASDAQ that it does not follow and the home country practice it follows instead [9][10]
- The exemption applies to board independence requirements, committee composition requirements, and most other governance provisions in the 5600 Series [9][10]
- Audit committee requirements under Rule 5605(c) and SEC Rule 10A-3 cannot be avoided through the home country practice exemption. Foreign private issuers must still have an audit committee that meets Rule 10A-3 independence standards, though the SEC allows certain accommodations for foreign private issuers (e.g., employee representatives on audit committees if required by home country law) [8][9][10]
- If a foreign private issuer ceases to qualify as a foreign private issuer, it has a six-month phase-in period to comply with domestic company requirements (except that it must immediately comply with audit committee independence requirements upon losing such status) [9][10][12]

### 3.4 Smaller Reporting Companies and Emerging Growth Companies

**Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs):**
- Under NASDAQ rules, smaller reporting companies are not granted any special exemption from the independent director or committee composition requirements under Rule 5605 [9]
- SRCs may be eligible for certain phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) for transitioning from other listing standards [9]
- SRCs benefit from certain SEC accommodations (e.g., scaled disclosure requirements under Regulation S-K and Regulation S-X), but these do not affect the NASDAQ independence rules [9]

**Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs):**
- Under the JOBS Act, EGCs are permitted to delay compliance with certain new or revised accounting standards, but this does not provide an exemption from the independent director requirements under NASDAQ Rule 5605 [9]
- EGCs do not receive any special exemption from the majority independent board requirement or the independent committee requirements under NASDAQ rules [9]
- EGCs are subject to the same phase-in periods as other IPO companies under Rule 5615(b) [9][12]

### 3.5 IPO and Initial Listing Phase-In Periods — Rule 5615(b)

Companies listing in connection with an initial public offering (IPO) or certain other initial listing events are granted the following phase-in periods under Rule 5615(b) [9][10][12]:

**Majority Independent Board:**
- 12 months from the listing date to comply with the majority independent board requirement under Rule 5605(b)(1) [10][12]

**Audit Committee (Rule 5605(c)):**
- By the listing date: at least one member on the audit committee meeting the independence and financial sophistication requirements [10][12]
- Within 90 days of the listing date: at least two members meeting the requirements [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: the full three-member audit committee meeting all independence and financial expertise requirements [10][12]
- No phase-in or exemption applies to certain audit committee independence requirements under SEC Rule 10A-3 — those must be met by the listing date [10][12]

**Compensation Committee (Rule 5605(d)):**
- By the listing date (or within five business days of the listing date): at least one independent member [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: full compliance with the independent compensation committee requirement (at least two independent members) [10][12]

**Nominating Committee (Rule 5605(e)):**
- By the listing date (or within five business days of the listing date): at least one independent member [10][12]
- Within one year of the listing date: full compliance with the independent nominating committee requirement [10][12]

**Companies Listing Through Carve-Out or Spin-Off Transactions:**
- These companies receive the same phase-in schedule as IPO companies under Rule 5615(b)(4) [10][12]

**Companies Emerging from Bankruptcy:**
- Must comply with audit committee requirements by the listing date unless exempt under Rule 10A-3 [10][12]
- Receive phase-in schedules for other governance requirements similar to IPO companies [10][12]

**Companies Transferring from Another Exchange:**
- Subject to the applicable phase-in provisions under Rule 5615(b) depending on their circumstances [10][12]

### 3.6 Cure Periods

For companies that become noncompliant with the independent director or committee requirements due to circumstances beyond their reasonable control (e.g., death, resignation, or disqualification of a director), NASDAQ provides cure periods [9][10][12]:

- Generally, the company has until the earlier of its next annual shareholders meeting or one year from the event causing noncompliance to regain compliance [9][10]
- If the next annual meeting is within 180 days, the company has at least 180 days to comply [10]
- **Important:** A company relying on a phase-in period under Rule 5615(b) is not eligible for a cure period immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period — unless the company demonstrated compliance during the phase-in period but fell out of compliance before its expiration [10][12]

### 3.7 Committee Composition Requirements

#### 3.7.1 Audit Committee — Rule 5605(c)(3)

- Must consist of at least three members [1][9][11]
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2) [9][11]
- Each member must also meet the heightened independence requirements of SEC Rule 10A-3 (no consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company, and no affiliation with the company or subsidiaries) [1][2][4][7]
- Each member must be financially sophisticated (able to read and understand basic financial statements) [9][11]
- At least one member must be an "audit committee financial expert" as defined by SEC rules [9][11]
- The committee must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities [9][11]
- The audit committee is directly responsible for the appointment, compensation, and oversight of the independent auditor [7][9][11]
- The committee must establish procedures for receiving and handling complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, and auditing matters [7][9]

#### 3.7.2 Compensation Committee — Rule 5605(d)(2)

- Must consist of at least two members [8][9][11]
- Each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2) [9][11]
- Members may not accept consulting or advisory fees from the company (or receive any other compensatory fees beyond director compensation) [8][9][11]
- The committee must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities, including the authority to engage compensation consultants, independent legal counsel, and other advisers [9][11]
- Before selecting or receiving advice from a compensation consultant, the committee must consider the independence of that consultant under applicable NASDAQ and SEC standards [9][11]

**Limited Exception:** Under Rule 5605(d)(2)(B), one non-independent director may serve on the compensation committee for up to two years under exceptional and limited circumstances (e.g., death or disability of an existing member), provided the board determines it is in the best interests of the company and shareholders [8][10].

#### 3.7.3 Nominating Committee — Rule 5605(e)(2)

- Must consist entirely of Independent Directors (or alternatively, independent directors must constitute a majority of the board and must select nominees through a board process, even if a formal nominating committee does not exist) [8][9][11]
- The committee (or the independent directors acting as a group) must have a formal written charter specifying its responsibilities [9][11]
- The committee is responsible for identifying and recommending candidates for director nomination [9][11]

**Limited Exception:** Under Rule 5605(e)(3), one non-independent director may serve on the nominating committee for up to two years under exceptional and limited circumstances, provided the board determines it is in the best interests of the company and shareholders [8][10].

### 3.8 Summary Table of Company Types and Applicability

| Company Type | Majority Independent Board (Rule 5605(b)(1)) | Audit Committee Independence (Rule 5605(c)/10A-3) | Compensation Committee Independence (Rule 5605(d)) | Nominating Committee Independence (Rule 5605(e)) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| All listed companies generally | Required | Required (3 members, heightened independence) | Required (2 members) | Required (or process by independent directors) |
| Controlled companies (Rule 5615(c)) | Exempt | Required (no exemption) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Foreign private issuers (Rule 5615(a)(3)) | May follow home country practice | Required (with limited SEC accommodations) | May follow home country practice | May follow home country practice |
| Smaller reporting companies | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Emerging growth companies | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| IPO companies (Rule 5615(b)) | Phase-in: 12 months | Phase-in: up to 1 year | Phase-in: up to 1 year | Phase-in: up to 1 year |

## Section 4: Key Regulatory Developments

### 4.1 2024 Amendments to Phase-In and Cure Periods (Rules 5605, 5615, 5810)

On August 26, 2024, the SEC approved amendments to NASDAQ's rules that [10][12]:

- Clarified and modified phase-in schedules for independent director and committee requirements
- Aligned NASDAQ's phase-in schedules with those of the NYSE
- Codified the policy that companies relying on phase-in periods are not eligible for cure periods immediately following the expiration of the phase-in period, unless the company demonstrated compliance during the phase-in period but fell out of compliance before its expiration
- Extended phase-in schedules to companies listing through carve-out or spin-off transactions and companies listing securities previously registered under Section 12(g)

### 4.2 Family Member Definition Amendment (2020)

In February 2020, the SEC granted accelerated approval to NASDAQ's amended proposal to modify the definition of "family member" in Rule 5605(a)(2) to [3][5]:

- Exclude stepchildren from the definition unless they reside with the director
- Exclude domestic employees sharing the director's home
- Align with the NYSE's definition of "immediate family member"

### 4.3 SEC Rule 10A-3 Requirements

SEC Rule 10A-3, adopted pursuant to Section 301 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, establishes the baseline independence requirements for audit committee members that NASDAQ must enforce through its listing standards [1][4][7]:

- No acceptance of consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company
- No affiliation with the company or any of its subsidiaries
- These requirements are in addition to the general independence standards under NASDAQ Rule 5605(a)(2)
