# New Zealand Structural Steel Fabrication Market Analysis (2018–2024, with Outlook to 2031)

## Executive Summary

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market has navigated a volatile period from 2018 to 2024, experiencing a pronounced construction cycle with clear peaks and troughs driven by macroeconomic forces, monetary policy, and seismic events. The industry, valued at approximately **NZD $1.8 billion** in 2025–2026 (IBISWorld ANZSIC C2221), comprises roughly **267 businesses** and employs between **4,100–5,000 workers**. Annual structural steel fabrication runs at approximately **85,000–100,000 tonnes** against a capacity that expanded from **120,000 to 150,000 tonnes** between 2018 and 2023.

Following the COVID-19 pandemic, an aggressive RBNZ tightening cycle (OCR rising from 0.25% in 2021 to 5.50% by May 2023) drove a sharp contraction in building activity. New dwelling consents peaked at 49,538 in 2022 before collapsing 25% to 37,239 in 2023 and further to 33,600 in 2024. Construction employment fell from 308,500 in June 2023 to 295,100 by December 2025. Steel fabricator revenues and tonnages followed this cycle downward.

However, the medium-term outlook is supported by a **$275 billion national infrastructure pipeline**. Structural steel demand from major projects (CRL, RoNS, hospitals, renewable energy, data centres) is estimated at 250,000–500,000+ tonnes over 2026–2031, exceeding current annual fabrication capacity and likely requiring either increased imports or domestic capacity expansion. The sector's key challenges—import competition, labour shortages, and trade policy uncertainty—are balanced by opportunities in green steel production, seismic retrofit demand, and technology adoption.

## 1. Multi-Year Time Series: Employment, Revenue, and Tonnage (2018–2024)

### 1.1 Data Sourcing Methodology

This report constructs year-by-year estimates for three key metrics—employment, revenue, and tonnage fabricated—for New Zealand's structural steel fabrication industry (ANZSIC C2221) from 2018 to 2024. Multiple sources have been triangulated, with clear attribution, confidence levels, and reconciliation of conflicting figures throughout.

**Key sources used:**
- IBISWorld industry reports (C2221NZ) — market size, employment, business counts
- Steel Construction New Zealand (SCNZ) triannual industry updates — capacity, tonnage, workforce
- Stats NZ Business Demography Statistics — geographic unit and enterprise counts
- Stats NZ Building Consents data — residential and non-residential construction activity
- Steel & Tube Holdings annual reports (NZX: STU) — proxy revenue and volume data
- Sustainable Steel Council (SSC) — industry-wide employment and production figures
- Heavy Engineering Research Association (HERA) — levy tonnage data

**Confidence levels:** Where data is directly reported by official sources (Stats NZ, confirmed SCNZ surveys), confidence is **High**. Where figures are interpolated from IBISWorld CAGR percentages or derived from proxy indicators, confidence is **Moderate**. Where figures are based on third-party estimates with wide variance, confidence is **Low** and has been noted.

**Explanation of discrepancies:**

1. **SCNZ's 10,670 FTEs (2019/2020)** covers the entire steel industry value chain—not just fabricators. 3. **IBISWorld's 4,100 employees (2025)** is explicitly tied to ANZSIC code C2221NZ, defined as "units mainly engaged in fabricating structural steel components of buildings or other structures." This is the most restrictive definition, excluding support roles, administrative functions, and non-fabrication activities that SCNZ membership would include [4][5].

4. **Sustainable Steel Council's 28,000+ FTEs** covers the entire steel value chain including production, distribution, fabrication, erection, and recycling. This is not comparable to C2221 employment [6].

#### 1.4.3 HERA Levy Tonnage (Proxy for Steel Throughput)

The Heavy Engineering Research Levy, collected under the Heavy Engineering Research Levy Act 1978, provides a proxy for steel throughput. The levy was set at $10 per tonne historically and increased to $20 per tonne in 2023 [20]. HERA's monthly levy tonnage updates for early 2026 indicate "stable tonnages" with an increase in importation of prefabricated items. The levy covers approximately $2 million in annual revenue to HERA, implying roughly 200,000 tonnes of levy-liable steel (Schedule 2 items) [20][21].

**Important distinction:** The HERA levy covers a broader range of heavy engineering steel products than just structural steel fabrication (C2221). It includes plate steel, pipe, and other fabricated items, so the tonnage is not directly comparable.

### 2.2 Duty-Free Lines (Including Those Under FTAs)

#### 2.2.1 Duty-Free Lines Under MFN

According to the WTO Tariff Profile for New Zealand (2024 data), **65.1% of non-agricultural tariff lines** are already duty-free under MFN (most favoured nation) rates [25]. This means that for the majority of tariff lines within Chapters 72 and 73, the MFN rate is already 0%, bringing the overall average down significantly.

The simple average MFN applied tariff for non-agricultural products is only **2.0%** (2024), and the weighted mean applied tariff across all products is **1.7%** [25][26][27].

#### 2.2.2 Duty-Free Lines Under Free Trade Agreements

New Zealand has **14 free trade agreements (FTAs)** covering virtually all major steel-exporting nations. The key FTA partners relevant to steel trade include [28]:

| FTA Partner | Agreement | Effective Date | Steel Tariff Status |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Australia** | ANZCERTA (CER) | 1 January 1983 | Zero-tariff since inception |
| **China** | NZ-China FTA | 1 October 2008 | All tariffs eliminated by 2016 |
| **Japan** | CPTPP | 30 December 2018 | All industrial goods tariffs eliminated |
| **South Korea** | Korea-NZ FTA | 20 December 2015 | Tariffs eliminated on ~98% of goods |
| **United Kingdom** | NZ-UK FTA | 31 May 2023 | Comprehensive tariff elimination from day one |
| **European Union** | NZ-EU FTA | 1 May 2024 | All EU goods tariffs eliminated at entry into force |
| **Canada, Mexico, Peru, Chile, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, Brunei** | CPTPP | 30 December 2018 | Industrial goods tariffs eliminated |
| **ASEAN + Australia + China + Japan + Korea** | RCEP | 1 January 2022 | Preferential rates (ultimately duty-free) for all major steel exporters |
| **ASEAN (Brunei, Malaysia, Singapore, Vietnam, Thailand, Philippines, Indonesia, Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos)** | AANZFTA | 1 January 2010 | Preferential duty rates (ultimately free) for originating goods |

[28][29][30][31][32][33][34][35]

**Practical effect:** Imports of structural steel from virtually all major steel-exporting nations enter New Zealand at **0% tariff**. The 5% MFN rate is applicable only to a small residual set of countries without FTAs—primarily Brazil, Russia, Turkey, India (pre-FTA), and the United States (which is not an FTA partner and whose steel exports to NZ are relatively small).

### 2.3 Weighted Average Effective Tariff Rate

#### 2.3.1 Calculation

| Metric | Rate | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| Simple average MFN applied tariff (non-agricultural, 2024) | **2.0%** | WTO Tariff Profile [25] |
| Weighted mean applied tariff (all products, 2022) | **1.7%** | World Bank / Trading Economics [26][27] |
| MFN applied duty-free share (non-agricultural, 2024) | **65.1%** | WTO Tariff Profile [25] |
| Simple average tariff on US imports into NZ | **1.5%** | MinterEllison [36] |

#### 2.3.2 Why the 5% MFN Rate Translates to a Much Lower Effective Rate

The headline 5% MFN rate for steel products appears straightforward, but the weighted average effective tariff rate (1.7%) is substantially lower due to four compounding factors:

**(a) High proportion of duty-free MFN lines:** 65.1% of non-agricultural products already enter New Zealand duty-free under MFN rates. For the majority of tariff lines within HS Chapters 72 and 73, the MFN rate is already 0%, pulling the overall average down significantly [25].

**(b) Comprehensive FTA coverage:** When the effectively applied rate is available (the lowest applicable rate, preferring preferential tariff rates when available, otherwise the MFN applied tariff), imports from FTA partner countries enter at zero or reduced rates. Since New Zealand's major steel suppliers (Australia, China, Japan, South Korea, UK, EU, ASEAN) all have FTAs providing duty-free access, the **vast majority of actual steel imports enter at 0%** rather than 5% [28][29][30].

**(c) Composition of imports by source country:** New Zealand Customs OIA data shows that major steel import sources include Australia, China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Thailand, and Indonesia [37]. All of these countries have FTA arrangements with New Zealand providing duty-free access. The weighted mean applied tariff is the average of effectively applied rates weighted by the product import shares corresponding to each partner country—since most steel imports come from FTA partners where the effective rate is 0%, the weighted average tilts heavily toward zero [26][38].

**(d) Zero-rated raw materials and semi-finished products:** Many raw materials, semi-finished forms, and inputs used by NZ manufacturers are already duty-free under MFN, further lowering the weighted average [39].

### 2.4 Summary Assessment of Tariff Impact on Local Fabricator Margins

#### 2.4.1 Direct Tariff Impact: Minimal

In New Zealand's low/zero-tariff environment (0% for virtually all FTA partners), the **direct tariff burden on imported steel inputs is near-zero** for the vast majority of imports. This has two implications for local fabricator margins:

1. **Low input cost advantage from tariffs:** Local fabricators do not benefit from a significant tariff-induced price advantage against imported fabricated steel. Imported structural steel from China, Japan, Korea, Australia, the EU, and the UK all enter at 0% duty.

2. **Low tariff protection for domestic producers:** Domestic steel producers (NZ Steel at Glenbrook, Pacific Steel) receive very little tariff protection. They must compete directly on price with duty-free imports.

#### 2.4.2 What Actually Drives Competitive Dynamics

Since tariffs provide no meaningful competitive buffer, fabricator margins are primarily driven by:

1. **Global steel prices:** Hot-rolled coil (HRC) steel prices as of May 27, 2026, are at **$1,166 USD/T**, up 39.64% year-over-year [40]. Global steel price volatility directly impacts input costs for both domestic and imported steel.

2. **NZD/USD exchange rates:** The NZD has weakened significantly. As of May 27, 2026, the NZD/USD rate is **0.5894**, down 1.86% year-over-year [41]. A weaker NZD increases the NZD cost of USD-denominated steel and shipping, providing a **natural buffer** for domestic fabricators against imports.

3. **Ocean freight costs:** The Shanghai Containerized Freight Index (SCFI) is at **2,218 points** as of May 25, 2026, up 39.85% year-over-year [42]. The Baltic Dry Index (BDI) is at **3,085 points**, up 138.04% year-over-year [43]. Soaring freight costs make imported steel more expensive in NZD terms.

4. **Quality certification costs:** The Beca technical paper on "Imported Structural Steel Issues & Considerations" documents that NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and the US. Steel from other sources requires rigorous testing, increasing project costs and narrowing the price advantage of imports [44].

#### 2.4.3 Anti-Dumping and Safeguard Duties

**Active anti-dumping duty:** Aluminium–zinc coated steel from the Republic of Korea is subject to an anti-dumping duty (effective from 1 January 2023, remaining in force until 29 March 2027) [45]. This applies to a specific coated steel product used mainly in roofing and cladding, **not** structural steel fabrication products such as beams, columns, trusses, or fabricated structural assemblies.

**Terminated anti-dumping duties:** Galvanised wire from Malaysia and China (terminated 30 October 2025); hollow steel sections from China and Malaysia (terminated 21 November 2018 with no duties imposed on China) [46][47].

**Countervailing duties:** There are currently **no goods subject to countervailing duties** in New Zealand [45].

#### 2.4.4 Overall Assessment

| Factor | Impact on Fabricator Margins | Rating |
|--------|---------------------------|--------|
| MFN tariff rate (5%) | Minimal—applies only to non-FTA countries | Low impact |
| FTA duty-free access | Eliminates tariff cost on imports from all major suppliers | Negative (no protection) |
| NZD depreciation | Increases cost of imported steel; benefits local fabricators | Positive buffer |
| Soaring freight costs | Increases cost of imported steel | Positive buffer |
| Quality certification costs | Reduces price advantage of imports | Positive buffer |
| Anti-dumping duties | Not applicable to structural steel products | No impact |

**Conclusion:** The New Zealand tariff structure provides **minimal direct protection** for local structural steel fabricators. The 5% MFN rate is largely irrelevant because virtually all steel imports enter duty-free under FTAs. However, natural buffers (NZD depreciation, high freight costs, quality certification requirements) provide more meaningful protection for domestic fabricators than tariff policy does. The competitive pressure from imports is a function of **global steel price dynamics, exchange rates, and logistics costs—not local tariff policy.**

#### 3.11.3 Comparison Against Industry Capacity

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Current annual structural steel fabrication capacity | **120,000–150,000 tonnes** | SCNZ [3][8] |
| Current annual output | **~85,000–100,000 tonnes** | SCNZ [3][8] |
| Current spare capacity | **28–41%** | SCNZ (varies by year) [3][8] |
| Structural steel demand 2026–2031 (low) | **250,000 tonnes** | This report (aggregated estimate) |
| Structural steel demand 2026–2031 (high) | **500,000+ tonnes** | This report (aggregated estimate) |
| Average annual structural steel demand (low) | **~42,000 tonnes/year** | Low estimate ÷ 6 years |
| Average annual structural steel demand (high) | **~83,000 tonnes/year** | High estimate ÷ 6 years |

**Key findings:**

1. **Existing spare capacity sufficient for low-demand scenario:** If structural steel demand averages ~42,000 tonnes per year, the industry's spare capacity (28–41% = 34,000–62,000 tonnes per year) is adequate.

2. **Capacity constraints under high-demand scenario:** If structural steel demand averages ~83,000 tonnes per year, this approaches the industry's current total output (~85,000–100,000 tonnes). The spare capacity of 34,000–62,000 tonnes would need to be fully utilized, and additional capacity may be required.

3. **Reinforcing steel demand far exceeds domestic capacity:** Pacific Steel's supply (estimated ~250,000 tonnes annually) plus NZ Steel's Glenbrook mill (660,000 tonnes) can theoretically meet reinforcing steel demand, but the volume of pipeline projects (600,000–1,200,000 tonnes reinforcing steel over 6 years) will require sustained high utilization or increased imports.

4. **The National Green Steel Plant (200,000 tonnes annual capacity)** will substantially increase domestic structural steel production capacity if completed as planned, potentially doubling the current 120,000–150,000 tonne capacity. This would resolve any capacity adequacy concerns [70][71].

5. **SCNZ's claim of "no need to import"** may be valid in the short term (2024–2025 with 41% spare capacity) but becomes questionable under the high-demand scenario for 2026–2031 [8].

### 4.1 Overview of the 2018–2024 Construction Cycle

The New Zealand construction industry experienced a complete cycle between 2018 and 2024: a pre-COVID peak, a pandemic-induced collapse, a post-COVID recovery and boom, and then an interest-rate-induced contraction. ### 4.3 Phase-by-Phase Mapping to Structural Steel Fabrication

#### Phase 1: Pre-COVID Peak (2018–2019)

**Macroeconomic conditions:**
- OCR stable at 1.75% throughout [76]
- GDP growth: ~2.8% forecast for 2018
- Building consents: new dwellings ~33,000–38,000; non-residential ~$7.5 billion
- Construction employment growing steadily

**Impact on structural steel fabrication:**
- Industry capacity: **120,000 tonnes** (10-year highs from ~110,000 tonnes in 2018) [3]
- Estimated fabrication: **~93,500–100,000 tonnes**
- Employment: **~5,300–5,500** (pre-COVID peak)
- SCNZ reported strong forward-workload across member fabricators
- Structural steel held over 50% of multilevel construction market [18]

#### Phase 2: COVID-19 Collapse (March–June 2020)

**Macroeconomic conditions:**
- OCR slashed from 1.75% to 0.25% in March 2020 [76]
- National Level 4 lockdown: 26 March – 28 April 2020
- GDP contracted sharply (construction sector output fell)
- Building consents: dropped sharply in April 2020 (2,174 new dwellings, lowest since 2013)

**Impact on structural steel fabrication:**
- Spare capacity **spiked to 47%** —the highest recorded [18]
- Implied fabrication dropped to **~63,600 tonnes** (from ~100,000 tonnes in 2019) [18]
- 36% decline in fabrication from 2019 peak
- SCNZ: "The structural steel industry is no stranger to challenge and has successfully navigated the global financial crisis and the Christchurch earthquakes of 2010 and 2011—but COVID-19 is unique" [18]
- Revenue per employee increased as workforce was cut faster than revenue declined

#### Phase 3: Post-COVID Recovery and Boom (2021–2022)

**Macroeconomic conditions:**
- OCR held at 0.25% until October 2021 (record low for 18 months) [76]
- Massive Government stimulus (wage subsidies, business support, infrastructure spending)
- New dwelling consents surged: 2021 saw 49,007 consents, 2022 peaked at 49,538
- Housing market boom: median house prices rose ~30% nationally
- Construction employment grew to ~308,500 by June 2023
- Steel prices surged: average steel pricing increased 27% year-on-year in FY2022

**Impact on structural steel fabrication:**
- Fabrication recovered from ~63,600 tonnes (Jun 2020) to ~75,600 tonnes (Mar 2022) [2][19]
- Steel & Tube reported **record revenue of $599.1 million** in FY2022 [12]
- Net profit after tax of $30.2 million (up 96.4% from previous year)
- SCNZ: "The structural steel sector turned approximately 100,000 tonnes of steel into buildings and bridges" —note that this figure is a benchmark, not a calculated annual figure [2]
- Industry expanded capacity from 120,000 to **150,000 tonnes** over 5 years [8]
- **Supply chain constraints** became a major issue: "Our buoyant construction market is being fettered by COVID-related supply constraints" [2]

#### Phase 4: Sharp Contraction (2023–2024)

**Macroeconomic conditions:**
- OCR peaked at **5.50%** in May 2023 (highest since 2008) [76]
- Annual inflation peaked at **7.3%** in June 2022 quarter [95]
- New dwelling consents collapsed: **-25% in 2023**; further **-9.8% in 2024**
- Construction employment declined: -6.1% by December 2024 [96]
- Non-residential consents declined from $9.5B peak to $9.3B

**Impact on structural steel fabrication:**
- Spare capacity **rose to 41%** by September 2023 (from 28% in March 2023) [3][8]
- Implied fabrication fell to **~70,800–88,500 tonnes** [8]
- Steel & Tube: revenue fell 19% in FY2024; further 20% in FY2025 [13][14]
- Steel & Tube net loss of **$24.4 million** in FY2025—worst result in a decade [14]
- Industry business count declined: 271 businesses in 2025, down from ~285 in 2021 [7]
- "Import penetration has plummeted in recent years, partly due to weaker local construction trends, the slump in global steel prices and the escalation in freight costs" [4]
- **Export volumes held up better than domestic.** NZ exported 12,546 tonnes of structures and parts of structures of iron or steel (HS 730890) in 2024, valued at US$42.11 million [97].

#### Phase 5: Stabilization and Recovery (Late 2024–2026)

**Macroeconomic conditions:**
- OCR began reducing in August 2024 from 5.50% to 4.75%, then further to 4.50% in October 2024 [76]
- Building consents: slight recovery in 2025 (36,619 new dwellings, up 9.0% year-on-year) [90]
- Construction industry sales: first annual growth in nearly 2 years (December 2025 quarter) [90]
- Ready-mixed concrete production: first increase since 2022 (December 2025 quarter) [90]

**Impact on structural steel fabrication (emerging):**
- Steel & Tube CEO Mark Malpass (May 2026): "We are starting to see early signs of recovery, and expect activity to continue to improve through FY26 as the benefits of lower interest rates take effect and stimulate confidence, spending and investment, along with Government-backed construction projects" [14]
- The $275 billion National Infrastructure Pipeline provides multi-year demand visibility [98]
- Infrastructure activity forecast to reach $19.6 billion by 2030 [99]

## 6. Strategic Risks and Opportunities

### 6.1 Top 3 Strategic Risks

#### Risk 1: Competition from Imported Fabricated Steel

**Nature:** New Zealand structural steel fabricators face sustained competition from cheaper imported fabricated steel, particularly from China. IBISWorld describes the level of competition as "high and increasing" [4].

**Evidence:**
- NZ imports from China of articles of iron or steel reached **US$367.92 million** in 2025 [120]
- Import penetration peaked following the COVID-19 recession but has since declined due to weaker local construction trends, the recent slump in global steel prices and the escalation in freight costs [4]
- The collapse of Challenge Steel (Christchurch, ~2018) illustrated the risks: debts of $10 million; quality assurance costs negated price advantage of Chinese steel [121]
- A Beca technical paper documents that NZS 3404 standards recognise steel only from Australia/NZ, Britain, Japan, and US; other sources require rigorous testing, increasing project costs [44]

**Corrected causal reasoning:** The competitive pressure from imports is a function of **global steel price dynamics, NZD/USD exchange rates, and ocean freight costs, not local tariff policy**. In NZ's zero-tariff environment (0% for all FTA partners), the 5% MFN rate is largely irrelevant for competition [28][29][30].

**Mitigating factors:**
- NZ's Steel Fabrication Certification (SFC) programme covering 92.5% of output provides quality assurance advantage [8]
- Seismic performance requirements favour local expertise and certified materials
- Quality compliance costs for imported steel (testing, certification) reduce price differential [44]
- Soaring freight costs (SCFI +39.85% YoY; BDI +138.04% YoY) make imported steel more expensive in NZD terms [42][43]

#### Risk 2: Labour and Skills Shortages

**Nature:** New Zealand faces a chronic shortage of skilled welders, fabricators, and engineering professionals, constraining capacity and increasing labour costs.

**Evidence:**
- Engineering New Zealand reports "New Zealand is experiencing a long-term engineering skills shortage, and this problem is increasing." An estimated 1,500–2,300 additional engineers are needed annually [122]
- Welding and fabrication roles (Fitter Welder, Welder, Metal Fabricator, Pressure Welder) were added to the Green List (Tier 1 Straight to Residence pathway) as of August 2025 [123]
- Despite unemployment rising to 5.2–5.3%, "skilled construction roles remain hard to fill" (RLB Infrastructure Forecast Q3 2025) [124]
- The average business in structural steel fabricating now employs fewer workers than five years ago, despite revenue per employee increasing [5]
- Construction employment fell from 308,500 (June 2023) to 295,100 (December 2025) [90]
- However, SCNZ reports that "70% of structural steel contractors employ an average of five apprentices" [3]

**Impact on fabricators:**
- Increases project costs and reduces competitiveness against imported steel
- Limits ability to scale production when infrastructure project pipeline accelerates
- Creates dependency on immigration policy and international labour markets

#### Risk 3: Global Trade Policy Uncertainty and Supply Chain Concentration

**Nature:** NZ steel fabricators face dual exposure: (1) global trade tensions affecting steel input costs and export competitiveness, and (2) concentration of supply chains through China and ASEAN.

**Evidence:**
- **US tariff escalation (50% on steel, 15% reciprocal on NZ):** On June 4, 2025 (NZT), US tariffs on steel and aluminium imports doubled to 50% under Section 232. On August 8, 2025, new 15% reciprocal tariffs were applied to NZ exports [125][126][127]
- The 50% US tariffs "could lead to a redistribution of trade flows to less protected markets" (Fitch Ratings, June 6, 2025), diverting steel to Asia-Pacific and affecting NZ pricing [128]
- Supply chain concentration: A Treasury-commissioned report notes NZ's "physical remoteness means that New Zealand's supply chains face unique physical connectivity risks" [129]
- Steel & Tube FY2025 results illustrate market impact: revenue fell 20% to $385.4M, volumes down 12% YoY, net loss of $24.4M. CEO Mark Malpass described "a very challenging bottom of cycle trading environment" [14]

**Impact on fabricators:**
- Global trade fragmentation creates uncertainty in steel pricing and availability
- Indirect exposure via NZ exporters facing US tariffs reduces overall economic activity
- Supply chain concentration (China accounts for 16% of NZ imports) creates vulnerability to disruptions

### 6.2 Top 3 Strategic Opportunities

#### Opportunity 1: Major Infrastructure Project Pipeline

**Nature:** New Zealand's National Infrastructure Pipeline represents an unprecedented volume of projects with significant structural steel demand, providing a multi-year demand horizon.

**Evidence:**
- **$275 billion** in projects across nearly 12,000 initiatives (September 2025) [98]
- **$181 billion** with committed or identified funding
- **$61 billion** currently under construction
- Between June and September 2025, **$28 billion** worth of projects progressed to confirmed funding
- **RoNS programme:** 17 prioritised roads, projected cost $40–50 billion [130]
- Infrastructure activity forecast to reach **$19.6 billion by 2030** [99]
- Structural steel demand from aggregated projects: **250,000–500,000+ tonnes** over 2026–2031

**Strategic implication:** Local fabricators with capacity, certification, and project-delivery capability are well-positioned to capture substantial work over the next 5–7 years. Steel & Tube CEO Mark Malpass confirmed: "The Government has advised that billions of dollars in Government-backed construction projects are set to get underway across New Zealand before the end of the year, and we are actively working to secure contracts for this work" [14].

#### Opportunity 2: Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication and Seismic Retrofit Demand

**Green Steel / Low-Carbon Fabrication:**
- **National Green Steel Limited** is developing NZ's first fully integrated structural steel manufacturing plant at Hampton Downs using EAF technology on recycled steel. Target: 200,000 tonnes annually [70][71]
- The **Sustainable Steel Council NZ (SSC)** has over 80 active members, developing a Steel RoadMap to Carbon Zero 2050 [6]
- **HERA's $10.3 million MBIE Endeavour-funded Construction 4.0** programme engages 50+ researchers on low-carbon design, AI-based quality monitoring, and circular economy systems [131]
- New Zealand Steel's $300 million EAF transition at Glenbrook will reduce site emissions by up to 1 million tonnes of CO2 annually and allow 56% scrap input [132]

**Seismic Retrofit Demand:**
- Structural steel holds >50% of NZ's multi-level construction market, >80% in Christchurch [3][8]
- Wellington has approximately **600 earthquake-prone buildings** (below 34% NBS), with about 20% designated as heritage structures [133]
- The 2016 Kaikōura earthquake significantly increased demand for proactive earthquake strengthening
- HERA's $9.47 million Endeavour-funded research aims to develop systems so buildings suffer no structural damage under severe earthquakes [131]

**Strategic implication:** Fabricators that invest in low-carbon production capabilities and SFC certification position themselves for premium pricing and preferred-supplier status on government and institutional projects with sustainability mandates.

#### Opportunity 3: Modular/Prefabricated Construction and Technology Adoption

**Evidence:**
- Global modular construction market: US$173.5 billion (2025) projected to US$302.0 billion by 2035, CAGR 5.7%. Steel leads material usage with 47.8% share [134]
- Structural steel offers **10-15% shorter project timelines** compared to alternative materials [18]
- **HERA's Construction 4.0 programme** ($10.3M) includes pioneering dynamic Life Cycle Assessment, AI-based quality monitoring, and Fab4.0Lab for advanced manufacturing [131]
- **D&H Steel Construction** embraces Industry 4.0 including robotics and data-driven decision-making [118]
- **Steel & Tube** reports adoption of 3D modelling and digital innovation investment [14]
- The Ministry of Education is committed to increasing Offsite Manufacturing (OSM) for school buildings, including volumetric supply and 3D printing structures [135]
- The Interim Inpatient Bed Capacity Programme ($314M) is delivering five modular, off-site constructed inpatient ward units at hospitals nationwide [136]

**Strategic implication:** Fabricators that invest in automation, BIM integration, and modular fabrication capabilities can capture margin improvements, reduce labour dependency, and differentiate themselves on speed and quality.

## 7. Conclusion

The New Zealand structural steel fabrication market enters mid-2026 at an inflection point following a pronounced construction cycle. The industry experienced a pre-COVID peak (2018–2019), a COVID-induced collapse (2020), a post-COVID boom driven by record-low interest rates (2021–2022), and then a sharp contraction caused by the most aggressive monetary tightening cycle in modern history (2023–2024).

**Key quantitative findings:**

1. **Industry size:** The structural steel fabrication industry (ANZSIC C2221) is valued at approximately **$1.8 billion** in 2025–2026, comprising **267 businesses** employing **4,100–5,000 workers**.

2. **Capacity and output:** Annual structural steel fabrication capacity expanded from 120,000 to 150,000 tonnes between 2018 and 2023. Current annual output is approximately **85,000–100,000 tonnes**, with spare capacity ranging from 28–41%.

3. **Tariff structure:** New Zealand's tariff regime provides **minimal protection** for local fabricators. The 5% MFN rate is effectively irrelevant because all major steel-exporting nations have FTAs providing duty-free access. The weighted average effective tariff rate is just **1.7%**. Competitive dynamics are driven by global steel prices, NZD/USD exchange rates, ocean freight costs, and quality certification requirements—not tariff policy.

4. **Infrastructure pipeline:** Aggregated structural steel demand from major projects (CRL, Te Kaha, hospitals, RoNS, renewable energy, data centres) is estimated at **250,000–500,000+ tonnes** over 2026–2031. While existing spare capacity is adequate for a low-demand scenario, the high-demand scenario will approach current capacity limits—supporting the case for the National Green Steel Plant (200,000 tonnes annual capacity) which would more than double current structural steel production capacity.

5. **Construction cycle:** The correlation between macroeconomic factors and steel fabrication is clear. New dwelling consents peaked at 49,538 in 2022 (OCR at 0.25–4.25%) and collapsed to 33,600 in 2024 (OCR at 5.50%). Steel & Tube's revenue followed a near-identical pattern: peaking at $599.1 million in FY2022 and falling to $385.4 million in FY2025. The easing of OCR from August 2024 is beginning to stimulate recovery, with Steel & Tube reporting "early signs of recovery" in May 2026.

The sector faces significant risks—import competition, labour shortages, and trade policy uncertainty—but is balanced by substantial opportunities in the infrastructure pipeline, green steel transition, seismic retrofit demand, and technology adoption. Fabricators that successfully invest in automation, quality certification, and low-carbon production will be best positioned to capture the multi-decade demand opportunity from New Zealand's infrastructure investment programme.
