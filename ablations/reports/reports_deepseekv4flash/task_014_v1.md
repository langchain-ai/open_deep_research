# Comprehensive Investment-Grade Analysis of NCD IPOs Open on December 8, 2025

## Executive Summary

This report provides a rigorous, investment-grade analysis of Non-Convertible Debenture (NCD) Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) open for subscription as of **December 8, 2025**, specifically evaluated for a retiree seeking fixed income. The analysis is designed to withstand scrutiny by institutional investment committees and compliance teams.

On December 8, 2025, **three NCD public issues were open for subscription**: Edelweiss Financial Services Limited (CRISIL A+/Stable), Muthoot Mercantile Limited (IND BBB/Stable), and KLM Axiva Finvest Limited (ACUITE BBB/IND BBB-/Stable). A fourth issue, Prachay Capital Limited (CRISIL BBB-/Stable), opened on December 11, 2025, and is included for comparative context.

**Key findings:**

- **No AAA or AA rated NCD IPOs were open** on December 8, 2025, representing a notable gap in the high-quality segment for risk-averse retirees.
- **Edelweiss Financial Services** (A+/Stable) offers the strongest credit profile among the open issues, with coupon rates ranging from 8.85% to 10.10% across ten series.
- **Section 80TTB tax benefits do NOT apply to NCD interest**, making post-tax yields for senior citizens lower than for bank fixed deposits or the Senior Citizens Savings Scheme (SCSS).
- **Credit risk is significant** for the BBB-rated issuers (Muthoot Mercantile, KLM Axiva, Prachay Capital) which carry higher default probabilities per historical CRISIL and India Ratings data.
- **The current interest rate environment is favorable** for fixed-income investors, with the RBI repo rate cut to 5.25% in December 2025, making NCD coupon rates relatively attractive on a spread basis.

**Primary Recommendation:** For risk-averse retirees specifically seeking fixed income, **Edelweiss Financial Services Series VIII (10-year, 10.10% annual coupon)** offers the best risk-adjusted yield among the open issues, though investors must accept the elevated debt-to-equity ratio (3.18x) and moderate credit rating. Retirees should consider allocating no more than 15-20% of their fixed-income portfolio to NCDs, with the remainder in safer instruments such as SCSS (8.2%), POMIS (7.4%), and bank FDs (up to 7.75% for senior citizens).

## 3. Post-Tax Yield Analysis

### 3.1 Assumptions

**Tax Regimes for FY 2025-26:**

**Senior Citizen (Age 60+, Old Tax Regime):**
- Assumed taxable income above ₹10 lakh (30% tax bracket)
- Tax rate: 30% + 4% cess = **31.2%**
- Section 80TTB: **NOT applicable to NCD interest** (only for bank/post office/co-operative society deposits) [11][12]
- Standard deduction: ₹50,000 (for pension/salary income only, not applicable to NCD interest)

**General Investor (Age <60, New Tax Regime - Default):**
- Assumed taxable income above ₹24 lakh (30% tax bracket)
- Tax rate: 30% + 4% cess = **31.2%**
- No deductions available for NCD interest
- New regime is default regime from FY 2023-24

### 3.2 Section 80TTB Clarification

A critical finding for senior citizen investors: **Section 80TTB of the Income Tax Act provides a deduction of up to ₹50,000 on interest income ONLY from deposits with banks, post offices, and co-operative societies.** Interest from corporate fixed deposits, bonds, or Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs) is **explicitly excluded** from this deduction [11][12]. This means senior citizens cannot claim any additional tax benefit on NCD interest beyond regular tax treatment.

- Investment grade defaults: **0.3%**
- Sub-investment grade defaults: **2.3%**
- Mean time to default: Increased to **42 months**

**Key Observations:**
- **AAA-rated instruments** have never defaulted in any 1- to 3-year period in CRISIL's history
- **A-rated instruments** (Edelweiss category) have a ~0.10% 1-year default probability, rising to ~1.0% over 5 years
- **BBB-rated instruments** (Muthoot Mercantile, KLM Axiva) have a ~0.40% 1-year default probability, rising to ~5.0% over 5 years
- **BBB- rated instruments** (Prachay Capital) carry even higher default risk

### 5.2 Major NCD Defaults in India - Lessons Learned

**IL&FS Crisis (September 2018): [19][20]**
- Defaulted on **₹91,000 crore** of debt despite being **AAA-rated** until shortly before collapse
- Credit spreads on NBFC AAA paper **doubled to ~160 bps** by February 2019
- Triggered systemic liquidity freeze, mutual fund redemption freezes
- **Key Lesson:** Even AAA ratings can fail; governance and liquidity management are critical

**DHFL Default (June 2019): [21][22]**
- Defaulted on secured and unsecured NCDs; downgraded from AAA to D
- Eventually resolved under IBC with Piramal Group acquisition at ~₹38,000 crore
- Consumer commission held rating agencies (CARE, Brickwork) and debenture trustee **legally liable** for negligence
- **Key Lesson:** Rating agencies and trustees can be held accountable for failures

**Reliance Capital (September 2019): [23][24]**
- ₹17,000 crore debt downgraded to 'D' after delayed NCD payment
- EPFO impacted with ₹535 crore interest default on ₹2,500 crore NCD investment
- **Key Lesson:** Concentration risk in single issuers can devastate portfolios

**SREI Group (2021): [25]**
- ₹4,730 crore in bonds defaulted; originally rated AA+ 
- NCLT moratorium left retail investors stranded
- **Key Lesson:** Even AA+ ratings can fail in stressed sectors

**Default Trends (FY2021 to FY2024): [18]**

### 5.4 Pattern Recognition for Current Cycle

The current environment shares characteristics with **pre-COVID (2019) conditions**:
- Moderate economic growth (GDP forecast 7.3% for 2025)
- Falling interest rates (125 bps cuts in 2025)
- Strong demand for yield from retail investors
- NBFC sector growing but with pockets of vulnerability

The key difference from 2018-19 is:
- **Stronger regulatory oversight** (SEBI's NCS Regulations 2021, mandatory listing, CDMDF)
- **Improved IBC framework** for recovery (~32% recovery rate on admitted claims)
- **Lower defaults** at historic lows
- **Better capital adequacy** across the NBFC sector

## 8. Regulatory Landscape & Policy Implications

### 8.1 SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021

The NCD IPO market operates under the SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021 (NCS Regulations), effective August 16, 2021. Key provisions: [34][35]

**Disclosure Requirements:**
- Filing of Draft Red Herring Prospectus (DRHP) with SEBI and designated stock exchange
- Mandatory disclosure of credit ratings, security creation, asset cover details
- Specialized disclosures by NBFCs/HFCs on lending portfolios and risk measures

**Listing Requirements:**
- NCDs must be listed on recognized stock exchanges (BSE/NSE) within **T+6 working days** from issue closure
- Allotment must be completed within **5 working days** of issue closure
- Effective January 1, 2024: All NCD issuances by entities with any listed NCDs outstanding as of December 31, 2023, must be **mandatorily listed**

**Allotment Process:**
- ASBA facility mandatory; UPI allowed for amounts up to ₹5 lakh
- Allotment on first-come, first-served basis for oversubscribed issues
- Minimum subscription: **75%** of the issue size

**Post-Listing Obligations:**
- Continuous disclosure of financial results, credit rating actions, and utilization of funds
- Reporting of any default in interest or principal payment

### 8.2 Debenture Trustee Requirements

- Mandatory appointment of debenture trustee for NCDs with maturity beyond 18 months
- Trustee monitors issuer compliance, enforces security on behalf of debenture holders
- Ensures adequate charged property is maintained
- SEBI Master Circular for Debenture Trustees issued May 16, 2024, consolidating all procedural requirements
- The 2025 DHFL consumer commission ruling established that trustees and rating agencies can be held legally liable for negligence, strengthening investor protection [22]

### 8.3 Tax Treatment - Key Changes

**TDS on NCD Interest (Section 193):**
- Effective April 1, 2023: TDS at **10%** on interest on **listed NCDs** (previous exemption removed)
- TDS threshold under Section 193: No TDS required on interest up to ₹10,000
- **Form 15H** (for senior citizens) or **Form 15G** (for others) can be submitted to avoid TDS if total income is below taxable limit
- **Union Budget 2025:** TDS threshold under Section 194A doubled to **₹1,00,000** for senior citizens (applies to bank FD interest, not NCD interest) [36]

**Capital Gains Tax on NCDs: [37][38]**

| Holding Period | Listed NCDs | Unlisted NCDs |
|---------------|------------|---------------|
| **Short-term** (≤12 months listed; ≤36 months unlisted) | Taxed at **slab rate** | Taxed at **slab rate** |
| **Long-term** (>12 months listed; >36 months unlisted) | **12.5% without indexation** (post-July 23, 2024) | 20% with indexation |

**Finance Act 2024 Changes (Effective July 23, 2024):**
- LTCG rate on listed bonds/NCDs reduced from 10% to **12.5%** but **indexation benefit removed** for most assets
- For land and buildings, taxpayers have option to choose: 20% with indexation or 12.5% without
- Holding period for listed securities simplified to **12 months** (from 36 months earlier)

**Section 80TTB:**
- Provides deduction of up to ₹50,000 on interest income from **bank deposits, post office deposits, and co-operative society deposits only**
- **NCD interest is NOT eligible** for this deduction
- Only available under the **old tax regime**
- Union Budget 2025 proposals to increase limit to ₹1,00,000 were **not enacted** [11][12]

### 8.4 Proposed Regulatory Changes

**NITI Aayog Report (December 2025) - "Deepening the Corporate Bond Market in India": [39]**

Key recommendations:
- Enhanced inter-regulatory coordination (SEBI, RBI, MCA)
- Simplified disclosure norms to reduce reliance on private placements (98% of market)
- Corporate Bond Dealers (CBDs) to improve liquidity
- Lower minimum investment thresholds for retail investors
- Standardized green bond taxonomies
- Target: Expand corporate bond market to ₹100-120 trillion by 2030

**SEBI's Corporate Bond Market Development Working Group:**
- Promoting reissuance of bonds under the same ISIN for liquidity
- Encouraging market making by banks, primary dealers, and brokers
- Uniform valuation framework across regulators
- Rationalizing stamp duties

**Pending Concerns:**
- AMFI has requested restoration of indexation benefits for debt mutual funds
- Calls for harmonization of holding periods for LTCG on direct debt vs. debt funds

5. **Favorable regulatory backdrop:** SEBI's strengthened NCS Regulations, mandatory listing, and enhanced debenture trustee oversight provide a better investor protection framework than during the IL&FS/DHFL era.

- Limit allocation to **10-15%** of total fixed-income portfolio

- **Diversify** across AAA PSU bonds and sovereign instruments for the core portfolio
