### 1.2 IFR Suppression Mechanism: Why Saudi Arabia Data Is Unavailable

The International Federation of Robotics (IFR) **suppresses Saudi Arabia-specific industrial robot installation data** and reports it only under the **"Rest of Asia" (RAS)** territory group. This suppression is due to the IFR Statistical Department's strict data confidentiality measures.

**The suppression mechanism explained:**

The IFR Statistical Department implements **reclassification and suppression mechanisms** to comply with data privacy and antitrust regulations [7]. The M2 confidentiality rules require that data is only published when:

1. **A minimum of 3 suppliers** report data for a given country or category
2. **No single supplier accounts for an overwhelming share** of the reported data

When these minimum observation thresholds are not met—as is the case for Saudi Arabia, which has a relatively small industrial robot market with few major suppliers—the specific country data is **suppressed** and aggregated into regional groups like "Rest of Asia" (RAS) [8].

The World Robotics report covers **approximately 40 countries** with detailed country-specific data from 2019–2024. Countries that do not meet the minimum thresholds for individual publication are grouped into regional residual categories [7]. The IFR Statistical Department contact for detailed data inquiries is Dr. Christopher Müller (Director), with project managers Nina Kutzbach (Industrial Robots) and Dr. Anne Jurkat (Service Robots). Phone: +49 69-6603-1191, E-mail: statistics(at)ifr.org [7].

**Results of suppression:** Saudi Arabia is NOT listed among the ~40 countries with individual robot density or installation data. The IFR's World Robotics 2025 report covers robot density for the top 20–30 countries globally; Saudi Arabia does not appear in these rankings [9][10][11].

**Alternative data from IndexBox:** While IFR data is suppressed, the IndexBox report provides an alternative estimate. The Middle East market for industrial robots reached **38,000 units** valued at $710 million in 2024. Saudi Arabia dominates with **75% of consumption** = approximately **28,500 units consumed in Saudi Arabia in 2024**. Saudi Arabia also accounts for **88% of production** (= ~29,000 units produced). Per capita consumption in Saudi Arabia was **779 units per million persons** in 2024 [12].

**Reconciling different estimates:** The IndexBox figure of ~28,500 units consumed in 2024 and the IMARC figure of 2,100 units in 2025 appear to represent different definitions. The IndexBox figure likely includes all industrial robot purchases/imports (including oil & gas and non-manufacturing applications, plus warehouse/distribution/logistics robots), while the IMARC figure covers robots in manufacturing specifically using a narrower definition. Grand View Research's $207.4M market in 2024, at an average price of roughly $15,000–$25,000 per robot, would imply roughly 8,000–14,000 units, sitting between the two estimates.

### 1.4 Triangulated Market Size Using Multiple Methodologies

#### Methodology A: GDP-Based Estimation

**Manufacturing GDP Data:** Saudi Arabia's manufacturing value added reached **$192.67 billion (SAR 722.5 billion)** in 2024, representing **15.57% of GDP** according to the World Bank [17]. Manufacturing, value added as a percentage of GDP was **16%** in 2024 [18].

**Global Automation Investment Benchmarks:** Global manufacturers invest approximately **2–5% of manufacturing output** in automation and control systems, with leading automotive and electronics sectors at the higher end (3–5%) and process industries at the lower end (1–3%). These benchmarks derive from the global factory automation market relative to global manufacturing output [19].

**Resulting Estimated Ranges:**
- Conservative (2% of manufacturing value added): **~$3.85 billion annually**
- Moderate (3% of manufacturing value added): **~$5.78 billion annually**
- Aggressive (5% of manufacturing value added): **~$9.63 billion annually**

#### Methodology B: Employment-Based Calculation

**Manufacturing Workforce Data:** Saudi Arabia's total workforce reached **19.03 million** by Q4 2025 [20]. Employment within NIDLP sectors exceeded **2.433 million workers** in 2024, with over **508,000 new jobs created** that year [21]. Factory workforce: 9,991 factories employing **980,000 workers** (Q4 2024) [22].

**Automation Capex Per Worker Benchmarks:** In developed economies, automation capital expenditure per manufacturing worker ranges from **$10,000 to $30,000** for initial automation investments [19]. For transitioning economies like Saudi Arabia, annual automation investment typically represents 5–10% of total capital expenditure per worker.

**Employment-based deployment estimate:** The total Saudi industrial workforce (2.43M) is about 1/15th of Germany's manufacturing workforce. Germany had ~27,000 new robots in 2024 and a stock of roughly 270,000. A proportional estimate (1/15th) would suggest a Saudi robot stock of roughly 18,000 units and annual installations around 1,800—which aligns closely with the IMARC figure of 2,100 units (2025) in manufacturing.

**Resulting Estimated Range:**
- Using 2.4 million industrial workers × estimated annual automation investment of $500–$3,000 per worker: **$1.2 billion to $7.2 billion annually**

#### Methodology C: IFR-Based Estimation

Since Saudi Arabia lacks standalone IFR data, triangulation through proxy estimates:

1. **IndexBox Saudi share (75%)** of Middle East consumption = ~28,500 units total consumed in 2024 [12]
2. **IMARC's manufacturing-only** estimate of 2,100 units in 2025 [3]
3. The difference (~1,400 units in non-manufacturing vs ~26,000–27,000 in manufacturing) suggests IMARC's definition may be much narrower, or IndexBox may capture inventory/warehouse/distribution robots plus flow-through of imports that are re-exported

**Conclusion on unit count:** Saudi Arabia likely had roughly **2,000–3,000 industrial robot installations per year in manufacturing in the 2023–2025 period**, with total industrial robot consumption (including service/warehouse/logistics/non-manufacturing) potentially significantly higher. Precise IFR-confirmed installation data is suppressed and only available through purchase of the full World Robotics database.

### 2.3 Future Factories Program (Factories of the Future / 4IR Program)

**Official Launch Date:** **July 20, 2022** — launched by the Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources [31].

**Primary Source:** https://www.mim.gov.sa/en/initiatives-programs/industrial-sector-initiatives/future-factories-program-initiative [31]

**4,000 Factory Target — Verified Against Primary Source:**

> *"The program aims to shift **4,000 factories** from dependence on low-wage and low-skilled laborers to operational efficiency, automation and application of advanced industrial solutions and practices. Through Two Main Paths: The first targets new factories so that they are designed and constructed according to high standards in manufacturing and production efficiency. The second is for the existing factories so that they are converted into factories that adopt the application of standards of operational excellence and advanced technologies."* [31]

**SIRI Methodology:** The program follows the SIRI methodology (Smart Industry Readiness Index), accredited by the World Economic Forum, involving stages including self-assessment, certified assessments (CERI), development and implementation of a digital transformation plan, and post-transformation certification [32].

**Two Tracks:**
1. Track for **new factories** — ensuring high standards in design and production efficiency
2. Track for **existing factories** — adopting operational excellence and advanced technologies, with classification based on employee count, investment size, and export value [31]

**Collaboration:** Operates in collaboration with the **International Center for Industrial Transformation (INCIT)** , sponsored by the NIDLP and the Ministry of Communications and Information Technology [31].

**Service Page:** https://industry.sa/en/services/fourth_industrial_revolution_program [33]

**Budget Figures:** No standalone budget figure was found for the Future Factories Program itself. However, the program is supported by **financial aid** including SIDF's Tanafusiya Program which offers financing for automation, digitization, and Industry 4.0 solutions with:
- Grace period up to **24 months**
- Minimum tenor of **7 years**
- Faster turnaround time within **8 weeks** for fast-track projects
- Financial incentives subject to terms and conditions [34]

**4IR Program Grant:**
- Covers up to **80% of project cost** (maximum SAR 300,000)
- Service and technology providers can join certified lists for direct contracting with factories [33]

**Related Initiative — Industrial Beacons Program:** "Aims to empower leading national factories to adopt Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies, improve their production efficiency, and qualify them for inclusion in the World Economic Forum's Global Lighthouse Network" — targeting **14 Saudi factories** to be recognized as global industrial beacons by 2030 [35].

**Achieved (2024):** MODON successfully converted **50 factories** into future advanced factories using Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies, aiming for 100 by 2025 [36].

## 4. Three Practical Implications for a Sales Representative

### 4.1 Implication 1: Target the "Saudization-Driven Automation" Sweet Spot

**The Opportunity:**
The combination of rising labor costs from **Nitaqat Saudization quotas** (30% for engineering professions effective July 27, 2025; phased quotas for accounting roles) and aggressive government financial incentives creates a **compelling ROI case for mid-sized manufacturers** (50–500 employees, SAR 10–100 million revenue) who currently rely heavily on expatriate labor [57].

**Actionable Sales Strategy:**

1. **Build an ROI calculator** modeling:
   - Annual labor cost savings from replacing expatriate workers at SAR 3,000–8,000/month
   - Capital cost reduction from SIDF financing (up to 75%)
   - Standard Incentives (up to 35% of initial investment, capped at SAR 50 million)
   - 5-year TCO comparison of automation vs. manual production

2. **Target manufacturers in sectors with highest Saudization pressure:**
   - Plastics manufacturing
   - Food and beverage processing
   - Building materials production
   - Metal fabrication and light manufacturing

3. **Develop partnerships with SIDF-approved consultants** who can help prospects navigate the loan application process—this differentiates from competitors selling only hardware.

4. **Leverage the Future Factories Program as a door-opener:**
   - Offer free SIRI self-assessment guidance
   - Position as a transformation partner rather than a hardware vendor
   - Reference the 4IR Program Grant covering up to 80% of project costs (max SAR 300,000)

### 4.2 Implication 2: Position Automation as a Local Content Enabler for IKTVA Compliance

**The Opportunity:**
IKTVA mandates that **70% of Aramco's procurement spending** stays in-Kingdom (as of February 2026, with a target of 75% by 2030). The program has identified **200 localization opportunities** across 12 industrial sectors representing an estimated **$28 billion annual market**. Aramco's annual procurement budget exceeds **$30 billion** [44][45][46].

**Step-by-Step IKTVA Vendor Registration Process:**

**Step 1: Establish Legal Entity** — Foreign companies must establish a licensed legal entity in Saudi Arabia (LLC or branch office) with licenses from:
- Ministry of Investment (MISA) — obtain investment license
- Commercial Registration (CR) from Ministry of Commerce
- VAT registration with ZATCA
- Chamber of Commerce membership

*Typical timeline: 8–16 weeks (22 weeks if entity setup included)* [100]

**Step 2: Register on SAP Ariba** — Access via: https://www.aramco.com/en/what-we-do/suppliers/become-a-supplier [101]

**Step 3: Pre-qualification** — Complete technical and financial pre-qualification, including possible factory inspections and audits

**Step 4: IKTVA Compliance** — Submit IKTVA survey and 5-year action plan. Procurement bid scoring significantly weights IKTVA scores [102]

**Step 5: Vendor Assessment & Formal Listing** — Assessment focuses on technical capability, financial health, and IKTVA localization score

**Documentation Required [101][103]:**
- Valid Commercial Registration
- MISA license (foreign investors)
- ZATCA tax certificates
- Insurance certificates
- Bank letters and authorized signatory letters
- Cybersecurity certification
- Aramco Supplier Code of Conduct acknowledgment

**Contact Information:**
- IKTVA Program Website: https://www.aramco.com/en/what-we-do/commercial-ecosystems/iktva
- IKTVA Portal: iktva.aramco.com (IKTVA Portal Solution / iPS)
- Aramco Supplier Network Communication (SNC) Portal

**Actionable Sales Strategy:**

1. **Create an "IKTVA Automation Package"** — bundled solution (PLC, SCADA, robotics, local maintenance support) that helps Aramco suppliers demonstrate local R&D and increase local value-add.

2. **Target Tier 2 and Tier 3 Aramco suppliers** (50–200 employees, often family-owned) who face the most pressure to improve IKTVA scores but lack capital or expertise.

3. **Offer IKTVA score improvement projections** as part of sales proposals — quantify how automation increases local content contribution.

4. **Frame automation investment as essential** for capturing the $28 billion annual market in localization opportunities identified by IKTVA.

### 4.3 Implication 3: Giga-Project Supply Chain Adjacency Strategy

**The Opportunity:**
While Siemens and ABB have had limited direct awards at giga-projects (see Section 3), the **supply chain opportunity is enormous**. Every giga-project creates demand for thousands of suppliers and sub-contractors who need to automate their own factories to meet project requirements.

**Step-by-Step NEOM Supplier Portal Registration:**

**Official Portal:** https://www.neom.com/en-us/our-business/suppliers [104]

**Step 1: Access the Ariba Supplier Self-Registration Form**
Access via: http://neom.supplier.mn2.ariba.com/ad/selfRegistration

**Step 2: Complete Self-Registration Form** — Upload relevant documents (commercial registration certificates or JV ZAKAT certificates for JVs), accept key agreements including NEOM's Supplier Code of Conduct and Confidentiality Agreement.

**Step 3: Receive Invitation Email** — Once submitted and reviewed, suppliers receive confirmation. If approved, an invitation email contains a registration link to finalize.
