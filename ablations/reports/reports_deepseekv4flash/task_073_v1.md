# Comprehensive Comparative Analysis of Autism Diagnosis Criteria: DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (2019)

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnostic criteria as defined in the DSM-5 (2013, with Text Revision in 2022) and the ICD-11 (2019, effective 2022), with a specific focus on how each manual defines and weights sensory processing differences. The analysis covers six distinct domains: a side-by-side criteria comparison, peer-reviewed evidence synthesis, clinical guideline positions, advocacy organization perspectives, diagnostic safety risks, and reimbursement/coding implications. The ICD-11, which came into effect on January 1, 2022, represents the first major revision to the WHO's classification system in 28 years and has been adopted by over 60 countries, while the DSM-5-TR (2022) remains the standard diagnostic framework in the United States. Both systems have converged significantly by abandoning the previous subcategories (Autistic Disorder, Asperger Syndrome, PDD-NOS) in favor of a unified autism spectrum diagnosis, yet important differences remain in their structure, specificity, and potential clinical impact.

## Section A: Side-by-Side Criteria Table

### A.1 Core Social-Communication Criteria

| **Dimension** | **DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR)** | **ICD-11 (2019/2022)** |
|---------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Criterion Structure** | Three sub-criteria (A1, A2, A3), **all three must be present** | Broad description with no fixed number of features required |
| **A1: Social-Emotional Reciprocity** | Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity, ranging from abnormal social approach and failure of normal back-and-forth conversation; to reduced sharing of interests, emotions, or affect; to failure to initiate or respond to social interactions | Persistent deficits in the ability to initiate and sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication |
| **A2: Nonverbal Communication** | Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors used for social interaction, ranging from poorly integrated verbal and nonverbal communication; to abnormalities in eye contact and body language or deficits in understanding and use of gestures; to total lack of facial expressions and nonverbal communication | Difficulties in understanding verbal and nonverbal social cues; integrating language with body language; initiating and maintaining conversations |
| **A3: Relationships** | Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships, ranging from difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts; to difficulties in sharing imaginative play or in making friends; to absence of interest in peers | Sustaining peer relationships; social awareness; empathy; sharing interests |
| **Minimum Features Required** | **All 3** social-communication criteria must be present (DSM-5-TR added the word "all": "as manifested by **all** of the following") | At least **1** social-communication feature documented alongside at least 1 repetitive behavior feature |
| **Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder** | Separate diagnosis available for those with social communication deficits not meeting full ASD criteria [APA, 2013] | No equivalent separate category |
| **Number of Possible Presentations** | Fixed polythetic approach with specific counting requirements | Approximately **304 different presentations** of autism described [Kamp-Becker, 2024] |

**Key Insight:** The DSM-5's requirement for deficits in *all three* social-communication areas creates a more restrictive threshold compared to the ICD-11's flexible approach. The DSM-5-TR (2022) added explicit language requiring "all of the following," strengthening this requirement. The ICD-11's allowance for diagnosis with only a single social-communication feature represents the most significant structural difference between the two systems.

### A.2 Sensory Processing Mention and Weighting

| **Aspect** | **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|------------|-----------|------------|
| **Location in Criteria** | Criterion B4 (under Restricted, Repetitive Patterns of Behavior) — one of four sub-criteria | Under restricted, repetitive behaviors domain — integrated as a diagnostic requirement |
| **Exact Wording** | "Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interests in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)" | "Atypical responses to sensory stimuli" / "atypical sensory sensitivities" |
| **Required for Diagnosis?** | **No** — only 2 of 4 B criteria must be met, so B4 alone is insufficient | **No** — it is one potential manifestation of RRBs, not individually required |
| **Change from Previous Edition** | **New** in DSM-5 (2013) — not present in DSM-IV | **New** in ICD-11 — not present in ICD-10 |
| **Sensory Coverage** | Hyper-reactivity, hypo-reactivity, and unusual sensory interests explicitly named | Atypical responses to sensory stimuli including sounds, textures, lights, pain, temperature |
| **Diagnostic Utility** | Questioned by research — Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015) found only "failure to respond to noises" had sensitivity >0.75, but specificity was poor (0.34) | Broader inclusion but may come at cost of diagnostic specificity |

**Key Insight:** Both systems now recognize sensory processing differences as diagnostically relevant, representing a major convergence. However, the clinical utility of sensory features as diagnostic criteria has been questioned. The DSM-5's placement of sensory issues as one of four B-criteria (requiring two total) creates a higher threshold for individuals whose primary presentation involves sensory differences.

### A.3 Severity Specifiers

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **3 Levels of Support** rated separately for (a) social communication and (b) restricted, repetitive behaviors | **5 Profiles** based on intellectual development and functional language ability |
| **Level 1:** "Requiring Support" — difficulty initiating social interactions, rigid behavior limits functioning | **6A02.0:** Without intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 2:** "Requiring Substantial Support" — marked deficits in verbal and nonverbal communication; inflexible behavior obvious to casual observer | **6A02.1:** With intellectual development disorder and with mild or no impairment of functional language |
| **Level 3:** "Requiring Very Substantial Support" — severe deficits in communication; extreme difficulty coping with change; behaviors interfere with all aspects of life | **6A02.2:** Without intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| Severity should not be used to determine service eligibility; requires individualized assessment [APA, 2013] | **6A02.3:** With intellectual development disorder and with impaired functional language |
| Only 24.7% of participants showed concordant severe classifications across autism symptoms, cognitive, and adaptive measures in one study [PMC, 2014] | **6A02.4:** With intellectual development disorder and with absence of functional language |
| | **6A02.5:** With other specified intellectual development disorder or language impairment |
| | Additional codes: 6A02.Y (Other specified) and 6A02.Z (Unspecified) |

**Key Insight:** The two systems take fundamentally different approaches to severity classification. The DSM-5 rates severity of core autism symptoms (social communication and RRBs separately), while the ICD-11 classifies based on co-occurring intellectual disability and language impairment. The ICD-11 approach may better capture functional outcomes but does not directly rate core autism symptom severity.

### A.4 Age of Onset and Developmental Period Requirements

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion C:** "Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period" | "The onset of the disorder occurs during the developmental period – typically in early childhood – but characteristic symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed limited capacities" |
| Removed previous age-of-onset cutoff of 3 years (from DSM-IV) | No specific age cutoff — onset "during the developmental period" |
| Symptoms "usually in the second year of life," though recognition can vary | Explicitly recognizes that some individuals experience distress and social challenges later in adolescence or adulthood |
| Symptoms "may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities" | Recognizes masking/camouflaging: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" |
| **No explicit mention of regression or loss of skills** in diagnostic criteria | **Explicitly includes "loss of previously acquired skills"** (regression) as a diagnostic consideration |
| Approximate age 8 or younger: "symptoms must be present in early childhood (age approximately 8 or younger)" [UW Guidelines] | "Onset of any time in life" — some sources indicate this is permissible [Autism Science Foundation Podcast] |

**Key Insight:** Both systems recognize that symptoms may not fully manifest until social demands exceed capacities, enabling late diagnosis in higher-functioning individuals. The ICD-11's explicit inclusion of regression and its potentially broader interpretation of onset timing represent meaningful differences that could affect diagnostic capture of certain populations.

### A.5 Functional Impairment Thresholds

| **DSM-5** | **ICD-11** |
|-----------|------------|
| **Criterion D:** "Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning" | "The symptoms result in significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational or other important areas of functioning" |
| **Treatment as standalone criterion** (must be met independently) | **Embedded** in overall diagnostic description |
| **Criterion E:** "Symptoms are not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay" — must be ruled out | Rett syndrome explicitly excluded (LD90.4) |
| Areas specified: social, occupational, or other important areas | Areas specified: personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important areas — broader range than DSM-5 |
| Masking/camouflaging implicitly recognized through onset flexibility | Explicitly recognizes masking: "some individuals may compensate effectively despite these deficits" — impairment can still be present |
| Observable impairment typically required | Impairment "observable in all settings, albeit with contextual variations" |

**Key Insight:** The ICD-11 includes "educational" and "personal" and "family" as explicit impairment domains, which are absent from DSM-5's wording. The ICD-11's explicit recognition of compensation/masking may better capture individuals who maintain surface-level functioning but experience significant internal distress.

## Section B: Evidence Synthesis of Peer-Reviewed Sources Comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 Criteria

### Critical Note on Available Evidence

As of May 2026, **no direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies** comparing DSM-5 and ICD-11 diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, or specificity for ASD have been published. The ICD-11 was released in 2019 and became effective on January 1, 2022, which has not allowed sufficient time for large-scale empirical validation studies comparing the two systems directly. The best available evidence consists of: (1) conceptual critiques comparing the two systems, (2) studies comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria (DSM-IV-TR or ICD-10), and (3) reliability studies of DSM-5 criteria. These are synthesized below.

### B.1 Kamp-Becker, I. (2024). "Autism spectrum disorder in ICD-11—a critical reflection of its possible impact on clinical practice and research." *Molecular Psychiatry*. Published online. PMCID: PMC11153155.

- **Study Design:** Perspective article / critical reflection
- **Sample Size:** Not applicable (conceptual analysis)
- **Key Statistics:** No empirical data; provides conceptual analysis
- **Key Findings:**
  - ICD-11 allows a "high variety in symptom combinations, resulting in an extremely diverse picture, possibly at the expense of precision"
  - "This conceptualization can hardly be differentiated from other mental disorders and autism-like traits"
  - ICD-11 "moves away from an observable, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorder to a disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively"
  - "This bears a **large danger of false positive diagnoses**, of further increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments"
  - ICD-11 allows diagnosis with only 1 social-communication feature + 1 repetitive behavior feature, with onset "at any time in life"
  - For research, the increased heterogeneity "will hamper replication, limit understanding of ASD etiology, and delay the realization of precision medicine"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Very Low** (perspective/opinion article; no empirical data collection; not a systematic review)

*Relevance:* This is the most directly relevant source comparing the conceptual underpinnings of DSM-5 and ICD-11 for autism. While not an empirical study, it provides expert critique of ICD-11's potential impact.

### B.2 McPartland, J.C., Reichow, B., & Volkmar, F.R. (2012). "Sensitivity and specificity of proposed DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for autism spectrum disorder." *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 51(4): 368-383. PMCID: PMC3424065.

- **Study Design:** Retrospective analysis of DSM-IV field trial data
- **Sample Size:** 933 participants total (657 clinically diagnosed with ASD including Autistic Disorder, Asperger's Disorder, and PDD-NOS; 276 diagnosed with non-autistic disorders)
- **Key Statistics:**
  - 60.6% of cases with clinical ASD diagnosis met proposed DSM-5 criteria
  - Specificity: **94.9%** — high rate of accurate exclusion
  - Sensitivity varied by subgroup: Autistic Disorder **76%**; Asperger's Disorder **25%**; PDD-NOS **28%**
  - IQ < 70: **70% met criteria**; IQ ≥ 70: **46% met criteria**
  - Altering DSM-5 criteria to allow 2 of 3 social-communication criteria instead of 3 of 3 improved sensitivity to **.75** and specificity to **.85** (vs .61 and .95 original)
  - "Revised criteria improve specificity, but exclude a substantial portion of cognitively able individuals and those with ASDs other than Autistic Disorder"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Low to Moderate** (large sample, but retrospective application of *proposed* criteria; no direct ICD-11 comparison)

*Limitation:* Compares DSM-5 to DSM-IV-TR/clinical diagnoses, NOT to ICD-11. Indirect relevance.

### B.3 Maenner, M.J., et al. (2014). "Potential Impact of DSM-5 Criteria on Autism Spectrum Disorder Prevalence Estimates." *JAMA Psychiatry*, 71(3): 292-300. PMCID: PMC4041577.

- **Study Design:** Population-based, cross-sectional surveillance study (ADDM Network)
- **Sample Size:** 644,883 eight-year-old children from 14 U.S. sites; 6,577 children identified with ASD based on DSM-IV-TR criteria
- **Key Statistics:**
  - Among 6,577 children with DSM-IV-TR ASD diagnosis: **5,339 (81.2%) met DSM-5 ASD criteria**
  - With intellectual disability: **86.6%** met DSM-5 criteria
  - Without intellectual disability: **72.5%** met DSM-5 criteria (P < .001)
  - 304 children met DSM-5 criteria but not ADDM Network ASD case status
  - ASD prevalence per 1,000 for 2008 would have been **10.0** using DSM-5 vs **11.3** using DSM-IV-TR criteria
  - Children with community professional identification of ASD: **84.8% met DSM-5 criteria** vs **69.7%** without (P < .001)
  - "Prevalence estimates will likely be lower under DSM-5 than under DSM-IV-TR diagnostic criteria, although this effect could be tempered by future adaptation of diagnostic practices"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (large population-based sample; retrospective record review; no direct child examinations; no ICD-11 comparison)

*Limitation:* Compares DSM-5 to DSM-IV-TR, NOT to ICD-11. Demonstrates the restrictive effect of DSM-5 criteria compared to previous systems.

### B.4 Wilson, C.E., et al. (2013). "Comparison of ICD-10R, DSM-IV-TR and DSM-5 in an adult autism spectrum disorder diagnostic clinic." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 43(11): 2515-2525.

- **Study Design:** Cross-sectional clinical study
- **Sample Size:** 150 intellectually able adults (mean IQ in normal range)
- **Key Statistics:**
  - Of those diagnosed with ASD using ICD-10R: **56% met DSM-5 ASD criteria**
  - Additional **19%** met DSM-5 draft criteria for Social Communication Disorder
  - Of those diagnosed with Autistic Disorder/Asperger Syndrome on DSM-IV-TR: **78% met DSM-5 ASD criteria**
  - "Sensitivity of DSM-5 was significantly increased by reducing the number of criteria required, or by rating 'uncertain' criteria as 'present', without sacrificing specificity"
  - "Reduced rates of ASD diagnosis may mean some ASD individuals will be unable to access clinical services"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Low to Moderate** (clinical sample; clear methodology; compares DSM-5 draft to ICD-10R, NOT to ICD-11)

*Limitation:* Compares DSM-5 (draft) to ICD-10R, NOT to ICD-11. Demonstrates reduced sensitivity of DSM-5 compared to ICD-10R in an adult high-functioning population.

### B.5 CDC ADDM Network / Rutgers University (2020). "Defining in Detail and Evaluating Reliability of DSM-5 Criteria for Autism Spectrum Disorder Among Children."

- **Study Design:** Multi-site reliability study using ADDM Network data
- **Sample Size:** Thousands of evaluations across 11 ADDM sites (2014 and 2016 surveillance years); 34 clinicians
- **Key Statistics:**
  - Phase 1 (South Carolina pilot): overall ASD case status kappa = **0.62 to 0.83** (substantial to excellent)
  - Phase 2 (11 ADDM sites): kappa up to **0.89** for ASD classification
  - "Interrater reliability for each of the DSM-5 diagnostic categories and overall ASD classification was high (defined as very good .60–.79 to excellent ≥ .80 Kappa values)"
  - Reliability was consistent across sex, race/ethnicity, and cognitive levels
  - Clinicians able to reliably distinguish criteria A1 (social-emotional reciprocity) and A3 (relationships)
  - "Consistency was found in criteria endorsement and ASD classification across multiple demographic factors and cognitive abilities, indicating robust applicability"
- **GRADE Quality Rating:** **Moderate** (large multi-site reliability study; well-designed; but only examines DSM-5, no ICD-11 comparison; limited to children ~8 years old; record review only)

*Limitation:* Only examines DSM-5, not ICD-11. Demonstrates good reliability for DSM-5 across diverse populations.

### Summary of Evidence Gaps

The most critical finding is the **absence of direct DSM-5 vs ICD-11 empirical comparisons**. The five studies above provide indirect evidence: DSM-5 significantly reduces ASD diagnoses compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R (by 19-44% depending on population), particularly for higher-functioning individuals. The Kamp-Becker (2024) critique raises concerns that ICD-11's much broader criteria may swing the pendulum toward over-diagnosis. A direct head-to-head comparison applying both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria to the same clinical population remains an urgent research priority.

## Section C: Clinical Guideline Positions

### C.1 World Health Organization (WHO)

The WHO designed and released the ICD-11 on June 18, 2018, after a decade of development and over 10,000 revision proposals. It was **unanimously adopted by the 72nd World Health Assembly on May 25, 2019**, and came into effect on January 1, 2022 [PAHO/WHO, 2022].

- **Implementation Status:** As of early 2026, **64 countries** are at different implementation stages of ICD-11, with some actively using it [Wikipedia, ICD-11]
- **Design Philosophy:** The WHO "designed the ICD-11 to be broadly applicable across cultures worldwide" — committee member David Skuse stated: "What we are trying to do with ICD-11 is to create a set of criteria that are so broad that they could be applied anywhere in the world" [The Transmitter, 2017]
- **Autism-Alignment:** The ICD-11 aligns closely with DSM-5 by consolidating Asperger's Syndrome, Childhood Disintegrative Disorder, and other pervasive developmental disorders under the umbrella term "Autism Spectrum Disorder" [Autisme Europe, 2018]
- **Sensory Change:** The ICD-11 includes atypical sensory responses as a diagnostic requirement for the first time, whereas ICD-10 did not consider sensory processing as a core diagnostic feature [PMC, 2023]
- **Scope:** ICD-11 covers approximately **85,000 entities**, is fully digital, and available online free of charge [Wikipedia, ICD-11]
- **WHO has stopped maintaining ICD-10** since 2018; future enhancements will only appear in ICD-11 [WHO, 2019]

### C.2 American Psychiatric Association (APA)

The APA publishes the **DSM-5 (2013) and DSM-5-TR (2022)** as the standard diagnostic manual for mental disorders in the United States.

- **Key Position:** The APA has **not issued a formal comparative position statement** endorsing one system over the other for autism specifically. The APA maintains the DSM as the US clinical standard while acknowledging that ICD codes are required for billing and insurance [APA Website]
- **DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 Alignment:** A comprehensive organization- and category-level comparison published in PMC (2021) found that among 103 disorders present in both DSM-5 and ICD-11: **19.4% have major diagnostic differences**, **40.8% have minor definitional differences**, **9.7% differ due to greater specification in DSM-5**, and **30.1% are essentially identical** [PMC, 2021]
- **Expert Consensus:** Experts cited in The Transmitter noted: "DSM-5 was conceptually correct and ICD-11 is following a similar view" (David Skuse), and "[ICD-11] is taking some of the better parts of DSM without falling into the same pitfalls" (Fred Volkmar) [The Transmitter, 2017]
- **DSM-5-TR Changes:** The 2022 Text Revision revised wording in criterion A to improve clarity, changing "manifested by the following" to "as manifested by **all** of the following" [TRICARE West, DSM-5-TR Checklist]
- **Key Difference in Audience:** The DSM-5 is "a purchase-only manual principally designed for clinicians" and is not publicly available online for free, whereas "the ICD-11 is available online" free of charge [Autism Science Foundation]

### C.3 American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

The AAP published its key clinical report **"Identification, Evaluation, and Management of Children With Autism Spectrum Disorder"** (updated January 2020) [AAP Clinical Report, 2020].

- **Screening Recommendations:** The AAP recommends "general developmental screening at 9, 18, and 30 months and autism screening at 18 and 24 months" [AAP, 2020]
- **DSM-5 as Reference Framework:** The AAP's clinical report uses **DSM-5 as the reference diagnostic framework** throughout—the AAP does not explicitly endorse ICD-11 over DSM-5, but aligns with DSM-5 as the US diagnostic standard
- **Position on Diagnostic Categories:** The AAP states that "DSM-5 established a single category of ASD to replace the subtypes of autistic disorder, Asperger syndrome, and pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified" [AAP, 2020]
- **Regression Recognition:** "Approximately one-quarter of children with ASD will be reported to have a regression in language or social skills, most typically between 18 and 24 months of age" — the AAP recognizes regression as a clinically important feature [AAP, 2020]
- **Sensory Processing:** The AAP report highlights the inclusion of sensory processing issues in DSM-5 diagnostic criteria and the introduction of Social Communication Disorder as a separate diagnosis [AAP, 2020]
- **Medical Home Model:** The AAP advocates a medical home model where primary care providers coordinate diagnosis, intervention, and management of comorbidities [AAP, 2020]
- **Key Recommendation:** "Pediatricians should not wait for an autism evaluation or diagnosis for children to get the care they need" [AAP, 2020]
- **Prevalence Note:** The report cites 1 in 59 children (approximately 1.7%); CDC's 2023 estimate is **1 in 36** [AAP, 2020; CDC, 2023]

### C.4 NICE (UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence)

**Guideline Title:** "Autism spectrum disorder in under 19s: recognition, referral and diagnosis" (NICE Guideline CG128)

- **Publication and Review Status:**
  - Originally published: September 2011
  - Last updated: December 20, 2017
  - Last reviewed: June 14, 2021 — "We found no new evidence that affects the recommendations" [NICE, 2021]
  - The 2021 surveillance confirmed: "We did not identify scientific evidence that suggested guideline recommendations do not represent best practice, therefore we will not update the following guidelines on autism at this time"
- **Position on Diagnostic Criteria:** NICE explicitly states that diagnostic decisions should **"use ICD-11 or DSM-5 criteria"** — endorsing both as equally acceptable diagnostic frameworks [NICE CG128]
  - "Do not rely on any autism-specific diagnostic tool alone to diagnose autism; use clinical judgment based on ICD-11 or DSM-5 criteria" [NICE CG128]
  - Diagnosis should be "informed by information from a range of sources, together with clinical judgement" [National Autistic Society]
- **Timeliness Standard:** Diagnostic assessments should **start within 3 months of referral** [NICE CG128]
- **Sensory Processing:** NICE has **not issued specific guidance weighting sensory processing criteria differently** between ICD-11 and DSM-5. Both frameworks are endorsed equally
- **Terminology Updates:** The 2021 surveillance added editorial amendments including updating language to **"autistic people"** instead of "people with autism" [NICE, 2021]
- **Additional Updates:** The 2021 surveillance added guidance on feeding difficulties and restricted diets, and melatonin for sleep issues [NICE, 2021]
- **Underdiagnosis Recognition:** NICE acknowledged concerns about underdiagnosis in females and the need for specific consideration of protected characteristics [NICE, 2021]

### Summary of Clinical Guideline Positions

| **Organization** | **Primary Framework Endorsed** | **Position on ICD-11** | **Sensory Processing Stance** |
|------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **WHO** | ICD-11 (its own publication) | Full endorsement; 64 countries implementing | Included as diagnostic requirement for 1st time |
| **APA** | DSM-5/DSM-5-TR (its own publication) | Acknowledges ICD-11 alignment; no formal comparison statement | Criterion B4; part of RRBs |
| **AAP** | DSM-5 (as US reference) | No explicit endorsement of ICD-11; uses DSM-5 framework | Recognized as diagnostic feature |
| **NICE** | Both DSM-5 and ICD-11 equally | Full dual endorsement; use either | No differential weighting between systems |

## Section D: Advocacy Organization Positions on Neurodiversity-Affirming Diagnostic Criteria

### D.1 Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)

**Organizational Background:** ASAN is "run by and for autistic individuals" and explicitly operates within the neurodiversity movement. Their core principles state: "Autism is a developmental disability. Disability is a natural part of human diversity" [ASAN, "What We Believe"].

**Position on Diagnostic Criteria:**

ASAN was **extremely active in lobbying the DSM-5 revision process from 2009 to 2012**, though they have not published a formal comparison of DSM-5 versus ICD-11. Their advocacy focused on preventing the DSM-5 from narrowing access to diagnosis. Key positions from their Joint Statement with the Autism Society of America (ASA) include:

- "First, do no harm: it is essential that DSM-5 criteria ensure those who qualified under DSM-IV maintain access to an ASD diagnosis" [Joint Statement, ASAN & ASA]
- "Evidence suggests racial and ethnic minorities, women and girls, adults, and individuals from rural and low-income communities face challenges accessing diagnosis" [Joint Statement]
- "Enhancing access to diagnosis can result in substantial improvements in quality of life and more competent service provision" [Joint Statement]
- "Community engagement and representation within the DSM-5 process itself is a critical component of ensuring accurate, scientific and research-validated diagnostic criteria" [Joint Statement]

**Academic Analysis of ASAN's Impact:**

The Springer Nature Link chapter "Lobbying Autism's Diagnostic Revision in the DSM-5" documents that ASAN "successfully influenced the final criteria and accompanying text through extensive communication, advocacy, and evidence-based policy briefs" [Springer Nature, 2020]. Key outcomes included preventing narrowing of diagnosis, incorporation of considerations for marginalized groups, rejection of "recovery" criteria, and modifications to the severity scale to limit misuse. Despite lacking formal representation on the DSM-5 Workgroup, ASAN's efforts represent "a historic example of a disability community influencing its own diagnostic criteria" [Springer Nature, 2020].

**Implicit Position on ICD-11 vs. DSM-5:**

ASAN has not explicitly ranked the two systems. However, based on their advocacy principles:
- Their **engagement was exclusively with the DSM-5** given its primacy in US healthcare
- They would likely view ICD-11's **flexibility** (allowing clinician judgment rather than rigid thresholds) as more neurodiversity-affirming, as it reduces the risk of excluding individuals who don't fit narrow criteria
- They would likely view ICD-11's recognition of masking/camouflaging favorably, as it aligns with ASAN's concerns about diagnostic equity across gender [The Transmitter, 2017]
- However, ASAN would likely critique the **medical model orientation** retained in both systems
- ASAN's guiding principle of "Nothing About Us Without Us" underscores that neither system was developed with sufficient autistic representation

**Neurodiversity-Affirming Assessment:** ASAN would likely consider the **ICD-11 somewhat more neurodiversity-affirming** due to its flexibility, reduced thresholds, cross-cultural applicability, and recognition of masking. However, ASAN has not issued a definitive statement to this effect.

### D.2 National Autistic Society (UK)

**Organizational Background:** The National Autistic Society is the UK's leading autism charity, providing information, support, and advocacy.

**Official Guidance on Diagnostic Criteria:**

The National Autistic Society's official guidance page states: "An autism diagnosis should be based on criteria listed in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition (DSM-5) or the International Classification of Diseases 11th Revision (ICD-11), according to the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines" [National Autistic Society, "Criteria and tools used in an autism assessment"].

Key positions:
- **Presents both DSM-5 and ICD-11 as equally valid diagnostic frameworks**, following NICE guidance
- Does **not express a preference** for one manual over the other
- Notes that "the core characteristics of autism must be present throughout a person's life for a diagnosis"
- Emphasizes that diagnosis "should be informed by information from a range of sources along with clinical judgement—not solely diagnostic instruments"
- Notes that "some commonly used diagnostic tools still reference Asperger syndrome although it is no longer a diagnostic term"

**Position on Neurodiversity:**

The organization states: "Autism is a lifelong neurodivergence and disability affecting how individuals experience and interact with the world" and supports the neurodiversity and social models of disability [National Autistic Society, "What is autism"]. However, this neurodiversity-affirming language is applied broadly to autism itself, not specifically to comparing diagnostic manuals.

**Position on Sensory Processing:**

The National Autistic Society has a detailed resource on sensory differences that recognizes "sensory processing differences as a core aspect of autism" [National Autistic Society, "Autism and sensory processing"]. The page notes that "identification of sensory differences informs autism diagnosis, though sensory processing is not required for diagnosis but can support it." This aligns with both DSM-5 and ICD-11's inclusion of sensory features.

**Neurodiversity-Affirming Assessment:** The National Autistic Society **does not express a preference** between DSM-5 and ICD-11. Their position appears to be pragmatic: both are valid under NICE guidance, and either can be used for a neurodiversity-affirming diagnosis when applied with appropriate clinical judgment.

### D.3 Autism Speaks

**Organizational Background:** Autism Speaks is one of the world's largest autism advocacy and research organizations, primarily US-based.

**Position on Diagnostic Criteria:**

Autism Speaks has a webpage explaining **DSM-5 criteria** for a general audience, stating: "To meet diagnostic criteria for ASD according to DSM-5, a child must have persistent deficits in each of three areas of social communication and interaction plus at least two of four types of restricted, repetitive behaviors" [Autism Speaks, "Autism diagnostic criteria: DSM-5"]. This page explains DSM-5 criteria **without critique or evaluation**.

**Position on ICD-11:**

**No specific ICD-11 position statement, policy paper, or blog post from Autism Speaks was found** in any of the searches conducted. Autism Speaks does not appear to have published a comparative analysis of DSM-5 versus ICD-11, nor a position on which manual is more aligned with neurodiversity-affirming principles.

**Advocacy Priorities:**

Autism Speaks' advocacy priorities focus on: increasing federal investment in autism research; lowering the age of reliable diagnosis (from 3 years to between 18 and 24 months); early intervention services; and addressing the annual cost of ASD in the US (estimated between $162 billion and $367 billion) [Autism Speaks, "Advocacy priorities"].

**Historical Context:**

The academic literature notes that ASAN "has protested Autism Speaks for promoting policies that are harmful to autistic people, promoting stigma, and excluding autistic voices" [Wikipedia, ASAN]. This context is relevant because Autism Speaks has faced criticism from autistic-led organizations regarding its approach to autism representation and messaging.

**Neurodiversity-Affirming Assessment:** Autism Speaks has **not taken a public position** comparing the two diagnostic manuals. Their focus on early detection and intervention aligns with clinical utility rather than comparative diagnostic philosophy.

### D.4 Summary Comparison of Advocacy Positions

| **ASAN** | No explicit comparison | **ICD-11 (likely)** — based on inferred principles of flexibility, masking recognition, cross-cultural applicability | ASAN's DSM-5 advocacy history; implicit from core principles |
| **National Autistic Society** | No — both equally valid | **Equal** — follows NICE dual endorsement | Explicit guidance page |

ASAN's history and principles suggest they would likely view ICD-11's flexibility favorably, but this remains inference rather than explicit position.

## Section E: Diagnostic Safety Risks

### E.1 Overview of Opposing Diagnostic Safety Profiles

A critical finding of this analysis is that DSM-5 and ICD-11 create **opposing diagnostic safety risks**. The DSM-5's more restrictive counting approach primarily risks **underdiagnosis** (false negatives), while the ICD-11's broader, more permissive approach primarily risks **overdiagnosis** (false positives).

### E.2 Under-Diagnosis Risks

**Under DSM-5:**

- **DSM-5 criteria reduce ASD diagnoses by 19-44% compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R** across multiple studies. A Vanderbilt IRIS brief reports reduction rates ranging from **25% to 68%** depending on the population [Vanderbilt IRIS, 2014]
- **Higher-functioning individuals, females, and those previously diagnosed with PDD-NOS or Asperger's** are most likely to be under-identified [McPartland et al., 2012; Wilson et al., 2013]
- **McPartland et al. (2012):** Only 25% of individuals with Asperger's and 28% with PDD-NOS met proposed DSM-5 criteria
- **Wilson et al. (2013):** Only 56% of adults with clinical ASD per ICD-10R met DSM-5 criteria; the remainder were excluded
- **Maenner et al. (2014):** 81.2% of children with DSM-IV-TR ASD met DSM-5 criteria, meaning nearly **1 in 5 children** lost their diagnosis
- **DSM-5-TR change (2022):** Adding the word "all" to social communication criteria may further increase underdiagnosis, particularly for females who mask
- **Female underdiagnosis:** "Recent research shows that nearly **80% of autistic females are undiagnosed as of age 18**" [UCLA Health]
- **ADOS limitations:** "The ADOS totally overlooks the way in which many, able autistic girls, are able to 'mask' their difficulties" [Dr. Judy Eaton]

**Under ICD-11:**

- Paradoxically, ICD-11 may **reduce underdiagnosis** in some populations (females, adults, masking individuals) due to its broader criteria and flexibility
- However, the Kamp-Becker (2024) critique suggests that ICD-11's reliance on "inner experience" rather than observable behaviors may create new avenues for underdiagnosis when clinicians are uncertain about how to operationalize subjective criteria
- The lack of mandatory thresholds means diagnostic capture depends heavily on clinician training and familiarity with autism presentations

### E.3 Over-Diagnosis Risks

**Under ICD-11 (Primary Concerns):**

- **Kamp-Becker (2024) in *Molecular Psychiatry*** provides the most rigorous academic critique: "This bears a **large danger of false positive diagnoses**, of further increased prevalence rates, limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments"
- **Mechanism for overdiagnosis:** With 304 possible symptom combinations and no mandatory minimum threshold, "a diagnosis can be made if 1 feature of social-communication and 1 feature of repetitive behaviors are documented, with an onset of any time in life" [Autism Science Foundation Podcast]
- The broad criteria "is causing a lot of confusion in the community because the presentations are not specific to autism" [Autism Science Foundation Podcast]
- ICD-11 "moves away from an observable, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorder to a disorder of inner experience that can hardly be measured objectively, containing many vague and subjective concepts leading to **non-falsifiable diagnoses**" [Kamp-Becker, 2024]

**Under DSM-5:**

- Overdiagnosis concerns also exist under DSM-5. **52.8% of clinicians believe that over 10% of ASD diagnoses are concluded despite not fully conclusive assessments** [Key Autism Services]
- **Re-evaluation studies:** "Nearly half of children previously diagnosed with ASD did not meet formal diagnostic criteria upon standardized re-evaluation, underscoring the need for more careful and multidisciplinary diagnostic assessments" [PMC overdiagnosis paper]
- **Liao & Fombonne (2026) in *JAMA Pediatrics*:** "The growth in autism prevalence has had harmful repercussions, diverting important services from individuals and families who need help the most. Services become diluted and the diagnosis becomes trivial, while specific problems may be overlooked in efforts to address autism"

### E.4 Sensory-Focused Presentations: Specific Safety Risks

The **different weighting of sensory criteria** between the two systems creates unique diagnostic safety risks for individuals with prominent sensory presentations:

| **Risk Type** | **Under DSM-5** | **Under ICD-11** |
|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Sensory-dominant presentation misses diagnosis** | **HIGHER RISK** — an individual with primarily sensory symptoms may not meet threshold of at least 2 of 4 B criteria unless they also have other RRBs | **LOWER RISK** — sensory sensitivities are integrated as part of RRB domain requirement |
| **Non-autistic sensory sensitivity diagnosed as autism** | **LOWER RISK** — sensory features alone insufficient for B criterion | **HIGHER RISK** — broader inclusion may capture non-autistic individuals with sensory processing disorder (SPD) or other conditions |
| **Diagnostic specificity of sensory features** | Grapel et al. (2015): Only "failure to respond to noises" had sensitivity >0.75 but specificity was poor (0.34). Sensory features "have limited utility as diagnostic features for autism in school-age and beyond" | Same concern applies — sensory features "are also frequent in other disorders" [Grapel et al., 2015] |

**Critical Evidence from Grapel, Cicchetti & Volkmar (2015):**

This study of 776 school-age and adult individuals found:
- While sensory issues are common in ASD (**69-95%** of individuals), they are **also frequent in other disorders**, lacking sufficient specificity for diagnosis
- These results "question the rationale for including sensory items as a diagnostic criterion for autism"
- Sensory issues "may be important treatment targets but have limited utility as diagnostic features for autism in school-age and beyond"

**Implication:** The inclusion of sensory features in both diagnostic systems, while clinically important, carries an inherent risk of false positives. The ICD-11's stronger integration of sensory features as a diagnostic requirement may increase this risk, particularly for children with primary Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) who may not have autism.

### E.5 Populations Differentially Affected

| **Population** | **Primary Risk** | **Evidence** |
|----------------|------------------|--------------|
| **Females** | Underdiagnosis under both systems, but especially under DSM-5 | 80% undiagnosed by age 18 [UCLA Health]; DSM-5-TR "all" requirement may worsen this [Dr. Judy Eaton] |
| **Adults** | Underdiagnosis; late recognition | Many remain undiagnosed due to masking [Neurology Advisor] |
| **Higher-functioning individuals** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | 46% met DSM-5 criteria with IQ ≥70 vs 70% with IQ <70 [McPartland, 2012] |
| **Culturally diverse groups** | Underdiagnosis; cultural factors affect expression and recognition | ICD-11 designed for broader global applicability [The Transmitter, 2017] |
| **Minimally verbal / intellectual disability** | Better captured under ICD-11's detailed specifiers | ICD-11 provides detailed guidelines distinguishing autism with/without ID [The Transmitter, 2017] |
| **Sensory-dominant presentations** | Underdiagnosis under DSM-5; potential overdiagnosis under ICD-11 | Grapel et al. (2015) questions diagnostic utility of sensory features |

### E.6 Inappropriate Interventions Resulting from Misdiagnosis

- **Mental health consequences of missed diagnosis:** "Misunderstanding and misinformation around variant and differing presentations means that misdiagnosed individuals and those who do not receive a diagnosis at all are often failed by medical, education, social care and criminal justice systems" [Dr. Judy Eaton]
- **Mislabeling with stigmatized conditions:** "Misdiagnosis and lack of diagnosis can lead to severe mental health consequences, including mislabeling with stigmatized conditions such as Borderline Personality Disorder and adverse outcomes like inpatient admissions" [Dr. Judy Eaton]
- **Dilution of services from overdiagnosis:** "Services become diluted and the diagnosis becomes trivial, while specific problems may be overlooked in efforts to address autism" [Liao & Fombonne, 2026]
- **Non-specific treatments:** Overdiagnosis leads to "limitations of access to ASD-specific services and increasing the non-specificity of treatments" [Kamp-Becker, 2024]
- **SPD vs. autism confusion:** SPD affects 5-16% of school-aged children; sensory symptoms overlap substantially with autism. Individuals with primary SPD may be misdiagnosed as autistic under ICD-11's broader sensory inclusion, or autistic individuals with prominent sensory symptoms may be misdiagnosed as SPD under DSM-5 [KidsClubABA]

## Section F: Reimbursement and Coding Summary

### F.1 United States: Current Status

**ICD-10-CM F84.0 — "Autistic Disorder"**

The United States currently uses **ICD-10-CM** for all healthcare billing and reimbursement. The primary autism code, **F84.0 ("Autistic disorder")**, has remained unchanged since its introduction in the 2016 edition:

- **2026 Edition:** Effective October 1, 2025 [icd10data.com]
- **Definition:** Includes "autism spectrum disorder, Kanner's syndrome, infantile autism, and infantile psychosis"
- **DRG Grouping:** MS-DRG 884, "Organic disturbances and intellectual disability"
- **Related Codes:** F84.1 (atypical autism), F84.5 (Asperger's syndrome), F84.2 (Rett's syndrome), F84.3 (childhood disintegrative disorder), F84.9 (PDD unspecified)
- **Screening Code:** Z13.41 for autism screening, effective October 1, 2024 [Advanced Therapy Clinic; Key Autism Services]

**The United States Has NOT Adopted ICD-11 for Billing**

- The US adopted ICD-10-CM on October 1, 2015, taking over two decades after its WHO release
- ICD-11 came into effect globally on January 1, 2022, but has **"hardly been discussed in the United States"** for billing purposes [AHIMA]
- The National Committee on Vital and Health Statistics (NCVHS) established an ICD-11 Workgroup in 2023 to gather information and develop recommendations regarding US adoption [NCVHS Letter, 2021]
- **Estimated timeline:** Transition timelines are projected around 2025-2027 for mortality coding and possibly **2027-2029 for morbidity/billing**, with some experts estimating full implementation could take **10-15 years** [AHIMA; MedLearn]
- **Transition costs:** Estimated at **$1-3 billion nationally** due to complex mapping (only **23.5% direct code mapping** between ICD-10 and ICD-11) [MedLearn]

**Current US Payer Requirements:**

- **Private insurers uniformly require a diagnosis based on DSM-5 (or DSM-5-TR) criteria** for coverage of autism-related services [Cigna EVERNORTH Policy EN0499, effective December 15, 2025]
- The ICD-10-CM code F84.0 is the billing code submitted to insurers, but **the clinical diagnosis itself must be per DSM-5**
- **Cigna's policy explicitly requires:** "the individual has a confirmed diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) based on DSM-5-TR criteria" for ABA therapy authorization [Cigna EN0499]
- **Medicaid:** Covers ABA therapy in most states; mandates from CMS (2014) require state Medicaid programs to provide medically necessary diagnostic and treatment services for children with autism
- **Medicare:** Original Medicare (Part A/B) does not generally cover ABA therapy for adults; Medicare Advantage plans (Part C) may offer coverage on a plan-by-plan basis

**Does ICD-11 vs. ICD-10 Code Adoption Affect Coverage?**

**Currently, no** — because ICD-11 codes (6A02) are not billable in the US. Payers process ICD-10-CM codes. The clinical diagnosis criteria (DSM-5 vs ICD-11) only matters if an insurer specifically requires one framework. In practice, US insurers reference DSM-5 for medical necessity determinations.

**If/when ICD-11 is adopted:**

1. The unified code 6A02 will likely **equalize access** across the spectrum by eliminating subtype distinctions (F84.0 vs F84.5)
2. **Post-coordination severity specifiers** may create new documentation requirements and potentially new eligibility tiers
3. Short-term **billing disruptions** are expected during crosswalk periods
4. The DSM-5 vs ICD-11 alignment (now closely matched) means clinical diagnostic changes should be minimal
5. Historical precedent from the ICD-9 to ICD-10 transition shows "the change to the new coding system did not meaningfully affect autism rates at the participating healthcare systems" [PMC multi-site study, 2024]

### F.2 European Union: Current Status

**United Kingdom (NHS): ICD-11 Already Adopted Diagnostically**

- **NHS England has adopted ICD-11 criteria for autism diagnosis.** The NHS England National Framework (published April 5, 2023, updated November 27, 2023) explicitly states: "An autism diagnosis is a clinical judgment based on **ICD-11 criteria**; there are no diagnostic biomarkers" [NHS England Framework]
- The unified 6A02 code has **not created reported disruptions** in service eligibility. It may have expanded access by eliminating the Asperger's/autism distinction
- **Current crisis:** Over **125,000 people** wait for autism assessments in England, due to a 34% increase since 2021, exceeding clinical guidelines' recommended timelines [NHS England Framework]
- The framework emphasizes that services must be ethical, evidence-based, respectful, delivered by skilled multidisciplinary teams, comprehensive, and accessible

**Germany: ICD-10-GM Still for Reimbursement**

- Germany still uses **ICD-10-GM (German Modification)** for statutory health insurance (GKV) reimbursement via the **G-DRG system**
- **No official ICD-11 adoption date** for billing/reimbursement has been set in Germany
- The **G-BA (Federal Joint Committee)** is Germany's highest decision-making body in the statutory health insurance system, covering approximately **75 million people** under statutory insurance
- Clinical practice is increasingly aligned with ICD-11 and DSM-5 diagnostic concepts, even though billing codes remain ICD-10-GM
- Autism services are reimbursed through the G-DRG system for inpatient care and EBM (Uniform Evaluation Scale) for outpatient care
- **Key difference from US:** ABA therapy is **less consistently covered** under GKV than in the US; behavioral therapy, speech therapy, and occupational therapy are more standard

**France: Dual System**

- France uses **ICD-10 (CIM-10)** for administrative and statistical coding
- The **Haute Autorité de Santé (HAS)** — the national health authority — has updated its clinical recommendations based on **both DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria** [HAS, 2023]
- TSA (Trouble du Spectre de l'Autisme) is classified under neurodevelopmental disorders per both DSM-5 and ICD-11
- Reimbursement is through the **Sécurité Sociale** system
- France has implemented three national Autism Plans (2005-2017) and a 2018-2022 National Autism Strategy focusing on early intervention, inclusive education, and multidisciplinary care
- **Prevalence data:** 119,260 individuals with PDD identified through the SNDS database in 2017; crude prevalence of 17.9 per 10,000, steadily increasing over 2010-2017 [Santé publique France]

### F.3 WHO ICD-11 Implementation Status

- WHO officially stopped maintaining ICD-10 in 2018; future enhancements will only appear in ICD-11
- **Over 60 countries** have already adopted ICD-11 and evaluated its performance across different scenarios [WHO]
- ICD-11 is available as a global public good with free access under a specific license ensuring open use
- The transition timeline is flexible, with no penalties for delayed adoption, but prompt transition is encouraged

### F.4 Summary: Practical Impact on Reimbursement

| **System** | **Current Billing Code** | **ICD-11 Adopted for Billing?** | **Impact of ICD-11 on Coverage** |
|------------|-------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **US Private Insurance** | ICD-10-CM F84.0 | No — DSM-5 criteria required clinically | None currently; future adoption may equalize access |
| **US Medicare/Medicaid** | ICD-10-CM F84.0 | No | None currently; DRG restructuring expected |
| **UK NHS** | ICD-11 6A02 (diagnostically) | **Yes** — adopted for clinical diagnosis | Positive — unified code may expand access |
| **Germany GKV** | ICD-10-GM F84.x | No — clinical alignment only | None currently; DRG recalibration needed |
| **France Sécurité Sociale** | CIM-10 (ICD-10) | No — clinical alignment with both DSM-5 and ICD-11 | None currently; PMSI system updates needed |

**Key Takeaway:** Adopting ICD-11 criteria **does not currently affect coverage or reimbursement** in the US or most EU systems because billing codes have not transitioned. The NHS (UK) is the notable exception, where ICD-11 is already used diagnostically with reported success. The significant structural change — replacing categorical subtypes (F84.0, F84.5) with the unified code 6A02 — has the potential to equalize access across the spectrum but also requires substantial administrative and technological investment in transition.

## Conclusion

The DSM-5 (2013/2022-TR) and ICD-11 (2019/2022) represent a significant convergence in autism diagnostic criteria, both adopting a unified autism spectrum diagnosis that consolidates previously separate subcategories. Both systems now include sensory processing differences as diagnostically relevant features — a major advance from ICD-10, which did not recognize sensory issues as a core diagnostic criterion.

However, important differences remain. The DSM-5 employs a more restrictive, counting-based approach requiring deficits in all three social-communication domains and at least two of four restricted/repetitive behavior domains. The ICD-11 takes a more flexible approach, allowing diagnosis with fewer documented features and providing clinicians greater judgment latitude. These structural differences create opposing diagnostic safety profiles: the DSM-5 primarily risks underdiagnosis (particularly for females, higher-functioning individuals, and those who mask), while the ICD-11 risks overdiagnosis (false positives due to broad criteria and lack of specificity).

The evidence base for comparing the two systems remains limited. No direct head-to-head empirical comparison studies have been published as of May 2026, representing an urgent research priority. The available evidence — primarily studies comparing DSM-5 to earlier criteria — consistently shows DSM-5 reduces ASD diagnoses by 19-44% compared to DSM-IV-TR/ICD-10R, with higher-functioning individuals and those with Asperger's or PDD-NOS most affected.

Clinical guideline organizations have taken different positions reflecting their national contexts. NICE (UK) endorses both frameworks equally. The AAP (US) uses DSM-5 as its reference framework. The WHO promotes ICD-11 globally, with 64 countries at various implementation stages. The APA maintains DSM-5 as the US clinical standard while acknowledging ICD codes for billing.

Advocacy organizations have not issued definitive comparative judgments. ASAN's history of advocacy during the DSM-5 revision suggests they would likely view ICD-11's flexibility favorably, but no formal position exists. The National Autistic Society treats both systems as equally valid under NICE guidance. Autism Speaks has not taken a public position on ICD-11.

The reimbursement landscape is currently dominated by the fact that the US has not adopted ICD-11 for billing, and most EU countries (except the UK) still use ICD-10 for administrative purposes. When full ICD-11 adoption occurs, the unified code 6A02 and its post-coordination severity specifiers will likely reshape service eligibility and reimbursement structures.

For clinicians, the key practical consideration is that both systems can yield valid autism diagnoses, but awareness of their different risk profiles is essential. The DSM-5 requires careful attention to ensure individuals who mask or have atypical presentations are not excluded. The ICD-11 requires careful differential diagnosis to ensure individuals with autism-like traits from other conditions are not inappropriately diagnosed. In either system, clinical judgment, comprehensive multi-source information gathering, and sensitivity to diverse presentations remain paramount.
