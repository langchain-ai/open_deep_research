# Revised and Deepened Research Report: Legal Details of Slavery in the 1730s British Empire

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of slavery law in the 1730s British Empire, addressing five critical areas: (1) the nuanced, bifurcated legal status of enslaved people within individual colonial statutes; (2) the precise chronology of legal borrowing and adaptation between colonies; (3) the scholarly debate surrounding the Molasses Act of 1733; (4) recent peer-reviewed scholarship (2015–2025) on the legal dehumanization of enslaved people; and (5) the transformative role of the Colonial Debts Act (1732) as an instrument of imperial legal harmonization. The report prioritizes exact statutory language from primary sources and traces how enslaved people were simultaneously chattel (for debt recovery) and real estate (for inheritance) within single legal codes—a complexity that previous scholarship has often oversimplified.

# Part I: Intra-Colonial Legal Nuance — Bifurcated Classifications Within Single Statutes

## 1. Barbados: The Foundational Bifurcation (1668–1672)

### 1A. The 1661 Barbados Slave Code: "Goods and Chattels"

The 1661 Barbados Slave Code, formally titled **"An Act for the better ordering and governing of Negroes"** (passed September 24, 1661), established the foundational principle that enslaved people were chattel property. The original 1661 manuscript has not survived; the earliest extant version is the amended 1667 manuscript [1][2][3].

The preamble contains this exact statutory language:

> "...soe farr to protect them as wee doe mens' other goods & chattels..."

> "...an heathenish, brutish and an uncertaine dangerous kind of people..."

The preamble states that the law's purpose was to "protect them [slaves] as we do men's other goods and Chattels," establishing that Black slaves were to be treated as chattel property in the island's court [1][2][3].

**Key Provisions of the 1661 Code:**
- *Partus sequitur ventrem* established: "All children borne in this country shall be held bond or free only according to the condition of the mother"
- Trial by peers denied: "being brutish slaves, [they] deserve not, for the baseness of their condition, to be tried by the legal trial of twelve men of their peers, as the subjects of England are"
- Master's immunity for killing own slave: "if any Negro or other slave under punishment by his master unfortunately shall suffer in life or member, which seldom happens, no person whatsoever shall be liable to any fine therefore"
- Willful killing of own slave: fine to the Publick Treasury and binding to good behaviour [1][2][3]

### 1B. The 1668 Barbados Act: The Radical Reclassification to Real Estate

On April 29, 1668, the Barbados Assembly passed **"An Act declaring the Negro Slaves of this Island to be Real Estate"** — a statute that fundamentally altered the legal classification of enslaved people for inheritance purposes [4].

**The exact statutory language of the 1668 Act:**

**Preamble:**
> "Whereas a very considerable part of the Wealth of this Island consists in our Negro slaves, without whose Labour and Service we should be utterly unable to manage our Plantations..."

**Operative Clause:**
> "All Negro Slaves... shall be held, taken, and adjudged to be Estate real, and not Chattels, and shall descend unto the Heirs and Widows... in the Manner and Customs of Lands of Inheritance held in Fee-Simple."

This meant that for purposes of **inheritance and descent**, enslaved people were classified as **real estate** — descending to heirs under primogeniture and entail rules, not as movable chattels. Widows would receive dower rights (a life estate in one-third) in enslaved people, just as they would in land [4][5].

### 1C. The 1672 Barbados Declarative Act: The Explicit Bifurcation

The 1672 Act, formally titled **"A Declarative ACT upon the Act making Negroes Real Estate,"** is the clearest articulation of the bifurcated legal status in any British colonial statute [5].

**The exact statutory language:**

> "Whereas by an Act of this Island, bearing date the twenty-ninth day of April, one thousand six hundred sixty eight, it is enacted and ordained, That Negroes shall be deemed Real Estate and not Chattels; Be it declared... that Negroes may be sued for and recovered by Action personal, as they usually were before the making of the said Act: And also, that by the true meaning of the Act aforesaid, Negroes continue Chattels for the payment of Debts."

**The Act then declares the residual rule:**

> "it is the true intent and meaning of this Act, that Negroes shall be taken and deemed Real Estate, to all other intents and purposes whatsoever, except what before excepted."

Thus the legal regime in Barbados created a clear tripartite bifurcation:

| Legal Context | Classification | Consequence |
|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Inheritance/Descent** | **Real Estate** | Descended to heirs under primogeniture/entail; widows received dower rights |
| **Debt Recovery** | **Chattels** | Seizable as personal property to satisfy creditors |
| **Lawsuits/Recovery** | **Action Personal** | Could be sued for and recovered by personal action, not real action |

Both Acts are preserved in Richard Hall's 1764 compilation, *Acts Passed in the Island of Barbados From 1643-1762, Inclusive* [6], and also appear in *Acts of Assembly, passed in the Island of Barbadoes, From 1648, to 1718* (John Baskett, 1732) [7].

### 1D. Scholarly Context for Barbados

Jerome S. Handler, in "The status of enslaved Africans in seventeenth-century Barbados," argues that "The legitimization or legalization of African slavery and the status of slaves were established in custom long before any slave laws were passed." He identifies three major features: "chattel property, lifetime servitude, and inheritance of slave condition from an enslaved mother." Handler notes that "the 1668 law declared slaves to be estates real and not chattels only in cases of intestate ownership, implicitly affirming the existing recognition of slaves as property" and that "The 1661 Barbados law is the first explicit identification of slaves as chattel property" [8].

The Cambridge University Press chapter "Chattel" (from *Bonds of Empire*) notes that "American colonists understood that each particular property category unlocked different ways of proceeding at law that impacted their ability to buy and sell slaves and to shield them from creditors" [9].

## 2. Jamaica: From Real Estate (Inheritance) to Goods and Chattels (Debt)

### 2A. The 1696 Jamaican Act: "An Act for the better Order and Government of Slaves"

The 1696 Jamaica Act contains the critical property classification provision that created a bifurcation parallel to Barbados [10][11].

**The exact statutory language:**

> "for payment of Debts and Legacies all Slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other Goods and Chattels."

The Act also provides: "No Slave shall be free by becoming a Christian."

**What this meant:** The 1696 Act declared that for **inheritance (descent) purposes**, slaves were categorized as **real estate** (inheritable by heirs, subject to primogeniture and dower rights). But for **debt payment and legacies**, they were to be deemed "as all other Goods and Chattels" — seizable by creditors as movable property [10][11].

**Additional Key Provisions of the 1696 Act:**
- "If any Slave shall offer any Violence to any White Person, such Slave shall be punished at the Discretion of Two Justices and Three Freeholders, who may inflict Death or any other Punishment"
- "All Slaves shall have Cloths once every year, on or before the Twenty Fifth Day of December, upon Penalty of Five Shillings for every Slave's wanting"
- "If any slave or slaves shall run away and continue but for the space of twelve months... shall be deemed rebellious... which slave or slaves so taken... shall be transported"
- "No Masters or Overseers shall suffer any Druming or Meeting of any Slaves... to Rendezvous, Feast, Revel, or cause any other Disturbance" [10][11]

The secondary literature from the JStor article "Rights Talk and the Law of Property in Early Eighteenth-Century Jamaica" confirms: "As noted, a 1696 Jamaican statute categorized slaves as real estate for the purpose of inheritance after a decedent's... The Act 'abolished the legal distinctions between real property, chattel property, and slaves in relation to...'" [12].

### 2B. The 1717 Jamaican Act: "An Act for the more effectual punishing of Crimes committed by Slaves"

This 1717 Act further regulated punishment and compensation, providing:
- "no one Slave shall be appraised at above the Sum of Forty Pounds, current Money of this Island" (for compensation when judicially executed)
- "No Slave shall be dismembered at the Will and Pleasure of his Master... under the Penalty of One hundred Pounds"
- For runaways: "such Slave to be punished, by cutting off one of the Feet" [13]

### 2C. The 1809 Jamaican Consolidating Act

The 1809 Act (ANNO 50 GEORGII III. CAP. 21) consolidated earlier provisions and repeated the bifurcation:

> "That no slave shall be free by becoming a Christian; and for payment of debts and legacies all slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels..."

It also provided: "all children of slaves, born in the possession of tenant for life or years, shall remain or revert... as inheritance" and "all slaves assigned for dower shall be liable, proportionably... to the payment of debts, but not of legacies" [14].

This confirms that the **real estate for inheritance / chattels for debt** bifurcation remained in force throughout the 18th century in Jamaica.

## 3. South Carolina: Pure Chattel Slavery (with Debt Provisions)

### 3A. The 1712 South Carolina Act

The 1712 Act "for the better ordering and governing of Negroes and Slaves" established hereditary slavery (following the condition of the mother) and provided [15][16]:

> "as to payment of debts, shall be deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels"

The Act also provided: "It shall be... lawful for any Negroe or Indian Slave, or any other Slave or Slaves whatsoever, to receive and profess the Christian Faith... But... such Slave or Slaves shall receive and profess the Christian Religion, and be Baptized, he or they shall not thereby be manumitted or set Free" [15][16].

### 3B. The 1740 Negro Act: The Most Explicit Chattel Classification

The 1740 Act, passed in direct response to the Stono Rebellion of September 1739, contains the most explicit statutory language classifying enslaved people as chattel property in any British colony [17][18].

**The exact statutory language:**

> "all negroes, Indians, (free Indians in amity with this government, and negroes, mulatos and mestizos who are now free excepted)... shall be and they are hereby declared to be and remain for ever hereafter absolute slaves... and shall follow the condition of the mother; and shall be deemed, held, taken, reputed and adjudged in law, to be chattels personal in the hands of their owners."

**The phrase "chattels personal"** was the strongest statutory language for pure movable property in any British colonial slave code. Unlike Barbados and Jamaica, which created bifurcated systems, South Carolina's 1740 Act declared enslaved people to be "chattels personal" — pure movable property [17][18].

**However**, for debt purposes, the Act also provided:

> "as to payment of debts, shall be deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels... and may be taken by execution, for that end, as other chattels or personal estate may be."

**Key provisions of the 1740 Act:**
- Any slave found outside Charleston or plantation without a ticket or white person could be whipped up to 20 lashes
- Any slave assaulting a white person "may be lawfully killed"
- Slaves committing homicide against a white person "shall... suffer death"
- Wilful murder of one's own slave: forfeit £700 current money and "be rendered... incapable of holding... any office... within this Province"
- No slave could be taught to write (fine of £100)
- Evidence of slaves and free Indians, without oath, was admissible in trying slave-related crimes
- Criminal trials by two justices and 3-5 freeholders
- Restrictions on slave gatherings, drums, horns, and firearms [17][18]

### 3C. Scholarly Analysis of South Carolina's Approach

The Cambridge chapter "Chattel" (from *Bonds of Empire*) explains that "South Carolina colonists treated enslaved people as chattel property, at first by custom and later via statute" and that they "ultimately adopted pure chattel slavery to facilitate commercial transactions involving enslaved people and to expand their credit with British merchants" [9].

The South Carolina Encyclopedia notes: "By the early eighteenth century, African population surpassed the white population, leading to the 1712 act which made slavery hereditary. The 1740 Negro Act constructed slaves as legal nonentities" [19].

## 4. Virginia: Real Estate Classification (with Chattel Exceptions)

### 4A. The 1705 Virginia Act: "An Act declaring Slaves to be real Estate"

Virginia took the opposite approach to South Carolina. The 1705 Act declared enslaved people to be real estate for inheritance, while making them chattels for debt [20][21].

**The exact statutory language:**

> "FROM and after the Passing of this Act, Negroes, Mulottoes and Indian Slaves, shall be real Estate (and not Chattels) and shall descend to the Heirs and Widows of Persons departing this Life, as Lands of Inheritance in Fee."

**But the Act immediately created exceptions for debt:**

> "ALL Slaves shall be liable to the Payment of Debts, and may be taken in Execution for that End, as other Personal Estate may be."

**Other provisos included:**
- Imported slaves remained personal chattels until sold
- If owner died without heirs, slaves reverted as chattels (would not escheat)
- Sales need not be recorded but remained valid
- No owner gained "Liberties and Priviledges as a Freeholder of Land" from owning slaves
- Upon intestate death with multiple children, slaves "shall be inventoried and appraised, and the Value thereof shall be equally divided among all the Children"
- Widows could not send slaves out of the colony without reversion-holder's consent [20][21]

Thus Virginia created the mirror-image bifurcation of Barbados: **real estate for descent/inheritance, but chattels for debt execution.**

# Part II: Chronology of Legal Adaptation — Colony-to-Colony Borrowing and 1730s Updates

## 1. Barbados 1661 → Jamaica 1664: The First Legal Transplant

The Barbados Slave Code of 1661 was the foundational slave code of the Anglo-Atlantic world. Just three years later, this code was transplanted to Jamaica [22][23].

**The Jamaica 1664 Act:** Formally titled **"An Act for the better ordering & Governing of Negro Slaves,"** it was adopted by the Jamaica Assembly and consented to by Governor Thomas Modyford on November 2, 1664. The Act is described as "almost an exact replica of the 1661 Barbados law, illustrating the practice of legal borrowing in the early years of slavery law" [22].

**Governor Thomas Modyford's Role:** Governor Thomas Modyford was a former Barbados planter who had been Speaker of the Barbados Assembly. He brought several hundred fellow Bajan planters to Jamaica, accelerating Jamaica's transition to plantation agriculture. Slaverylawpower.org notes: "It is interesting to compare this document to the first Jamaican Slave code (1664), which was based, as the new governor there admitted, on the Barbados code of 1661" [3].

**Key provisions of the 1664 Jamaican Act:**
- "No Master or Mistresse...shall give theyre Slaves leave on Sundays holy days or in the night time to goe out of theyre plantations...without a ticket"
- "If any Slaves either Man or Woman shall offer any vyolence to any Christian...for his or her fyrst offense...severely whipped...for his second offense...nose slitt & hee burned in some part of his face, & for thyrd offense...greater corporall punishment as they shall thinke meet to inflict"
- "If any Negro shall make insurrection or rise in rebellion...the Governor of the Island...shall...proceed by Marshall Law against the Actors...and then punish by death or some other paines as theire crimes shall deserve"
- The act was to be read in all parish churches twice yearly [22][23]

## 2. Barbados → South Carolina (1690, 1696, 1712): The Mainland Transplant

### 2A. The 1690 Carolina Slave Code

The 1690 Carolina Slave Code was the **first comprehensive slave code enacted in a mainland British American colony**, heavily influenced by earlier Barbados and Jamaica slave codes [24][25].

**Key textual differences from the Caribbean codes:**
- **Dispensed with the preamble altogether** (unlike Barbados and Jamaica codes which had extensive preambles about "heathenish, brutish" people)
- **Uniquely used "White" to contrast enslaved people** instead of "Christians" as in the Caribbean codes — a critical shift toward race-based rather than religion-based categorization
- Addressed both **"Negroes" and "Indians"** as enslaved persons, reflecting Carolina's significant Indigenous enslaved population

Key provisions: "If any Nigro or Indian Slave shall offer any violence to any white person," severe and escalating corporal punishments applied, potentially leading to death; "No slave shall be free by becoming a Christian but shall be treated as property regarding debts" [24][25].

The code was **repealed in 1691** but influenced later versions in Carolina. A revised **1696 code** maintained the principles of the 1690 code, also based on Barbadian statutes [25].

### 2B. The 1712 South Carolina Slave Code

The 1712 Act was titled **"An ACT for the better ordering and governing of Negroes and Slaves"** and was ratified on June 7, 1712 [15][16].

**Textual Connections to Jamaica:** The Wikipedia article on South Carolina slave codes states that South Carolina established its first slave code in 1695 (or 1696) **based on the 1684 Jamaica slave code**, itself based on the 1661 Barbados Slave Code [19]. The JStor article on "The Development of Mastery and Race in the Comprehensive Slave Codes" notes: "the [South Carolina] Assembly adopted Jamaica's 1684 Slave Act as South Carolina's own. They added 'Indian' to the definition of slave and dropped a few provisions of the Jamaica law" [26][27].

Bradley J. Nicholson's article **"Legal Borrowing and the Origins of Slave Law in the British Colonies"** is the key source on this. The JStor summary notes: "By 1750, Barbados, Jamaica, South Carolina, Georgia, Maryland, and Virginia—four had either directly or indirectly borrowed their slave codes from Barbados" [28].

Edward B. Rugemer's "Slave Law and the Politics of Resistance" traces the interconnected legal histories: Barbados' 1661 Slave Act → Jamaican codes (1684, 1696) → South Carolina codes (1690, 1696, 1712, 1740) [27].

---

## 3. The Barbados 1688 Comprehensive Rewrite

The Barbados Slave Code was reenacted with modifications in 1676, 1682, and **1688** [1][3].

The 1688 revision was significant. The Slavery, Law, and Power website explains: **"This law was comprehensively rewritten in 1688, at which point Walrond's name and the date (1661) finally disappeared"** [3]. The Wikipedia article confirms: "The code was reenacted with minor changes in subsequent years (1676, 1682, 1688)" [1].

This was the version of the Barbados slave code that remained in force throughout the 1730s.

## 4. Jamaica 1696, 1717, and 1730s Statutes

### 4A. Jamaica 1696: "An Act for the better Order and Government of Slaves"

This Act repealed earlier legislation and established new comprehensive provisions, including the critical bifurcation discussed above: real estate for inheritance, goods and chattels for debt [10][11].

### 4B. Jamaica 1717/1718: Punishment and Compensation

"An Act for the more effectual punishing of Crimes committed by Slaves" (1717) and "An Act for the Encouragement of voluntary Parties to suppress rebellious and runaway Negroes" (1718) [13][29].

### 4C. Jamaica Acts in the 1730s

- **1725:** "An Act to inflict further and other Punishments on the Transgressors... for the preventing the transportation of Slaves"
- **1739:** "An Act for confirming the Articles executed by Colonel John Guthrie, Lieutenant Francis Sadler, and Cudjoe the Commander of the Rebels... and for granting Freedom to Five Negroes who were Guides to Parties" [29]

The 1739 Act was the treaty ending the **First Maroon War (1728–1739)** , in which the Jamaican government agreed to grant land and autonomy to the Maroons under Cudjoe in exchange for their agreement to return runaway slaves and cease hostilities [29].

**No comprehensive consolidated slave code was enacted in Jamaica during the 1730s.** The 1696 Act (as amended by the 1717 Act and subsequent legislation) remained the governing framework throughout the decade.

## 5. Antigua in the 1730s: The 1736 Conspiracy

The critical event in Antigua's 1730s was the **Antigua Slave Conspiracy of 1736** :

- 132 enslaved persons were convicted, and **88 executed** in the conspiracy trials (1736–1737), which lasted upward of seven months
- Methods of execution: **five were broken on the wheel, six were gibbeted, and 77 were burned at the stake**
- The conspiracy was allegedly led by **Prince Klaas**, who planned to use a 10-gallon barrel of gunpowder to blow up a ball, signaling a mass slave revolt
- However, historians dispute whether a coordinated plot actually existed. The evidence largely consisted of confessions obtained under duress, and no physical evidence (weapons or gunpowder) was found [30]

The Laws of Enslavement and Freedom database does not list a specific Antigua Act from the immediate years following the 1736 conspiracy in the 1730s, but the 1723 Act ("An Act for attainting several Slaves now run away from their Master's Service, and for the better Government of Slaves") remained in effect [31].

## 6. The 1732 British Imperial Debt Recovery Act: Overriding All Colonial Classifications

The British Parliament's 1732 Act (5 Geo. 2 c.7), "An act for the more easy recovery of debts in his Majesty's plantations and colonies in America," had a transformative impact on the classification of enslaved people across all colonies.

**The exact statutory language:**

> "From and after the 29th day of September 1732... houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments... shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts... and shall be assets for the satisfaction thereof, in like manner as real estates are by the law of England liable to the satisfaction of debts" [32][33].

The Act required that real property, houses, and **slaves** be treated as legally equivalent to chattel property for debt satisfaction, **overriding colonial laws that shielded real estate from creditors** [32][33].

The clause respecting the seizure of Negro slaves for debt was later repealed in 1797 by **37 George III Cap. CXIX**, which stated "so much of the said in Part recited Act, as relates to Negroes in his Majesty's Plantations, is hereby repealed and made void, and shall be of no Effect in future" [34].

# Part III: The Molasses Act (1733) — Scholarly Debate on Trade Regulation vs. Slave Control

## 1. The Act's Text: No Language About Enslaved People

The Molasses Act of 1733 (6 Geo. II, c. 13), formally titled **"An act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty's sugar colonies in America,"** contains **no mention whatsoever of enslaved people, slavery, or the slave trade** [35][36][37].

**What the Act actually does:**
- Imposes duties on foreign rum (9d/gallon), molasses (6d/gallon), and sugar (5s/hundredweight)
- Requires proper customs entries and payment of duties before goods can be landed
- Imposes penalties including forfeiture of goods, fines, and imprisonment for smuggling
- Was set for a duration of five years from June 24, 1733 [35][36]

**The Act's preamble states:**
> "Whereas the Welfare and Prosperity of Your Majesty's Sugar Colonies in America are of the greatest Consequence and Importance to the Trade, Navigation, and Strength of this Kingdom."

## 2. The Traditional Interpretation: Mercantilist Trade Regulation

The dominant and traditional interpretation treats the Molasses Act as a **mercantilist trade regulation and protectionist measure** designed to protect British West Indian sugar planters from French competition [35][36][37].

**Evidence for the trade regulation interpretation:**

**Parliamentary Intent:** In Parliamentary debate, Colonel Martin Bladen of the British Board of Trade stated that "the duties proposed would not prove an absolute prohibition, but he owned that he meant them as something that should come very near it, for in the way the northern colonies are, they raise the French Islands at the expense of ours, and raise themselves also too high, even to an independency" [37].

**Protection of British West Indian Sugar Planters:** The Act was passed largely due to pressure from British West Indies plantation owners to protect their economic interests. The Act specifically aimed at reserving a practical monopoly of the American sugar market to British West Indies sugarcane growers [35][36].

**Colonial Objections:** American colonists protested the Act, claiming that the British West Indies alone could not produce enough molasses. Richard Partridge, colonial agent, argued in 1733: "Besides the injury, it will be of in itself almost tantamount to a prohibition. It is divesting them of their rights and privileges as ye King's natural born subjects and Englishmen in levying subsidies against their consent when they ... have no representatives in Parliament" [37].

**Enforcement Failure:** Despite the Act, widespread smuggling and illicit trade flourished. Colonists preferred cheaper molasses from French West Indies (especially Saint Domingue/Haiti), which was up to 40% cheaper than British molasses. By the late 1750s, only 2,500 hogsheads of molasses reached Rhode Island from British sources, while 11,500 were landed illegally from competitors [37].

## 3. The Slave Control Interpretation: What the Evidence Shows

Research conducted for this report reveals that **the explicit framing of the Molasses Act as primarily a "slave-control measure" does not appear as a distinct, identifiable position in the existing scholarly literature.** The scholarship overwhelmingly treats the Act as a trade regulation/mercantilist protection measure [35][36][37].

**The connection to slavery is treated as contextual rather than causal:**

- **Triangular Trade Context:** Multiple sources document that the molasses trade was integral to the triangular trade. Molasses from the Caribbean was distilled into rum in New England, which was shipped to Africa to purchase enslaved people, who were then sold in the West Indies. As John Adams reflected in 1818, molasses was "an essential ingredient in American independence" [37].

- **The West India Interest:** The West India Interest, a British lobbying group representing Caribbean sugar trade and plantation owners, supported the Act. This group comprised West Indian colonial agents, merchants trading with the colonies, and plantation owners residing in London. They represented a slave-based economy [38].

- **Protection of Slave-Based Economy:** The Act protected a slave-based economy. But no source argues that the Act was specifically designed *to limit the growth of slavery* or *to control the slave trade*.

**A scholar wishing to argue the Act was "fundamentally about slave control" would be making a novel interpretive claim** — one that could rely on:
1. The Act's connection to the triangular trade
2. Its protection of a slave-based plantation economy
3. The role of the slave-owning West India Interest in lobbying for it
4. The fact that British West Indian sugar planters relied entirely on enslaved labor

**But this framing does not yet exist as an established position in the literature found.**

## 4. Key Scholarship on the Molasses Act

**Richard B. Sheridan** — "The Molasses Act and the Market Strategy of the British Sugar Planters," *The Journal of Economic History* (March 1957, Vol. 17, No. 1, pp. 62-83). This is the classic economic interpretation article specifically focused on the Act. Sheridan also wrote *Sugar and Slavery: An Economic History of the British West Indies, 1623-1775* (1974) [39][40].

**Christian J. Koot** — *Empire at the Periphery: British Colonists, Anglo-Dutch Trade, and the Development of the British Atlantic, 1621-1713* (NYU Press, 2011). # Part IV: Additional Peer-Reviewed Scholarship (2015–2025) on Legal Dehumanization

## 1. Holly Brewer, "Creating a Common Law of Slavery for England and its New World Empire," *Law and History Review* 39, no. 4 (November 2021): 765–834

This article is the most significant recent contribution to understanding the legal foundations of slavery in the British Empire [45].

**Key findings:**
- "The common law became an instrument of crown policy within a federal empire... English high court judges thus provided the legal foundation for an imperial common law of slavery that allowed people to be deemed absolute property."
- "These powerful legal mechanisms made it possible to 'recover' enslaved people as assets for debts, a legal definition that was essential for a market in people to function successfully."
- "American slavery therefore did not emerge 'beyond the line' of English justice, but within it."
- The article reveals that "the absolutist character of early capitalism" and illustrates "how the development of capitalist markets relied heavily on legal definitions that conferred property status on people."
- The article traces how Charles II and James II, unable to pass parliamentary slave codes, used courts to establish precedents that treated non-Christian, foreign peoples as absolute, hereditary property.
- The Royal African Company's bankruptcy in 1667 precipitated royal judicial interventions that made slave ownership legally enforceable across the empire.

This is an **open-access article** published in a priority journal (*Law and History Review*).

## 2. Dana Y. Rabin, "Slavery, Law, and Race in England and its New World Empire," *Law and History Review* (October 20, 2022)

This article critically engages with Holly Brewer's work, arguing that race must be centered as an analytical category [46].

**Key findings:**
- "Brewer traces a process begun by Charles I, in collaboration with his appointed judges, to legalize the buying and selling of people in England and its Empire."
- **Critique of Brewer:** "Yet race does not receive any engagement as a category of analysis. Missing is an account of the historical processes whereby laws about slavery contributed to racial formation."
- "Race was a 'feature, not a bug' of Lockean society post-1688," showing race was central to legal and societal structures.
- "The common law of slavery did not emerge neatly or coherently from abstract principles of English justice: it emerged in violent collision with colonial peoples of color."
- "Although the metropole may have been imagined as the exclusive producer and giver of law, the colonial legislatures and their governors made law and fed it back to London or across the imperial web to other colonial sites as slave law moved from the Bahamas, to Barbados, Virginia, South Carolina, and Jamaica."
- British law defined equal legal protection as a privilege afforded only to whites.

Published in a priority journal (*Law and History Review*).

## 3. Nicholas Leah, "Confronting the Yorke-Talbot Slavery Opinion and its Legacy within English Law" (2021)

This article provides an in-depth historical legal analysis of the **Yorke-Talbot Opinion of 1729** and its critical role in establishing and legitimizing slavery within English law and across the British Empire [47].

**Key findings:**
- Before this Opinion, the legal status of slaves varied greatly across colonies, with no uniform recognition of chattel slavery in England itself.
- The Opinion held that: "A Slave, by coming from the West-Indies to Great Britain or Ireland... doth not become free"; baptism did not confer freedom; owners could legally compel slaves to return to colonies.
- **Direct impact on slavery expansion:** "The number of slaves imported into Virginia increased from 211 in 1728 to 1,291 in 1732" and "Jamaica... more than doubled from 5,350 in 1728 to 13,552 in 1732."
- The Opinion was complemented by the **Colonial Debts Act of 1732**, which treated slaves as chattel property for debt recovery.
- George Berkeley sought the opinion to reassure slave owners that religious conversion did not alter the status of slaves.
- Lord Mansfield partially overturned Yorke-Talbot's pro-slavery stance in *Somerset v. Stewart* (1772).

## 4. Lee B. Wilson, *Bonds of Empire: The English Origins of Slave Law in South Carolina and British Plantation America, 1660–1783* (Cambridge University Press, 2021)

This book explores how English law gave the institution of slavery its ability to thieve and grow [9][48].

**Key findings:**
- "English law imbued plantation slavery with its staying power even as it insulated slave owners from contemplating the moral implications of owning human beings."
- "Most daily legal practices surrounding slave ownership were derived from English law: colonists categorized enslaved people as property using English legal terms, they bought and sold them with printed English legal forms, and they followed English legal procedures as they litigated over enslaved people in court."
- "Bonds of Empire ultimately shows that plantation slavery and the laws that governed it were not beyond the pale of English imperial legal history; they were yet another invidious manifestation of English law's protean potential."
- Wilson directly cites Claire Priest: "As Claire Priest has shown, English merchants were aware of this practice, and ultimately secured the passage of the Debt Recovery Act of 1732 as a result."
- **Chapter 1 ("Chattel"):** "American colonists understood that each property category unlocked different legal procedures impacting buying, selling, and creditor protections for slaves." South Carolina colonists "ultimately adopted pure chattel slavery to facilitate commercial transactions involving enslaved people and to expand their credit with British merchants."

## 5. Edward Rugemer, *Slave Law and the Politics of Resistance in the Early Atlantic World* (Yale University Press, 2018)

This book examines the development and eventual collapse of slave societies in seventeenth- and eighteenth-century English colonies, specifically Barbados, Jamaica, and South Carolina [27].

**Key findings:**
- "The success of the English colony of Barbados in the seventeenth century... spawned the slave societies of Jamaica and South Carolina."
- "A draconian system of laws and enforcement allowed slave masters to maintain control despite resistance and recurrent slave revolts."
- "In South Carolina, slaveholders exploited newly formed levers of federal power to deflect calls for abolition and to expand slavery."
- "In Jamaica... whites fought a losing political battle against Caribbean rebels and British abolitionists acting through Parliament."
- The comparative history spans two centuries, revealing the evolution of slave law and resistance leading to slavery's abolition via British legislation in one case and violent civil war in the other.

The book received the MacMillan Center's Gustav Ranis International Book Prize for Best Book.

## 6. Jennifer L. Morgan, "Partus sequitur ventrem: Law, Race, and Reproduction in Colonial Slavery," *Small Axe* 22, no. 1 (55) (March 2018): 1–17

This article examines how the 1662 Virginia slave code legally enshrined the principle that the status of a child followed that of the mother [49].

**Key findings:**
- "All children borne in this country shall be held bond or free only according to the condition of the mother—Partus Sequitur Ventrem."
- Maternity became legally and ideologically linked to property and race.
- The law "overrode traditional European patrilineal inheritance by prioritizing maternal descent to sustain hereditary slavery."
- "The child did not in fact always follow the condition of the mother, particularly if that child was seen as assimilable."
- "The law imposes order as it both configures those children as property and asserts its right to do so."
- The article emphasizes "the pain and violence of transforming kinship into property through racialized reproductive control, an underpinning of racial capitalism and colonial slavery."

Morgan is a MacArthur Fellow (class of 2024) and also published *Reckoning with Slavery: Gender, Kinship, and Capitalism in the Early Black Atlantic* (Duke University Press, 2021).

## 7. Christian R. Burset, *An Empire of Laws: Legal Pluralism in British Colonial Policy* (Yale University Press, September 2023)

This book offers a comprehensive analysis of Britain's use of law to shape its empire in the eighteenth century [50].

**Key findings:**
- After the Seven Years' War (1754–63), Britain shifted to a strategy of legal pluralism, allowing colonies to retain legal systems other than English law.
- "Policymakers thought English law could turn any territory into an anglicized, commercial colony; legal pluralism, in contrast, would ensure a colony's economic and political subordination."
- "Legal pluralism in the British Empire was not a given, but a choice—one that has profoundly shaped theories of law's role in economic development."
- The work provides the first empire-wide evaluation of legal policy in the British Empire.

## 8. John N. Blanton, "This Species of Property: Slavery and the Properties of Subjecthood in Anglo-American Law, 1619-1783" (Doctoral Dissertation, CUNY Graduate Center, February 2016)

This dissertation analyzes the evolution of Anglo-American slave law and the political debate over enslaved persons as property versus persons [51].

**Key findings:**
- "There was a long and vibrant debate over the legitimacy of the chattel principle – the definition of enslaved persons as a type of property."
- "Enslaved people and their allies pushed for the recognition of the legal humanity or subjecthood of the enslaved in colonial and metropolitan courts."
- "This antislavery legal tradition culminated in the famous Somerset decision, handed down by the Court of King's Bench in 1772."
- "Enslaved persons influenced the development of slavery by making claims on the local and imperial state through the courts, forcing Anglo-Americans to grapple with the contradictions inherent to property-in-man."

## 9. "English Societal Laws as the Origins of the Comprehensive Slave Laws of the British West Indies," in *Common Law, Civil Law, and Colonial Law* (Cambridge University Press)

This article examines the origins of comprehensive slave laws in the British West Indies, focusing primarily on the 1661 Barbados Slave Code [52].

**Key findings:**
- The Barbados code defined slaves legally as property (chattels or in some instances as real estate) with limited rights, subjecting them to harsh control and punishment.
- "The Barbadian Assembly decided to 'revive whatsoever wee have found necessary and usefull' in former laws and create their own where English laws were lacking."
- "The Barbados Code's preamble stipulated that the enslaved would be protected as 'other goods and chattels.'"
- "The Barbados Slave Code served as a progenitor for other slave laws throughout the British West Indies and North American colonies."

## 10. John Donoghue, "The Unfree Origins of English Empire-Building in the Seventeenth Century" (in *Brill*, 2015)

This chapter explores the unfree labor foundations of English empire-building in the seventeenth-century Atlantic [53].

**Key findings:**
- "The Navigation Act helped inaugurate England's rise as a slave trading Atlantic empire."
- "African slaves began outnumbering European indentured servants certainly by the early 1660s" in Barbados.
- Colonial transportation policies forcibly sent prisoners, political opponents, and Irish Catholics to labor in colonies, with indentured servitude described as "legally chattel bondage where servants were bought and sold without consent."

# Part V: The Colonial Debts Act (1732) — Imperial Legal Harmonization

## 1. Exact Statutory Language

The Colonial Debts Act of 1732 (5 Geo. II, c. 7) was formally titled **"An act for the more easy recovery of debts in his Majesty's plantations and colonies in America"** [32][33].

**Effective Date:** September 29, 1732.

**Preamble (Purpose):**
> "Whereas his majesty's subjects trading to the British plantations in America lie under great difficulties, for want of more easy methods of proving, recovering, and levying of debts..."

**Section on Affidavits (Procedural Mechanism):**
> "From and after the twenty ninth day of September one thousand seven hundred and thirty two...it shall and may be lawful...to verify or prove any matter or thing by affidavit or affidavits in writing upon oath...made before any Mayor, or other chief magistrate...in Great Britain..."

**CRITICAL SECTION – Chattel Provision for Enslaved People:**
> "The houses, lands, negroes, and other hereditaments and real estates situate or being within any of the said plantations belonging to any person indebted, shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts...and shall be assets for the satisfaction thereof...and shall be subject to the like remedies, proceedings and process...for seizing, extending, selling or disposing...towards the satisfaction of such debts, in like manner as real estates are by the law of England liable to the satisfaction of debts" [32][33].

## 2. Mechanism of Operation

The Act operated as a powerful tool of imperial legal harmonization through **two core mechanisms**:

**A. Procedural Harmonization (Debt Proof):** The Act authorized creditors in Great Britain to prove debts owed by colonial planters through sworn affidavits made before magistrates in Britain, without requiring in-person testimony in colonial courts. These affidavits were given the same legal force and effect as viva voce (live) testimony in open court. This dramatically reduced the cost and complexity of British merchants suing colonial debtors.

**B. Substantive Harmonization (Debt Recovery – Chattelization of Enslaved People):** The Act mandated that **houses, lands, and negroes (enslaved people)** in the colonies "shall be liable to and chargeable with all just debts" and treated as assets for debt satisfaction "in like manner as real estates are by the law of England liable to the satisfaction of debts."

## 3. Overriding Local Colonial Classifications

Claire Priest explains that the 1732 Act **"abolished legal distinctions between land and chattel property in relation to the claims of creditors"** across all British colonies [54][55].

This was a direct override of local colonial laws that had classified enslaved people differently:

- **Virginia (1705):** Had declared slaves "real Estate...and not Chattels" for inheritance, but made them liable for debts "as other Personal Estate." The 1732 Act went further by making them fully seizable.
- **Jamaica (1696):** Had treated slaves as real estate for inheritance and goods and chattels for debt. The 1732 Act confirmed and imperialized the debt treatment.
- **Barbados (1672):** Had already established the bifurcation. The 1732 Act imposed a uniform imperial framework.

Priest emphasizes: "The Act promoted the slave trade by ensuring that English merchants who lent money for slave imports would be protected from strategic behavior by planters to avoid paying their debts" [55].

The Act "required that slaves, land, and houses be legally treated like chattel (ordinary goods) in credit agreements" [55].

## 4. Claire Priest's Scholarship

### 4A. "Creating an American Property Law: Alienability and Its Limits in American History," *Harvard Law Review* 120, no. 2 (2006): 385–459

This is Priest's landmark article analyzing the Colonial Debts Act of 1732 in depth [54].

**Key Findings:**
- "Traditional English law protected an individual's freehold interest in land from unsecured creditors both during life and inheritance proceedings."
- "In 1732, Parliament enacted the Act for the More Easy Recovery of Debts in His Majesty's Plantations and Colonies in America, treating real property, houses, and slaves as chattel for debts."
- "The Act dismantled English inheritance protections and prioritized unsecured creditors' claims to land over heirs."
- "The legal transformation contributed to greater commodification of real property, expansion of slavery, and increased capital for economic development."
- "Under the Act, land and slaves were treated as legally equivalent to chattel property for debt recovery."

### 4B. *Credit Nation: Property Laws and Institutions in Early America* (Princeton University Press, 2021)

Priest's book examines how colonial America laid foundations for American capitalism through property laws prioritizing credit access [55].

**Key Findings:**
- "Colonial governments created local legal institutions that enabled people to further leverage their assets to obtain credit."
- "Loans backed with slaves as property fueled slavery from the colonial era through the Civil War."
- "Increased access to credit was key to the explosive growth of capitalism in nineteenth-century America."
- "British Parliament enacted laws that deviated from traditional English protections of land and inheritance, instead privileging creditors by designating land and slaves as commodities to satisfy debts."

**Notable praise:** Katharina Pistor wrote: "Claire Priest's carefully researched book places the development of property rights in the United States in the context of the colonial economy and its dependence on debt finance and slaves as collateral."

## 5. Impact and Significance

### 5A. Interaction with Local Colonial Laws

The Act created a significant legal disjuncture because local colonial laws had treated enslaved people differently for different purposes. As Priest explains: "The Act's central significance was overriding local colonial classifications — if slaves were treated as real estate for inheritance in one colony, the 1732 Act required chattel treatment for debt recovery."

Lee B. Wilson's *Bonds of Empire* (2021) confirms: "Different classifications carried distinct legal consequences affecting transactions and creditor protections" [9].

### 5B. Effect on Colonial Economies and Slaveholding Practices

- **Expansion of Credit Markets:** By making enslaved people seizable assets for debt, the Act enabled British merchants to extend more credit to planters. Eleanor Brown explains: "The Debt Recovery Act made it possible for creditors of colonial debtors to execute debt judgments on real property as well as on enslaved people for the recovery of debts" [56].

- **Fueling the Slave Trade:** Priest emphasizes that "The Act promoted the slave trade by ensuring that English merchants who lent money for slave imports would be protected from strategic behavior by planters to avoid paying their debts" [55].

- **Commodification of Human Beings:** The Act legally "abolished legal distinctions between land and chattel property in relation to the claims of creditors," meaning that for debt purposes, enslaved people were legally equivalent to goods, livestock, and other movable property.

- **Post-Independence Continuity:** "Most states reenacted the Act for the More Easy Recovery of Debts in the founding era, exempting minimal amounts of property from creditors." This meant the Act's framework continued to shape American property law through the 1840s.

### 5C. The 1797 Repeal

The 1797 Act (37 George III, c. 119) that repealed the chattel provision of the 1732 Act specifically "for negroes" shows that the provision had become politically contentious by the late 18th century, as the abolition movement gained strength in Britain. The repeal act declared: "so much of the said in part recited Act, as relates to Negroes in his Majesty's Plantations, is hereby repealed and made void, and shall be of no Effect in future" [34].

### 5D. Jefferson's Annotations

The Slavery Law & Power website documents that Thomas Jefferson, as a colonial legal apprentice, closely annotated the Act. As an American statesman after the Revolution, Jefferson "worked toward ensuring that individual landowners and their heirs retained their inviolable 'natural rights' to property; a view that would clash with 'commercial republicans' during the partisan debates of the 1790s" [33].

## 6. Scholarly Consensus

The scholarship demonstrates that the 1732 Act was one of the most significant Parliamentary interventions into colonial property law in the 1730s, and a key exception to the general pattern of Parliamentary non-intervention in slavery regulation. As Claire Priest argues, it "substantially dismantled the legal framework of the English inheritance system by giving unsecured creditors priority to a deceased's land over heirs" [54]. The Act functioned as a **unifying legal mechanism** across the fragmented colonial slave codes, creating a uniform imperial framework for treating enslaved people as assets that could be reached by creditors across colonial boundaries.

# Conclusion: Implications for the Historical Novel

The legal landscape of slavery in the 1730s British Empire was characterized by **complexity, contradiction, and fragmentation** — not a uniform system of chattel slavery. The most important finding for historical accuracy is the **bifurcated legal status** within individual colonies:

1. **In Barbados and Jamaica:** Enslaved people were simultaneously **real estate** (for inheritance) and **chattels** (for debt recovery). A character could legitimately argue that slaves "descend to the heir like land itself" or, equally, that they "may be seized and sold for debts like any other goods."

2. **In South Carolina:** The 1740 Act (just after the 1730s) established **pure chattel slavery** — the most explicit statutory language in the British Empire.

3. **The Colonial Debts Act of 1732** overrode all local classifications, making slaves seizable for debt across all colonies — a powerful assertion of imperial authority.

4. **The Molasses Act of 1733** was entirely about trade regulation and contained no language about enslaved people. However, it operated within the triangular trade system that connected molasses, rum, and the slave trade.

5. **The Yorke-Talbot Opinion (1729)** — while not a court judgment — was widely cited to support the claim that slaves remained property even in England, and was followed by a sharp increase in slave imports.

For the novelist, this complexity offers rich dramatic possibilities: characters could debate the legal status of enslaved people, lawyers could argue about whether slaves were "goods and chattels" or "real estate," and the imperial reach of the 1732 Act could drive plot complications about debt, inheritance, and the power of British merchants over colonial planters.
